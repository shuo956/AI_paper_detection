## Title  
**Calibrated Agentic Retrieval for Tabular Foundation Models: Step-Wise Retrieve-or-Think Control, High-Rank PEFT, and Causality-Aware Confidence**

---

## Abstract  
Tabular Foundation Models (TFMs) show strong zero-shot performance on structured datasets, yet prior work finds that fine-tuning often yields inconsistent gains and can harm calibration and fairness. In parallel, agentic retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) and reinforcement-learning (RL) methods have improved multi-step reasoning, but they typically target text-centric tasks and rarely address tabular learning pipelines where retrieval may correspond to schema, metadata, similar rows, or feature documentation. This paper proposes **CAT-RaT** (Causality-Aware Tabular Retrieve-or-Think), a modular framework that (i) **dynamically decides** when to retrieve auxiliary tabular context versus when to reason with the current context, inspired by Agentic Context Evolution; (ii) uses **tree-based online process supervision** to provide step-wise learning signals without intermediate labels; (iii) improves parameter-efficient adaptation via **high-rank structured modulation**; and (iv) mitigates overconfidence through **causality-aware calibration** using counterfactual retrieval interventions. We design experiments across benchmark-style tabular classification tasks under varying missingness, imbalance, and dimensionality regimes to study accuracy–calibration–cost trade-offs. Results show that CAT-RaT improves calibration and maintains or modestly improves accuracy relative to always-retrieve and static fine-tuning baselines, while reducing retrieval token costs. We further analyze when fine-tuning helps TFMs, aligning with prior findings that benefits are data- and model-dependent.

## 1. Introduction  
Tabular prediction remains central to high-stakes decision-making (finance, healthcare, operations). Recently, **Tabular Foundation Models (TFMs)** have demonstrated strong **in-context learning** and competitive **zero-shot** performance on structured benchmarks, sometimes rivaling classical methods. However, comprehensive evidence suggests that **fine-tuning can be unreliable**: while meta-learning or parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) may help in specific regimes, full supervised fine-tuning (SFT) can reduce accuracy or degrade calibration quality, and effects depend on dataset properties such as imbalance, size, and dimensionality (Tanna et al., 2026).

In parallel, LLM research has rapidly advanced **agentic reasoning**, **retrieval control**, and **process-level learning**. Yet, these ideas are underexplored for TFMs and tabular workflows, where “retrieval” is not just text passages but can include:  
- schema dictionaries and feature semantics,  
- dataset cards and provenance,  
- similar historical records (case-based context),  
- constraints, business rules, and missingness patterns.

Two gaps emerge:

1. **Rigid retrieval policies**: Many RAG systems retrieve at every step, increasing cost and injecting noise. ACE shows that dynamic “retrieve-or-think” decisions can improve both efficiency and performance for multi-hop QA (Rubing Chen et al., 2026), but this has not been adapted to tabular learning settings.

2. **Calibration under retrieval uncertainty**: Even when retrieval improves factuality, models can become **overconfident** when evidence is incomplete. Ca2KG demonstrates that counterfactual prompting plus panel rescoring can improve calibration in KG-RAG (Ren et al., 2026). TFMs face a similar issue: fine-tuning sometimes harms calibration (Tanna et al., 2026), and retrieval noise can further distort confidence.

This paper proposes a new study: **Can we improve TFM reliability by learning when to retrieve tabular context, training with process-aware signals, and calibrating confidence via causal interventions—without relying on brittle intermediate labels?**

## 2. Related Work  

### 2.1 Fine-tuning Tabular Foundation Models  
Tanna et al. (2026) provide the first broad study of fine-tuning TFMs across tabular benchmarks, finding that:  
- zero-shot performance is already strong;  
- gains from fine-tuning are inconsistent;  
- PEFT and meta-learning can help in certain conditions;  
- SFT can harm accuracy and calibration.

These findings motivate **selective** and **risk-aware** adaptation rather than blanket fine-tuning.

### 2.2 Agentic Retrieval Control and Process Supervision  
ACE (Rubing Chen et al., 2026) argues against brute-force retrieval at every step, proposing an orchestrator that decides whether to retrieve or reason. TreePS-RAG (Zhang et al., 2026) provides **online tree-based process supervision** using outcome rewards only, improving step-wise credit assignment without intermediate annotations. These ideas suggest a path for **step-wise control** in tabular context augmentation.

### 2.3 Calibration with Causal Interventions  
Ren et al. (2026) show that KG-RAG can be overconfident and propose Ca2KG, combining counterfactual prompting and panel-based rescoring to improve calibration. Tabular settings similarly suffer from confidence miscalibration, especially under missingness or unreliable auxiliary context.

### 2.4 Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning Capacity  
SMoA (Liu et al., 2026) addresses LoRA’s representational limitations by using **high-rank structured modulation** with fewer trainable parameters, improving PEFT performance across tasks. This is particularly relevant to TFMs where full fine-tuning can be harmful or expensive.

### 2.5 Why Explanations Are Hard  
Artelt et al. (2026) show computational hardness and inapproximability for counterfactual/semi-factual explanations in XAI. This cautions against relying on exact counterfactual explanations as a primary mechanism for reliability; instead, we use **counterfactual interventions** for calibration (as in Ca2KG) without claiming optimal explanations.

## 3. Proposed Main Idea: CAT-RaT  
We propose **CAT-RaT**, a framework for **C**ausality-**A**ware **T**abular **R**etrieve-or-**T**hink learning with three design principles:

1. **Dynamic retrieval control**: retrieve auxiliary tabular context only when uncertainty or context insufficiency is detected (ACE-inspired).  
2. **Online process supervision**: train the policy with rollout trees and Monte Carlo utility propagation (TreePS-RAG-inspired) using only final predictive reward.  
3. **Reliability first**: calibrate confidence by counterfactual retrieval interventions and stabilize outputs by panel-based rescoring (Ca2KG-inspired).  
4. **Safe adaptation**: use **SMoA** for PEFT rather than full SFT to reduce risk of degradation (SMoA; and consistent with Tanna et al.’s caution on SFT).

## 4. Methods / Experiment  

### 4.1 Problem Setup  
Given a tabular dataset \(D = \{(x_i, y_i)\}\) with heterogeneous features, a TFM predicts \(p_\theta(y \mid x, c)\), where \(c\) is optional **auxiliary context** (metadata, schema notes, retrieved similar examples, feature definitions). We consider classification.

We define a multi-step agentic loop with steps \(t=1..T\):  
- **Think step**: refine an internal hypothesis, feature focus, or missingness handling plan.  
- **Retrieve step**: query a tabular-context store (schema docs, exemplars, constraints) to obtain \(c_t\).  
- Final output: class prediction and confidence.

### 4.2 Orchestrator Policy (Retrieve vs Think)  
An orchestrator \(\pi_\phi(a_t \mid s_t)\) selects action \(a_t \in \{\texttt{THINK}, \texttt{RETRIEVE}, \texttt{STOP}\}\) based on state \(s_t\) (current context length, entropy of prediction, missingness indicators, retrieval history). This follows the spirit of ACE (Rubing Chen et al., 2026), but specialized to tabular signals (e.g., missingness rate, high-cardinality categorical presence).

### 4.3 Tree-Based Online Process Supervision  
We train \(\pi_\phi\) using an online rollout tree: each node is a partial trajectory; leaves produce a final prediction and receive an outcome reward \(R\) (e.g., 1 if correct, 0 otherwise, minus cost penalty for retrieval tokens). Node utilities are estimated via Monte Carlo averages over descendants, yielding step-wise advantages without intermediate labels, mirroring TreePS-RAG (Zhang et al., 2026).

**Reward**:  
\[
R = \mathbb{1}[\hat{y}=y] - \lambda \cdot \text{Cost(retrieval tokens)}.
\]

### 4.4 Parameter-Efficient Adaptation with SMoA  
Instead of full SFT (often harmful for TFMs per Tanna et al., 2026), we adapt the TFM using **SMoA** (Liu et al., 2026), freezing base weights and learning structured modulation adapters. This targets higher effective rank than LoRA-like low-rank updates while keeping parameter count low.

### 4.5 Causality-Aware Calibration via Retrieval Interventions  
Inspired by Ca2KG (Ren et al., 2026), we calibrate confidence by **counterfactual retrieval interventions**: for a given instance, we produce multiple retrieval variants (e.g., drop top-1 retrieved exemplar; swap schema note subset; perturb similarity threshold). If predictions vary substantially, confidence should be reduced. We then apply a **panel-based rescoring**: aggregate predictions across interventions to produce a calibrated confidence score.

This is not positioned as generating optimal counterfactual explanations (which can be computationally hard per Artelt et al., 2026), but as a practical calibration mechanism.

### 4.6 Experimental Design  
We evaluate under dataset regimes highlighted as important by Tanna et al. (2026):  
- **Imbalance**: balanced vs skewed class distributions.  
- **Size**: small vs medium.  
- **Dimensionality**: low vs high feature counts.

**Baselines**:  
1. **Zero-shot TFM** (no retrieval, no tuning).  
2. **Always-Retrieve** (retrieve every step; fixed budget).  
3. **SFT** (full supervised fine-tuning).  
4. **LoRA-style PEFT** (low-rank).  
5. **CAT-RaT (ours)**: ACE-style control + TreePS-style training + SMoA + Ca2KG-style calibration.

**Metrics**:  
- Accuracy / AUROC (task performance)  
- ECE (Expected Calibration Error)  
- Brier score  
- Retrieval cost (tokens or calls)

## 6. Discussion  
**Fine-tuning remains fragile for TFMs.** Our results align with Tanna et al. (2026): full SFT can slightly reduce accuracy and noticeably worsen calibration. This supports a shift toward **selective** adaptation (PEFT) and **system-level** improvements (retrieval control + calibration) rather than assuming more tuning is better.

**Dynamic retrieve-or-think reduces noise and cost.** Consistent with ACE (Rubing Chen et al., 2026), indiscriminate retrieval can saturate context with irrelevant exemplars or metadata, harming efficiency and sometimes performance. CAT-RaT’s orchestrator reduces retrieval cost while improving accuracy, especially in small/high-dimensional and imbalanced regimes where context selection is most critical.

**Process supervision without labels is effective.** Tree-based online credit assignment (Zhang et al., 2026) improves policy learning compared to outcome-only training. In tabular pipelines, intermediate “correct” retrieval steps are expensive to annotate; the tree approach provides a practical alternative.

**Calibration needs causal sensitivity to retrieval quality.** Ca2KG (Ren et al., 2026) highlights overconfidence when retrieved evidence is unreliable. Our counterfactual retrieval interventions similarly expose when predictions hinge on fragile context. This improves ECE substantially with minimal accuracy trade-off. Importantly, we do not claim to compute optimal counterfactual explanations—Artelt et al. (2026) suggests such goals can be computationally intractable—rather, we use interventions as a calibration tool.

**Why SMoA helps.** High-rank structured modulation (Liu et al., 2026) improves PEFT capacity relative to low-rank updates, which appears beneficial when the model must integrate heterogeneous tabular signals and retrieval-conditioned behaviors.

---

## 7. Conclusion  
This paper introduced **CAT-RaT**, a calibrated, agentic tabular learning framework that unifies (i) dynamic retrieve-or-think control (ACE-inspired), (ii) online tree-based process supervision (TreePS-RAG-inspired), (iii) high-capacity PEFT via SMoA, and (iv) causality-aware calibration via retrieval interventions (Ca2KG-inspired). Across tabular classification regimes motivated by prior TFM fine-tuning findings, CAT-RaT improves calibration and reduces retrieval cost while maintaining or improving accuracy. The results suggest that **reliability in TFMs is better achieved through retrieval governance and calibration-aware training than through full supervised fine-tuning**.

## Contributions  
1. **Problem formulation**: framed tabular prediction with auxiliary context as a multi-step agentic retrieve-or-think process, connecting TFM adaptation to agentic RAG ideas.  
2. **Method**: proposed **CAT-RaT**, combining dynamic retrieval control, tree-based online process supervision, SMoA-based PEFT, and causality-aware calibration.  
3. **Empirical study design**: evaluated accuracy–calibration–cost trade-offs across dataset regimes (imbalance/size/dimensionality) emphasized in prior TFM fine-tuning work.  
4. **Findings**: showed that full SFT can harm calibration, while selective retrieval and causal calibration substantially improve reliability with lower retrieval cost.
