## Title  
**Foreshadowed Feelings: Codifying Setup–Trigger–Payoff Structure for Interpretable Multimodal Emotion Understanding with Large Language Models**

---

## Abstract  
Multimodal emotion understanding systems increasingly integrate large language models (LLMs) for flexible prediction and explanation, yet they often remain weak at (i) producing faithful, verifiable rationales and (ii) maintaining long-range causal consistency between earlier context and later emotional outcomes. Prior work on EEG-to-LLM affective reasoning introduces interpretable instruction-tuned pipelines for neural signals, while narrative generation research shows that LLMs frequently fail to realize foreshadowed commitments unless narrative mechanics are codified as executable predicates. Motivated by these limitations, we propose **SETUP2AFFECT**, a new research framework that **codifies emotional setup–trigger–payoff structure** across multimodal inputs (text + image, optionally physiological signals) and trains an LLM-based model to (1) predict emotion and sentiment, and (2) generate **structured, auditable explanations** grounded in explicit triggers. We design a lightweight training recipe that combines expert-guided multimodal fusion, instruction tuning, and a secondary self-critique stage to reduce spurious rationales. Experiments on a constructed benchmark derived from existing multimodal emotion settings demonstrate consistent gains in payoff-consistency and explanation precision with minimal loss in raw classification accuracy. Our study bridges codified narrative dependency modeling with affective computing, suggesting that explicit causal codification improves both interpretability and robustness.

## Introduction  
Emotion understanding from multimodal signals is central to affective computing, moderation, education, and human–AI interaction. Recent systems increasingly couple multimodal encoders with LLMs to unify classification and generation, improving flexibility and cross-lingual transfer (e.g., expert-guided multimodal fusion with pseudo-token injection and prompt conditioning) (Qiao et al., 2026). In parallel, EEG-focused MLLM frameworks show that careful alignment and instruction tuning can yield interpretable affective reasoning from neural signals (Ma et al., 2026).

However, two gaps persist:

1. **Long-range affective consistency is under-modeled.** LLMs can describe emotions fluently but may fail to connect early contextual “setups” to later emotional “payoffs,” analogous to unfired “Chekhov’s guns” in story generation (Yun et al., 2026). In emotion-heavy dialogues or narratives, this yields explanations that sound plausible yet do not reflect the actual causal chain.

2. **Interpretability is often rhetorical rather than auditable.** Even when models provide rationales, they can be post-hoc and error-prone. Self-reflective critique has been shown to reduce false positives and misinterpretations in automated qualitative coding (Dunivin et al., 2026), and citation-grounded generation improves verifiability in summarization (Droste et al., 2026). Yet emotion systems rarely incorporate structured verification of “why” an emotion is predicted.

### Main idea  
We propose that **emotion understanding should explicitly model setup–trigger–payoff structure**: early cues establish affective commitments (setup), later events activate them (trigger), and the final emotional state should resolve them (payoff). We operationalize this via **codified causal predicates** (inspired by CFPG) and integrate them into an LLM-based multimodal emotion framework.

## Related Work  

### LLMs for interpretable emotion understanding  
E²-LLM introduces a multi-stage pipeline (emotion-discriminative pretraining, cross-modal alignment, instruction tuning with chain-of-thought) to bridge EEG signals and interpretable affect analysis (Ma et al., 2026). Our work shares the goal of interpretable affective reasoning, but targets **causal consistency** of explanations across long context and multimodal cues.

EGMF unifies emotion recognition and sentiment analysis by expert-guided multimodal fusion and LLM conditioning, enabling a single generative framework for classification/regression (Qiao et al., 2026). We build on this fusion insight but add **codified narrative-style supervision** to improve causal faithfulness.

### Codifying long-range dependencies  
CFPG shows that explicitly encoding Foreshadow–Trigger–Payoff triples as executable predicates improves payoff realization in generated narratives (Yun et al., 2026). We adapt the core idea—**codification of long-range commitments**—to affective trajectories rather than plot events.

### Self-reflection and verifiability  
Secondary LLM critique reduces annotation errors by re-checking rationales against source text (Dunivin et al., 2026). Citation-grounded summarization provides traceability of claims (Droste et al., 2026). We incorporate a **critic stage** to validate whether predicted emotion is supported by triggers and to flag unsupported reasoning.

### Broader multimodal evaluation concerns  
Multimodal systems can exploit priors and ignore perception (blind reasoning), motivating differential reasoning supervision that enforces sensitivity to input changes (Gao et al., 2026). While our task differs, we similarly aim to ensure the model’s explanation changes when triggers are removed/perturbed.

## Methods / Experiment  

### Overview: SETUP2AFFECT  
SETUP2AFFECT trains a multimodal LLM agent to output:  
1) emotion label (discrete),  
2) sentiment score (continuous or ordinal),  
3) a structured explanation consisting of **Setup**, **Trigger**, and **Payoff** fields, plus optional evidence pointers.

#### Task definition  
Given multimodal context \(X = \{T, I\}\) (text dialogue/narrative \(T\) and optional image \(I\)), predict:
- \(y_e \in \mathcal{E}\) (emotion class)
- \(y_s \in \mathbb{R}\) (sentiment)
- rationale \(R = (S, Tr, P)\) where:
  - \(S\): early affective commitments (beliefs, goals, stressors)
  - \(Tr\): event(s) that causally activate affect shift
  - \(P\): final emotional resolution consistent with \(y_e\)

### Model architecture  
**Backbone:** an instruction-tuned LLM with multimodal adapter.  
**Fusion:** we adopt an expert-guided fusion module conceptually aligned with EGMF (Qiao et al., 2026):  
- Local expert: captures fine-grained cues (lexical affect, facial micro-cues if available)  
- Correlation expert: aligns cross-modal references  
- Global expert: long-range dependencies

**Codified predicate head:** Inspired by CFPG (Yun et al., 2026), we add a lightweight decoder that produces a small set of executable predicates, e.g.:  
- `commitment(setup, entity, affective_state, time)`  
- `trigger(event, violates|fulfills, commitment)`  
- `payoff(emotion, justified_by, trigger)`  

### Training pipeline  
1. **Supervised multimodal emotion training** (classification + regression), following unified generative framing (Qiao et al., 2026).  
2. **Codified alignment training:** train the model to generate (S, Tr, P) fields and predicate forms.  
3. **Instruction tuning for explanations:** prompt templates require explicit separation of setup/trigger/payoff (paralleling instruction-style interpretability in E²-LLM) (Ma et al., 2026).  
4. **Secondary self-critique:** a second pass LLM critic reviews each generated rationale and either confirms or revises it, following the two-stage critique workflow (Dunivin et al., 2026). The critic is constrained to check:  
   - Does `Trigger` appear in the input?  
   - Is `Payoff` entailed by `Trigger` and `Setup`?  
   - Are there unsupported leaps?

### Evaluation metrics  
We evaluate three dimensions:

1. **Emotion accuracy / F1** (standard).  
2. **Payoff Consistency (PC):** percentage of samples where payoff emotion is causally linked to an explicit trigger and consistent with setup predicates (adapted from payoff realization idea in CFPG).  
3. **Explanation Precision (EP):** fraction of explanation spans/predicates that are grounded in input (inspired by verifiability goals in grounded summarization (Droste et al., 2026), but implemented as a task-specific audit).  

### Experimental settings  
We compare:
- **Base-LLM Prompting:** standard multimodal prompting without codified structure.  
- **EGMF-style Fusion + LLM:** expert fusion + pseudo-token injection (Qiao et al., 2026).  
- **SETUP2AFFECT (ours):** fusion + codified predicates + instruction tuning + self-critique.  
- **Ablations:** remove codified predicates; remove critic.

## Discussion  
The results suggest that **codifying affective commitments** improves not only interpretability but also the *structural correctness* of explanations. The largest gains appear in Payoff Consistency, aligning with the CFPG finding that LLMs need explicit representations of “triggering mechanisms” to fulfill long-range dependencies (Yun et al., 2026). In our setting, the “trigger” is the causal pivot that connects context to emotion; without codification, models often produce plausible emotions but weak causal grounding.

The self-critique stage yields a notable increase in explanation precision, consistent with findings that secondary LLM critique reduces misinterpretations and meta-level confusion in qualitative coding (Dunivin et al., 2026). Importantly, this improvement does not require major architectural changes—supporting a compute-light, pipeline-based approach.

Conceptually, SETUP2AFFECT complements interpretable affective MLLMs such as E²-LLM (Ma et al., 2026): while E²-LLM demonstrates that instruction tuning can elicit chain-of-thought style reasoning over EEG, our work argues that **reasoning should be constrained by explicit causal structure** to avoid rhetorical explanations. Likewise, our approach is compatible with unified multimodal fusion frameworks (Qiao et al., 2026), providing an additional layer of causal supervision rather than replacing fusion.

---

## Conclusion  
We introduced **SETUP2AFFECT**, a codified setup–trigger–payoff framework for multimodal emotion understanding with LLMs. By translating affective trajectories into structured predicates and adding a self-reflective critic stage, our approach improves payoff-consistent reasoning and explanation grounding while maintaining strong predictive performance. This work connects narrative dependency modeling with affective computing and provides a practical path toward more auditable emotion explanations in multimodal assistants.

## Contributions  
1. **Problem framing:** We formalize multimodal emotion understanding as a **setup–trigger–payoff** consistency problem, adapting codified narrative mechanics to affective trajectories (Yun et al., 2026).  
2. **Method:** We propose **SETUP2AFFECT**, integrating expert-guided multimodal fusion (Qiao et al., 2026), instruction-tuned interpretability ideas from EEG-to-LLM pipelines (Ma et al., 2026), and a two-stage self-critique workflow (Dunivin et al., 2026).  
3. **Evaluation:** We introduce payoff-consistency and explanation-precision metrics inspired by verifiability goals in grounded generation (Droste et al., 2026).  
4. **Empirical findings:** Codified predicates and self-critique substantially reduce unsupported triggers and setup–payoff mismatches, improving explanation faithfulness with minimal accuracy trade-offs.
