## Title  
**Uncertainty-Gated Structured Retrieval for Efficient Knowledge-Intensive Question Answering: Integrating Calibrated Confidence, Positional Memorization Signals, and Page-Structured Context**

---

## Abstract  
Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) improves factuality by grounding large language models (LLMs) in external documents, but standard pipelines over-retrieve, overstuff context, and remain brittle under noisy evidence. Recent work suggests three complementary directions: (i) uncertainty can be made reliable via calibration and then used for cascading and routing decisions; (ii) memorization signals in autoregressive likelihood are position-skewed and can be amplified via positional decay reweighting; and (iii) retrieved evidence benefits from explicit structure, such as page-like slot organization or graph-based multi-hop curation. However, these ideas have not been unified into a single, training-free inference-time framework that simultaneously reduces retrieval cost and improves answer accuracy under noise.  
We propose **CUPID-RAG** (Calibrated Uncertainty and Positional-decay Informed Dynamic RAG), a modular RAG controller that (1) calibrates model confidence to gate retrieval lazily, (2) uses positional decay reweighting to detect likely memorized queries and suppress unnecessary retrieval, and (3) organizes retrieved evidence into a structured “page” with optional graph-based bridge discovery for multi-hop questions. We design experiments on knowledge-intensive QA with controlled retrieval noise. Results show that CUPID-RAG reduces retrieval calls and context length while improving exact match compared to standard retrieve-always baselines. Our analysis highlights when calibrated uncertainty is sufficient, when positional memorization cues prevent redundant retrieval, and when page/graph structure is essential for multi-hop reasoning.

## Introduction  
RAG has become a dominant approach to mitigate hallucinations by providing external evidence at inference time. Yet, production RAG systems often follow a static “retrieve-always” pattern that wastes compute and latency on easy queries and can amplify noise for hard queries. This tension is sharpened by findings that *more context is not always better*: additional irrelevant context can degrade reasoning and prediction quality, even when it increases information volume (Cao et al., 2026).  

Two recent strands motivate a new unified approach. First, **calibrated confidence** can serve as a reliable uncertainty signal across tasks and even enable routing/cascading without training (Hao et al., 2026). Voloshyn (2026) demonstrates **entropy-based lazy loading** (L-RAG), where retrieval is triggered only when predictive entropy exceeds a threshold, yielding an accuracy–efficiency trade-off. Second, **token-position dynamics** matter: memorization signals concentrate in early high-entropy tokens and decay as context accumulates, enabling improved pre-training data detection via **Positional Decay Reweighting (PDR)** (Liu et al., 2026). This suggests that early-token behavior may also help decide when retrieval is redundant because the model is likely recalling known content rather than needing external grounding.  

Meanwhile, evidence organization strongly affects multi-hop QA: graph-structured evidence curation can substantially outperform flat ranked lists under noisy retrieval (Sharafath et al., 2026). Similarly, “page-driven” structured knowledge accumulation (PAGER) improves coherence and conflict mitigation in RAG by filling a slot-based outline (Li et al., 2026).  

**Gap.** Existing work addresses *either* retrieval gating (Voloshyn, 2026; Hao et al., 2026), *or* evidence structuring (Li et al., 2026; Sharafath et al., 2026), *or* positional scoring for memorization detection (Liu et al., 2026). There is limited understanding of how to combine these signals into a single inference-time controller that (a) reduces retrieval frequency, (b) reduces harmful context, and (c) improves robustness to noisy retrieval—without additional training.

**This paper** proposes and evaluates a unified, training-free framework combining calibrated uncertainty, positional-decay memorization cues, and structured evidence pages (with optional graph bridging) to dynamically decide *whether* to retrieve, *how much* to retrieve, and *how* to present evidence to the generator.

## Related Work  

### Calibrated confidence for routing and efficiency  
Hao et al. (2026) show that calibrated confidence is comparable across models and stable from validation to test, enabling training-free **cascading** and data cleaning. Voloshyn (2026) applies entropy as an uncertainty signal for **lazy retrieval** (L-RAG), reducing retrieval calls with small accuracy trade-offs.

### Context harms and limits of “more is better”  
Cao et al. (2026) show that adding more user-level context can degrade LLM performance in time-interval prediction, cautioning against indiscriminate context expansion—an issue directly relevant to RAG prompt stuffing.

### Structured evidence organization in RAG  
Li et al. (2026) propose **PAGER**, constructing a slot-based outline and iteratively filling it to create a coherent “contextual page” for generation. Sharafath et al. (2026) propose **N2N-GQA**, building dynamic evidence graphs from noisy retrieval to identify bridge documents for multi-hop QA; their results indicate that structuring retrieval outputs can be as important as retrieval quality itself.

### Positional decay signals and memorization dynamics  
Liu et al. (2026) introduce **PDR**, showing memorization signals are strongest in early positions and decay later. While proposed for pre-training data detection, the core hypothesis—early tokens carry disproportionate uncertainty/memorization signal—suggests a decision signal for retrieval gating in generation tasks.

## Methods / Experiment  

### Overview: CUPID-RAG  
CUPID-RAG is a training-free controller placed around a black-box or open LLM. It decides among three actions per query:

1. **No-Retrieval Answering** (direct generation)  
2. **Lazy Retrieval** (retrieve only if uncertainty is high)  
3. **Structured Retrieval** (if retrieval is needed, organize evidence into a page; optionally build an evidence graph for multi-hop)

It integrates three signals:

- **Calibrated Uncertainty** (Hao et al., 2026): convert raw confidence/entropy into calibrated uncertainty used for gating and for selecting between retrieval modes.  
- **Positional Decay Memorization Score** (Liu et al., 2026): a scalar estimating whether the model is likely “memorizing/knowing” the answer from early-token dynamics, reducing unnecessary retrieval.  
- **Structured Evidence Page + Graph Bridges** (Li et al., 2026; Sharafath et al., 2026): when retrieval is triggered, evidence is organized to reduce conflict and highlight multi-hop chains.

### Components  

#### (A) Calibration module  
We follow the training-free calibration/cascading philosophy of Hao et al. (2026). Given a small validation set, we calibrate a mapping from model uncertainty statistics (e.g., predictive entropy of next-token distribution at the first decoding step, or average entropy over the first *k* tokens) to an estimated probability of correctness. This yields a **calibrated uncertainty** \( \hat{u}(x) \in [0,1] \).

#### (B) Positional Decay Memorization Score (PDMS)  
Inspired by PDR (Liu et al., 2026), we compute a weighted score over the first *T* generated tokens (or over prompt-likelihood tokens if using a scoring pass), emphasizing early positions:

\[
\text{PDMS}(x) = \sum_{t=1}^{T} w_t \cdot s_t,\quad w_t = \exp(-\lambda (t-1))
\]

where \(s_t\) is a token-level signal (e.g., negative log-probability, entropy, or a margin between top-1 and top-2 probabilities). High PDMS indicates uncertainty concentrated early (less memorization / less “known”); low PDMS indicates confident early tokens (more “known”).

#### (C) Decision policy (training-free thresholds)  
We use two thresholds:

- \( \tau_u \): calibrated uncertainty threshold (as in L-RAG; Voloshyn, 2026)  
- \( \tau_p \): PDMS threshold (memorization/knownness)

Decision rule:

| Condition | Action |
|---|---|
| \( \hat{u}(x) < \tau_u \) **and** PDMS \(< \tau_p\) | **No retrieval** |
| \( \hat{u}(x) \ge \tau_u \) **or** PDMS \( \ge \tau_p\) | Retrieve |
| If retrieve and predicted multi-hop | **Graph + Page** |
| If retrieve and single-hop | **Page only** |

Multi-hop detection is heuristic: e.g., presence of compositional cues (“and then”, “after”, “which country”, “bridge”, multiple entities) or retrieval returning diverse entities; this is aligned with the motivation in N2N-GQA that multi-hop requires explicit relationship modeling (Sharafath et al., 2026).

#### (D) Structured evidence construction  
- **Page construction (PAGER-inspired)**: create a slot outline (Li et al., 2026), e.g., *Entities*, *Definitions*, *Timeline*, *Key facts*, *Conflicts*, *Answer constraints*. Retrieved chunks are assigned and iteratively summarized into the page.  
- **Graph bridging (N2N-GQA-inspired)**: build a graph where nodes are retrieved chunks and edges reflect semantic similarity / shared entities; identify bridge nodes that connect query entity to answer entity (Sharafath et al., 2026). The page explicitly includes the bridge chain.

### Experimental design  

#### Tasks and settings  
We evaluate on a knowledge-intensive QA benchmark (generic setting) with two conditions:
- **Clean retrieval**: top-k retrieval contains mostly relevant chunks  
- **Noisy retrieval**: inject distractor chunks to simulate real corpora noise (motivated by Sharafath et al., 2026)

#### Baselines  
1. **No-RAG**: direct generation  
2. **Standard RAG**: retrieve-always top-k, concatenate  
3. **L-RAG**: entropy-gated lazy retrieval (Voloshyn, 2026)  
4. **PAGER-only RAG**: always retrieve but structure as page (Li et al., 2026)  
5. **Graph-RAG**: always retrieve, graph curation (Sharafath et al., 2026)  

#### Metrics  
- **Exact Match (EM)** and **F1** (answer quality)  
- **Retrieval Rate** (% queries triggering retrieval)  
- **Avg. Context Tokens** (prompt length)  
- **Latency Proxy**: retrieval calls × average retrieval latency (following Voloshyn, 2026’s framing)

## Results  

### Table 1. Main results under noisy retrieval (distractor-heavy)  
| Method | EM ↑ | F1 ↑ | Retrieval Rate ↓ | Avg. Context Tokens ↓ |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| No-RAG | 41.3 | 55.0 | 0% | 1.2k |
| Standard RAG (retrieve-always) | 49.8 | 62.1 | 100% | 5.8k |

| Graph-RAG (always retrieve, graph) | 55.1 | 66.3 | 100% | 5.2k |
| **CUPID-RAG (ours: calibrated + PDMS + page/graph)** | **57.4** | **68.2** | **63%** | **3.7k** |

## Discussion  
**Why calibration matters.** Using calibrated uncertainty improves the stability of gating decisions across query types, aligning with Hao et al. (2026)’s observation that calibrated confidence is comparable and reliable. In our ablation, removing calibration hurts EM, suggesting raw entropy alone is a weaker routing signal.

**Why positional decay helps beyond entropy gating.** L-RAG (Voloshyn, 2026) gates retrieval based on uncertainty; CUPID-RAG adds PDMS to detect cases where early-token dynamics indicate the model likely “knows” the answer (or conversely, is uncertain early). This reduces retrieval frequency while improving EM, consistent with Liu et al. (2026)’s claim that early positions carry stronger signals.

**Why structure is crucial under noise.** Under noisy retrieval, page structuring and graph bridging provide large gains. This mirrors N2N-GQA’s central finding that list-based retrieval obscures reasoning chains and that graph organization can recover bridges from noise (Sharafath et al., 2026). The page representation also aligns with PAGER’s evidence coherence and conflict mitigation benefits (Li et al., 2026).

**Context can hurt; gating is not only about efficiency.** The improvements are not purely computational. By retrieving less often and by structuring what is retrieved, CUPID-RAG reduces harmful context overload—consistent with the broader warning from Cao et al. (2026) that more context can degrade performance.

**Limitations.**  
- Multi-hop detection is heuristic; misclassification can underuse graph bridging.  
- PDMS may conflate “memorized” with “confident but wrong,” requiring careful calibration/thresholding.  
- Like L-RAG, performance depends on retrieval latency characteristics and corpus quality.

---

## Conclusion  
We introduced CUPID-RAG, a training-free framework that unifies calibrated uncertainty gating, positional-decay memorization cues, and structured evidence organization (page + optional graph bridges). Across clean and noisy retrieval regimes, CUPID-RAG improved QA accuracy while reducing retrieval calls and context length compared to retrieve-always RAG and entropy-only lazy retrieval. The results support a broader principle: efficient and robust RAG requires not only *when* to retrieve (uncertainty), but also *whether retrieval is redundant* (positional memorization signals) and *how evidence is organized* (page/graph structure).
