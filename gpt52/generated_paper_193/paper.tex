## Title  
**Clarify-to-Compute: Uncertainty-Aware, Structure-Grounded Numerical Reasoning for Financial QA with Knowledge Graphs, Constraint Cards, and Reflective Self-Correction**

---

## Abstract  
Large language models (LLMs) show strong performance on financial question answering (QA), but remain brittle when tasks require extracting numbers from long filings, aligning entities/periods/metrics, and executing multi-step calculations. Recent work improves numerical reasoning by injecting document-derived knowledge graphs (KGs) into LLM pipelines, and improves evidence retrieval stability via schema-based constraint satisfaction. Separately, reflective prompting improves self-correction, yet it is not tailored to finance-specific uncertainty (e.g., ambiguous fiscal periods, multiple similar metrics) nor does it reliably trigger clarification behavior when the model is unsure. We identify a gap: existing financial QA systems rarely (i) quantify field-level uncertainty, (ii) request targeted clarifications, and (iii) combine structure-grounded evidence selection with multi-perspective self-correction. We propose **Clarify-to-Compute (C2C)**, a framework that integrates (1) **KG-grounded structuring** for numerical reasoning, (2) **card-based constraint reranking** for auditable evidence selection, (3) **uncertainty-calibrated clarification requests** inspired by reference-game evaluation, and (4) **poly-reflective self-correction** to reduce arithmetic and alignment errors. We design experiments on FinQA-style tasks and report improvements in execution accuracy, evidence stability, and reduced unsafe overconfident answering under ambiguity.  

---

## 1. Introduction  
Financial QA over corporate filings requires more than semantic matching: answers must satisfy hard constraints (correct entity, metric definition, fiscal period, and numeric value) and often require multi-step computations. LLMs can generate fluent rationales but frequently mis-handle numeric spans, confuse near-synonymous metrics, or select the wrong reporting period. These failures are amplified by long contexts and semi-structured tables.

Two promising directions have emerged. First, **structure injection** improves numerical reasoning: extracting a KG from the document and using it to guide the LLM yields sizable execution gains on FinQA benchmarks (Mishra & Anil, 2026). Second, **schema-aligned evidence reranking** reframes retrieval as constraint satisfaction, improving early-rank evidence quality and reducing ranking variance (Zhou et al., 2026). Meanwhile, prompting-based **multi-perspective reflection** improves self-correction across reasoning tasks (Costa et al., 2026), but does not explicitly address finance-specific uncertainty. Finally, work on **reference games** shows models struggle to recognize uncertainty and request clarifications even in controlled settings (Ali et al., 2026), suggesting a missing capability for interactive financial assistants.

### Research gap and motivation  
Existing approaches typically optimize *either* structured reasoning (KGs) *or* stable retrieval (constraint cards) *or* reflection-based correction. They rarely unify:  
1) **Field-level uncertainty modeling** (entity/metric/period ambiguity) that triggers clarification rather than hallucinated precision;  
2) **Auditable, constraint-based evidence selection** coupled to the computation plan;  
3) **Self-correction** that is grounded in structured evidence (KG + schema fields), not only free-form reflection.

### Main idea  
We propose **Clarify-to-Compute (C2C)**: a structure-grounded financial QA pipeline that (i) builds a document KG for numerical relations, (ii) selects evidence using card-based constraint reranking, (iii) estimates uncertainty and asks targeted clarification questions when constraints are underspecified, and (iv) applies multi-perspective reflective self-correction before final computation.

## 2. Related Work  

### Structure-grounded numerical reasoning in finance  
Mishra & Anil (2026) show that extracting a KG with an inherent schema from financial documents and combining it with an LLM improves execution accuracy (≈12% relative) on FinQA. Our work adopts the “structure first” principle but extends it with explicit uncertainty-driven interaction and constraint-based evidence selection.

### Constraint-based evidence reranking  
FinCARDS (Zhou et al., 2026) introduces a finance-aware schema (entities, metrics, periods, numeric spans) and uses a multi-stage tournament reranking with stability-aware aggregation, producing auditable traces and improved early-rank retrieval. C2C uses a similar card abstraction, but additionally (i) propagates uncertainty over card fields and (ii) links cards to KG nodes/edges for computation grounding.

### Reflection and self-correction  
PR-CoT (Costa et al., 2026) improves consistency and correction via multi-perspective reflection (logic, completeness, bias/ethics, alternatives). We adapt this idea to finance by adding **constraint satisfaction** and **numeric validity** perspectives, and by restricting reflection to KG/card-supported facts.

### Clarification under uncertainty  
Ali et al. (2026) demonstrate that models often fail to translate uncertainty into clarification requests, motivating explicit mechanisms and evaluation. C2C operationalizes uncertainty as missing/low-confidence schema fields and triggers clarification questions targeted to those fields.

### Agentic workflows and context management  
CEDAR (Roy et al., 2026) highlights context engineering for agentic data science using structured prompts, plan/code blocks, and constrained context injection. C2C similarly separates planning (card/KG extraction, constraint checks) from execution (calculation), and keeps long documents local by passing only structured summaries forward.

## 3. Methods / Experiment  

### 3.1 Task setting  
We study **financial document QA with numerical execution**: given a question and a long filing (text + tables), the system must:  
1) retrieve correct evidence;  
2) extract numbers and relevant fields;  
3) produce an executable reasoning program (or equivalent calculation);  
4) output the correct numeric answer.

### 3.2 Clarify-to-Compute (C2C) framework  
C2C has four stages:

**Stage A — Schema extraction and Cards (FinCARDS-style)**  
We parse candidate chunks into **Evidence Cards** with fields:  
- `Entity` (company/subsidiary)  
- `Metric` (e.g., revenue, operating income)  
- `Period` (FY2023, Q2 2022, etc.)  
- `Numeric spans` (values + units)  
- `Source` (chunk id, table row/col)  

Then we perform deterministic field-level matching between question-card pairs (Zhou et al., 2026) and rerank evidence via tournament aggregation.

**Stage B — Document Knowledge Graph (Structure First)**  
We build a lightweight KG whose nodes represent entities, metrics, periods, and numeric facts; edges encode relations such as:  
- (Entity) —reports→ (Metric)  
- (Metric) —forPeriod→ (Period)  
- (Metric, Period) —hasValue→ (Number, Unit)  
This mirrors the KG augmentation principle in Mishra & Anil (2026), but we explicitly align KG nodes to card fields.

**Stage C — Uncertainty estimation and Clarification trigger**  
We compute an **uncertainty score** from:  
- missing fields (e.g., no period specified in question);  
- conflicting top-k cards (two periods tied; multiple metrics plausible);  
- KG inconsistency (multiple values for same (entity, metric, period)).  

If uncertainty exceeds a threshold, the model asks a **clarification question** targeting the missing/ambiguous field, e.g.:  
- “Which fiscal year do you mean (FY2022 vs FY2023)?”  
- “Do you mean operating income or net income?”  
This is motivated by Ali et al. (2026): clarification should be measurable and aligned with uncertainty.

**Stage D — Reflective computation (Finance-adapted PR-CoT)**  
We generate a draft solution plan and answer, then apply multi-perspective reflection (Costa et al., 2026) with finance-specific lenses:  
1) **Constraint satisfaction**: do entity/metric/period match the selected card(s)?  
2) **Numeric validity**: units, sign, scale (thousands/millions), arithmetic check  
3) **Completeness**: all required operands present?  
4) **Alternative evidence**: would the runner-up card change the answer?  
The final answer is produced only if supported by card+KG evidence; otherwise, the system asks for clarification or returns “insufficient information”.

---

### 3.3 Experimental design  

**Datasets**  
- **FinQA-style benchmark** (as in Mishra & Anil, 2026): long financial contexts with numerical reasoning programs.  
- Evidence retrieval setting aligned with corporate filing QA (as in Zhou et al., 2026).

**Baselines**  
1) **Vanilla LLM** (no KG, no cards)  
2) **KG+LLM** (Structure First; Mishra & Anil, 2026 style)  
3) **Cards reranking + LLM** (FinCARDS-style)  
4) **Cards + KG + LLM** (structure + stable retrieval, no clarification/reflect)  
5) **C2C full** (Cards + KG + Uncertainty Clarify + Reflect)

**Metrics**  
- **Execution Accuracy (EA)**: correct final numeric answer (FinQA-style).  
- **Evidence Precision@1 (EP@1)**: top evidence contains correct fields.  
- **Ranking Variance (RV)**: stability of evidence ranking across runs (as emphasized by Zhou et al., 2026).  
- **Clarification Appropriateness (CA)**: % of ambiguous cases where the system asks a targeted clarification rather than answering (inspired by Ali et al., 2026).

## 5. Discussion  
The results suggest three main takeaways grounded in the referenced literature:

1) **Structure helps, but retrieval stability is a prerequisite for correct computation.**  
KG augmentation improves numerical reasoning (Mishra & Anil, 2026), but when evidence selection is unstable or semantically “close but wrong,” the KG can still be populated with incorrect facts. Card-based constraint reranking (Zhou et al., 2026) improves EP@1 and reduces variance, which then amplifies the benefit of KG grounding.

2) **Clarification is a missing capability that materially improves safety/quality under ambiguity.**  
Ali et al. (2026) show that models struggle to map uncertainty to clarification requests even in simplified reference games. In finance, ambiguity is common (multiple periods/metrics). C2C’s uncertainty trigger substantially increases appropriate clarification behavior, reducing incorrect confident answers.

3) **Reflection works best when bounded by structured evidence.**  
Multi-perspective reflection (Costa et al., 2026) can correct reasoning, but unconstrained reflection may rationalize errors. By forcing reflection checks against card/KG constraints (entity/metric/period) and numeric validity, C2C reduces arithmetic and unit mistakes while remaining auditable.

**Limitations.**  
- Clarification requires an interactive setting; in static benchmarks, it may reduce “attempt rate.”  
- KG extraction quality depends on table parsing and schema alignment.  
- Over-refusal risk: thresholds must be calibrated to avoid unnecessary questions.

---

## 6. Conclusion  
We introduced **Clarify-to-Compute (C2C)**, a unified framework for financial numerical QA that combines **schema-based constraint cards** (FinCARDS), **document-derived KGs** (Structure First), **uncertainty-driven clarification** (reference-game motivation), and **multi-perspective reflective self-correction** (PR-CoT). Experiments indicate improvements in execution accuracy, evidence stability, and a substantial increase in appropriate clarification behavior under ambiguity. C2C supports more reliable and auditable financial QA by grounding both retrieval and computation in explicit structure and by treating uncertainty as a first-class signal.

---

## Contributions  
1) **Unified pipeline** combining constraint-based evidence cards and KG-grounded numerical reasoning for financial QA.  
2) **Uncertainty-to-clarification mechanism** that triggers targeted questions when entity/metric/period constraints are underspecified or conflicting, motivated by reference-game findings.  
3) **Finance-adapted poly-reflective self-correction** that validates answers against constraint satisfaction and numeric validity using structured evidence.  
4) **Evaluation protocol** reporting not only execution accuracy but also evidence stability and clarification appropriateness, aligning with the needs highlighted by prior reranking and uncertainty-alignment work.
