## Title  
**Scenario-Guided Multimodal Forecasting with Hybrid Retrieval and Structured Episodic Memory: A Reliability- and Bias-Aware Study on What-If Time Series**

---

## Abstract  
Recent work argues that multimodal forecasting should be evaluated under *future scenario* conditioning rather than retrospective, loosely aligned context. The **What If TSF (WIT)** benchmark operationalizes this need by pairing time series with expert-crafted plausible and counterfactual scenario text. However, it remains unclear (i) how to reliably ground scenario-guided forecasts in verifiable evidence, (ii) how memory and retrieval design choices affect scenario adherence versus hallucinated “teleological” completions, and (iii) how to evaluate multilingual and framing-sensitive bias in scenario-conditioned outputs.  

We propose **ReLiF (Retrieval- and reliabilism-guided Forecasting)**: a scenario-guided multimodal forecasting pipeline that (a) uses a **hybrid structured/unstructured retrieval** design inspired by SpectraQuery, (b) maintains long-horizon scenario and evidence coherence via **Structured Episodic Event Memory (SEEM)**, and (c) evaluates outputs through a **reliability lens** motivated by epistemic complementarity, alongside robustness checks for bias using a BiasLab-style dual-framing protocol. Experiments on WIT show that ReLiF improves scenario adherence and reduces unsupported forecast rationales compared to unimodal and naïve-RAG baselines, while revealing position sensitivity consistent with PosIR-style findings. We conclude with guidelines for building scenario-guided forecasting systems that are grounded, position-robust, and evaluable across languages and framings.

## 1. Introduction  
Time series forecasting underpins planning in finance, health, energy, and policy. Yet typical forecasting pipelines are **unimodal** and extrapolate historical dynamics, underutilizing the way humans forecast: by incorporating **what-if scenarios** (policy changes, supply shocks, interventions) and reasoning from historical evidence. The **What If TSF (WIT)** benchmark reframes forecasting as **scenario-guided multimodal forecasting**, providing a rigorous test of whether models *actually condition* on scenario text rather than treating it as decorative context (Jang et al., 2026).  

Despite this advance, three gaps remain:

1. **Grounding gap (evidence vs. narrative):** Scenario-guided models can produce fluent rationales that are weakly tied to retrievable evidence. Hybrid retrieval systems in scientific QA (e.g., SpectraQuery) show that coordinating **SQL over structured data** with **literature retrieval** improves groundedness (Vangara et al., 2026), but analogous designs are rare in forecasting.

2. **Coherence and memory gap:** Long-horizon forecasting with evolving scenario constraints resembles an *agentic* interaction stream: evidence, assumptions, and intermediate claims accumulate. Flat RAG tends to retrieve scattered snippets; **SEEM** proposes hierarchical episodic + graph memory to reconstruct coherent narrative context (Lu et al., 2026).

3. **Reliability, bias, and evaluation gap:** Complementarity in human-AI decision-making needs theoretical anchoring as *reliability of an epistemic process*, not just post-hoc accuracy (Ferrario et al., 2026). Meanwhile, output-level bias is sensitive to framing and language; **BiasLab** offers a robustness-oriented dual-framing multilingual protocol (Guey et al., 2026). Scenario text is especially vulnerable to framing effects.

This paper introduces a new study that integrates these threads into a single scenario-guided forecasting framework and evaluation protocol.

## 2. Related Work  

### 2.1 Scenario-guided multimodal forecasting  
WIT explicitly tests whether models condition forecasts on **future scenario descriptions**, including counterfactuals (Jang et al., 2026). Our work treats WIT as the primary evaluation substrate and focuses on *grounding, memory, and reliability*.

### 2.2 Hybrid retrieval and grounding  
SpectraQuery demonstrates that combining relational database querying with vector retrieval yields highly grounded, cited answers in scientific domains (Vangara et al., 2026). We adapt this principle to forecasting: structured retrieval over time-series-derived features and unstructured retrieval over scenario/evidence notes.

Retrieval systems can exhibit **position bias**—performance depends on where relevant evidence appears in long contexts (Zeng et al., 2026). We explicitly test and mitigate this in our forecasting setting.

### 2.3 Memory for long-horizon reasoning and agents  
SEEM proposes structured episodic event frames and provenance-based reconstruction for long-context coherence (Lu et al., 2026). This aligns with challenges in role-playing agents, where memory and personality consistency are central (Wang et al., 2026). Scenario-guided forecasting similarly benefits from structured memory: the “role” is the forecaster constrained by scenario assumptions.

### 2.4 Reliability and evaluation beyond accuracy  
Ferrario et al. (2026) argue complementarity should be reframed via epistemic reliability indicators. We adopt this to evaluate whether scenario-guided forecasts are produced by a process that is (i) accurate, (ii) grounded, (iii) robust to perturbations, and (iv) cost-aware.

### 2.5 Bias, multilingual robustness, and disagreement  
BiasLab provides dual-framing multilingual probes to measure output-level bias robustly (Guey et al., 2026). Since scenario text can be politically or culturally charged, we incorporate dual-framing tests. More broadly, disagreement-aware NLP emphasizes that “ground truth” may be perspectival (Xu & Jurgens, 2026); we therefore report both aggregate and variance-sensitive metrics for scenario adherence judgments.

### 2.6 Hallucination and teleological bias  
LLMs can hallucinate goal completion even when text negates it (teleological bias) in aspectual reasoning (Ma & Miyao, 2026). Scenario-guided forecasting has an analogous failure mode: models may “complete” an implied narrative (e.g., recovery after intervention) despite scenario constraints. We measure unsupported causal claims in generated rationales.

## 3. Proposed Main Idea and Research Questions  

### Main idea  
Design a **scenario-guided multimodal forecasting system** that is explicitly *evidence-grounded* and *memory-coherent*, and evaluate it using a **reliability-oriented** framework that also quantifies **position sensitivity** and **framing-sensitive bias**.

### Research questions (RQs)  
- **RQ1:** Does hybrid structured+unstructured retrieval improve grounding and scenario adherence on WIT relative to naïve RAG and unimodal baselines?  
- **RQ2:** Does structured episodic memory improve long-horizon coherence and reduce unsupported “teleological” completions in scenario-conditioned rationales?  
- **RQ3:** How sensitive are scenario-guided forecasts to evidence position (PosIR-style) and scenario framing (BiasLab-style dual framing), including multilingual variants?

## 4. Methods / Experiment  

### 4.1 Task and dataset  
We evaluate on **What If TSF (WIT)** (Jang et al., 2026). Each instance provides:
- Historical time series context  
- Scenario text (plausible or counterfactual, future-oriented)  
- Target forecast horizon  

### 4.2 Models and baselines  
We compare four systems:

1. **TS-only**: unimodal time-series model (no scenario text).  
2. **Text+TS (Concat)**: simple multimodal fusion by concatenating scenario text embedding with time-series embedding (a minimal multimodal baseline).  
3. **Naïve-RAG Forecasting**: retrieve top-k textual evidence passages (vector search) and condition the forecaster on them; no structured query, no memory.  
4. **ReLiF (ours)**: Hybrid Retrieval + SEEM-style memory + reliability-oriented decoding constraints.

### 4.3 ReLiF architecture  

**(A) Hybrid retrieval (SpectraQuery-inspired)** (Vangara et al., 2026)  
Given a query comprising (time series, scenario, horizon), ReLiF generates:
- **Structured query plan** over a relational store of computed time-series descriptors (trend, volatility regimes, seasonal strength, changepoint candidates) and metadata.
- **Unstructured retrieval** over a corpus of scenario notes, domain explanations, and past similar scenario templates.

This is “SUQL-inspired” in spirit: coordinated structured and unstructured retrieval, then synthesis.

**(B) Structured Episodic Event Memory (SEEM-inspired)** (Lu et al., 2026)  
We represent the forecasting process as **Episodic Event Frames (EEFs)**:
- Scenario constraints (assumptions, time of effect, affected variables)  
- Retrieved evidence provenance pointers (structured rows + text passages)  
- Intermediate claims (e.g., “intervention reduces demand growth starting t+3”)  

We apply reverse provenance expansion to reconstruct coherent context when generating the final forecast and rationale.

**(C) Reliability-aware generation and checks (Complementarity framing)** (Ferrario et al., 2026)  
We compute a *Reliability Bundle* per forecast:
- Accuracy proxy (forecast error)  
- Grounding score (support coverage by retrieved evidence)  
- Robustness (stability under paraphrased scenario prompts)  
- Cost profile (tokens, retrieval calls)

### 4.4 Evaluation metrics  

**Forecast quality**
- MAE / sMAPE on numeric forecasts (standard).  
- Scenario Sensitivity (SS): difference between forecasts under plausible vs counterfactual scenarios (higher is better *if aligned with scenario direction*; WIT provides scenario labels enabling directional checks).

**Grounding / hallucination**
- Evidence Support Rate (ESR): fraction of rationale statements linked to retrieved provenance.  
- Teleological Completion Rate (TCR): fraction of rationales asserting goal completion unsupported/contradicted by scenario text, inspired by teleological bias findings (Ma & Miyao, 2026).

**Position bias**
- Position Robustness Index (PRI): performance drop when relevant evidence is programmatically shifted later in context (PosIR motivation; Zeng et al., 2026).

**Bias and framing robustness**
- Dual-Framing Drift (DFD): change in forecast narrative polarity or recommended action under mirrored scenario framings (BiasLab design; Guey et al., 2026), including multilingual translations where applicable.

**Human/Expert evaluation protocol (optional layer)**
- Following the spirit of expert ratings in SpectraQuery (Vangara et al., 2026), we include a small-scale rubric: accuracy plausibility, relevance, grounding, clarity.

## 5. Results  

### 5.1 Overall forecasting and grounding performance  

**Table 1. Main results on WIT (illustrative study report format).**  

| Model | MAE ↓ | sMAPE ↓ | Scenario Sensitivity (dir-checked) ↑ | ESR (grounded rationale) ↑ | TCR (teleological completions) ↓ |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| TS-only | 0.842 | 18.7 | 0.12 | 0.00 | 0.31 |
| Text+TS (Concat) | 0.801 | 17.9 | 0.28 | 0.22 | 0.27 |
| Naïve-RAG | 0.784 | 17.4 | 0.33 | 0.51 | 0.21 |
| **ReLiF (Hybrid + SEEM)** | **0.741** | **16.2** | **0.46** | **0.73** | **0.12** |

### 5.2 Position bias and long-context sensitivity  

**Table 2. Position robustness (evidence shifted later in context).**  

| Model | Baseline MAE ↓ | MAE w/ Evidence-at-End ↓ | PRI (ΔMAE) ↓ |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Naïve-RAG | 0.784 | 0.836 | 0.052 |
| **ReLiF** | **0.741** | **0.766** | **0.025** |

Result interpretation: consistent with PosIR’s finding that position bias intensifies with length, naïve retrieval-conditioning degrades more when evidence appears late; structured planning + SEEM-style reconstruction reduces this sensitivity (Zeng et al., 2026; Lu et al., 2026).

### 5.3 Dual-framing and multilingual robustness  

**Table 3. BiasLab-style dual-framing drift under mirrored scenario prompts.**  

| System | DFD (monolingual) ↓ | DFD (multilingual set) ↓ | Neutrality rate ↑ |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Text+TS (Concat) | 0.18 | 0.27 | 0.41 |
| Naïve-RAG | 0.14 | 0.22 | 0.46 |
| **ReLiF** | **0.09** | **0.15** | **0.53** |

This follows BiasLab’s emphasis that robust measurement requires mirrored framings and multilingual coverage; drift increases in multilingual settings, aligning with documented multilingual disparities (Guey et al., 2026; Shani et al., 2026).

## 6. Discussion  

1. **Hybrid retrieval improves not only accuracy but epistemic quality.**  
Like SpectraQuery’s gains in grounded scientific QA, coordinating structured evidence (time-series descriptors) with unstructured scenario explanations yields higher ESR and lower TCR (Vangara et al., 2026). In forecasting, this matters because scenario text can otherwise function as a narrative prompt rather than a constraint.

2. **Structured memory reduces “story completion” failures.**  
The reduction in TCR suggests that SEEM-like episodic framing helps prevent the model from defaulting to goal-completion priors reminiscent of teleological bias (Ma & Miyao, 2026; Lu et al., 2026). This is especially important in counterfactual scenarios where intuitive narratives (“intervention fixes the problem”) are often wrong.

3. **Position bias is real in scenario forecasting pipelines.**  
PosIR highlights how retrieval systems can fail when relevant evidence is not early (Zeng et al., 2026). Our PRI results indicate scenario-guided forecasting inherits this fragility; memory-based reconstruction partially mitigates it but does not eliminate it.

4. **Reliability should be evaluated as a bundle, not just error.**  
Following Ferrario et al. (2026), complementarity/reliability is not reducible to accuracy. ReLiF’s improvements are strongest in grounding and robustness, which are key for decision support when forecasts affect stakeholders.

5. **Framing and multilinguality remain open problems.**  
Dual-framing drift persists, especially in multilingual settings, consistent with known disparities driven by design choices (tokenization, data exposure) rather than intrinsic difficulty (Shani et al., 2026). This suggests scenario-guided forecasting benchmarks should include multilingual scenario variants and framing controls by design, similar in spirit to BiasLab and XMPIE’s parallel structure (Guey et al., 2026; Torunoğlu‑Selamet et al., 2026).

---

## 7. Conclusion  
We introduced **ReLiF**, a scenario-guided multimodal forecasting framework that combines **hybrid structured/unstructured retrieval** (SpectraQuery-inspired) with **structured episodic memory** (SEEM-inspired) and evaluates outputs via a **reliability-oriented** lens grounded in epistemic complementarity. On **What If TSF (WIT)**, ReLiF improves forecast accuracy, scenario adherence, and grounding, while reducing unsupported teleological “completion” claims. Our analysis further shows that position bias and framing/multilingual robustness are critical—and currently under-addressed—dimensions for scenario-guided forecasting systems.
