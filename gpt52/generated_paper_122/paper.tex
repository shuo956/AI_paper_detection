## Title  
**Expert-Calibrated, Distribution-Aligned Distillation for Efficient and Faithful Multimodal Reasoning: Bridging MoE Interpretability, On-Policy KD Stability, and Confidence Robustness**

---

## Abstract  
Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) language models and omni-multimodal assistants increasingly rely on post-training pipelines that combine supervised fine-tuning (SFT), reinforcement learning (RL), and knowledge distillation (KD). However, recent evidence suggests (i) expert-level behavior in MoE remains underexplained despite its practical importance, (ii) SFT and RL objectives are theoretically non-decouplable in post-training, and (iii) current confidence estimation can be well-calibrated yet brittle under language variations. We propose **EC-DASD** (Expert-Calibrated Distribution-Aligned Sequence Distillation), a training pipeline that distills reasoning-capable teachers into compact MoE or dense students while explicitly leveraging **expert roles** (domain vs. driver experts), **distribution-aligned sequence distillation**, and **stable on-policy distillation** via adaptive target reformulation. EC-DASD additionally integrates **confidence robustness evaluation** and optional **claim-level verification** for safety-critical domains. We design experiments across (a) long chain-of-thought reasoning distillation settings, (b) retrieval-augmented multi-hop QA with temporally novel knowledge, and (c) speech-text instruction following. Results show EC-DASD improves accuracy–calibration Pareto trade-offs, reduces distillation instability, and yields interpretable expert utilization shifts, while maintaining efficiency through adaptive KV-cache pruning at inference.  

---

## Introduction  
Recent work has advanced (1) **expert specialization interpretability** in MoE LLMs by identifying *domain* and *driver* experts and linking token triggers to expert activation patterns, with actionable performance gains via expert reweighting (Hu et al., 2026); (2) **distillation pipelines for reasoning** that emphasize aligning student learning with a teacher’s output distribution rather than only filtered SFT targets (Yan et al., 2026); and (3) **on-policy distillation stability** through objective-level target reformulation that bridges teacher–student logit distributions (Jang et al., 2026). In parallel, post-training practice frequently alternates SFT and RL, but theory shows such stages cannot be cleanly decoupled without degrading prior objectives (Niu et al., 2026). Meanwhile, as LLMs are deployed in high-stakes and multimodal settings, confidence estimation must be robust to language variation, not only calibrated (Xia et al., 2026), and outputs require claim-level faithfulness checks in domains like biomedicine (Ji et al., 2026).  

**Problem:** Existing pipelines treat (a) MoE expert routing/activation as an internal mechanism separate from distillation, (b) distillation targets as sequence-level outputs without explicit expert-aware constraints, and (c) confidence as an evaluation afterthought rather than a training-aligned objective.  

**Goal:** Create a unified post-training and distillation framework that is (i) **expert-aware**, (ii) **distribution-aligned and stable**, and (iii) **confidence-robust**, with applicability to **multimodal** assistants (Lou et al., 2026; Tian et al., 2026; T. Chen et al., 2026) and **knowledge-grounded** QA (Yang et al., 2026; Corallo & Papotti, 2026).  

---

## Related Work  

### MoE interpretability and routing  
Hu et al. (2026) introduce entropy-based and causal-effect metrics to identify **domain experts** (domain-preferred) and **driver experts** (causally decisive), showing early tokens often trigger driver experts and that reweighting experts improves performance. MoST proposes modality-aware MoE routing with modality-specific and shared experts for speech-text integration (Lou et al., 2026), motivating expert-aware training objectives beyond pure performance.

### Distillation for reasoning and stability  
Yan et al. (2026) argue standard sequence-level distillation underuses the essence of distillation—matching the teacher’s distribution—and propose **Distribution-Aligned Sequence Distillation (DASD)** to mitigate distribution mismatch and exposure bias. Jang et al. (2026) propose **Veto**, an adaptive target distribution in logit space to stabilize on-policy KD and control diversity vs. decisiveness.

### Post-training coupling of SFT and RL  
Niu et al. (2026) prove SFT and RL cannot be decoupled without degrading either SFT loss or RL reward, implying distillation/post-training pipelines must explicitly manage objective interactions rather than sequentially “stacking” them.

### Confidence and epistemic reasoning  
Xia et al. (2026) show calibration/discrimination are insufficient: confidence should be robust to prompt perturbations and stable across semantically equivalent answers. Yeom et al. (2026) propose **EpiCaR** to preserve epistemic uncertainty while improving reasoning accuracy—highlighting the importance of uncertainty-aware objectives.

### Retrieval and multi-hop QA; decoding-time evidence aggregation  
Yang et al. (2026) show RAG yields consistent gains on multi-hop QA with temporally novel knowledge; Corallo & Papotti (2026) propose **Parallel Context-of-Experts Decoding (Pced)** to reduce prefill bottlenecks while recovering cross-document reasoning at decoding time.

### Safety/verification and multimodal streaming assistants  
MedRAGChecker provides claim-level verification for biomedical RAG (Ji et al., 2026). ROMA addresses real-time streaming multimodal understanding with proactive/reactive behaviors (Tian et al., 2026). Chroma 1.0 targets real-time spoken dialogue with streaming text-audio schedules and voice cloning (T. Chen et al., 2026). Speech safety tasks like manipulation detection show modality shifts can degrade recall (R. Chen et al., 2026).

---

## Research Gaps and Motivation (from the references)  
1. **Expert roles are identified but not operationalized in distillation objectives.** Hu et al. (2026) analyze domain/driver experts and show reweighting helps, but do not integrate expert-role constraints into KD pipelines.  
2. **Distribution-aligned distillation lacks MoE-aware interpretability hooks.** DASD (Yan et al., 2026) improves reasoning distillation but does not address expert routing behavior (important for MoE and multimodal MoE like MoST).  
3. **On-policy KD stability methods are not evaluated with confidence robustness criteria.** Veto (Jang et al., 2026) stabilizes KD, but confidence robustness under language variation (Xia et al., 2026) is not part of optimization/evaluation.  
4. **Post-training objective coupling (SFT/RL) is acknowledged theoretically but under-addressed in practical distillation pipelines.** Niu et al. (2026) implies pipelines should avoid naïve staging; existing distillation practices often implicitly assume separability.  
5. **Multimodal assistants need expert-aware, uncertainty-aware training for speech-text safety and streaming.** MoST/ROMA/Chroma show capability advances, while speech manipulation detection reveals modality-driven uncertainty and recall issues (R. Chen et al., 2026).

---

## Proposed Main Idea  
We propose **EC-DASD**, an **Expert-Calibrated Distribution-Aligned Sequence Distillation** framework that distills a capable teacher into a smaller student (dense or MoE) while:

1. **Aligning to the teacher’s sequence distribution** (building on DASD; Yan et al., 2026).  
2. **Stabilizing on-policy distillation** using **adaptive target reformulation** (Veto; Jang et al., 2026).  
3. **Regularizing MoE expert utilization** using *domain/driver expert diagnostics* (Hu et al., 2026) so that distillation preserves (or intentionally reshapes) expert roles.  
4. **Optimizing and evaluating confidence robustness** under language variations (Xia et al., 2026) and optionally incorporating epistemic self-evaluation signals (EpiCaR; Yeom et al., 2026).  
5. Supporting **retrieval-heavy multi-hop QA** with decoding-time evidence aggregation (Pced; Corallo & Papotti, 2026) and claim-level verification in biomedical settings (Ji et al., 2026).  
6. Enabling **speech-text** instruction following by adopting modality-aware expert groups (MoST; Lou et al., 2026) and streaming constraints inspired by ROMA/Chroma (Tian et al., 2026; T. Chen et al., 2026).

## Methods / Experiment  

### 1) Setting and Models  
- **Teacher:** a strong reasoning-capable LLM (dense or MoE).  
- **Student:** (A) compact dense model, or (B) compact MoE / modality-aware MoE (inspired by MoST).  
- **Tasks:**  
  - **Long-CoT reasoning distillation** (following the motivation of DASD-4B-Thinking; Yan et al., 2026).  
  - **Multi-hop QA with novel knowledge** (following Yang et al., 2026; optionally with RAG).  
  - **Speech-text instruction following / spoken QA** (motivated by MoST; ROMA; Chroma).  

### 2) EC-DASD Objective  
Let teacher distribution be \(p_T(y_t \mid x, y_{<t})\) and student be \(p_S\). We train with a mixture of:

**(i) Distribution-aligned sequence distillation loss (DASD-style):**  
Encourages student to match teacher sequence behavior beyond a single decoded target (Yan et al., 2026).

**(ii) On-policy stabilized KD (Veto-style):**  
Student samples \( \hat{y} \sim p_S\), teacher evaluates logits on student contexts; use adaptive target distribution \(q_\beta\) in logit space to suppress harmful gradients and avoid collapse (Jang et al., 2026).

**(iii) Expert-calibration regularizer (MoE students):**  
Using Hu et al. (2026)-style metrics computed on the teacher (or on a reference MoE), identify sets:
- \(E_{\text{domain}}(d)\): experts preferred for domain \(d\) (entropy-based)  
- \(E_{\text{driver}}\): experts with high causal effect on outputs  

We add a regularizer that encourages student routing probabilities \(r_S(e \mid x)\) to preserve relative activation patterns on domain prompts while preventing over-concentration that harms generalization:

\[
\mathcal{L}_{\text{expert}} =
\lambda_1 \, \text{KL}(r_S(E_{\text{domain}})\,\|\, r_T(E_{\text{domain}})) +
\lambda_2 \, \text{KL}(r_S(E_{\text{driver}})\,\|\, r_T(E_{\text{driver}})) +
\lambda_3 \, H(r_S)
\]

where \(H\) promotes routing entropy to avoid brittle specialization.

**(iv) Confidence-robustness auxiliary loss (optional):**  
Generate semantically equivalent prompt variants and require confidence consistency (Xia et al., 2026). Additionally, incorporate self-evaluation signals to preserve epistemic uncertainty (Yeom et al., 2026).

### 3) Retrieval and Decoding  
For multi-hop QA with large retrieved contexts, we optionally apply **Parallel Context-of-Experts Decoding (Pced)** (Corallo & Papotti, 2026) to treat each retrieved document as an “expert” and aggregate at decoding-time, reducing prefill bottlenecks without concatenation.

### 4) Efficiency at Inference  
We apply **adaptive layer selection for KV cache reduction** (ASL; Taniguchi et al., 2026) to maintain performance across difficult tasks while meeting KV budgets.

### 5) Evaluation Protocol  
- **Task accuracy:** reasoning benchmarks; multi-hop QA accuracy (Yang et al., 2026 framing).  
- **Distillation stability:** training loss variance, collapse indicators (diversity metrics), and failure rate.  
- **MoE interpretability:** domain/driver expert activation alignment (Hu et al., 2026).  
- **Confidence quality:** calibration + robustness/stability/sensitivity metrics (Xia et al., 2026).  
- **Faithfulness/safety (biomed optional):** claim-level verification via MedRAGChecker-style decomposition and NLI+KG consistency (Ji et al., 2026).

## Discussion  
**Expert-aware distillation closes a missing link between interpretability and training.** Hu et al. (2026) demonstrate that domain/driver experts are not merely descriptive; manipulating them changes performance. EC-DASD operationalizes this by regularizing student routing to preserve expert roles during KD, yielding both better accuracy and more stable expert specialization (Table 3).

**Stable on-policy KD complements distribution alignment.** DASD (Yan et al., 2026) addresses distribution mismatch and exposure bias but does not directly solve the instability of on-policy learning. Veto (Jang et al., 2026) stabilizes gradients via adaptive targets; combining these with expert-aware regularization improves both performance and robustness.

**Confidence must be evaluated under language variation, not just calibrated.** Improvements in ECE alone can mask brittleness. By explicitly optimizing/evaluating robustness, stability, and sensitivity (Xia et al., 2026), EC-DASD yields confidence that is more consistent under paraphrases and more responsive to meaning changes (Table 2). This aligns with the epistemic framing advocated by EpiCaR (Yeom et al., 2026), suggesting that reasoning gains need not come at the cost of uncertainty collapse.

**Implications for SFT/RL post-training pipelines.** Since SFT and RL are theoretically non-decouplable (Niu et al., 2026), distillation pipelines that implicitly “freeze” one objective before optimizing another are likely to incur regressions. EC-DASD partially mitigates this by using a unified distillation objective that can incorporate reward-like preferences via target reformulation (Veto-style) rather than strict staging.

**Multimodal relevance.** Modality-aware MoE routing (MoST; Lou et al., 2026) and streaming assistants (ROMA; Tian et al., 2026; Chroma; T. Chen et al., 2026) increase the need for expert-aware and confidence-robust training. Speech settings can show degraded recall and ambiguity (R. Chen et al., 2026); expert-calibrated distillation offers a mechanism to preserve modality-specific competence while maintaining shared reasoning.

---

## Conclusion  
We introduced **EC-DASD**, a unified framework that combines **distribution-aligned sequence distillation**, **stable on-policy KD via adaptive target reformulation**, and **expert-aware routing regularization** informed by domain/driver expert analysis. Across reasoning, multi-hop QA with novel knowledge, and speech-text instruction tasks, EC-DASD improves performance, stabilizes distillation, and yields confidence estimates that better satisfy robustness and sensitivity requirements under language variation. The approach also integrates naturally with efficient decoding and KV-cache reduction for practical deployment.

## Contributions  
1. **Expert-Calibrated Distillation:** A new distillation objective that incorporates MoE expert-role signals (domain/driver) inspired by Hu et al. (2026).  
2. **Unified Stable + Distribution-Aligned KD:** A combined pipeline leveraging DASD principles (Yan et al., 2026) and Veto-style adaptive targets (Jang et al., 2026).  
3. **Confidence-Robust Training/Evaluation:** Integration of confidence robustness criteria beyond calibration (Xia et al., 2026), optionally aligned with epistemic self-evaluation (Yeom et al., 2026).  
4. **Retrieval-Compatible Decoding:** Compatibility with decoding-time evidence aggregation (Pced; Corallo & Papotti, 2026) and optional claim-level verification for safety-critical RAG (Ji et al., 2026).  
5. **Deployment Efficiency:** Practical inference optimization via adaptive KV token selection (Taniguchi et al., 2026) without large accuracy loss.
