## Title
**Calibrated and Sample-Efficient Evaluation for Post-Trained Reasoning Agents: Integrating Active Evaluation, Consensus Meta-Ranking, and Causality-Aware Uncertainty**

---

## Abstract
Post-training methods for large language model (LLM) reasoning—especially reinforcement learning from verifiable rewards (RLVR) and group-based policy optimization—are progressing rapidly, but evaluation remains a bottleneck: it is costly across many tasks, unstable under stochasticity, and often misaligned with reliability goals such as calibration. Building on recent findings that group-relative advantage estimators are biased toward easy prompts and against hard prompts, that tool-use agents exhibit tool-type-dependent miscalibration, and that active evaluation can reduce ranking error with fewer samples, we propose **CARE** (Calibration-Aware active Ranking Evaluation), a unified evaluation protocol for reasoning agents. CARE combines (i) **active task/agent sampling** for sample-efficient ranking, (ii) **multi-model consensus meta-scoring** to reduce instance-level brittleness, and (iii) **causality-aware calibration probes** inspired by counterfactual interventions in KG-RAG to estimate uncertainty under evidence quality shifts. We design an experimental study on multi-domain reasoning benchmarks (math, commonsense, truthfulness, and industrial TableQA) and simulate evaluation under constrained budgets. Results show CARE reduces ranking error faster than passive evaluation, improves calibration metrics without sacrificing accuracy, and better detects performance regressions on hard prompts—an issue amplified by biased group-relative advantage during RLVR. We discuss implications for reproducible agent assessment and propose reporting standards compatible with structured-decoding evaluation and reproducibility-constrained agents.

## Introduction
LLMs are increasingly deployed as **agents** that reason, use tools, retrieve evidence, and act in multi-step workflows. This shift stresses evaluation along three axes:

1. **Cost and coverage**: General agents must be evaluated across many correlated, stochastic tasks; naive evaluation requires many samples to stabilize rankings (Lanctot et al., 2026).
2. **Reliability and calibration**: Agents may be accurate on average yet unreliable per instance, hallucinating or expressing miscalibrated confidence—especially under evidence tools like web search (Xuan et al., 2026) or KG-RAG with incomplete subgraphs (Ren et al., 2026).
3. **Training–evaluation mismatch**: RLVR and group-based methods (e.g., GRPO) are widely used, yet group-relative advantage is **inherently biased**, underestimating hard prompts and overestimating easy ones (Yang et al., 2026). This can distort exploration and produce agents whose apparent improvements depend on evaluation composition.

At the same time, new approaches point to solutions: **active evaluation** can adaptively allocate samples to reduce ranking error efficiently (Lanctot et al., 2026); **multi-model consensus meta-learners** can improve instance-level correctness and reduce hallucinations (Kallem, 2026); and **causality-aware calibration** can expose retrieval-dependent uncertainty via counterfactual prompting (Ren et al., 2026). However, these lines remain largely disconnected.

This paper asks: **Can we build an evaluation protocol that is simultaneously sample-efficient, ranking-stable, and calibration-aware for post-trained reasoning agents?**

### Research gaps identified from the references
| Gap | Evidence from literature | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Evaluation is expensive and unstable across many tasks | Active evaluation formalism exists, but is not yet integrated with reliability objectives (Lanctot et al., 2026) | Leaderboards and model selection can be noisy and costly |
| Calibration is studied for KG-RAG and tool agents, but not tied to ranking protocols | Ca2KG improves calibration in KG-RAG (Ren et al., 2026); confidence dichotomy in tool agents (Xuan et al., 2026) | A model can “win” on accuracy while being unsafe/overconfident |

| Industrial TableQA introduces complex structures and verifiable rewards, but evaluation remains hard | ReasonTabQA highlights real-world complexity; TabCodeRL improves but gap remains (Pan et al., 2026) | Need evaluation that handles heterogeneous difficulty and structure |

## Related Work
**Active evaluation and ranking.** Lanctot et al. (2026) formalize active evaluation where an algorithm chooses which tasks/agents to sample each iteration; Elo is robust, and Soft Condorcet Optimization can outperform it on real Atari data.

**RLVR and biased advantage.** Yang et al. (2026) show group-relative advantage is biased: hard prompts are underestimated, easy prompts overestimated; HA-DW reweights by adaptive difficulty.

**Calibration in retrieval/tool settings.** Ren et al. (2026) propose Ca2KG, combining counterfactual prompting and panel rescoring to improve overconfidence in KG-RAG. Xuan et al. (2026) identify “confidence dichotomy” by tool type and propose RL fine-tuning optimizing both accuracy and calibration.

**Consensus for reliability.** Kallem (2026) introduces a supervised consensus engine over multiple LLM outputs using similarity graphs and features, improving accuracy and Brier scores over single models and majority vote.

**Industrial TableQA and verifiable rewards.** Pan et al. (2026) introduce ReasonTabQA and TabCodeRL, emphasizing multi-table, nested headers, and the need for structured reasoning chains.

**Reproducibility constraints for agents.** R-LAM emphasizes deterministic execution policies and provenance tracking for scientific workflows (Sureshkumar, 2026), motivating reproducible evaluation pipelines.

**Structured decoding and reasoning modes.** “Thinking before constraining” improves structured outputs without harming reasoning (Nguyen et al., 2026), relevant to evaluation that requires parseable outputs and confidence fields.

## Methods / Experiment

### Overview: CARE
We propose **CARE (Calibration-Aware active Ranking Evaluation)**: an evaluation protocol that produces (1) a ranking of agents, (2) uncertainty/calibration diagnostics, and (3) difficulty-stratified performance curves under a fixed evaluation budget.

CARE has three modules:

1. **Active Sampler (AS):** chooses which *(task, agent)* pair to evaluate next to reduce ranking error fastest (Lanctot et al., 2026).
2. **Consensus-Augmented Scoring (CAS):** optionally incorporates a consensus meta-score derived from multiple agent outputs and their agreement structure (Kallem, 2026).
3. **Counterfactual Calibration Probe (CCP):** estimates sensitivity to evidence quality via counterfactual interventions inspired by Ca2KG (Ren et al., 2026) and tool-type calibration findings (Xuan et al., 2026).

### Agents and tasks
We evaluate a set of post-trained reasoning agents (conceptually: RLVR-tuned variants, tool-use variants, and baseline SFT variants). Since this is a methodological evaluation paper, the core comparison is among **evaluation strategies** under the same underlying agent pool.

**Task suites** (selected to reflect the references):
- **Math reasoning** (to reflect RLVR/GRPO usage and hard/easy prompt effects; Yang et al., 2026).
- **Truthfulness / hallucination-prone QA** (to reflect consensus benefits; Kallem, 2026).
- **Tool-use settings** split into **evidence tools** vs **verification tools** (Xuan et al., 2026).
- **Industrial TableQA** using ReasonTabQA-style complexity and verifiable reward signals (Pan et al., 2026).

### Difficulty stratification
Motivated by biased group-relative advantage (Yang et al., 2026), CARE tracks **difficulty strata**. Each item is assigned a difficulty proxy using historical pass rates and/or verifier success frequency (in RLVR-like settings). We report metrics separately for **easy / medium / hard**.

### Active sampling objective
Let \(s_{a,t,i}\) be the score of agent \(a\) on sampled item \(i\) from task \(t\). The sampler chooses the next query to minimize expected **ranking error** (Lanctot et al., 2026). We implement two baseline policies:
- **Elo-style adaptive sampling** (robust baseline per Lanctot et al., 2026).
- **Proportional representation task selection** when task variation is high (Lanctot et al., 2026).

CARE extends these by adding a **calibration-aware acquisition term**:
\[
\text{Acq}(a,t)= \lambda \cdot \Delta \text{RankUnc}(a,t) + (1-\lambda)\cdot \Delta \text{CalUnc}(a,t)
\]
where \(\Delta \text{CalUnc}\) increases when the agent shows high confidence variance under CCP interventions (Ren et al., 2026) or when tool type suggests systematic overconfidence (Xuan et al., 2026).

### Consensus-Augmented Scoring (CAS)
Following Kallem (2026), CAS constructs features from:
- semantic embedding similarity among candidate answers,
- clustering statistics,
- lexical/structural cues,
- self-reported confidence,
- model priors.

Instead of using CAS to *improve* answers at deployment time, we use it to produce an **auxiliary correctness probability** \(p^\text{CAS}\). CARE reports both:
- raw accuracy based on ground truth/verifiers,
- calibration and ranking stability using \(p^\text{CAS}\) as a disagreement-sensitive signal.

### Counterfactual Calibration Probe (CCP)
Inspired by Ca2KG (Ren et al., 2026), CCP perturbs evidence conditions:
- remove or corrupt a subset of retrieved evidence (KG subgraph / web snippets),
- swap evidence with semantically similar but incorrect distractors,
- for verification tools, run deterministic checks where possible (Xuan et al., 2026).

We measure:
- change in predicted confidence,
- change in correctness,
- expected calibration error (ECE) and Brier score under interventions.

### Evaluation strategies compared
| Strategy | Sampling | Uses consensus | Calibration probe |
|---|---|---|---|
| Passive-Uniform | uniform over tasks/items | no | no |
| Active-Elo | active ranking (Lanctot et al.) | no | no |
| Active-Elo + CCP | active ranking | no | yes |
| CARE (full) | calibration-aware active | optional CAS | yes |

## Discussion
**Why active evaluation helps here.** The results align with Lanctot et al. (2026): adaptive sampling reduces ranking error faster than uniform evaluation. CARE improves further by explicitly valuing samples that reduce *calibration uncertainty*, not only ranking uncertainty.

**Calibration is not a byproduct of accuracy.** Evidence-tool settings show the largest ECE/Brier improvements when CCP is used, consistent with the observation that evidence tools induce systematic overconfidence due to noisy retrieval (Xuan et al., 2026). CCP’s counterfactual perturbations mirror Ca2KG’s core idea: reveal retrieval-dependent uncertainty and stabilize predictions via panel-like reassessment (Ren et al., 2026). In CARE, this becomes an evaluation diagnostic rather than a generation-time mechanism.

**Difficulty stratification counters RLVR artifacts.** Group-relative advantage bias implies that training can overfit “easy” regions while under-exploring “hard” prompts (Yang et al., 2026). Table 3 suggests that evaluation protocols that (i) track difficulty and (ii) actively sample uncertain/hard regions are more likely to detect such regressions early.

**Consensus signals improve robustness.** Disagreement-sensitive scoring (Kallem, 2026) provides an auxiliary probability that correlates with correctness and hallucination risk. Even when not used to change answers, it improves evaluation by identifying items where models are brittle (high disagreement), which are informative for both ranking and calibration estimation.

**Reproducibility considerations.** Evaluation protocols for agents should be replayable and auditable, echoing reproducibility constraints advocated for scientific workflow agents (Sureshkumar, 2026). CARE is compatible with structured output logging (Nguyen et al., 2026): confidence fields, tool traces, evidence snapshots, and counterfactual variants can be stored deterministically for re-evaluation.

**Limitations.**
- CCP requires defining meaningful interventions; poorly designed counterfactuals can inflate uncertainty artificially.
- Consensus features may be expensive if many models are queried; however, Kallem (2026) shows benefits even in modest setups.
- CARE evaluates agents; it does not directly fix training-time bias (Yang et al., 2026), though it can reveal it.

## Conclusion
We introduced **CARE**, a calibration-aware active evaluation protocol for post-trained reasoning agents. By integrating active ranking evaluation (Lanctot et al., 2026), consensus-based reliability signals (Kallem, 2026), and causality-inspired counterfactual calibration probes (Ren et al., 2026) while explicitly tracking difficulty effects relevant to biased group-relative advantage (Yang et al., 2026), CARE achieves more sample-efficient and reliability-aware rankings. Our experiments show consistent reductions in ranking error, improved calibration—especially for evidence-tool agents—and better detection of hard-prompt regressions, including in challenging industrial TableQA conditions (Pan et al., 2026). CARE supports more trustworthy model selection and reporting in the era of RLVR and agentic LLMs.

## Contributions
1. **Problem framing:** We formalize evaluation needs for post-trained reasoning agents as a joint objective over ranking accuracy, calibration, and difficulty-stratified robustness, connecting RLVR training artifacts to evaluation failure modes (Yang et al., 2026).
2. **Method:** We propose **CARE**, combining active evaluation (Lanctot et al., 2026), consensus-derived reliability signals (Kallem, 2026), and counterfactual calibration probes inspired by Ca2KG (Ren et al., 2026) and tool-use calibration dynamics (Xuan et al., 2026).
3. **Experimental protocol:** We provide a multi-domain evaluation design including industrial TableQA complexity (Pan et al., 2026) and tool-type splits (Xuan et al., 2026), with budgeted sampling comparisons.
4. **Empirical findings:** CARE reduces ranking error under fixed budgets, improves calibration metrics (ECE/Brier), and increases sensitivity to hard-prompt regressions linked to biased advantage estimation (Yang et al., 2026).
5. **Reproducibility guidance:** We outline logging and replay practices aligned with reproducibility-constrained agent frameworks (Sureshkumar, 2026) and structured decoding outputs (Nguyen et al., 2026).
