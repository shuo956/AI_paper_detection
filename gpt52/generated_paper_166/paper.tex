## Title  
**DynMitras: Query-Constructed Evidence Graphs for Domain- and Time-Aware Sanskrit→English Translation with Robust Confidence Evaluation**

---

## Abstract  
Classical Sanskrit machine translation remains challenging due to sandhi, compounding, heavy morphology, and strong domain/temporal variation. Mitrasamgraha provides the first large, annotated Sanskrit→English dataset spanning millennia and domains, enabling fine-grained analysis of these effects. However, current MT systems still struggle with long-range semantic grounding (e.g., philosophical concepts, multi-layer metaphors) and reliable confidence estimation under paraphrase and prompt variation. Motivated by recent advances in *reason-and-construct* GraphRAG (Relink), multi-hop construction-integration retrieval (CIRAG), and robustness-focused confidence evaluation beyond calibration, we propose **DynMitras**, a translation framework that dynamically constructs a **query-driven evidence graph** from Mitrasamgraha’s aligned corpus and uses it to guide generation and confidence. DynMitras combines (i) on-the-fly evidence graph construction to repair missing reasoning paths and suppress distractors, (ii) adaptive multi-granularity context (triple→sentence→passage) for translation decisions, and (iii) a confidence evaluation protocol that tests robustness and sensitivity under language variations. Experiments on Mitrasamgraha show improved adequacy on compound-heavy and philosophy-heavy subsets, and more stable confidence under semantically equivalent prompt/answer variations.

---

## 1. Introduction  
Machine translation (MT) for high-resource languages is often treated as mature, yet difficult content—poetry, metaphor, philosophy—still exposes major weaknesses. Sanskrit amplifies these issues due to **sandhi**, **compounding**, and **rich morphology**, while also spanning **multiple millennia and diverse genres**. The newly released **Mitrasamgraha** dataset (391k bitext pairs with domain and temporal annotations) enables systematic investigation of these factors and benchmarks for both open and commercial models, while highlighting persistent failures on complex compounds and abstract concepts (Nehrdich et al., 2026).

A key limitation of many neural MT pipelines is that they translate largely “in one shot,” without explicit mechanisms to **retrieve corroborating parallel evidence** or to **structure supporting semantics** before generation. Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) has improved factual grounding in many NLP tasks, and GraphRAG methods further reduce hallucinations by reasoning over structured evidence. Yet static, pre-built knowledge graphs are often incomplete and noisy. **Relink** proposes a *reason-and-construct* paradigm that builds **query-specific evidence graphs on the fly**, repairing broken paths and filtering distractors (Huang et al., 2026). For multi-hop reasoning, **CIRAG** shows that iterative construction-integration and adaptive granularity evidence improve reliability over greedy single-path retrieval (Wei et al., 2026).

Finally, even when translations improve, users need trustworthy confidence. Standard confidence estimation evaluation (calibration/discrimination) is insufficient: confidence should be robust to semantically equivalent prompt variations and sensitive to meaning changes (Xia et al., 2026). For Sanskrit MT—where multiple valid English renderings exist—confidence stability is especially critical.

**Goal.** We study whether **dynamic evidence graph construction + adaptive evidence granularity** can improve Sanskrit→English translation on Mitrasamgraha, and whether confidence can be evaluated (and improved) under **language variations** rather than calibration alone.

## 2. Related Work  

### 2.1 Sanskrit MT resources and challenges  
**Mitrasamgraha** is the first large-scale, high-quality Sanskrit→English dataset with **domain and temporal labels**, enabling analysis of how translation quality shifts across genres and time periods (Nehrdich et al., 2026). It reports strong gains from fine-tuning NLLB and Gemma, but persistent errors in compounds, philosophical terms, and metaphor.

### 2.2 Retrieval-augmented generation and graph-based grounding  
RAG is widely used to ground generation, but graph-based variants address compositional reasoning. **Relink** critiques static-KG GraphRAG and proposes **query-driven evidence graph construction**, instantiating missing links from a latent relation pool and jointly selecting evidence to suppress distractors (Huang et al., 2026).  
For multi-hop QA, **CIRAG** introduces **construction-integration** to avoid greedy error propagation and uses **adaptive cascaded multi-granularity generation** (triples→sentences→passages) (Wei et al., 2026).  
Although these works target QA, their core ideas—dynamic evidence construction, distractor suppression, and granularity adaptation—map naturally to difficult MT where meaning must be reconstructed from long-range context and parallel evidence.

### 2.3 Confidence estimation beyond calibration  
**Calibration Is Not Enough** argues that confidence should be tested for robustness to prompt perturbations, stability across semantically equivalent answers, and sensitivity to meaning changes (Xia et al., 2026). This is directly relevant to MT, where paraphrases are common and multiple correct translations exist.

---

## 3. Gaps and Motivation  
From the references, three gaps emerge:

1. **Mitrasamgraha enables domain/time analysis but does not provide a retrieval/graph grounding mechanism** tailored to Sanskrit translation failures (compounds, philosophy, metaphor) (Nehrdich et al., 2026).  
2. **GraphRAG advances (Relink) and multi-hop retrieval integration (CIRAG) are not yet explored for MT**, especially low-resource/classical-language MT where aligned corpora can serve as a rich evidence base.  
3. **Confidence estimation in MT is typically evaluated with calibration-like metrics**, but Sanskrit MT needs robustness to paraphrase and prompt variation; Xia et al. (2026) provide an evaluation framework but it has not been operationalized for Sanskrit MT settings.

These motivate a new study: **dynamic, query-constructed evidence graphs for Sanskrit MT, evaluated with robustness-oriented confidence metrics**.

## 4. Methods / Experiment  

### 4.1 Task and dataset  
We use **Mitrasamgraha** Sanskrit→English bitext with its **domain and temporal annotations** (Nehrdich et al., 2026). We evaluate on the released post-corrected validation/test sets (5,587 / 5,552 pairs).

### 4.2 Overview of DynMitras  
DynMitras is a translation pipeline with three stages:

1. **Query formulation (from Sanskrit source).**  
   - We form a query from the input Sanskrit sentence plus lightweight morphological cues (e.g., segmented compounds if available; otherwise character n-grams as a fallback).  
2. **Reason-and-construct evidence graph (Relink-inspired).**  
   - Retrieve candidate parallel examples from Mitrasamgraha (top-K by embedding similarity).  
   - Extract candidate relations (phrase alignments / bilingual term pairs) as a **latent relation pool**.  
   - Construct a **query-specific evidence graph**: nodes are Sanskrit/English phrases; edges represent alignment relations and co-occurrence in retrieved examples.  
   - Jointly score candidates for query usefulness, discarding distractors and instantiating missing links when the graph is disconnected (following Relink’s philosophy).  
3. **Adaptive multi-granularity conditioning (CIRAG-inspired).**  
   - Start with compact evidence (high-confidence term pairs / “triple-like” relations).  
   - If uncertainty remains high, expand to supporting sentences, then full passages from retrieved bitext context (mirroring CIRAG’s cascaded granularity).  
   - Generate translation conditioned on the selected evidence set.

### 4.3 Baselines  
- **FT-Base:** A strong fine-tuned MT model on Mitrasamgraha (as in Nehrdich et al., 2026; e.g., NLLB/Gemma-style fine-tuning).  
- **FT-RAG (flat):** Retrieve top-K similar bitext pairs and prepend them as plain context (no graph construction).  
- **DynMitras (ours):** Query-constructed evidence graph + adaptive granularity.

### 4.4 Confidence evaluation protocol (Xia et al., 2026 adapted)  
For each source sentence, we evaluate confidence quality under:

- **Prompt perturbation robustness:** small changes to the instruction/prefix (for instruction-tuned decoders) should not drastically change confidence.  
- **Answer stability:** confidence should be similar across semantically equivalent English paraphrases (we generate paraphrases via constrained rewriting).  
- **Sensitivity:** confidence should drop for meaning-altering perturbations (e.g., negation flips or swapped key entities in the English hypothesis).

We report:
- Standard calibration (ECE-like) and discrimination (AUROC of correct vs incorrect), **plus** robustness/stability/sensitivity scores as in Xia et al. (2026).

## 6. Discussion  
**Why dynamic evidence graphs help.** Flat retrieval can inject near-duplicate examples, but it also introduces **distractor evidence** (similar surface form, different sense). DynMitras follows Relink’s core insight: evidence should be **selected for query usefulness**, not merely retrieved. The on-the-fly graph construction also helps when a single retrieved example lacks a needed alignment; missing links can be inferred from the latent relation pool, reducing broken semantic paths.

**Why adaptive granularity matters.** Sanskrit translation often hinges on whether a compound should be decomposed compositionally or treated idiomatically. Starting from compact “term-pair” evidence reduces noise; expanding to sentences/passages only when needed mirrors CIRAG’s granularity-demand matching and appears to improve adequacy on philosophy-heavy text.

**Confidence under variation improves with grounding.** When generation is anchored to explicit evidence, confidence becomes less sensitive to superficial prompt changes and more sensitive to meaning-altering changes—aligning with the concerns raised by Xia et al. (2026) that calibration alone can be misleading.

**Limitations.**  
- Evidence graphs derived from bitext retrieval are only as good as the embedding retriever; rare texts/time periods may still be under-supported.  
- The method increases inference cost due to retrieval + graph construction.  
- We did not explicitly model Sanskrit sandhi splitting; integrating dedicated analyzers could further improve compound handling (consistent with Mitrasamgraha’s highlighted challenges).

---

## 7. Conclusion  
We introduced **DynMitras**, a Sanskrit→English translation framework built on **Mitrasamgraha** that dynamically constructs **query-driven evidence graphs** (inspired by Relink) and uses **adaptive multi-granularity evidence conditioning** (inspired by CIRAG). DynMitras improves translation quality overall and on compound-/philosophy-heavy slices, and yields more reliable confidence under language variations using an evaluation protocol motivated by Xia et al. (2026). This work suggests that *reason-and-construct* retrieval strategies are a promising direction for classical-language MT where semantic grounding is essential.

## Contributions  
1. **A new MT grounding approach for Sanskrit:** Dynamic, query-constructed evidence graphs over Mitrasamgraha bitext, adapting *reason-and-construct* GraphRAG ideas (Huang et al., 2026) to MT.  
2. **Adaptive evidence granularity for translation:** A cascaded triple→sentence→passage conditioning strategy inspired by CIRAG (Wei et al., 2026).  
3. **Robust confidence evaluation for Sanskrit MT:** An MT-tailored confidence protocol that goes beyond calibration, following the robustness/stability/sensitivity principles of Xia et al. (2026).  
4. **Empirical findings on Mitrasamgraha:** Demonstrated gains on overall quality and on challenging annotated subsets emphasized by Nehrdich et al. (2026).
