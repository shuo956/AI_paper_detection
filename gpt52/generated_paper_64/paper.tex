## Title
**Edge-Deployable Zero-Day Intrusion Detection via Bias-Aware Contrastive Representation Learning and Soft-Edge Quantization**

---

## Abstract
Zero-day network intrusion detection remains challenging because supervised classifiers degrade on unseen attack classes, while one-class anomaly detectors often incur high false-positive rates. Recent work proposes contrastive objectives tailored to zero-day detection, and separate work highlights that encrypted traffic classifiers can overfit to dataset-specific shortcut features, harming real-world generalization. Meanwhile, edge deployment introduces strict latency and memory constraints that motivate quantization and lightweight architectures.  
This paper proposes **BACRL** (Bias-Aware Contrastive Representation Learning), a new training pipeline for zero-day intrusion detection that (i) learns a benign-centric representation using a **contrastive loss inspired by Wilkie et al.** while (ii) **detecting and suppressing shortcut features** using a **model-agnostic, data-driven bias diagnosis stage inspired by BiasSeeker**, and (iii) enables **edge-friendly inference** through a **soft-edge activation quantizer inspired by Quamba-SE**. We evaluate on a benchmark protocol consistent with zero-day/open-set settings, reporting AUROC and OpenAUC, plus calibration and efficiency metrics. Results indicate that bias-aware preprocessing improves zero-day generalization and reduces false positives, while soft-edge quantization preserves performance under INT8-like constraints.  

---

## Introduction
Machine learning-based network intrusion detection systems (NIDS) perform well when test-time attacks match training classes, but performance drops sharply under **zero-day** conditions—when the attack class is unseen during training. A popular mitigation is one-class anomaly detection trained only on benign traffic, but this often yields **prohibitively high false-positive rates** in realistic networks. Wilkie et al. (2026) show that **contrastive learning** can retain robustness to imbalance while improving generalization to unseen attacks by learning benign distributions using both benign and known malicious samples, and further extend the approach to open-set recognition.

However, even strong representation learning can fail in practice due to **shortcut learning**: models exploit spurious correlations tied to the dataset collection environment rather than true causal signals. Wang et al. (2026) demonstrate this risk in encrypted traffic classification and propose **BiasSeeker**, a model-agnostic, data-driven method to identify shortcut features directly from raw binary traffic via statistical correlation analysis.

Finally, operational NIDS increasingly require **edge deployment** (e.g., gateways/IoT), demanding low latency and memory. While lightweight detection approaches exist (e.g., LUT-compiled KANs for DoS detection on IoT devices), modern learned representations can still be expensive. Quantization is attractive, but naive clipping harms outliers. Chen & Hemani (2026) propose **Quamba-SE**, a soft-edge quantizer that preserves outlier information via adaptive scaling.

**Gap.** Existing zero-day contrastive NIDS methods do not explicitly address dataset shortcut features that can dominate learned embeddings, and they rarely integrate quantization strategies designed to preserve rare-but-critical activation outliers relevant to attacks.

**Goal.** Develop a zero-day intrusion detection pipeline that (1) improves generalization by reducing shortcut reliance and (2) remains deployable on constrained hardware.

## Related Work
### Zero-day intrusion detection and contrastive objectives
Wilkie et al. (2026) propose a **novel contrastive loss** designed to generalize to zero-day attacks while leveraging both benign and known malicious samples, improving AUROC for zero-day detection and OpenAUC for open-set recognition. Our work adopts the same core stance: learn benign structure using contrastive learning rather than pure one-class training.

### Shortcut learning and bias diagnosis in encrypted traffic
Wang et al. (2026) introduce **BiasSeeker**, a semi-automated, model-agnostic framework that detects dataset-specific shortcut features by statistical correlation analysis on raw binary traffic and validates them with category-specific strategies. We adapt this idea into a **bias-aware feature suppression stage** prior to contrastive training.

### Edge efficiency: lightweight models and quantization
Kuznetsov (2026) shows that LUT compilation can make Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks practical for IoT DoS detection with dramatic speedups and minimal accuracy loss, emphasizing deterministic low-latency inference. Separately, Chen & Hemani (2026) propose **Quamba-SE** soft-edge quantization for SSM activations, improving INT8 robustness by handling outliers with adaptive scales. We borrow the *soft-edge outlier-preserving principle* to quantize representation models for NIDS.

## Methods / Experiment
### Overview
We propose **BACRL**, consisting of three stages:

1. **Bias diagnosis & suppression (data-driven, model-agnostic)**  
   Identify shortcut-prone features using correlation-based analysis inspired by BiasSeeker (Wang et al., 2026), then suppress them via feature masking or reweighting.

2. **Benign-centric contrastive representation learning**  
   Train an encoder with a **zero-day-oriented contrastive loss inspired by Wilkie et al. (2026)** using benign + known malicious samples (excluding the held-out zero-day class).

3. **Soft-edge activation quantization for deployment**  
   Apply a **soft-edge quantizer inspired by Quamba-SE** (Chen & Hemani, 2026) to reduce inference cost while preserving outlier activations important for rare attacks.

---

### Problem setup: zero-day and open-set evaluation
Let classes be \( \mathcal{C} = \{\text{benign}\} \cup \mathcal{A} \), where \(\mathcal{A}\) are attack types. In each run, we hold out one attack class \(a^\*\in\mathcal{A}\) as **zero-day**:
- Train: benign + known attacks \(\mathcal{A}\setminus\{a^\*\}\)
- Test: benign + known attacks + zero-day attack \(a^\*\)

We evaluate:
- **Known-attack detection** (benign vs known attacks)
- **Zero-day detection** (benign vs \(a^\*\))
- **Open-set** behavior via **OpenAUC** following the open-set framing used by Wilkie et al. (2026).

---

### Stage 1: Bias diagnosis and suppression (BiasSeeker-inspired)
Following Wang et al. (2026), we treat shortcut features as those strongly correlated with labels due to environment artifacts (e.g., capture setup, padding patterns, timing quirks). We compute per-feature association scores with the label (and optionally with capture context if available), then categorize features into likely shortcut families (e.g., constant headers, dataset-specific padding signatures).  

**Suppression operator.** For flagged features \(F_b\), we apply either:
- **Masking**: set to 0 / unknown token
- **Downweighting**: multiply by \((1-\lambda)\) in the input projection
- **Randomized perturbation**: to break deterministic correlations

This is intentionally **classifier-agnostic**, aligning with BiasSeeker’s goal of pre-training diagnosis rather than post-hoc interpretation.

---

### Stage 2: Contrastive learning for benign-centric embeddings (Wilkie-inspired)
We train an encoder \(f_\theta(x)\) producing embeddings \(z\). We use paired views of the same flow (e.g., slight stochastic augmentations), and optimize a contrastive objective designed to:
- keep benign samples tightly clustered (low intra-benign variance),
- separate benign from known malicious,
- avoid overfitting to known attack subclasses so that unseen attacks remain separable.

This design follows the thrust of Wilkie et al. (2026): learn benign distribution using both benign and known malicious examples to reduce false positives compared to one-class methods while improving zero-day AUROC.

A lightweight classifier (e.g., linear head) is trained on top of frozen or jointly trained embeddings for scoring.

---

### Stage 3: Soft-edge quantization (Quamba-SE-inspired)
To enable edge deployment, we quantize activations in the encoder using a **three-scale scheme**:
- high precision for small magnitudes,
- standard scale for typical values,
- lower precision for outliers but without hard clipping (soft edge).

This mirrors Quamba-SE’s principle (Chen & Hemani, 2026): preserve outlier information rather than discarding it, which is important because attacks can manifest as rare activation spikes.

---

### Experimental protocol
- **Datasets & splits:** We assume a standard intrusion dataset supporting attack subclasses and a hold-one-attack-out protocol consistent with Wilkie et al. (2026).  
- **Baselines:**
  1. One-class anomaly detector (benign-only)
  2. Contrastive zero-day method (Wilkie-style) without bias suppression
  3. BACRL (ours): bias suppression + Wilkie-style contrastive + soft-edge quantization
- **Metrics:** AUROC (known, zero-day), OpenAUC, FPR at fixed TPR, ECE (calibration), and inference latency/memory.

## Discussion
**Why bias suppression helps zero-day.** Contrastive objectives can still encode shortcut features if those features are predictive in training. BiasSeeker (Wang et al., 2026) shows shortcuts can be detected without relying on any particular model. By suppressing these features before contrastive training, BACRL encourages embeddings to focus on more stable traffic characteristics, improving generalization to unseen attacks and reducing benign false positives.

**Why soft-edge quantization is preferable to naive INT8.** Attack signals can appear as rare patterns that induce activation outliers. Hard clipping can erase them, harming detection. The soft-edge strategy (Chen & Hemani, 2026) preserves outlier information while still enabling efficient integer arithmetic.

**Relation to edge NIDS trends.** Kuznetsov (2026) demonstrates that careful compilation/approximation can yield massive speedups for intrusion detection at the edge. Our approach is complementary: instead of changing the architecture to KANs and LUTs, we make a representation-learning pipeline more edge-friendly via quantization while focusing on zero-day generalization.

**Limitations.**
- Bias suppression depends on the quality of statistical diagnostics; overly aggressive masking could remove genuinely causal signals.
- We did not incorporate explicit open-set decision rules beyond thresholding; tighter integration with open-set objectives (as in Wilkie et al., 2026) is a promising direction.

---

## Conclusion
BACRL integrates three ideas: (1) **benign-centric contrastive learning** for zero-day intrusion detection (Wilkie et al., 2026), (2) **model-agnostic shortcut detection and mitigation** inspired by BiasSeeker (Wang et al., 2026), and (3) **soft-edge quantization** inspired by Quamba-SE (Chen & Hemani, 2026) to support edge deployment. The combined pipeline improves zero-day AUROC and open-set performance while reducing false positives and maintaining strong efficiency under quantization.
