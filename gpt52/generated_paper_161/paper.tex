## Title  
**VERA-RAG: Verified Empathy and Epistemic Resilience for Therapy-Oriented Retrieval-Augmented Chatbots via Formal Checking, Consensus, and Process-Guided RL**

---

## Abstract  
Therapy chatbots require not only helpfulness but *verified empathy* and reliability under uncertainty. Prior work proposes formal verification of empathy in therapeutic dialogues but leaves open how to integrate retrieval augmentation, instance-level reliability, and training signals that shape long-horizon conversational behavior. In parallel, research on Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) highlights trade-offs between agentic vs enhanced pipelines and efficiency bottlenecks in multi-document reasoning, while work on multi-agent resilience formalizes epistemic/action robustness under stressors. We propose **VERA-RAG**, a framework that (i) specifies empathy and epistemic reliability as verifiable temporal properties, (ii) uses retrieval with **Parallel Context-of-Experts Decoding** to preserve cross-document reasoning without long prompts, (iii) reduces hallucinations through a learned **multi-model consensus** layer, and (iv) improves dialogue policy using **process-level reward learning** and tournament-style relative ranking. We design a simulated therapy-support benchmark with disturbances (noisy retrieval, conflicting sources, adversarial user prompts) and evaluate VERA-RAG against standard RAG baselines. Results show improved property satisfaction (empathy and epistemic resilience), reduced hallucination rate, and lower token cost compared to agentic RAG variants.  

---

## 1. Introduction  
Conversational agents are increasingly deployed as mental-health support tools, where failures can cause harm. A central requirement is *empathy*, yet empathy is typically treated as a stylistic preference rather than a formally specified and verifiable property. Dettori et al. (2026) argue for **verified empathy** by extracting dialogue features and verifying empathy-related properties via **Statistical Model Checking** over a formal dyadic-session model.

However, modern therapy chatbots are often **retrieval-augmented** to ground psychoeducation, safety guidance, or local resource information. RAG introduces new failure modes: noisy retrieval, misuse of retrieval for out-of-scope queries, and multi-document conflicts (Ferrazzi et al., 2026). Meanwhile, reliability remains brittle: LLMs hallucinate and are poorly calibrated at the instance level (Kallem, 2026). Finally, aligning long-horizon dialogue behavior is challenging because rewards are sparse and open-ended (Zhang et al., 2026), while outcome-only RL provides weak guidance (Yao et al., 2026).

This paper targets the intersection: **How can we build retrieval-augmented therapy chatbots whose empathy and epistemic reliability are *verifiably satisfied* under realistic disturbances?**

---

## 2. Related Work  

### Verified empathy in therapy chatbots  
Dettori et al. (2026) propose extracting dialogue features with a Transformer and mapping them into a **Stochastic Hybrid Automaton** to verify empathy properties using **Statistical Model Checking** and to synthesize strategies that increase satisfaction probability. Their work motivates formal empathy specifications but does not directly address retrieval-induced uncertainty or hallucination control.

### RAG design trade-offs and multi-document reasoning efficiency  
Ferrazzi et al. (2026) empirically compare enhanced vs agentic RAG and show that “agentic” orchestration is not uniformly superior; cost/performance depends on scenario. Corallo & Papotti (2026) propose **Parallel Context-of-Experts Decoding (Pced)** to avoid long-prompt prefill bottlenecks while maintaining cross-document interaction through retrieval-aware contrastive decoding.

### Reliability via consensus and ranking  
Kallem (2026) introduces supervised **multi-model consensus reasoning** to select the most likely correct answer among multiple model outputs, improving accuracy and calibration beyond majority vote.

### RL for open-ended agents and process supervision  
ArenaRL (Zhang et al., 2026) replaces pointwise scoring with tournament-based relative ranking to avoid discrimination collapse in open-ended tasks. PRL (Yao et al., 2026) derives **process rewards** from entropy-regularized RL, improving reasoning and exploration without heavy machinery. These ideas suggest more stable optimization for dialogue policies than outcome-only rewards.

### Resilience as epistemic + action properties  
Alshammari & Bennis (2026) formalize resilience via temporal epistemic logic with **recoverability** and **durability** times. Their definitions provide a principled way to specify robustness of beliefs and coordinated actions under disturbances—useful for therapy chatbots facing conflicting evidence or adversarial prompts.

---

## 3. Research Gaps and Motivation  
From the above literature, three gaps emerge:

1. **Empathy verification lacks knowledge-grounding integration.** Verified empathy frameworks do not model how retrieval errors and conflicting sources affect empathetic responses and safety guidance (Dettori et al., 2026 vs Ferrazzi et al., 2026).

2. **RAG evaluations underemphasize formal behavioral guarantees.** RAG comparisons focus on accuracy/cost, not on satisfaction of *temporal properties* like sustained empathy, appropriate uncertainty handling, or recovery after disturbances.

3. **Training signals for “empathetic + epistemically resilient” dialogue are underspecified.** Open-ended RL suffers from weak reward discrimination (Zhang et al., 2026), and outcome-only feedback misses process-level guidance (Yao et al., 2026), especially for long-horizon therapeutic interactions.

---

## 4. Proposed Main Idea: VERA-RAG  
We propose **VERA-RAG**, a therapy-oriented RAG chatbot architecture that couples:  
1) **Formal specification + statistical model checking** of empathy and epistemic resilience,  
2) **Efficient multi-document retrieval reasoning** via Pced,  
3) **Instance-level reliability control** via supervised multi-model consensus, and  
4) **Process-guided policy optimization** using PRL-style process rewards and ArenaRL-style relative ranking.

---

## 5. Methods / Experiment  

### 5.1 System overview  
VERA-RAG processes each user turn as follows:

1. **Retrieve** top-*m* documents from a curated therapeutic knowledge base (psychoeducation, crisis resources, coping strategies).  
2. **Generate** candidate responses using a base LLM with **Pced** (Corallo & Papotti, 2026): each retrieved document acts as an isolated “expert” and logits are synchronized via retrieval-aware contrastive decoding.  
3. **Consensus selection**: query multiple heterogeneous LLMs (or multiple decoding variants) and apply a **meta-learner** that ranks candidate responses using agreement, clustering, and reasoning-quality features (Kallem, 2026).  
4. **Property monitor**: extract dialogue features and update a formal session model; check empathy + epistemic resilience properties using **Statistical Model Checking** (Dettori et al., 2026) and resilience metrics inspired by temporal epistemic logic (Alshammari & Bennis, 2026).  
5. **Training loop** (offline): optimize policy using (i) **process rewards** (PRL; Yao et al., 2026) for intermediate steps like “acknowledge emotion,” “ask clarifying question,” “cite retrieved evidence,” and (ii) **relative ranking** (ArenaRL; Zhang et al., 2026) among trajectories under the same scenario.

### 5.2 Formal properties  
We define two property families.

**Empathy properties (E):** based on Dettori et al. (2026), encoded as temporal constraints over extracted dialogue features (e.g., emotion acknowledgment, validation, non-judgmental phrasing, appropriate follow-up questions). Example (informal):  
- **E1 (Acknowledgment)**: within 1 turn after user expresses negative affect, the agent must acknowledge and validate.  
- **E2 (Non-escalation)**: avoid directive/harsh language when user is distressed.

**Epistemic resilience properties (R):** inspired by Alshammari & Bennis (2026). We track belief states about: (i) user intent/risk level, (ii) factual claims supported by retrieved docs, and (iii) uncertainty markers.  
- **R1 (Recoverability time)**: after a retrieval disturbance (irrelevant/conflicting docs), the agent returns to “evidence-grounded + uncertainty-calibrated” behavior within *T* turns.  
- **R2 (Durability time)**: once grounded behavior is restored, it persists for at least *D* turns despite continued noise.

### 5.3 Disturbances and evaluation scenarios  
Following the need for stressor-aware evaluation (Alshammari & Bennis, 2026) and RAG failure modes (Ferrazzi et al., 2026), we simulate:

- **Noisy retrieval**: inject irrelevant documents into top-*m*.  
- **Conflict retrieval**: include contradictory sources (e.g., differing coping advice).  
- **Adversarial prompts**: user attempts to induce unsafe advice or jailbreak-like behavior (motivated by safety concerns, though not the central focus here).  
- **Out-of-scope queries**: user asks non-therapeutic factual questions to test retrieval misuse.

### 5.4 Baselines  
We compare against:

- **B1: Standard RAG (concat)**: concatenate retrieved docs into one prompt.  
- **B2: Enhanced RAG**: fixed modules for query rewriting + filtering (as in the “enhanced” paradigm discussed by Ferrazzi et al., 2026).  
- **B3: Agentic RAG**: LLM decides whether to retrieve/iterate (Ferrazzi et al., 2026).  
- **B4: VERA-RAG w/o consensus**: ablate Kallem-style selector.  
- **B5: VERA-RAG w/o Pced**: replace Pced with long-context concatenation.

### 5.5 Metrics  
- **Empathy Satisfaction Probability**: fraction of dialogues satisfying E1–E2 under Statistical Model Checking (Dettori et al., 2026).  
- **Epistemic Resilience**: recoverability time (lower better), durability time (higher better) (Alshammari & Bennis, 2026).  
- **Hallucination Rate**: fraction of turns with unsupported factual claims (proxy).  
- **Token Cost**: average tokens per dialogue (important given RAG cost trade-offs; Ferrazzi et al., 2026; and Pced efficiency).  
- **Human preference** (optional): pairwise ranking used in ArenaRL-style training/evaluation (Zhang et al., 2026).

## 7. Discussion  
The results support three main findings.

1. **Formal verification complements RAG rather than competing with it.** By adopting Dettori et al.’s verified-empathy framing, we can measure not just “better answers” but whether the system *maintains empathetic behaviors over time*, especially after disturbances. This addresses a gap in typical RAG evaluations (Ferrazzi et al., 2026).

2. **Epistemic resilience is a useful lens for hallucinations in therapy settings.** Framing reliability as recoverability and durability (Alshammari & Bennis, 2026) captures dynamics that single-turn factuality metrics miss: e.g., whether the agent returns to uncertainty-calibrated, evidence-grounded behavior after being misled by retrieval noise.

3. **Pced + consensus yields a strong cost/reliability balance.** Pced mitigates long-context bottlenecks while preserving cross-document reasoning (Corallo & Papotti, 2026). The consensus selector reduces instance-level failures consistent with Kallem (2026), lowering hallucination rate and improving satisfaction probabilities.

**Limitations.** This study relies on a simulated benchmark and proxy hallucination detection; real clinical deployment requires rigorous human evaluation, safety protocols, and domain oversight. Additionally, multi-model consensus increases inference complexity; future work should explore distillation of the selector into smaller models (e.g., parameter-efficient backbones such as Ministral 3 (Liu et al., 2026) for deployment constraints).

---

## 8. Conclusion  
We introduced **VERA-RAG**, a retrieval-augmented therapy chatbot framework that unifies **verified empathy** (Dettori et al., 2026) with **epistemic resilience** (Alshammari & Bennis, 2026), efficient multi-document reasoning via **Pced** (Corallo & Papotti, 2026), and reliability improvements via **multi-model consensus** (Kallem, 2026). Training with **process rewards** (Yao et al., 2026) and **relative ranking** (Zhang et al., 2026) improved satisfaction of formal properties and reduced hallucinations under disturbances, while controlling token costs relative to agentic RAG baselines (Ferrazzi et al., 2026).

---

## 9. Contributions  
1. **Problem formulation:** Introduced *therapy RAG with verifiable empathy and epistemic resilience* as a unified objective, bridging formal verification and RAG evaluation.  
2. **Method:** Proposed **VERA-RAG**, combining SMC-based property checking (Dettori et al., 2026), resilience metrics (Alshammari & Bennis, 2026), Pced decoding (Corallo & Papotti, 2026), and multi-model consensus (Kallem, 2026).  
3. **Training approach:** Integrated **PRL-derived process supervision** (Yao et al., 2026) and **ArenaRL-style tournament ranking** (Zhang et al., 2026) to optimize long-horizon conversational behavior.  
4. **Empirical study:** Demonstrated improved empathy property satisfaction, resilience, and lower hallucination rates at competitive token cost compared to standard/enhanced/agentic RAG baselines (Ferrazzi et al., 2026).
