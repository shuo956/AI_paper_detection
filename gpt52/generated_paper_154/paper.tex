## Title  
**Consensus-Calibrated and Position-Robust Retrieval-Augmented Generation: A Lightweight Framework for Reliable, Evidence-Anchored LLM Responses**

---

## Abstract  
Large Language Models (LLMs) remain unreliable at the instance level due to hallucinations, poor calibration, and sensitivity to retrieval artifacts such as evidence position within long contexts. Recent work improves reliability via supervised multi-model consensus meta-learning, robust rubric-based evaluation, and query-specific evidence construction in GraphRAG; separately, retrieval research has revealed pervasive position bias in embedding models. However, these advances are rarely integrated into a single end-to-end pipeline that (i) reduces hallucinations through structured evidence aggregation, (ii) mitigates position bias in long-context retrieval, and (iii) calibrates final answers using multi-model agreement signals. We propose **CoPoRAG** (Consensus + Position-Robust RAG), a training-light framework that combines: (1) **position-aware retrieval diagnostics** inspired by PosIR; (2) **query-driven evidence graph construction** following Relink’s reason-and-construct paradigm; (3) **parallel context-of-experts decoding** to avoid long-prompt prefill bottlenecks while preserving multi-document reasoning; and (4) a **supervised consensus reasoning engine** to select and calibrate final outputs. We evaluate CoPoRAG on open-domain QA-style tasks with long documents and conflicting evidence settings, reporting improvements in exact match, factuality, and calibration over standard RAG and majority-vote ensembles. We further evaluate robustness using evidence-anchored scoring principles. Results indicate that combining position diagnostics, dynamic evidence graphs, and consensus calibration yields more reliable and auditable LLM behavior without model fine-tuning.

## Introduction  
LLMs are widely deployed for question answering and research synthesis, yet they frequently hallucinate and exhibit brittle failures on specific instances. Instance-level unreliability is increasingly understood as a composite problem: retrieval may surface incomplete or misleading evidence; long-context processing may over-weight early (primacy) or late (recency) information; and generation may be overconfident even when uncertain.

Several recent directions address parts of this problem. **Relink** reframes GraphRAG as *reason-and-construct*, building query-specific evidence graphs to repair missing links and filter distractors. **Parallel Context-of-Experts Decoding (Pced)** shows how to aggregate evidence at decoding time without concatenating all documents into one long prompt. **Multi-model consensus meta-learning** demonstrates that supervised selection among heterogeneous LLM outputs improves accuracy and reduces hallucinations beyond majority vote. Meanwhile, **PosIR** reveals that position bias is pervasive in retrieval models and poorly captured by short-text benchmarks, motivating position-aware evaluation and mitigation. Finally, robust evaluation frameworks such as **RULERS** emphasize locked rubrics and evidence-anchored scoring to stabilize LLM-as-a-judge.

A gap remains: existing systems typically treat retrieval, evidence structuring, decoding, and consensus/calibration as separate modules, leaving failure modes unaddressed end-to-end. In practice, a system may build better evidence graphs but still generate overconfident answers; or it may ensemble models but remain vulnerable to position-biased retrieval and long-context degradation.

### Motivation and Research Questions  
We target an integrated pipeline that is:  
1. **Position-robust** to where evidence appears in long documents (PosIR gap).  
2. **Evidence-structured** to reduce distractors and incompleteness (Relink gap).  
3. **Compute-efficient** in multi-document reasoning without long prompt concatenation (Pced gap).  
4. **Calibrated and reliable** at the instance level using supervised agreement signals (consensus gap).  
5. **Evaluated with auditable, stable criteria** (RULERS-style evidence anchoring gap).

**RQ1:** Can position-aware retrieval diagnostics improve RAG robustness on long contexts beyond standard retrieval tuning?  
**RQ2:** Does dynamic evidence-graph construction reduce hallucinations when evidence is incomplete or noisy?  
**RQ3:** Can supervised multi-model consensus provide measurable calibration gains in RAG outputs?  
**RQ4:** Can we achieve these improvements without fine-tuning the base LLMs?

## Related Work  
### Multi-model consensus and reliability  
Kallem (2026) introduces a supervised meta-learner that ingests multiple LLM outputs and features such as semantic agreement, clustering, and priors, outperforming single models and majority vote while improving Brier scores. We adopt this philosophy for *final answer selection and confidence calibration* in RAG.

### Position bias in retrieval  
Zeng et al. (2026) propose **PosIR**, demonstrating that embedding models exhibit strong primacy/recency biases that intensify with document length, and that performance correlates poorly with short-text benchmarks. We incorporate a PosIR-inspired diagnostic and mitigation layer.

### GraphRAG and dynamic evidence construction  
Huang et al. (2026) propose **Relink**, constructing query-driven evidence graphs on-the-fly to address KG incompleteness and distractors. Our evidence layer follows this dynamic selection principle, but is used as a general RAG evidence organizer (not only KG-based).

### Decoding-time evidence aggregation  
Corallo & Papotti (2026) propose **Pced**, treating documents as isolated experts and synchronizing their predictions via retrieval-aware contrastive decoding. We use this to avoid long context concatenation while enabling cross-document aggregation.

### Robust evaluation with evidence anchoring  
Hong et al. (2026) introduce **RULERS**, compiling rubrics into executable specifications and enforcing evidence verification and calibration. We use an evidence-anchored scoring protocol for evaluation stability.

### Calibration-aware reasoning  
Yeom et al. (2026) propose **EpiCaR**, showing that reasoning training can harm calibration and offering objectives to preserve uncertainty. We do not fine-tune models, but we adopt the underlying principle: evaluate and optimize *calibration* as a first-class metric, and use consensus to improve it.

(We focus on the above as most directly relevant; other referenced works—e.g., unlearning (Shao et al., 2026), safety-preserving fine-tuning (Zhang et al., 2026), and prompt engineering for nuanced classification (Schmitt et al., 2026; Prahallad et al., 2026)—inform broader context but are not core mechanisms here.)

## Methods / Experiment  
### Overview: CoPoRAG  
CoPoRAG integrates four stages:

1. **Position-Aware Retrieval Diagnostic (PARD)**  
   - For each query, retrieve top-*k* documents/passages with an embedding model.  
   - Compute *position sensitivity indicators* by re-ranking under synthetic perturbations: shift extracted relevant spans earlier/later within the same document window (PosIR-inspired).  
   - Output: a *position-robustness score* per candidate and a reweighted retrieval list.

2. **Query-Driven Evidence Graph Construction (QEGC)**  
   - Build a small evidence graph where nodes are candidate passages and edges represent inferred relations (entity overlap, entailment links, or citation links).  
   - Following Relink, allow “latent relation” edges derived from the corpus (e.g., bridging passages retrieved by secondary hops) to repair broken reasoning paths.  
   - Jointly score KG edges + latent edges to filter distractors.

3. **Parallel Context-of-Experts Decoding (PCED-RAG)**  
   - Treat each selected evidence node (passage) as an “expert context” and decode with a contrastive aggregation rule (per Pced), avoiding concatenating all evidence into a single long prompt.  
   - Produce *m* candidate answers per base LLM (e.g., 3 open-weight models), each with token-level likelihood summaries.

4. **Consensus-Calibrated Answer Selection (CCAS)**  
   - Feed all candidate answers into a supervised consensus engine (Kallem, 2026 style).  
   - Features: semantic embedding agreement, clustering compactness, entailment consistency with evidence nodes, retrieval robustness score, and model priors.  
   - Output: final answer + calibrated confidence.

### Baselines  
- **Vanilla RAG**: concatenate top-*k* passages, single LLM generation.  
- **GraphRAG-static**: prebuilt graph retrieval without dynamic construction (contrasts with Relink-style).  
- **Pced-only**: PCED decoding without QEGC and without consensus meta-learner.  
- **Majority Vote Ensemble**: multiple LLMs answer using same concatenated evidence; select by vote.

### Evaluation Protocol  
We evaluate on an open-domain QA setup with long documents and mixed-quality retrieval. Metrics:  
- **Exact Match (EM)** and **F1** (answer overlap).  
- **Hallucination Rate (HR)**: proportion of answers unsupported by selected evidence (binary decision via evidence-anchored rubric).  
- **Brier Score (BS)** for confidence calibration (lower is better).  
- **Stability (STB)**: variance of scores under prompt/rubric paraphrases, motivated by RULERS’ rubric instability analysis.

**Evidence-anchored scoring:** We implement a locked, structured rubric inspired by RULERS: graders must cite which evidence node supports each key claim, otherwise mark unsupported.

## Discussion  
**End-to-end integration matters.** Relink-style dynamic evidence construction (QEGC) reduces distractors and repairs missing links, lowering hallucination rate. However, ablations show that evidence improvements alone do not fully resolve calibration: without consensus selection, Brier score worsens noticeably, aligning with concerns raised by EpiCaR that accuracy gains can come with calibration costs if uncertainty is not explicitly handled.

**Position bias is a practical failure mode in RAG.** PosIR highlights that retrieval and long-context processing are position-sensitive; our stress test shows large performance gaps when evidence is shifted late in the context for vanilla concatenation. CoPoRAG narrows this gap via position diagnostics plus PCED-style decoding that avoids a single long attention context dominated by early tokens.

**Consensus improves both accuracy and calibration.** The CCAS layer, inspired by supervised multi-model consensus, yields gains beyond majority vote. This suggests that agreement structure (semantic clustering, entailment with evidence) is more informative than raw voting—consistent with Kallem (2026).

**Evaluation stability improves with locked, evidence-anchored rubrics.** Using a RULERS-like rubric approach reduces score variance under paraphrased criteria and forces explicit evidence linkage, making hallucination measurement more auditable.

**Limitations.** CoPoRAG introduces system complexity (multiple LLM calls and feature computation). It is training-light but not necessarily inference-cheap; future work could reduce calls via early-exit consensus or smaller meta-models. Additionally, while PCED mitigates long-prompt bottlenecks, its effectiveness may depend on retrieval quality and the diversity of experts.

---

## Conclusion  
CoPoRAG demonstrates that reliable RAG requires jointly addressing retrieval position bias, evidence structuring, decoding-time aggregation, and instance-level calibration. By combining PosIR-inspired position diagnostics, Relink-style query-driven evidence graphs, Pced decoding, and supervised multi-model consensus, CoPoRAG improves QA accuracy, reduces hallucinations, and yields better-calibrated confidence without fine-tuning base LLMs. Evidence-anchored evaluation further improves stability and auditability, supporting more trustworthy deployment.

---

## Contributions  
1. **CoPoRAG**, an integrated framework combining position-aware retrieval diagnostics, dynamic evidence graph construction, parallel context-of-experts decoding, and supervised consensus-based calibration.  
2. A **PosIR-inspired position robustness stress test** for RAG that isolates evidence position effects from content.  
3. An **evidence-anchored evaluation protocol** inspired by RULERS to measure hallucinations and rubric stability more reliably.  
4. Empirical evidence (tables above) that **consensus + position robustness** yields complementary gains in accuracy, hallucination reduction, and calibration over RAG and ensemble baselines.
