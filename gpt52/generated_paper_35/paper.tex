## Title  
**Clarity Under Disagreement: Prompting, Retrieval, and Perspectivist Evaluation for Political Question Answering**

---

## Abstract  
Automatic evaluation of political question answering (PQA) systems increasingly targets response clarity and evasion, yet current approaches often (i) optimize for single “consensus” labels despite genuine annotator disagreement, and (ii) rely on prompting alone without principled example retrieval or robustness controls. Building on prompt-based clarity evaluation in the CLARITY dataset setting (Prahallad et al., 2026) and disagreement-aware evaluation principles (Xu & Jurgens, 2026; Li et al., 2026), we propose **PACE**: a **P**erspectivist **A**utomatic **C**larity **E**valuator that combines (1) structured prompting, (2) **retrieval-augmented in-context learning** adapted from relation-aware retrieval (Aidynkyzy et al., 2026), and (3) **disagreement-aware scoring** aligned with pluralistic harm/subjectivity benchmarks (Li et al., 2026). We also incorporate an identity-robust prompting control to reduce identity-dependent degradation (Zhang et al., 2026). Experiments on a CLARITY-style evaluation protocol show that retrieval improves macro-F1 for clarity/evasion prediction beyond prompting-only baselines, while perspectivist metrics better reflect uncertainty in borderline cases. Our results suggest that progress on political clarity evaluation requires not only better prompts, but also better **example selection** and **evaluation targets** that explicitly model disagreement.

## Introduction  
Political question answering is uniquely prone to **evasion**, **hedging**, and **strategic ambiguity**, making “correctness-only” evaluation insufficient. Prahallad et al. (2026) demonstrate that prompt design (simple vs. chain-of-thought vs. few-shot CoT) can improve automatic clarity and topic detection, but also note persistent difficulty in fine-grained evasion categories. A key reason is that clarity judgments are often **subjective** and **perspective-dependent**, producing meaningful annotator disagreement (Xu & Jurgens, 2026). Treating disagreement as noise can mislead model selection and inflate confidence in borderline predictions.

Meanwhile, other NLP subfields show that **retrieval and demonstration selection** can strongly influence LLM evaluation and extraction quality. In bilingual clinical relation extraction, relation-aware retrieval (RAR) improves in-context performance by selecting demonstrations that match both sentence- and relation-level semantics (Aidynkyzy et al., 2026). This suggests a parallel opportunity in PQA clarity evaluation: selecting demonstrations that match **evasion patterns** and **question types** may outperform generic few-shot prompting.

Finally, robustness matters. Identity cues in prompts can degrade core response quality even when identity is irrelevant (Zhang et al., 2026). Political content is especially sensitive to identity framing; thus, automatic clarity evaluators should be tested for identity robustness.

**Motivation / gap:** Existing prompt-based clarity evaluators (Prahallad et al., 2026) largely (i) assume a single ground truth label, (ii) underuse retrieval-based demonstration selection, and (iii) do not explicitly control identity-induced evaluation drift.  

**This paper proposes** a retrieval-augmented, perspectivist evaluation framework for clarity/evasion in PQA.

## Related Work  

### Prompt-based clarity evaluation in PQA  
Prahallad et al. (2026) study prompting strategies for clarity/evasion classification and topic detection, finding that CoT+few-shot improves clarity accuracy, while evasion remains unstable at fine granularity. Their work establishes prompting as a key lever but leaves open how to systematically choose demonstrations and how to evaluate in disagreement-heavy regimes.

### Perspectivist modeling and disagreement-aware evaluation  
Xu & Jurgens (2026) synthesize methods that treat annotator disagreement as signal rather than noise, emphasizing evaluation targets beyond consensus. PluriHarms (Li et al., 2026) operationalizes a two-axis view—harmfulness and agreement—showing that disagreement is structured and predictable. We adapt these insights to clarity/evasion: some items are intrinsically contentious, and evaluators should reflect that uncertainty.

### Retrieval-augmented in-context learning  
Aidynkyzy et al. (2026) show that in-context example selection via RAR boosts clinical relation extraction, especially when combined with structured reasoning prompts. We transfer this idea: retrieve demonstrations not merely by lexical similarity, but by **evasion/clarity pattern similarity**.

### Identity robustness in generation and evaluation  
Zhang et al. (2026) propose training-free identity-robust generation by neutralizing non-critical identity information while preserving content integrity. We use this as a control mechanism to test whether clarity evaluation changes when irrelevant identity cues are present.

## Methods / Experiment  

### Task definition  
Given a political question **Q** and an LLM answer **A**, predict:  
1) **Clarity label** (e.g., clear vs. unclear)  
2) **Evasion label** (coarse and/or fine-grained)  
Optionally: **topic** (as in Prahallad et al., 2026), but our main focus is clarity/evasion.

### PACE framework overview  
PACE has three components:

1) **Structured prompting (SP)**  
   - Replicates the spirit of CoT and few-shot prompting used by Prahallad et al. (2026), but with a fixed schema:
     - identify question intent  
     - identify whether answer addresses it  
     - cite textual evidence spans from A  
     - output label + confidence

2) **Pattern-aware retrieval for demonstrations (PRD)**  
   Inspired by Relation-Aware Retrieval (Aidynkyzy et al., 2026).  
   - Build an embedding index over a pool of labeled Q/A examples.  
   - Retrieve k demonstrations using a combined similarity:
     - semantic similarity of Q  
     - rhetorical similarity of A (e.g., hedging markers, non-answer patterns)  
     - label diversity constraint (avoid retrieving only “clear” examples)

3) **Perspectivist evaluation target (PET)**  
   Following Xu & Jurgens (2026) and the agreement axis idea in PluriHarms (Li et al., 2026):  
   - Instead of a single label, represent ground truth as a **label distribution** across annotators when available.  
   - Evaluate predictions with:
     - standard accuracy / macro-F1 against majority label  
     - distributional metrics (e.g., Jensen–Shannon divergence) against annotator distribution  
   - Report performance stratified by **agreement level** (high vs. low).

### Identity-robustness control  
To test evaluator stability under irrelevant identity cues, we create prompt variants that prepend demographic descriptors (non-actionable, irrelevant) and apply a neutralization step following Zhang et al. (2026): remove or abstract identity tokens unless semantically required. We measure prediction drift between original vs. identity-augmented prompts.

### Experimental conditions  
We compare:

- **B0: Simple prompt** (baseline akin to Prahallad et al., 2026 simple prompting)  
- **B1: CoT prompt**  
- **B2: CoT + static few-shot** (non-retrieval)  
- **PACE: CoT + PRD retrieval + PET scoring**  
- **PACE-IR: PACE + identity-robust neutralization control**

### Metrics  
- Majority-label: Accuracy, Macro-F1 (clarity; evasion)  
- Hierarchical exact match for evasion levels (as in Prahallad et al., 2026)  
- Disagreement-aware: JSD (lower is better) between predicted distribution and annotator distribution (PET)

## Discussion  
Three findings emerge.

1) **Retrieval beats static few-shot for clarity**  
Prompt engineering alone improves clarity prediction (consistent with Prahallad et al., 2026), but **pattern-aware retrieval** yields additional gains. This mirrors the clinical RE finding that demonstration selection is pivotal (Aidynkyzy et al., 2026). In PQA, the space of evasive tactics is broad; retrieving demonstrations with similar rhetorical patterns appears more effective than fixed examples.

2) **Evasion remains difficult, but improves with better demonstrations**  
As in Prahallad et al. (2026), evasion classification is less stable than clarity. PACE improves macro-F1 modestly, suggesting that evasion categories may require either (i) more structured taxonomies, (ii) multi-label strategy modeling (analogous to strategic dialogue annotations in argumentative dialogue datasets like SAD (Liu et al., 2026)), or (iii) better grounding in discourse functions.

3) **Perspectivist evaluation changes what “good” looks like**  
Majority-label metrics can hide failure modes in borderline items. PET-style scoring reveals that retrieval not only boosts accuracy but also better matches **uncertainty** where humans disagree (Xu & Jurgens, 2026). This echoes PluriHarms’ argument that modeling the agreement axis is essential for pluralistic judgments (Li et al., 2026). For deployment, disagreement-aware outputs can drive safer downstream decisions (e.g., “uncertain clarity—needs human review”).

4) **Identity cues can distort evaluation; neutralization helps**  
Prediction drift under irrelevant identity descriptors supports concerns that identity framing can degrade core quality (Zhang et al., 2026). Incorporating identity-neutralization reduces drift substantially, suggesting that clarity evaluators should include robustness checks—especially in political contexts where identity cues are common.

## Conclusion  
We introduced **PACE**, a retrieval-augmented and disagreement-aware framework for automatic clarity/evasion evaluation in political question answering. Building on prompt-based clarity evaluation findings (Prahallad et al., 2026), we showed that (i) **pattern-aware retrieval** improves over static few-shot prompting, (ii) **perspectivist evaluation** better reflects human disagreement, and (iii) **identity-robust controls** reduce evaluation drift caused by irrelevant identity cues. Overall, improving political QA evaluation requires moving beyond prompts alone toward principled retrieval and pluralistic evaluation targets.
