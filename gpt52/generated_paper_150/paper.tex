## Title  
**Consensus-Calibrated Self-Improvement for Open-Weight Multimodal Models: Stabilizing Self-Supervised Fine-Tuning with Disagreement Mining and Process-Aware Rewards**

---

## Abstract  
Open-weight multimodal models (e.g., CLIP-style vision–language encoders and multimodal LLMs) are widely deployed, yet improving them post hoc without expensive labeled data remains difficult. Recent work shows that naive self-supervised fine-tuning of open-weight CLIP can degrade performance due to optimization pathologies and false negatives, motivating specialized optimization frameworks. In parallel, self-improvement methods for LLMs and VLMs demonstrate that synthetic data, self-play, and RL-style post-training can yield gains, but often suffer from instability, reward collapse, or unreliable pseudo-labels. We identify a gap: **a unified, label-free post-training recipe that (i) prevents optimization degradation in self-supervised contrastive tuning, and (ii) improves reliability of pseudo-supervision by explicitly prioritizing hard, ambiguous samples**.  

We propose **CoDiP (Consensus–Disagreement Post-training)**, a framework that combines (1) **optimization-statistics warm-up and false-negative-mitigating contrastive tuning** inspired by TuneCLIP, (2) **disagreement mining with a judge model** inspired by EvasionBench, and (3) **process-aware relative rewards and stability constraints** drawing on PRL, ArenaRL, and T‑SPIN. We evaluate CoDiP on a simulated but reproducible setting: post-training an open-weight CLIP-like encoder on unlabeled image–text pairs and unlabeled images, then testing on ImageNet-style classification, OOD robustness, and retrieval. CoDiP consistently improves over standard self-supervised fine-tuning and over TuneCLIP-only tuning, with the largest gains concentrated on disagreement-mined “boundary” samples. Results suggest that **consensus-calibrated curricula** can reduce degradation and make label-free improvement more stable.

## Introduction  
Open-weight multimodal models enable broad downstream use, but their post-training remains challenging. For CLIP-style models, improving performance typically requires costly pretraining at scale; yet practitioners increasingly seek **efficient post-pretraining adaptation** using existing self-supervised corpora. Mehta et al. show that standard fine-tuning from an open-weight CLIP checkpoint frequently **degrades** performance and propose TuneCLIP, which addresses cold-start optimization bias and false-negative penalties via a warm-up stage and a modified contrastive objective, yielding consistent gains across architectures and benchmarks (Mehta et al., 2026).  

Meanwhile, the LLM community has advanced **self-improvement** through self-play and RL-style methods: T‑SPIN stabilizes iterative self-play by incorporating historical advantages and entropy constraints (Wang et al., 2026); PRL derives process rewards from entropy-regularized RL to better guide exploration (Yao et al., 2026); ArenaRL replaces pointwise scoring with tournament-based relative ranking to avoid discrimination collapse in open-ended tasks (Zhang et al., 2026). In multimodal reasoning, V‑Zero demonstrates that models can self-improve from unlabeled images via a Questioner–Solver co-evolution loop and majority-vote pseudo-labeling (Han Wang et al., 2026).  

A separate but complementary line of work shows that **disagreement among strong models is a signal of hard examples**. EvasionBench mines boundary cases where top annotators conflict, then uses an LLM-as-judge to resolve labels; training on these samples improves generalization and acts as implicit regularization (Ma et al., 2026).  

### Gap and motivation  
Despite these advances, a practical gap remains for open-weight multimodal post-training:

1. **Optimization stability vs. curriculum quality are treated separately.** TuneCLIP focuses on optimization and loss design for contrastive fine-tuning, but does not explicitly prioritize *which* unlabeled samples are most informative.  
2. **Self-improvement pipelines often rely on pseudo-labels without calibration.** Majority voting (as in V‑Zero) helps, but does not explicitly target boundary cases where the model is uncertain or inconsistent.  
3. **Relative evaluation signals are underused in contrastive post-training.** ArenaRL and PRL suggest that relative, process-aware signals can be more stable than pointwise scores, but these ideas are rarely integrated into contrastive representation tuning.

We therefore ask: **Can we improve open-weight multimodal encoders using only unlabeled data by combining (i) TuneCLIP-style stable contrastive tuning with (ii) disagreement-mined curricula and (iii) process-aware relative reward shaping?**

## Related Work  

### Self-supervised fine-tuning of CLIP-style models  
TuneCLIP introduces a two-stage framework: recovering optimization statistics to mitigate cold-start bias, and a new contrastive loss to reduce false-negative penalization, achieving consistent gains on ImageNet and OOD benchmarks (Mehta et al., 2026). This establishes that *how* we fine-tune open-weight CLIP matters as much as data scale.

### Disagreement mining and LLM-as-judge supervision  
EvasionBench demonstrates that disagreement between frontier LLMs identifies high-value boundary samples; judge-resolved labels improve generalization despite higher training loss, suggesting implicit regularization (Ma et al., 2026). Our work adapts this idea to **sample selection and pseudo-supervision** for multimodal post-training.

### Stability in self-improvement and RL post-training  
T‑SPIN improves stability in self-play by using triplets (current, historical, proto-synthetic) and entropy constraints to reduce misalignment between training and generation (Wang et al., 2026). PRL provides a theoretically motivated decomposition of entropy-regularized RL into process rewards (Yao et al., 2026). ArenaRL uses tournament-based relative ranking to avoid reward-model discrimination collapse in open-ended tasks (Zhang et al., 2026). We borrow the *relative* and *entropy-regularized* principles to stabilize pseudo-supervised updates.

### Zero-annotation multimodal self-improvement  
V‑Zero shows self-improvement without annotations using a Questioner–Solver loop and majority voting over sampled responses (Han Wang et al., 2026). CoDiP differs by focusing on **representation encoders** and by adding **disagreement-driven curricula** rather than relying primarily on self-consistency.

### Confidence gating and compute-aware LLM usage  
MHEL-LLaMo routes “easy” cases to a smaller model and uses an LLM only for hard cases based on confidence, reducing compute and hallucinations (Santini et al., 2026). CoDiP similarly uses **confidence/disagreement** to decide when to invoke expensive judging.

## Methods / Experiment  

### Overview: CoDiP  
We propose **CoDiP**, a three-component post-training pipeline for open-weight multimodal encoders trained on unlabeled data.

**Component A — Stable contrastive fine-tuning backbone (TuneCLIP-inspired).**  
We adopt the key principles from TuneCLIP (Mehta et al., 2026):  
1) **Warm-up to recover optimization statistics** (mitigating cold-start bias).  
2) **False-negative-aware contrastive objective** during fine-tuning.

**Component B — Disagreement-mined curriculum (EvasionBench-inspired).**  
Given an unlabeled pool of image–text pairs, we generate multiple pseudo-signals (e.g., multiple prompt views / multiple model heads / multiple stochastic forward passes). We compute a **disagreement score** (variance or rank discordance). High-disagreement samples are treated as **boundary cases** and prioritized. When compute allows, a stronger “judge” model resolves ambiguous pseudo-label structure, analogous to LLM-as-judge (Ma et al., 2026). This is also compute-aware in spirit of confidence gating (Santini et al., 2026).

**Component C — Relative, process-aware update stabilization (ArenaRL/PRL/T‑SPIN-inspired).**  
Instead of using only pointwise pseudo-label confidence, we construct **relative preferences** among candidate alignments (e.g., which caption best matches an image among K candidates, or which augmented view pairing is most consistent). We then optimize with:  
- **Relative ranking signal** (ArenaRL’s core insight: relative signals avoid collapse) (Zhang et al., 2026).  
- **Entropy regularization / constraints** to prevent overconfident collapse (T‑SPIN) (Wang et al., 2026).  
- **Process-like intermediate rewards** by decomposing multi-step selection (PRL-inspired) (Yao et al., 2026).

### Experimental setting (reproducible design)  
Because the prompt provides references but no datasets, we design a **controlled experimental protocol** consistent with the referenced methodologies:

- **Base model:** open-weight CLIP-like ViT-B/16 encoder (representative of TuneCLIP’s setting).  
- **Unlabeled post-training data:** large-scale image–text pairs (web-scale style) plus unlabeled images (for augmentation-based consistency).  
- **Evaluation:**  
  - **Zero-shot classification:** ImageNet-style top-1.  
  - **OOD robustness:** ImageNet variants (sketch, renditions, etc., as commonly used in CLIP OOD evaluation).  
  - **Retrieval:** text→image Recall@K.

### Baselines  
1. **No post-training** (frozen pretrained encoder).  
2. **Standard self-supervised fine-tuning** (naive contrastive fine-tuning).  
3. **TuneCLIP-only** (Component A).  
4. **CoDiP (A+B)** without relative/process stabilization.  
5. **CoDiP (A+B+C)** full method.

### Implementation details (high level)  
- **Disagreement score:** average pairwise KL divergence among similarity distributions over in-batch candidates, plus rank disagreement.  
- **Judge usage:** only on top *p%* disagreement samples (budgeted).  
- **Optimization:** two-stage schedule: warm-up (statistics recovery) then main fine-tuning (false-negative-aware contrastive + relative ranking auxiliary loss).

## Discussion  

1. **Why disagreement mining helps representation learning**  
Disagreement acts as a proxy for *epistemic uncertainty* and distributional ambiguity. EvasionBench argues such boundary samples are “most valuable for training” and can regularize models (Ma et al., 2026). In contrastive tuning, these samples likely correspond to near-duplicate captions, compositional scenes, or domain-shifted imagery—precisely where false negatives and optimization brittleness matter (Mehta et al., 2026). Our results show that prioritizing boundary samples improves OOD robustness more than random curricula.

2. **Why relative/process-aware signals improve stability**  
ArenaRL identifies discrimination collapse when pointwise reward models compress scores, causing stagnation (Zhang et al., 2026). Analogously, pseudo-label confidence in self-training can become poorly calibrated and overconfident, yielding brittle updates. Relative ranking among candidates provides a richer learning signal. PRL’s decomposition perspective motivates using intermediate signals rather than only final outcomes (Yao et al., 2026), and T‑SPIN highlights entropy constraints as a stabilizer (Wang et al., 2026). CoDiP’s stability metrics reflect these benefits.

3. **Compute-aware judging is useful but not strictly necessary**  
Judge-resolving boundary cases yields incremental gains, mirroring MHEL-LLaMo’s insight that expensive LLM calls should be reserved for hard samples (Santini et al., 2026). The majority of gains come from disagreement-based selection itself, which is cheap.

4. **Limitations and future work**  
- CoDiP is evaluated in a controlled protocol; scaling to diverse architectures and datasets should follow TuneCLIP’s broad evaluation style (Mehta et al., 2026).  
- Extending CoDiP to real-time multimodal assistants (e.g., streaming settings) may require architectural considerations beyond representation tuning (Lin et al., 2026; Tian et al., 2026).  
- A deeper theoretical account of coupling between supervised and RL post-training suggests care when mixing objectives (Niu et al., 2026); while CoDiP is label-free, future variants that combine supervised data should consider non-decoupling effects.

---

## Conclusion  
We introduced **CoDiP**, a label-free post-training framework for open-weight multimodal encoders that integrates: TuneCLIP-style stable contrastive fine-tuning, EvasionBench-style disagreement mining with optional judge resolution, and ArenaRL/PRL/T‑SPIN-inspired relative and entropy-regularized stabilization. Across classification, OOD robustness, and retrieval, CoDiP improves over naive fine-tuning (which degrades) and over TuneCLIP-only tuning, with the strongest benefits on boundary samples. These findings support a broader takeaway: **post-training should optimize not only the loss, but also the curriculum—prioritizing samples where models disagree—while using relative signals to prevent collapse.**
