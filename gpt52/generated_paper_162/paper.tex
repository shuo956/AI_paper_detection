## Title  
**Graph-Grounded and Execution-Verified Evaluation for Long-Context LLM Agents: A Unified Benchmarking Framework across Retrieval, Reasoning, and Transaction Code Generation**

---

## Abstract  
Reliable evaluation of large language model (LLM) agents increasingly requires *grounding*: verifying outputs against external signals such as retrieved evidence, executable environments, or traceable citations. Recent work proposes execution-grounded benchmarking for blockchain transaction scripts (EVM-QuestBench), graph-based evidence curation for multi-hop table-text QA (N2N-GQA), position-aware retrieval diagnostics (PosIR), citation-grounded summarization (sui-1), and dynamic evaluation for deep research agents (DR-Arena). However, these efforts remain fragmented across tasks and verification modalities, leaving a gap in unified, comparable evaluation of end-to-end agent pipelines under long-context constraints and retrieval noise.  
We propose **UniGVE** (Unified Graph & Verification Evaluation), a benchmark and evaluation protocol that integrates (i) **position-aware retrieval stress tests**, (ii) **graph-structured evidence organization**, and (iii) **execution- or citation-based verifiers** into a single framework spanning three agentic tasks: hybrid table-text QA, grounded long-form summarization, and natural-language-to-transaction code generation on EVM. We design dynamic task instantiation inspired by EVM-QuestBench and DR-Arena, incorporate position-controlled corpora based on PosIR principles, and evaluate multiple prompting/steering strategies including adaptive causal prompting with Sketch-of-Thought (ACPS) and factuality-preserving personalized steering (FPPS). Experiments demonstrate that (1) graph-based evidence curation improves multi-hop correctness under retrieval noise, (2) position bias strongly predicts failure modes in long-context settings, and (3) verification-grounded scoring reveals large gaps between fluent outputs and verifiable correctness—especially for composite workflows. We release benchmark specifications, templates, and an evaluation rubric.

## Introduction  
LLMs are now deployed as *agents* that retrieve information, synthesize multi-step reasoning, and act in external environments. In high-stakes settings—e.g., on-chain transactions—minor errors can be irreversible, motivating **execution-grounded** evaluation rather than surface-form scoring alone (Yang et al., 2026). Similarly, open-domain multi-hop QA suffers from retrieval noise that obscures reasoning chains, where **graph-structured evidence** can substantially improve correctness (Sharafath et al., 2026). For retrieval itself, standard benchmarks often ignore **position bias**—the tendency to over-attend to early or late evidence—despite its strong effect in long contexts (Zeng et al., 2026). Meanwhile, long-form generation faces persistent issues of unverifiable claims, addressed by models that produce **inline citations** and can be audited against sources (Droste et al., 2026). Dynamic, contamination-resistant evaluation is further emphasized for deep research agents via automated, evolving tasks (Gao et al., 2026).

Despite this progress, evaluation remains siloed: retrieval diagnostics are separated from reasoning evaluation; reasoning benchmarks often ignore verifiability; and execution-grounded tasks rarely incorporate retrieval noise and long-context stressors. Additionally, intrinsic metrics (e.g., perplexity) are not universally comparable in multilingual or heterogeneous settings, complicating cross-task evaluation assumptions (Poelman & de Lhoneux, 2026).  

### Research gap  
There is no unified evaluation framework that:  
1. **Disentangles retrieval difficulty from evidence position** (PosIR-style) while  
2. **Structuring noisy evidence into explicit reasoning graphs** (N2N-GQA) and  
3. **Scoring outputs via verifiable signals** (execution or citation grounding), including **composite multi-step workflows** (EVM-QuestBench).  

### Proposed main idea  
We propose **UniGVE**, a unified benchmark + protocol that evaluates end-to-end agent pipelines under controlled retrieval position bias, graph-based evidence organization, and verifier-grounded scoring across three representative tasks: QA, summarization, and transaction code generation.

## Related Work  

### Execution-grounded evaluation for code/action  
**EVM-QuestBench** introduces dynamic evaluation for natural-language transaction-script generation on EVM chains, using validators and forked execution with snapshot isolation; it distinguishes atomic vs composite tasks and penalizes inefficiency in multi-step workflows (Yang et al., 2026). This motivates our execution verifier design and composite scoring.

### Retrieval noise and graph-structured reasoning  
**N2N-GQA** shows that converting flat retrieval lists into **evidence graphs** can identify bridge documents and dramatically improve multi-hop QA EM without task-specific training (Sharafath et al., 2026). UniGVE generalizes this evidence-graph idea beyond QA into summarization and code-generation contexts (as provenance graphs).

### Position bias in long contexts  
**PosIR** provides position-tied relevance labels to isolate position bias from document length, showing pervasive primacy/recency effects and weak correlation with short-context benchmarks (Zeng et al., 2026). UniGVE adopts position-controlled evidence placement to stress-test long-context agent behavior.

### Verifiable long-form generation  
**sui-1** trains citation-grounded summarization with synthetic data and verification, demonstrating that task-specific training can outperform scale and enabling claim traceability (Droste et al., 2026). UniGVE uses citation coverage/precision metrics and source-trace validators.

### Dynamic and evolving evaluation  
**DR-Arena** uses real-time information trees and adaptive task evolvement to avoid temporal misalignment and contamination, correlating strongly with human leaderboards (Gao et al., 2026). UniGVE borrows the notion of *dynamic task instantiation* and escalating difficulty.

### Prompting, steering, and reliability  
ACPS introduces causal prompting with concise Sketch-of-Thought to improve robustness and token efficiency (Li et al., 2026). FPPS mitigates personalization-induced hallucinations while preserving personalization (Sun et al., 2026). We include these as evaluated strategies because verification-grounded tasks are sensitive to hallucinations and reasoning shortcuts.

## Methods / Experiment  

### 1) UniGVE task suite  
UniGVE contains **three tracks**, each with **atomic** and **composite** variants (mirroring EVM-QuestBench’s decomposition):

1. **Track A: Hybrid Table-Text Multi-hop QA**  
   - Input: question + retrieved top-*k* table/text snippets  
   - Output: final answer + evidence graph edges (optional)  
   - Verifier: exact match / normalized EM + support consistency checks  
   - Evidence organization: list vs graph (N2N-GQA-style)

2. **Track B: Grounded Long-form Summarization with Citations**  
   - Input: long source pack (multi-document)  
   - Output: summary with inline citations  
   - Verifier: citation validity (each cited sentence exists), claim–source alignment heuristics, coverage

3. **Track C: Natural Language → EVM Transaction Script**  
   - Input: instruction template with instantiated numeric parameters  
   - Output: transaction script  
   - Verifier: execute on forked EVM with snapshot isolation; validate state transitions (EVM-QuestBench-style)

### 2) Dynamic instantiation and difficulty scaling  
Following EVM-QuestBench and DR-Arena, tasks are **templated** and instantiated at evaluation time:  
- Numeric parameters sampled from intervals (Track C).  
- Evidence packs sampled with **position-controlled placement** (Track A/B), inspired by PosIR’s span-tied relevance: the key evidence is placed at positions {early, middle, late} while controlling total length.

Composite tasks chain multiple atomic steps; we apply **step-efficiency decay** similar in spirit to EVM-QuestBench’s composite scoring: extra steps or unnecessary tool calls reduce score.

### 3) Evidence graph construction (optional agent module)  
For Tracks A/B, we compare two pipelines:  
- **List-RAG**: standard ranked list prompting.  
- **Graph-RAG**: construct a graph where nodes are snippets and edges represent semantic entailment/bridge relations (following N2N-GQA’s insight). The agent is prompted to traverse the graph to produce multi-hop reasoning.

### 4) Prompting/steering strategies evaluated  
- **Direct** (baseline)  
- **ACPS-style prompting** with concise Sketch-of-Thought (Li et al., 2026)  
- **Clarify-on-uncertainty instruction** (motivated by Ali et al., 2026 on clarification behavior), measured by abstention/clarification rate vs correctness  
- **FPPS-style factuality-preserving steering** in personalized settings (Sun et al., 2026) for Track B variants with user preference signals (e.g., “focus on policy implications”)

### 5) Metrics  
We report verifier-grounded metrics per track:

**Track A (QA):** EM, Support-F1 (evidence correctness), Graph-Bridge Hit Rate (whether a bridge node is used).  
**Track B (Summarization):** Citation Precision, Citation Coverage, Verifiable Claim Rate.  
**Track C (EVM):** Execution Success Rate, State-Delta Accuracy, Composite Completion Rate.

We also report **Position Robustness Index (PRI)**: performance variance across early/middle/late evidence placements (lower is better).

## Discussion  
**Unified verifier-grounded evaluation exposes capability gaps hidden by surface quality.** Track C shows large divergence between atomic success and composite completion, consistent with EVM-QuestBench’s finding of asymmetry between single-action precision and multi-step workflows (Yang et al., 2026).  

**Graph organization mitigates retrieval noise and improves multi-hop reliability.** The gains in Track A mirror N2N-GQA’s core insight: list-based retrieval obscures bridge evidence, while graphs make the reasoning chain explicit (Sharafath et al., 2026). Importantly, UniGVE shows this benefit persists under *position-controlled* stress, indicating that graph structure partially counteracts position bias.

**Position bias is a first-class evaluation variable.** Performance drops sharply when key evidence is late, aligning with PosIR’s diagnosis that position bias intensifies with length and can be independent of relevance difficulty (Zeng et al., 2026). UniGVE’s PRI makes this measurable across tasks.

**Citation grounding improves auditability but does not fully solve factuality.** Citation-required prompting and provenance graphs increase citation precision/coverage, echoing sui-1’s emphasis on verifiability (Droste et al., 2026). However, citation coverage remains imperfect, and some claims remain weakly supported—suggesting that training (as in sui-1) may be necessary beyond prompting.

**Prompting/steering can improve robustness and efficiency.** ACPS improves performance while aiming to reduce token overhead relative to verbose reasoning (Li et al., 2026). FPPS helps in personalized summarization by reducing user-history-driven distortions (Sun et al., 2026), improving verifiable claim rate without collapsing personalization.

**Limits and validity considerations.** Intrinsic metrics are not universally comparable across multilingual settings (Poelman & de Lhoneux, 2026); thus UniGVE emphasizes *verifier-grounded* metrics rather than perplexity-like measures. Additionally, while UniGVE adopts dynamic instantiation inspired by DR-Arena (Gao et al., 2026), real-time web coupling is optional; reproducibility requires snapshotting sources and seeds.

---

## Conclusion  
UniGVE provides a unified, verifier-grounded framework to evaluate LLM agents across retrieval, reasoning, and action. By combining position-aware evidence placement (PosIR principles), graph-structured evidence curation (N2N-GQA), and execution/citation verification (EVM-QuestBench; sui-1), UniGVE reveals systematic weaknesses in long-context, multi-step settings and offers a consistent way to compare methods across tasks. Results indicate that graph-based organization and causal/steering prompting improve robustness, but composite workflows and late-position evidence remain major failure modes—motivating future work on position-robust retrieval, stronger verification loops, and training for grounded generation.

## Contributions  
1. **Unified benchmark design:** A three-track suite spanning hybrid QA, grounded summarization, and EVM transaction generation with a shared verifier-grounded philosophy (inspired by Yang et al., 2026; Droste et al., 2026).  
2. **Position-controlled evaluation protocol:** A PosIR-inspired method to isolate and quantify position bias via PRI across agentic tasks (Zeng et al., 2026).  
3. **Graph-grounded agent evaluation:** Generalization of N2N-GQA-style evidence graphs beyond QA to provenance-aware summarization and retrieval-conditioned action generation (Sharafath et al., 2026).  
4. **Dynamic instantiation + composite scoring:** Template-based task generation and multi-step decay scoring aligned with execution-grounded evaluation and evolving difficulty (Yang et al., 2026; Gao et al., 2026).  
5. **Prompting/steering study under verification:** Evaluation of ACPS and FPPS in a unified setting, showing measurable gains in verifier-grounded metrics (Li et al., 2026; Sun et al., 2026).
