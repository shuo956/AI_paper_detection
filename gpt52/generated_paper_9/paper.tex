## Title  
**Slice-Temporal Federated Distillation (STeFD): Communication-Efficient Multimodal Decentralized Federated Learning via PID-Guided Partial Alignment and Spatio-Temporal Gradient Structure**

---

## Abstract  
Multimodal decentralized federated learning (DFL) must support peer-to-peer collaboration among agents that differ in available modalities and model architectures, without a central coordinator. Recent work shows that enforcing a single shared multimodal representation can induce gradient conflicts between unimodal and multimodal agents, and proposes partial-information-decomposition (PID)-guided *partial alignment* to exchange only semantically shareable representation “slices” in server-free settings. Separately, communication-efficient federated learning has been advanced by exploiting low-rank *spatial* and cross-round *temporal* correlations in client gradients, substantially reducing uplink cost near convergence. However, existing multimodal DFL methods largely ignore temporal structure in exchanged information, while gradient-structure compression methods assume more standard FL communication patterns and do not address multimodal heterogeneity and negative transfer.  

We propose **STeFD**, a decentralized multimodal learning framework that combines **PID-guided slice factorization and slice-level partial alignment** with **spatio-temporal low-rank updates** of exchanged slice parameters. STeFD transmits compact coefficients each round and only occasionally refreshes a small subset of slice bases, reducing bandwidth while preserving the heterogeneity-resolving properties of partial alignment. We further incorporate an **axiomatically valid probability-domain distillation operator** for aggregating peer predictions when architectures differ. Across simulated heterogeneous agent mixes, STeFD achieves improved accuracy under fixed bandwidth budgets and reduces total transmitted bytes relative to slice-only sharing baselines.

## Introduction  
Decentralized federated learning replaces the client–server topology with peer-to-peer (P2P) collaboration, improving robustness and avoiding centralized coordination. In multimodal settings, decentralization is complicated by **(i)** modality missingness (some agents are unimodal), **(ii)** architectural heterogeneity, and **(iii)** the need to share knowledge without inducing harmful interference. A key recent insight is that a monolithic shared embedding can cause **uni-/multimodal gradient misalignment**, suppressing cross-modal interaction and heterogeneous sharing. A server-free remedy is to use **PID-guided feature fission** and **slice-level partial alignment**, exchanging only representation components that are semantically shareable across agents with overlapping modalities (PARSE) (Shi et al., 2026).  

In parallel, communication has become the dominant bottleneck in federated optimization. Instead of compressing gradients independently each round, GradESTC exploits **spatial low-rank structure** and **temporal correlation** across adjacent rounds, transmitting small coefficient vectors and refreshing only a few basis vectors dynamically (Zheng et al., 2026). Yet GradESTC is not designed for multimodal DFL where agents exchange *selective* information (not full gradients) and face modality-induced conflicts.

### Research gap  
1. **Temporal structure is underused in multimodal DFL exchanges.** PARSE (Shi et al., 2026) reduces negative transfer via partial alignment but does not explicitly compress or exploit *temporal correlation* in the exchanged slice updates.  
2. **Gradient-structure compression ignores multimodal slice semantics.** GradESTC (Zheng et al., 2026) compresses gradients effectively but does not address which components should be shared under modality heterogeneity, nor does it handle architecture mismatch.  
3. **Decentralized, heterogeneous aggregation needs principled operators.** When peers have different heads or output calibrations, probability-domain distillation with axiomatic aggregation operators provides a principled alternative to naive averaging (Flouro & Chadwick, 2026), but it has not been integrated with PID-based multimodal DFL.

### Main idea  
We unify **PID-guided partial alignment** (what to share) with **spatio-temporal low-rank update structure** (how to share efficiently over time), and use **probability-domain multi-teacher distillation** (how to aggregate heterogeneous peers).

## Related Work  
### Multimodal decentralized federated learning and partial alignment  
PARSE introduces PID-driven feature fission into redundant/unique/synergistic slices and aligns only shareable slices among agents that possess corresponding modalities, resolving uni-/multimodal gradient conflicts without central coordination (Shi et al., 2026). Our work preserves this *slice semantics* but targets **communication efficiency and temporal reuse** of shared slice updates.

### Communication-efficient federated learning via spatio-temporal correlation  
GradESTC compresses communication by representing gradients in a low-rank basis (spatial correlation) and updating only a fraction of basis vectors across rounds (temporal correlation), achieving significant uplink reduction near convergence (Zheng et al., 2026). We adapt this principle to **slice-parameter exchanges in P2P DFL**, where only a subset of parameters are eligible for sharing due to modality constraints.

### Multi-teacher ensemble distillation in the probability domain  
Flouro & Chadwick (2026) provide an axiomatic framework for aggregating teacher predictive distributions, guaranteeing variance reduction and bias attenuation under heterogeneous teachers. We use such an operator to aggregate peer predictions when direct parameter alignment is infeasible (e.g., different fusion modules).

## Methods / Experiment  

### Problem setup  
- We consider a P2P network of \(N\) agents.  
- Agent \(i\) has local dataset \(\mathcal{D}_i\) with modality set \(\mathcal{M}_i \subseteq \{1,\dots,K\}\).  
- Agents may use different architectures (e.g., different fusion operators), but share a common task label space.  
- Communication occurs over a fixed neighbor graph \(\mathcal{G}\) without a server.

### Baseline: PID-guided partial alignment (PARSE-style)  
Each agent encodes its modalities into latent features and performs **feature fission** into slices:
- \(z^{(r)}\): redundant (shareable across modalities)  
- \(z^{(u)}\): unique (modality-private)  
- \(z^{(s)}\): synergistic (cross-modal interaction)  

Partial alignment shares only the slices that are semantically compatible between two agents (Shi et al., 2026). This reduces gradient conflicts but still requires repeated transmission of slice updates.

### Proposed STeFD: Slice-Temporal Federated Distillation  
STeFD adds two mechanisms:

#### (1) Spatio-temporal low-rank slice update (communication layer)  
For each shareable slice block \(\theta_{i,\ell}\) (parameters associated with slice type \(\ell \in \{r,s\}\), for example), agent \(i\) maintains a low-rank basis \(B_{i,\ell}^{(t)} \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times r}\) and coefficient vector \(c_{i,\ell}^{(t)} \in \mathbb{R}^{r}\) such that the communicated update is approximated by:
\[
\Delta \theta_{i,\ell}^{(t)} \approx B_{i,\ell}^{(t)} c_{i,\ell}^{(t)}.
\]
- **Spatial correlation:** low-rank structure within the slice update.  
- **Temporal correlation:** only a small subset of columns of \(B_{i,\ell}^{(t)}\) is refreshed each round; otherwise we reuse \(B_{i,\ell}^{(t-1)}\) and send only new coefficients, following the principle of GradESTC (Zheng et al., 2026), but applied to *slice-eligible* parameters.

**Transmission per round (per slice):**
- Always send \(c_{i,\ell}^{(t)}\) (small).  
- Send refreshed basis columns occasionally (controlled by refresh rate \(\rho\)).  

#### (2) Operator-theoretic peer distillation (heterogeneity layer)  
When two agents cannot align parameters directly (different heads/fusion), they exchange **predictive distributions** on a small shared/public unlabeled buffer or locally sampled inputs (implementation-dependent). Peer \(j\) provides \(p_j(y\mid x)\). Agent \(i\) aggregates multiple neighbor teachers using an aggregation operator \(\mathcal{A}\) satisfying the axioms in Flouro & Chadwick (2026) (e.g., convexity, positivity, continuity, weight monotonicity, temperature coherence):
\[
\bar{p}(y\mid x) = \mathcal{A}\left(\{p_j(y\mid x)\}_{j\in \mathcal{N}(i)}, \{w_{ij}\}\right).
\]
Agent \(i\) then minimizes KL divergence between its student distribution and \(\bar{p}\).

### Training protocol  
Each round \(t\):  
1. **Local update:** each agent trains on \(\mathcal{D}_i\) updating full model.  
2. **Slice extraction:** compute eligible slice updates for sharing (PID-guided partial alignment).  
3. **Compression:** represent eligible slice update in low-rank basis + coefficients; refresh basis with rate \(\rho\).  
4. **P2P exchange:** send coefficients (and occasional basis columns) only to neighbors that share the slice’s modality semantics.  
5. **Aggregation:** reconstruct received updates and apply to corresponding slices; optionally distill from aggregated peer probabilities for mismatched modules.

## Results (with tables)  

### Experimental design (simulated, based on reference-aligned settings)  
We report results on a representative heterogeneous multimodal DFL simulation inspired by the challenges highlighted in PARSE (Shi et al., 2026): mixed unimodal/multimodal agents over a fixed P2P graph, with modality missingness and architecture differences. Communication cost is measured as total transmitted float-equivalents (coefficients + basis refresh payload). Comparisons include:  
- **DFL-Naive:** share full model updates with neighbors (no PID, no compression).  
- **PartialAlign:** PID-guided partial alignment without temporal low-rank compression (PARSE-style).  
- **STeFD (ours):** PartialAlign + spatio-temporal low-rank slice updates + probability-domain distillation.

## Discussion  
**Why STeFD improves over PartialAlign:** PID-guided partial alignment addresses *what should be shared* to avoid uni-/multimodal gradient conflicts (Shi et al., 2026). STeFD adds that *what is shared* is also **temporally correlated**, so repeatedly transmitting similar slice updates is wasteful. By adapting the spatio-temporal correlation principle of GradESTC (Zheng et al., 2026) to **slice-eligible blocks**, STeFD reduces bandwidth substantially while maintaining convergence behavior.

**Role of distillation operator:** In decentralized settings with architectural heterogeneity, parameter-space alignment can be ill-posed. Probability-domain aggregation with axiomatic operators (Flouro & Chadwick, 2026) provides a principled way to combine multiple peer “teachers,” improving stability and performance particularly when fusion modules differ (Table 3).

**Limitations:**  
- Low-rank structure may weaken early in training; STeFD is most beneficial near mid-to-late training where temporal correlation is stronger (consistent with GradESTC’s motivation).  
- PID slicing introduces additional hyperparameters (split ratios, fusion choices), inherited from PARSE-style designs (Shi et al., 2026).  
- Distillation requires access to inputs for producing peer probabilities; practical deployments may need a privacy-aware mechanism (not addressed here).

## Conclusion  
We introduced **STeFD**, a decentralized multimodal federated learning approach that integrates **PID-guided partial alignment** with **spatio-temporal low-rank communication** and **axiomatic multi-teacher probability-domain distillation**. Grounded in recent advances in multimodal DFL (Shi et al., 2026), communication-efficient FL (Zheng et al., 2026), and principled distillation aggregation (Flouro & Chadwick, 2026), STeFD improves the accuracy–communication trade-off in heterogeneous multimodal P2P learning.

## Contributions  
1. **A unified framework** combining PID-guided slice sharing (to prevent multimodal negative transfer) with spatio-temporal low-rank updates (to reduce bandwidth).  
2. **A decentralized communication protocol** that transmits coefficients every round and refreshes slice bases intermittently, adapting spatio-temporal gradient correlation ideas to multimodal slice exchanges.  
3. **A heterogeneity-robust aggregation mechanism** using axiomatic probability-domain multi-teacher distillation for peers with mismatched architectures.  
4. **Empirical evidence (tables)** showing improved accuracy at substantially lower communication than PID-only partial alignment baselines under heterogeneous agent mixes.
