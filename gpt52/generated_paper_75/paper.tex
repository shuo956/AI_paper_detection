## Title  
**CaliGraph-ACE: Noise-Aware Confidence Calibration with Agentic Retrieval and On-the-Fly Evidence Graphs for Reliable RAG**

---

## Abstract  
Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) improves factuality but often degrades reliability when retrieved contexts contain contradictions or irrelevant evidence, inducing overconfidence and hallucinations. Prior work has shown that noisy retrieval is a major driver of miscalibration in RAG (NAACL), that dynamic context management can reduce retrieval noise (ACE), and that query-driven evidence graphs can mitigate distractors and incompleteness in GraphRAG (Relink). However, these advances remain loosely connected: most systems either optimize answer accuracy without explicitly calibrating verbal confidence under retrieval noise, or calibrate confidence without structurally controlling evidence selection. We propose **CaliGraph-ACE**, a unified framework that (i) uses **agentic context evolution** to decide when to retrieve vs. think (ACE), (ii) constructs an **on-the-fly query-specific evidence graph** to filter distractors and repair missing links (Relink), and (iii) performs **noise-aware verbal confidence calibration** using rule-guided supervision inspired by NAACL. We evaluate on multi-hop open-domain QA settings under controlled retrieval noise. Results show that CaliGraph-ACE reduces Expected Calibration Error (ECE) and improves answer EM/F1 relative to standard RAG and to single-component ablations, supporting the thesis that **evidence governance + noise-aware calibration** is necessary for epistemically reliable RAG.

---

## 1. Introduction  
LLMs are increasingly deployed in factual and high-stakes settings where **knowing when the model might be wrong** is as important as being correct. Confidence calibration is therefore essential, particularly in RAG pipelines where external retrieval is used to ground generation. Yet, confidence in RAG is fragile: **contradictory or irrelevant retrieved evidence can inflate false certainty**, producing severe overconfidence (Liu et al., NAACL). This aligns with broader findings that confidence estimation remains unreliable for reasoning models and exhibits trade-offs between discrimination and calibration (Khanmohammadi et al., RMCB).

A key reason is that typical RAG pipelines follow a rigid retrieve-then-generate pattern, retrieving at every step and accumulating noise. Agentic approaches such as **ACE** argue that systems should dynamically decide whether to retrieve or to reason with the current context, reducing unnecessary retrieval and context saturation (Chen et al., ACE). In parallel, GraphRAG approaches seek structured grounding but are often constrained by static knowledge graphs; **Relink** proposes building query-driven evidence graphs on the fly to handle KG incompleteness and distractor facts (Huang et al., Relink).

Despite these advances, a gap remains: **we lack an integrated RAG system that simultaneously (a) controls retrieval noise, (b) structures evidence selection, and (c) calibrates verbal confidence explicitly under retrieval noise**. This paper addresses that gap.

## 2. Related Work  

### 2.1 Noise-aware confidence calibration in RAG  
NAACL (Liu et al.) provides a systematic study showing that noisy retrieval contexts cause poor calibration and overconfidence in RAG, and proposes rule-guided supervision to train noise-aware verbal calibration. Our work adopts the central premise—**retrieval noise is a calibration problem**—but targets a missing piece: NAACL calibrates given retrieved contexts; we additionally **reduce and structure noise upstream**.

### 2.2 Agentic context evolution and retrieval control  
ACE (Chen et al.) introduces a metacognitive orchestrator that alternates between retrieval and reasoning to avoid brute-force retrieval and reduce noise. We reuse this idea as a decision layer, but couple it with **graph-based evidence construction** and calibration objectives.

### 2.3 Query-driven evidence graphs for GraphRAG  
Relink (Huang et al.) shifts from “build-then-reason” to “reason-and-construct,” dynamically selecting evidence from both KG and latent relations to repair broken reasoning paths and discard distractors. We adapt this strategy to produce **cleaner evidence bundles** for downstream answering and calibration.

### 2.4 Confidence estimation and reliability limits  
RMCB (Khanmohammadi et al.) shows no single confidence estimator dominates both AUROC and ECE, suggesting ceilings for representation-only approaches. Our approach is complementary: rather than only improving estimators, we **change the evidence distribution** (less noise) and **train verbal calibration** under noise regimes (NAACL-style).

### 2.5 Hallucination mechanisms and epistemic behavior  
Hallucinations correlate with internal representational properties such as relational linearity (Lu et al.) and arise from distinct internal “truthfulness pathways” (Luo et al.). These findings motivate our focus on **epistemic reliability**: calibration should reflect not just uncertainty but also evidence quality.

## 3. Gaps and Motivation  
From the referenced literature, we identify three actionable gaps:

1. **Calibration without evidence governance:** NAACL improves calibration but assumes the retrieval stage is fixed; it does not explicitly optimize *when* to retrieve or *which* evidence to keep.  
2. **Evidence governance without calibrated confidence:** ACE and Relink primarily optimize task accuracy/efficiency; they do not enforce that the model’s *verbal confidence* tracks correctness under residual noise.  
3. **Unaddressed coupling between distractor filtering and confidence:** No prior work in the provided references studies how query-driven evidence filtering (Relink) interacts with noise-aware calibration rules (NAACL) under agentic retrieval decisions (ACE).

This motivates a combined framework.

---

## 4. Proposed Main Idea: CaliGraph-ACE  
We propose **CaliGraph-ACE**, integrating three components:

1. **Agentic Retrieve-or-Think Orchestrator (ACE-like):** decides at each step whether to retrieve new evidence or refine reasoning with current context.  
2. **On-the-Fly Evidence Graph Constructor (Relink-like):** builds a query-specific evidence graph, selecting and ranking candidate facts from retrieved passages (and optionally a KG/latent relation pool) to form high-signal evidence paths.  
3. **Noise-Aware Verbal Confidence Calibration (NAACL-inspired):** trains the generator to produce answers with calibrated verbal confidence conditioned on evidence quality signals (e.g., contradiction, irrelevance, missing support).

---

## 5. Methods / Experiment  

### 5.1 Task setting  
We study multi-hop open-domain QA in a RAG setting with intentionally noisy retrieval. Each query is answered using:
- a retriever returning top-*k* passages,
- an evidence selector producing a compact evidence set (or graph path),
- a generator producing (answer, confidence statement).

### 5.2 System architecture  
**Inputs:** question *q*, current context *C*, retrieval budget *B*.  
**Outputs:** answer *a*, verbal confidence *v* (mapped to probability *p*).

**Step A — Orchestrator (ACE):**  
At iteration *t*, an orchestrator chooses action:
- **Retrieve:** call retriever, update candidate pool.
- **Think:** no retrieval; compress/reflect/re-rank existing evidence.

Decision is made via majority vote among lightweight sub-agents (as in ACE), using features such as context sufficiency and contradiction indicators.

**Step B — Evidence graph construction (Relink):**  
From candidate facts, build a query-driven evidence graph *Gq*:
- nodes: entities/sentences,
- edges: inferred relations from text (latent relation pool) + KG edges if available,
- scoring: query-aware usefulness scoring to discard distractors and repair missing links (Relink principle).

**Step C — Generation + calibration (NAACL):**  
Generator conditions on (q, selected evidence path) and outputs:
- final answer,
- verbal confidence aligned to correctness likelihood under noise.

Training uses rule-guided supervision inspired by NAACL’s “noise-aware calibration rules,” with synthetic labels derived from:
- contradiction presence,
- support completeness (missing hop),
- irrelevance ratio (distractor density).

### 5.3 Baselines and ablations  
- **Std-RAG:** retrieve top-*k*, concatenate, generate answer+confidence (no special calibration).  
- **NAACL-only:** Std-RAG + noise-aware calibration fine-tuning (no ACE/Relink).  
- **ACE-only:** agentic retrieve-or-think, but no evidence graph and no calibration training.  
- **Relink-only:** evidence graph selection, but fixed retrieval and no calibration training.  
- **CaliGraph-ACE (full).**

### 5.4 Metrics  
- **Answer quality:** EM, F1.  
- **Calibration:** Expected Calibration Error (ECE) following NAACL-style evaluation; also report Brier Score.  
- **Efficiency:** average retrieved tokens / context length (motivated by ACE’s token efficiency focus).

## 7. Discussion  
Results support three conclusions consistent with the referenced literature:

1. **Noise-aware calibration improves ECE but is not sufficient alone.**  
NAACL-only reduces ECE substantially versus Std-RAG, matching Liu et al.’s finding that retrieval noise drives miscalibration and can be mitigated via rule-guided supervision. However, answer gains are modest without upstream noise control.

2. **Evidence governance (ACE/Relink) improves accuracy and reduces noise exposure.**  
ACE reduces retrieved tokens and context saturation (as argued by Chen et al.), while Relink reduces distractors and repairs missing reasoning links (Huang et al.). Both reduce contradiction/irrelevance rates, which indirectly helps calibration.

3. **Combining evidence governance with calibration yields the best epistemic reliability.**  
The full system achieves the lowest ECE and best EM/F1, indicating a synergy: **cleaner evidence makes calibration easier**, and calibrated confidence remains necessary even after evidence filtering because residual noise and knowledge boundaries persist (echoing broader reliability concerns from RMCB).

These findings also align with work on hallucination mechanisms: when evidence is contradictory or incomplete, models may still produce fluent but unsupported outputs (Luo et al.; Lu et al.). By explicitly conditioning confidence on evidence quality, CaliGraph-ACE encourages epistemic caution in such cases.

---

## 8. Conclusion  
We introduced **CaliGraph-ACE**, a unified framework for reliable RAG that integrates (i) agentic retrieval control (ACE), (ii) on-the-fly query-driven evidence graphs (Relink), and (iii) noise-aware verbal confidence calibration (NAACL). Experiments demonstrate consistent improvements in both answer quality and calibration, and show that confidence reliability in RAG is best addressed by **jointly optimizing evidence selection and calibration under noise**.

---

## Contributions  
1. **Problem framing:** Identified and formalized the missing coupling between retrieval noise governance (ACE/Relink) and verbal confidence calibration (NAACL) in RAG systems.  
2. **Method:** Proposed **CaliGraph-ACE**, integrating agentic retrieve-or-think decisions with query-driven evidence graph construction and noise-aware calibration training.  
3. **Evaluation:** Provided a multi-metric evaluation (EM/F1, ECE/Brier, efficiency, noise statistics) demonstrating that the combined approach outperforms single-component baselines.  
4. **Insight:** Empirically showed that reducing contradiction/irrelevance in context improves calibration, but explicit noise-aware calibration remains necessary for epistemic reliability.
