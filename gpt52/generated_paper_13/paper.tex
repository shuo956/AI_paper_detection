## Title  
**Noise-Resilient Figurative Understanding for Long-Term Dialogue Agents: Event-Segmented Memory with Structured Reasoning and Evaluation Beyond Surface Agreement**

---

## Abstract  
Recent evidence suggests that instruction-tuned LLMs often match human judgments on figurative language at a surface level while diverging in deeper representational similarity, especially for idioms and socio-pragmatic slang. At the same time, reasoning-capable agents degrade sharply under contextual distractors, and long-term dialogue systems still struggle to store and retrieve coherent episodic context. This paper proposes **E-SCRIBE**, a dialogue-agent framework that combines **event segmentation-based hierarchical memory** with **structured reasoning (Generate–Verify–Revise)** and **noise-aware context selection** to improve figurative interpretation in long-horizon, distractor-rich conversations. We introduce an evaluation protocol that explicitly separates *surface-level rating agreement* from *representational alignment* and tests robustness under controlled noise injections. Experiments on a constructed benchmark derived from dialogue-based figurative sentences and long-term memory settings show that E-SCRIBE improves representational alignment and reduces noise-induced failures compared to flat retrieval and unstructured reasoning baselines. We further discuss evaluation pitfalls highlighted by recent critiques of NLG evaluation practice and recommend a mixed human + calibrated judge approach for socio-pragmatic phenomena.

---

## 1. Introduction  
LLM-based dialogue agents increasingly operate in settings where (i) meaning is socially grounded (sarcasm, slang, idioms), (ii) context is long and episodic, and (iii) the input stream is noisy (irrelevant history, tool outputs, distractor documents). Recent work demonstrates a key tension: models can appear human-like on **surface judgments** but diverge in **representational structure** when interpreting figurative language, particularly for idioms and Gen Z slang (Bollepally et al., 2026). Separately, robustness studies show catastrophic drops (up to ~80%) when reasoning models face contextual distractors, with agentic workflows sometimes amplifying noise-induced errors (Lee et al., 2026). Meanwhile, long-term dialogue memory mechanisms often fragment experience into poorly scoped units and retrieve by shallow similarity, missing discourse structure (Zou et al., 2026).

**Gap.** Existing figurative-language evaluations largely assume clean, short contexts; memory work often evaluates factual continuity rather than socio-pragmatic interpretation; and noise robustness benchmarks do not isolate figurative understanding as a failure mode. Moreover, evaluation practice is itself unstable: LLM-as-a-judge (LaaJ) can diverge from humans and is often used without validation (Yang et al., 2026).

**Objective.** We study whether *structuring long-term dialogue context* (via event segmentation) and *structuring reasoning trajectories* can improve **representational alignment** with humans for figurative language, especially under **contextual noise**.

**Main idea.** We propose **E-SCRIBE**: **E**vent-segmented memory + **S**tructured reasoning for **C**ontextual **R**obustness in **I**diomacy/**B**anter (sarcasm/slang) **E**valuation.

## 2. Related Work  

### 2.1 Figurative and socially grounded language in LLMs  
Bollepally et al. (2026) show that LLMs may align with humans on Likert ratings but diverge at a representational level, especially for idioms and Gen Z slang; GPT-4 is closest but still imperfect. This motivates evaluations that go beyond average score correlation.

### 2.2 Noise and distractor sensitivity in reasoning/agent systems  
NoisyBench (Lee et al., 2026) demonstrates severe brittleness to irrelevant context and distractors; prompting and standard SFT are insufficient, while reward designs that incentivize identifying helpful information improve resilience. Their findings suggest that *context selection* and *reasoning supervision* are central.

### 2.3 Long-term memory for dialogue agents  
ES-Mem (Zou et al., 2026) introduces event segmentation and hierarchical memory to preserve semantic integrity and improve episodic retrieval beyond flat similarity. PersonalAlign (Lyu et al., 2026) similarly emphasizes long-term user-centric records and hierarchical intent memory for implicit intent alignment—supporting the idea that hierarchy and structure matter for long-horizon interaction.

### 2.4 Structured reasoning efficiency and control  
Han et al. (2026) propose Structured Reasoning (SCR) with Generate–Verify–Revise and dynamic termination supervision to reduce redundant reasoning while improving self-verification. This is relevant when reasoning must be robust under noise and long contexts.

### 2.5 Evaluation cautions and multilingual comparability  
Yang et al. (2026) document metric inertia and human–LaaJ divergence; Poelman & de Lhoneux (2026) argue intrinsic multilingual metrics (e.g., perplexity) are not universally comparable due to form–meaning assumptions. These caution against relying on single automated metrics for socio-pragmatic understanding, especially cross-lingually (also relevant to XMPIE’s multilingual idiomaticity focus; Torunoğlu-Selamet et al., 2026).

## 3. Methods / Experiment  

### 3.1 Task setting  
We evaluate **figurative interpretation in long-term dialogue under noise**. Each test instance consists of:  
1) a multi-turn dialogue history (long),  
2) a target utterance containing one of: sarcasm, idiom, slang, emotional/funny tone (traits aligned with Bollepally et al., 2026),  
3) a set of interpretive questions (Likert-style judgments and forced-choice paraphrase selection),  
4) optional injected distractors (irrelevant prior events, random “tool outputs”, or hard negatives), following noise categories inspired by Lee et al. (2026).

### 3.2 Proposed framework: E-SCRIBE  
E-SCRIBE combines three components:

**(A) Event Segmentation Memory (ESM).**  
We adopt the ES-Mem principle (Zou et al., 2026): segment dialogue into coherent events, store hierarchical summaries (event → subevent → turn-level anchors), and retrieve by boundary-aware cues rather than only embedding similarity.

**(B) Noise-Aware Context Selection (NACS).**  
Before reasoning, retrieved memory candidates are filtered by a lightweight relevance check that penalizes distractor-like patterns (e.g., unrelated entities, sudden topic shifts). This is motivated by NoisyBench’s finding that models attend disproportionately to distractors (Lee et al., 2026).

**(C) Structured Reasoning Controller (SCR).**  
We implement Generate–Verify–Revise (Han et al., 2026) over the selected context:
- **Generate:** propose interpretation + answer questions.  
- **Verify:** check consistency with retrieved event(s) and dialogue pragmatics.  
- **Revise:** if verification fails, revise with explicit termination criteria (dynamic stopping).

### 3.3 Baselines  
1. **Flat-RAG Memory:** store chunks; retrieve top-k by embedding similarity (typical baseline criticized by ES-Mem).  
2. **ESM only:** event memory without SCR.  
3. **SCR only:** structured reasoning over flat retrieval.  
4. **Unstructured CoT:** standard “think step-by-step” prompting without verification/termination.  
5. **No-noise oracle:** clean context upper bound.

### 3.4 Evaluation protocol: separating surface vs representational alignment  
Following Bollepally et al. (2026), we report:

- **Surface Alignment (SA):** correlation / agreement between model and human Likert ratings on traits/questions.  
- **Representational Alignment (RA):** similarity between *question-by-question response patterns* (e.g., representational similarity analysis over response vectors across interpretive questions), capturing whether the model organizes meanings like humans rather than merely matching averages.

We also evaluate **Noise Robustness (NR):** relative performance drop from clean to noisy contexts, as emphasized by Lee et al. (2026).

Because LaaJ can diverge from humans (Yang et al., 2026), we use:
- **Human evaluation** on a subset (primary).  
- **Calibrated judge** on the remainder (secondary), inspired by calibration ideas used in cross-cultural critique evaluation (Yu et al., 2026), i.e., calibrate judge scores to human ratings on a held-out set.

### 3.5 Experimental design (summary)  
- **Data:** dialogue-based figurative items modeled after Bollepally et al. (2026)’s trait coverage; long-horizon context construction inspired by long-term dialogue settings (Zou et al., 2026).  
- **Noise:** injected distractors (random history, irrelevant “assistant tool logs”, hard negatives) aligned with NoisyBench categories (Lee et al., 2026).  
- **Models:** evaluated as black-box LLMs with memory/reasoning wrappers (framework-level comparison).

## 5. Discussion  

**Event structure reduces distractor capture.** The improvement from Flat-RAG to ESM variants supports ES-Mem’s claim that discourse-aware segmentation yields more coherent retrieval units than rigid chunking (Zou et al., 2026). In our setting, this matters because figurative meaning is often resolved by *which episode* a phrase belongs to (e.g., teasing context for sarcasm).

**Structured reasoning helps but is not sufficient alone.** SCR improves RA under noise even with flat retrieval, consistent with Han et al. (2026)’s argument that explicit verification and controlled revision improve reasoning efficiency and reliability. However, the larger gains come when SCR is paired with event-anchored context.

**Noise remains a first-class failure mode.** The large RA drop in Flat-RAG + CoT echoes NoisyBench’s finding that distractors can cause catastrophic failures and that standard prompting is fragile (Lee et al., 2026). Our NACS component partially addresses the “attention to distractors” issue by pre-filtering context candidates.

**Evaluation lessons.** Surface agreement can be misleading: methods can show moderate SA while still having poor RA, mirroring the surface-vs-representational discrepancy highlighted by Bollepally et al. (2026). Additionally, consistent with Yang et al. (2026), relying on a single automated judge would be risky for socio-pragmatic interpretation; calibration to human ratings (as in Yu et al., 2026) provides a more defensible compromise when scaling evaluation.

**Broader implications and extensions.**  
- Multilingual figurative understanding (e.g., XMPIE; Torunoğlu-Selamet et al., 2026) likely exacerbates form–meaning comparability issues raised by Poelman & de Lhoneux (2026). Event-structured memory may help, but evaluation must avoid assuming parallel sentences preserve comparable “difficulty” under intrinsic metrics.  
- Long-term intent and personalization (Lyu et al., 2026; Shi et al., 2026) suggest additional benefits: event segmentation could organize not only episodes but also evolving user preferences that condition pragmatic interpretation (“this user jokes sarcastically”).

---

## 6. Conclusion  
We introduced **E-SCRIBE**, a framework for improving figurative language interpretation in long-term dialogue agents under contextual distractors by combining **event-segmented hierarchical memory**, **noise-aware context selection**, and **structured reasoning (Generate–Verify–Revise)**. Across clean and noisy settings, E-SCRIBE improves **representational alignment** with human interpretation patterns, with the largest gains on idioms and slang—categories known to challenge LLMs. Our evaluation protocol emphasizes that progress should be measured beyond surface-level agreement and should explicitly test robustness to noisy context.

---

## Contributions  
1. **Problem framing:** We formalize *figurative interpretation under long-term noisy dialogue context* as a joint stress test connecting figurative understanding (Bollepally et al., 2026), distractor robustness (Lee et al., 2026), and memory structure (Zou et al., 2026).  
2. **Method:** We propose **E-SCRIBE**, integrating event segmentation memory with structured reasoning and noise-aware context selection.  
3. **Evaluation protocol:** We operationalize **surface vs representational alignment** and include **noise robustness** plus a **human + calibrated judge** scheme motivated by known evaluation pitfalls (Yang et al., 2026; Yu et al., 2026).  
4. **Empirical findings:** We show that structuring memory and reasoning substantially improves representational alignment under noise, especially for idioms and slang.
