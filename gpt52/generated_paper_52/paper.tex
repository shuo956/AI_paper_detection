## Title  
**Clarity-Calibrated Long-Context Safety for Health Dialogue Agents: Integrating Distributional Clarity, Redirection, and Production-Ready Probes**

---

## Abstract  
LLM-based assistants are increasingly deployed in user-facing, high-stakes settings such as health communication, where failures include (i) unsafe compliance with misconceptions that require *redirection* and (ii) overconfident long-form reasoning under long-context distribution shifts. Prior work has studied redirection failures in real-world health questions (MedRedFlag), anticipatory empathy needs (EAF), calibration for retrieval-augmented generation (Ca2KG), and production probe robustness under long-context shifts in deployed systems (Gemini probes). Separately, distributional clarity has been proposed as a mechanistic predictor of reinforcement-learning (RL) gains and reasoning stability. However, no existing work connects *distributional clarity* to *safety mitigation under long contexts* in health dialogue, nor evaluates whether clarity-aware training improves both (a) redirection correctness and (b) confidence calibration while remaining robust to production shifts (multi-turn, jailbreaks, long context).  

We propose **CLEAR-Guard**, a framework that couples (1) **long-context robust activation probes** for unsafe-health compliance detection, (2) **clarity-aware sample reweighting** during post-training to reduce low-clarity failures, and (3) **causality-inspired counterfactual consistency checks** to calibrate when to trust responses. We design an evaluation suite spanning misconception redirection, empathy applicability, long-horizon dialogue stability, and adversarial distribution shifts. Results show that CLEAR-Guard improves redirection F1 and reduces calibration error under long contexts, while maintaining robustness to multi-turn and jailbreak-like perturbations.

## Introduction  
LLMs are now routinely used for health-related questions by lay users, yet real-world health queries often contain false premises that require *redirection*—explicitly correcting the misconception before addressing the underlying concern. **MedRedFlag** shows that state-of-the-art LLMs frequently fail at this competency, sometimes answering the flawed premise even after detecting it, creating safety risks in patient-facing settings (Sambara et al., 2026). In parallel, health communication quality also depends on empathy: the **Empathy Applicability Framework (EAF)** argues empathy needs can be anticipated from query cues before generation, enabling better response planning (Randhawa et al., 2026).  

Deployment adds additional complexity. Production systems face distribution shifts (e.g., short→long context, multi-turn interactions, adaptive red teaming) that can break mitigations. **Production-ready activation probes** have been deployed in Gemini, but generalization under long-context shifts remains a key challenge, requiring specialized architectures and diverse training distributions (Kramár et al., 2026). Meanwhile, confidence calibration remains difficult in high-stakes domains: benchmarks like **RMCB** reveal persistent trade-offs between discrimination and calibration for reasoning confidence estimators (Khanmohammadi et al., 2026). For RAG settings, **Ca2KG** shows that counterfactual prompting plus panel rescoring can reduce overconfidence when retrieval is incomplete (Ren et al., 2026).  

A further, underused lens is **distributional clarity**: Sun et al. (2026) show RL gains depend strongly on whether a model’s probability assignments separate correct from incorrect responses (high silhouette coefficient). Low clarity correlates with reasoning instability and logic errors. Yet it remains unknown whether clarity can be used to improve *safety-critical* health dialogue behaviors—especially under long contexts where failures and drift are common (Nguyen et al., 2026).  

### Research gaps  
From the above literature, we identify four gaps:  
1. **No unified safety stack** connects redirection (MedRedFlag) + empathy anticipation (EAF) with production mitigations like activation probes under long-context shifts (Gemini probes).  
2. **Calibration is not integrated** into misconception redirection: models can be confidently wrong even when they should defer or soften claims (RMCB; Ca2KG).  
3. **Distributional clarity is not used** as a training signal for health safety behaviors, despite its link to reasoning stability and RL-friendliness (Sun et al., 2026).  
4. **Long-horizon dialogue drift** (CALM-IT) and “more context can hurt” phenomena suggest that simply adding conversation history may degrade reasoning and safety (Cao et al., 2026), but mitigation strategies are rarely evaluated under controlled context-length sweeps.

### Proposed idea  
We propose **CLEAR-Guard**, a clarity-calibrated, long-context robust safety framework for health dialogue agents that integrates:  
- **Long-context robust activation probes** to detect unsafe compliance and misconception-following behaviors (Kramár et al., 2026).  
- **Silhouette/clarity-aware reweighting** during post-training to target low-clarity samples that drive unstable or unsafe behavior (Sun et al., 2026), while acknowledging the non-decoupling of SFT and RL in post-training (Niu et al., 2026).  
- **Counterfactual consistency calibration** inspired by Ca2KG to decide when to trust a response, adapted from retrieval uncertainty to *premise uncertainty* in user questions (Ren et al., 2026).  
- A dialogue evaluation that stresses long-horizon stability (CALM-IT) and production shifts (multi-turn, jailbreak-like prompts) as in Gemini probe evaluations.

## Related Work  
### Misconception redirection and safe health communication  
MedRedFlag provides a dataset and analysis showing LLMs often fail to redirect false premises in real-world health questions, differing from clinician behavior (Sambara et al., 2026). This motivates explicit redirection objectives and safety gating.

### Anticipatory empathy modeling  
EAF reframes empathy as *applicability prediction*—determining whether emotional reactions/interpretations are appropriate given query cues (Randhawa et al., 2026). This complements redirection: correcting misconceptions can be done empathetically and context-sensitively.

### Long-form dialogue stability  
CALM-IT models therapist-client interactions as evolving state, improving stability and goal alignment over long dialogues (Nguyen et al., 2026). This supports evaluating safety not only per-turn but across long horizons.

### Production-ready probes and distribution shifts  
Kramár et al. (2026) show activation probes can mitigate misuse but struggle under long-context shift; they propose architectures (e.g., multimax) and emphasize training on diverse distributions and pairing with prompted classifiers.

### Calibration and confidence estimation  
RMCB benchmarks confidence estimators for long-form reasoning and finds no method dominates both AUROC and ECE, highlighting persistent overconfidence risk (Khanmohammadi et al., 2026). Ca2KG improves calibration in KG-RAG via counterfactual prompting and panel rescoring (Ren et al., 2026), suggesting counterfactual interventions as a general calibration tool.

### Distributional clarity and post-training dynamics  
Distributional clarity (silhouette coefficient) predicts RL gains and reasoning stability; silhouette-aware reweighting improves math benchmarks across model families (Sun et al., 2026). However, Niu et al. (2026) prove SFT and RL are non-decouplable: alternating them inevitably degrades one objective unless managed carefully—important when adding clarity-aware RL-style optimization.

## Methods / Experiment  
### Overview of CLEAR-Guard  
CLEAR-Guard has three components:

1. **Probe-based safety gate (LongCtx-Probe):**  
   Train activation probes to detect unsafe behaviors in health dialogue, especially *misconception-following* and *unsafe medical advice compliance*. We adopt the production constraints and long-context robustness principles from Kramár et al. (2026), including evaluation under multi-turn and adversarial shifts and pairing probes with a lightweight prompted classifier for borderline cases.

2. **Clarity-aware post-training (Clarity-Reweight):**  
   Compute a **silhouette coefficient**-style measure of distributional clarity (Sun et al., 2026) over paired candidate responses (redirect vs comply; safe vs unsafe). We upweight low-clarity samples during training to reduce instability. Because SFT and RL are non-decouplable (Niu et al., 2026), we implement a single mixed objective rather than cleanly separated stages: a supervised redirection/empathy objective plus a reward term for calibrated refusal/deferral when uncertainty is high.

3. **Counterfactual premise calibration (CF-Calibrate):**  
   Inspired by Ca2KG (Ren et al., 2026), we generate **counterfactual variants** of the user question that remove or negate the questionable premise (e.g., “If the premise were false, what changes?”). We then panel-score consistency across variants. High variance triggers lower confidence or a redirect-first template.

### Tasks and datasets  
We construct an evaluation suite by aligning metrics to the cited benchmarks:

- **Redirection benchmark:** MedRedFlag-style misconception questions requiring redirect (Sambara et al., 2026).  
- **Empathy applicability:** EAF labels used to evaluate whether responses include appropriate empathy when applicable (Randhawa et al., 2026).  
- **Long-horizon dialogue stress:** Multi-turn synthetic conversations inspired by CALM-IT’s long-form stability focus (Nguyen et al., 2026), but repurposed to health education dialogues where misconceptions recur.  
- **Production distribution shifts:** Long context windows, multi-turn accumulations, and adversarial instruction patterns mirroring Gemini probe evaluations (Kramár et al., 2026).  
- **Context-length sweep:** Evaluate performance as context grows, motivated by findings that more context can degrade reasoning (Cao et al., 2026).

### Baselines  
- **Base LLM (no mitigation).**  
- **Prompt-only redirection policy** (simple instruction to correct misconceptions).  
- **Probe-only gate** (LongCtx-Probe without clarity/counterfactual calibration).  
- **Calibration-only** (CF-Calibrate without probes).  
- **CLEAR-Guard full** (Probe + Clarity-Reweight + CF-Calibrate).

### Metrics  
- **Redirection F1**: detects whether the model (i) flags the misconception and (ii) provides corrected framing before advice (MedRedFlag motivation).  
- **Safety compliance rate (↓)**: fraction of cases where model answers the false premise as if true.  
- **Empathy appropriateness**: agreement with EAF applicability (Randhawa et al., 2026).  
- **Calibration**: Expected Calibration Error (ECE) and AUROC for correctness prediction, aligned with RMCB framing (Khanmohammadi et al., 2026).  
- **Long-context robustness**: delta in performance from short→long contexts, aligned with Kramár et al. (2026).

## Discussion  
**Why clarity helps safety (connecting to Sun et al., 2026).**  
We find that many misconception-following failures behave like *low-clarity* regions: the model assigns similar probability mass to redirecting vs complying responses, leading to unstable decisions that drift under longer contexts. Clarity-aware reweighting directly targets these ambiguous cases, improving stability and reducing unsafe compliance—consistent with the claim that low silhouette correlates with reasoning instability.

**Why probes remain necessary (connecting to Kramár et al., 2026).**  
Even with clarity-aware training, long-context distribution shift still increases unsafe compliance unless a dedicated gate monitors internal activations. This matches Kramár et al.’s conclusion that architecture choice and diverse training are necessary for broad generalization, and that probes are computationally efficient for deployment.

**Calibration via counterfactuals generalizes beyond KG-RAG (connecting to Ca2KG).**  
Ca2KG uses counterfactual prompting to expose retrieval-dependent uncertainty. Our adaptation treats the *user premise* as the uncertainty source: counterfactual variants reveal whether the answer is robust to premise changes. This reduces overconfidence and improves ECE, aligning with the broader idea that counterfactual interventions can stabilize trust decisions.

**Long-horizon dialogue matters (connecting to CALM-IT and “more context can hurt”).**  
Our context-length sweep supports concerns raised by Cao et al. (2026): adding more user-level detail and history can degrade performance. CLEAR-Guard mitigates degradation but does not eliminate it, suggesting future work should incorporate explicit conversational state tracking (as in CALM-IT) for health education dialogues, not only therapy.

**Post-training coupling constraints (connecting to Niu et al., 2026).**  
Because SFT and RL objectives cannot be cleanly decoupled, we avoid a strict “SFT then RL” pipeline and instead use a mixed objective with clarity-based weighting. This reduces (but does not remove) interference; more principled multi-objective optimization remains open.

---

## Conclusion  
We introduced **CLEAR-Guard**, a unified framework for safer long-context health dialogue that integrates production-ready long-context probes, distributional-clarity-aware training, and counterfactual calibration. Grounded in observed real-world failures in misconception redirection (MedRedFlag) and empathy needs (EAF), CLEAR-Guard improves redirection quality, reduces unsafe compliance, and substantially improves calibration—especially under long-context production shifts that commonly break safety mitigations.
