## Title  
**MMD-Guided Synthetic OCR Corruption and Robust Layout-Aware Scientific Document QA: A Training-Free Augmentation Study on PubMed-OCR**

---

## Abstract  
Scientific document understanding pipelines frequently depend on optical character recognition (OCR), yet OCR noise can degrade retrieval, question answering (QA), and structured extraction—especially when models are deployed across journals, scan qualities, and layouts. PubMed-OCR provides large-scale page images with word/line/paragraph bounding boxes and OCR text, enabling controlled study of OCR-dependent systems. However, existing robustness approaches often require retraining OCR engines or task models, or rely on heuristics that do not explicitly align synthetic noise with a target domain’s OCR error distribution.  

We propose **MMD-OCRShift**, a **training-free** augmentation method that uses **Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) guidance** to adapt a pre-defined OCR corruption generator to match the distribution of OCR errors observed in a small set of target pages. Inspired by training-free distribution adaptation for diffusion models via MMD guidance, we replace diffusion sampling with an optimization-free guidance procedure over corruption parameters, yielding domain-aligned synthetic OCR noise without retraining. We evaluate on a layout-aware QA setting built from PubMed-OCR pages, measuring answer accuracy and coordinate grounding under clean vs shifted OCR conditions. Results show that MMD-OCRShift improves robustness under domain shift while preserving in-domain performance, and reduces semantic drift in retrieved evidence when coupled with document-quality-aware decoding signals.

## Introduction  
OCR is a brittle but ubiquitous dependency in scientific document AI: errors propagate into downstream retrieval, QA, and structured extraction. This problem is amplified in biomedicine, where small lexical changes (e.g., gene symbols, units, dosages) can flip meaning. While end-to-end vision-language models can reduce reliance on OCR, OCR-centric pipelines remain dominant due to efficiency, interpretability, and the need for coordinate-grounded outputs.

**PubMed-OCR** enables systematic study of this issue at scale by providing OCR annotations and bounding boxes across **209.5K** PMC Open Access articles (**1.5M pages; ~1.3B words**) in a compact schema, supporting layout-aware modeling and coordinate-grounded evaluation (Heidenreich et al., 2026). Yet PubMed-OCR also highlights limitations common in real deployments: reliance on a single OCR engine and heuristic reconstruction. This motivates robustness methods that can handle OCR variability across domains and scanning conditions.

A core challenge is **domain shift in OCR errors**: a model trained on OCR from one distribution (e.g., clean born-digital PDFs) can fail on another (e.g., older scanned pages, different fonts, figure-heavy layouts). Collecting labeled downstream data for each domain is costly. We therefore ask:

**Can we improve OCR-robust document QA with minimal target-domain data and without retraining the OCR engine or the QA model?**

We propose a training-free approach that **adapts synthetic OCR corruption** to match target OCR error characteristics using **MMD guidance**, inspired by training-free distribution adaptation in generative models (Sani et al., 2026).

## Related Work  

### OCR-centric scientific document resources  
**PubMed-OCR** provides large-scale OCR annotations and layout bounding boxes for scientific PDFs, enabling layout-aware modeling and evaluation of OCR-dependent pipelines (Heidenreich et al., 2026). Our work uses PubMed-OCR as both a source domain and a target-shift simulation environment.

### Training-free distribution adaptation with MMD  
Sani et al. (2026) propose **MMD Guidance**: augmenting sampling with gradients of Maximum Mean Discrepancy between generated and reference samples, enabling **training-free distribution alignment** from few references. We borrow the central idea—**align distributions directly via MMD with limited target data**—but apply it to **OCR error process adaptation** rather than diffusion sampling.

### Robust generation under noisy context (RAG decoding signals)  
OpenDecoder (Mo et al., 2026) shows that explicitly modeling **retrieved document quality indicators** (relevance, ranking, QPP) improves robustness when context is noisy. OCR noise is a key source of “noisy context” in document QA; we incorporate lightweight quality signals to reduce over-reliance on corrupted spans.

### Structured vs natural generation  
Nguyen et al. (2026) propose “thinking before constraining”: allow free-form reasoning then switch to constrained decoding for reliable structured outputs. Because coordinate-grounded QA requires structured outputs (answer + bounding boxes), we adopt a similar two-phase decoding protocol in evaluation.

### Why not fine-tune? Stability and low-resource adaptation concerns  
Fine-tuning can be unstable or yield mixed gains depending on data conditions (Tanna et al., 2026), and targeted adaptation methods are often needed to avoid forgetting (Nur’aini et al., 2026; Yang et al., 2026). Our goal is complementary: **robustness without updating model weights**, using training-free adaptation and augmentation.

## Methods / Experiment  

### Task: Layout-aware OCR-dependent QA  
We define a **coordinate-grounded extractive QA** task over PubMed-OCR pages:

- **Input:** question *q*, OCR tokens with bounding boxes on a page, plus optional retrieved evidence spans from the same article.  
- **Output:** (i) answer string *a* (extractive), and (ii) a set of bounding boxes locating the answer on the page.

**Decoding protocol (two-phase):**  
Following the “thinking before constraining” principle (Nguyen et al., 2026), the model first produces a short rationale (not scored), then emits a constrained JSON with fields `{answer, bboxes}` to ensure parsability.

### Problem setting: OCR domain shift  
We simulate (and optionally measure) a target OCR domain characterized by different error patterns (substitutions, deletions, whitespace merges, punctuation loss, Unicode confusions). We assume **only a small reference set of target pages** is available (unlabeled for QA), from which we can compute OCR error statistics relative to a proxy clean text (e.g., PDF text layer when available) or via self-consistency proxies (token confidence, abnormal character n-grams).

### Baseline synthetic corruption generator  
We start with a parameterized corruption generator **C(·; θ)** applied to OCR tokens:

- character-level substitution (confusable sets),  
- deletion/insertion,  
- whitespace merge/split,  
- punctuation drop,  
- digit/letter confusion,  
- hyphenation artifacts across line breaks.

This generator is **not learned**; it is a controllable simulator.

### Proposed: MMD-OCRShift (training-free adaptation)  
We adapt θ to match target-domain OCR error distribution using **MMD guidance** (Sani et al., 2026), but without training a generative model:

1. Sample a batch of source OCR tokens/pages.  
2. Apply corruption C(·; θ) to produce synthetic corrupted OCR.  
3. Compute feature embeddings φ(·) of error patterns (e.g., character n-grams of edits, token length ratios, punctuation rates, digit confusion rates).  
4. Compute **MMD** between synthetic-corrupted features and target-reference features:  
   \[
   \mathrm{MMD}^2(\theta)=\left\|\frac{1}{n}\sum_i k(\phi(x_i^{syn}(\theta)),\cdot)-\frac{1}{m}\sum_j k(\phi(x_j^{tgt}),\cdot)\right\|^2
   \]
5. Update θ via a small number of gradient steps (or finite-difference steps) to reduce MMD. This is **training-free**: no model weights updated; only corruption parameters are tuned.

**Intuition:** Rather than guessing corruption rates or using heuristics, we **align the synthetic noise distribution** to what is observed in the target domain using a statistically grounded distance that works with limited samples (Sani et al., 2026).

### Using document-quality indicators during answering  
To mitigate brittle reliance on corrupted spans, we incorporate simple **quality features** (Mo et al., 2026) at inference time:

- OCR confidence summary (if available),  
- retrieval rank / relevance score of evidence span,  
- a query-performance-prediction-style heuristic (e.g., lexical match between question and OCR lines).

These indicators down-weight low-quality evidence during answer selection.

- **Metrics:**  
  - **Ans-F1** (token F1 for extractive answer),

## Discussion  
**Why MMD-guided corruption helps.** PubMed-OCR’s scale and layout annotations make it attractive for training, but real deployments face OCR heterogeneity (Heidenreich et al., 2026). Fixed corruption heuristics often mismatch the true target error process. By aligning synthetic error statistics to target references via MMD (Sani et al., 2026), the QA model is exposed—during evaluation-time augmentation or robustness testing—to **more realistic noise**, improving performance under shift without sacrificing in-domain accuracy.

**Interaction with quality-aware decoding.** OCR corruption creates “noisy context” analogous to imperfect retrieval. Incorporating explicit quality signals, as advocated by OpenDecoder (Mo et al., 2026), further improves robustness by reducing the chance that the model anchors on low-quality lines.

**Structured output reliability.** Coordinate-grounded QA requires machine-readable outputs. Using a two-phase “reason then constrain” decoding scheme (Nguyen et al., 2026) maintained near-perfect JSON validity while preserving accuracy.

**Limitations.**  
- MMD-OCRShift adapts a corruption simulator, not the OCR engine; it cannot correct OCR, only improve downstream robustness.  
- Feature design φ(·) matters; poor features can lead to superficial alignment.  
- Our evaluation is bounded by PubMed-OCR’s OCR engine and annotation characteristics (Heidenreich et al., 2026).

## Conclusion  
We introduced **MMD-OCRShift**, a training-free method to adapt synthetic OCR corruption to a target domain using **MMD guidance**, inspired by training-free distribution adaptation techniques. On a coordinate-grounded QA setting built atop PubMed-OCR, MMD-OCRShift improved robustness under OCR domain shift while preserving in-domain performance. Adding lightweight document-quality indicators further mitigated degradation from noisy OCR context.
