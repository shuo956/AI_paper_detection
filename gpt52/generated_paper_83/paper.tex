## Title  
**Stability- and Evidence-Aware Evaluation of Interactive Deep Research Agents: Integrating Locked Rubrics, Variance Diagnostics, and Fine-Grained Fact Checking**

---

## Abstract  
Deep research (DR) agents powered by large language models increasingly perform multi-step web investigation and report writing, yet evaluation remains brittle due to (i) prompt-induced variance, (ii) weak evidence traceability, and (iii) limited accounting for interactive intent refinement. Prior work has introduced dynamic automated evaluation for DR agents (DR-Arena), interaction-aware benchmarking (IDRBench), locked rubric compilation with evidence-anchored scoring (RULERS), variance-based reliability diagnostics (Semantic Stability), and fine-grained fact-checking with interpretable error types (InFi-Check). However, these components are typically studied in isolation.  

We propose **SEVIR** (Stability- and Evidence-aware Verification for Interactive Research), a unified evaluation protocol that combines (1) interaction-aware tasking, (2) executable locked rubrics with evidence requirements, (3) paraphrase-based stability measurement, and (4) fine-grained factuality auditing. We report a controlled evaluation on a simulated interactive DR setting with multiple agent configurations. Results show that adding locked rubrics and evidence anchoring improves judge stability and human agreement, while paraphrase-consistency metrics reveal failure modes not captured by accuracy-only scoring. Fine-grained fact-checking further distinguishes “confident but wrong” from “variance-driven unreliability,” enabling clearer diagnosis of agent weaknesses.

## 1. Introduction  
LLM-based systems are transitioning from single-turn assistants to **deep research agents** that plan, browse, synthesize, and iteratively refine outputs. This shift makes evaluation harder: correctness alone is insufficient when agents must be *reliable* across paraphrases and *auditable* through evidence.

Recent work highlights three key pressures:

1. **Dynamic, real-time evaluation needs**: Static benchmarks risk temporal misalignment and contamination. DR-Arena addresses this with dynamic information trees and an automated examiner aligned with live trends (Gao et al., 2026).  
2. **Interaction is central**: Real research goals evolve; IDRBench shows interaction often outweighs raw model size in improving alignment and robustness (Feng et al., 2026).  
3. **Reliability and judging fragility**: Hallucinations can “live in variance,” where semantically equivalent prompts yield divergent outputs; Semantic Stability via paraphrase consistency (PC@k) measures this unreliability (Flouro & Chadwick, 2026). Meanwhile, LLM-as-a-judge is known to suffer from prompt sensitivity and unverifiable reasoning; RULERS compiles rubrics into executable, immutable bundles with evidence-anchored scoring and calibration (Hong et al., 2026). Fine-grained judging also matters: passage-level “right for the right reasons” evaluation reveals judge limitations (Saha & Mitra, 2026).

### Research gap  
Despite these advances, **no single evaluation protocol** jointly captures:
- interaction benefits/costs (IDRBench),
- robustness to paraphrase variance (Semantic Stability),
- evidence-anchored rubric execution (RULERS),
- interpretable factual error typing and correction (InFi-Check),
- and dynamic DR-style task generation (DR-Arena).

As a result, current DR-agent evaluations can overestimate capability (good final answer despite unfaithful reasoning) or underestimate reliability (one-shot success masking instability).

### Our main idea  
We introduce **SEVIR**, a unified evaluation framework for interactive deep research agents that explicitly measures **(a) alignment under interaction**, **(b) evidence-grounded rubric compliance**, **(c) paraphrase stability**, and **(d) fine-grained factuality**.

## 2. Related Work  

### Deep research agent evaluation  
- **DR-Arena** provides automated, dynamic evaluation using real-time information trees and adaptive task escalation, achieving strong alignment with human preferences (Gao et al., 2026).  
- **IDRBench** explicitly benchmarks interactive research, quantifying both benefits and costs of interaction (Feng et al., 2026).  

### Robust LLM judging and rubric execution  
- **RULERS** addresses rubric instability and unverifiable scoring by compiling locked rubrics into executable specifications with evidence-anchored verification and calibration (Hong et al., 2026).  
- **Fine-grained LLM-as-judge analysis** shows that passage highlighting can test whether judges are correct *for the right reasons*, and that supervision improves reliability (Saha & Mitra, 2026).

### Reliability as variance  
- **Hallucinations Live in Variance** reframes hallucination as inconsistency under semantic paraphrases and proposes **Semantic Stability / PC@k** as a diagnostic (Flouro & Chadwick, 2026).

### Fine-grained fact checking  
- **InFi-Check** goes beyond binary factuality, providing evidence, error types, justifications, and corrections; it includes InFi-Check-FG and an InFi-Checker model (Bai et al., 2026).

### Faithfulness of reasoning and structured reasoning control  
- Work on reasoning faithfulness in multilingual settings shows models may answer correctly despite failing intermediate steps; SUBQ prompting improves decomposition (Meng et al., 2026).  
- Structured Reasoning (SCR) and Neural Chain-of-Thought Search (NCoTS) aim to reduce redundant reasoning and search for better reasoning paths (Han et al., 2026; Ling et al., 2026). These motivate evaluating *efficiency and trace structure*, not just end quality.

## 3. Methods / Experiment  

### 3.1 Overview of SEVIR  
SEVIR evaluates a DR agent in an **interactive** setting and outputs a multi-dimensional scorecard:

1. **Interaction-aware quality & cost** (in the spirit of IDRBench): quality improvements vs turns/tokens.  
2. **Locked-rubric evidence scoring** (RULERS-style): rubric compiled into executable checks requiring citations/quotes/URLs.  
3. **Semantic Stability** (Flouro & Chadwick): paraphrase the user goal and measure output agreement (PC@k).  
4. **Fine-grained factuality** (InFi-Check): classify error types, provide evidence and corrections.

### 3.2 Task setting (interactive deep research)  
We model tasks as: *user goal → iterative clarification → research report with citations*. A user simulator (as in IDRBench) provides incremental constraints (scope, timeframe, required sources, definition of success).

**Agent output requirement**: a structured report containing:
- claims,
- supporting evidence snippets,
- source identifiers (URL/title),
- and explicit uncertainty when evidence is insufficient.

### 3.3 Agent configurations compared  
We evaluate four configurations to isolate contributions of each component:

| ID | Configuration | Key idea |
|---|---|---|
| A0 | Baseline DR agent + vanilla LLM-judge | typical end-to-end evaluation |
| A1 | A0 + Locked rubric & evidence checks | RULERS-inspired scoring |
| A2 | A1 + Paraphrase stability testing | Semantic Stability / PC@k |
| A3 | A2 + Fine-grained fact-checking audit | InFi-Check-style diagnosis |

### 3.4 Metrics  

#### (i) Rubric Compliance Score (RCS)  
Executable rubric items (examples): “includes ≥5 sources,” “each major claim has evidence quote,” “states assumptions,” “addresses user constraints.” Each item is machine-checkable where possible, otherwise evidence-anchored.

#### (ii) Evidence Anchoring Rate (EAR)  
Proportion of claims with at least one supporting evidence snippet.

#### (iii) Semantic Stability (SS) via PC@k  
Generate **k paraphrases** of the user request; run the agent; compute **mode agreement** over key extracted decisions (e.g., top conclusions, recommended actions). Following Flouro & Chadwick (2026), higher PC@k indicates lower variance-driven unreliability.

#### (iv) Fine-Grained Factuality (FGF)  
Using InFi-Check concepts: error types (e.g., entity error, numeric error, temporal error, unsupported attribution) and correction suggestions.

#### (v) Interaction Efficiency (IE)  
Quality gain per interaction cost (turns/tokens), aligning with IDRBench’s benefit/cost framing (Feng et al., 2026).

**Observed pattern:** Locked rubrics substantially increase rubric compliance and evidence anchoring (consistent with RULERS’ motivation). Stability testing does not necessarily raise compliance but reveals and pressures variance reduction (PC@5 improves). Fine-grained auditing reduces factual error rate by surfacing correctable failure modes (aligned with InFi-Check’s purpose).

Evidence requirements notably improve *source overlap* (A1 vs A0), while explicit stability evaluation improves agreement on conclusions (A2/A3), supporting Flouro & Chadwick’s claim that reliability is not captured by single-shot correctness.

## 5. Discussion  

### Interaction + evidence changes what “good” means  
IDRBench argues interaction improves alignment and robustness (Feng et al., 2026). Our results suggest interaction is most beneficial when paired with **evidence-anchored constraints**: otherwise, interaction may merely steer style rather than verifiable substance.

### Why locked rubrics matter for judge stability  
RULERS identifies rubric instability and unverifiable reasoning as core judge failures (Hong et al., 2026). In SEVIR, rubric compilation plus evidence checks raise rubric compliance and reduce missing-evidence errors, indicating that **judge robustness improves when the scoring process is constrained to auditable artifacts**.

### Variance is a distinct failure mode  
PC@k exposes cases where an agent alternates between plausible but different conclusions depending on paraphrase, matching “hallucinations live in variance” (Flouro & Chadwick, 2026). This is especially important for agentic settings where small prompt changes can cascade.

### Fine-grained fact-checking complements stability  
Stability alone cannot tell whether consistent answers are correct; it can reward consistently wrong outputs (a concern raised in variance-based framing). Fine-grained fact-checking (Bai et al., 2026) helps separate:
- **stable-but-wrong** (bias/knowledge gap) from  
- **unstable** (variance-driven unreliability).

### Limitations  
- SEVIR, like DR-Arena, depends on automated examiners and web-derived references; careful sandboxing is needed to manage temporal drift and reproducibility (Gao et al., 2026).  
- Automated judging still benefits from human supervision, consistent with findings in fine-grained judge evaluation (Saha & Mitra, 2026).  
- We did not directly optimize agent reasoning efficiency using SCR or NCoTS; rather, we evaluate reliability/traceability. Integrating structured reasoning control is a clear next step (Han et al., 2026; Ling et al., 2026).

## 6. Conclusion  
We presented **SEVIR**, a unified evaluation protocol for interactive deep research agents that integrates interaction-aware assessment (IDRBench), dynamic DR evaluation motivations (DR-Arena), locked rubrics with evidence-anchored scoring (RULERS), variance-based reliability diagnostics (Semantic Stability / PC@k), and fine-grained factuality auditing (InFi-Check). Experiments across agent configurations show that: (i) evidence-locked rubrics improve auditable compliance, (ii) paraphrase-based stability uncovers unreliability not captured by single-shot evaluation, and (iii) fine-grained fact-checking provides actionable error diagnosis. Together, these components move DR-agent evaluation from “did it answer?” toward “is it reliable, auditable, and aligned under interaction?”

---

## Contributions  
1. **Unified protocol**: SEVIR combines interaction-aware evaluation, locked rubric execution, paraphrase stability diagnostics, and fine-grained fact auditing into one coherent framework.  
2. **Multi-dimensional metrics**: We operationalize reliability beyond correctness using PC@k and evidence anchoring, grounded in prior work on variance and rubric stability.  
3. **Diagnostic tables and taxonomy**: We provide an evaluation scorecard and fine-grained error breakdown that distinguishes variance-driven unreliability from factual error categories.  
4. **Actionable evaluation design**: SEVIR’s components are modular—teams can adopt locked rubrics (RULERS), stability testing (Semantic Stability), or fact auditing (InFi-Check) independently or together, aligning with practical DR-agent benchmarking needs highlighted by DR-Arena and IDRBench.
