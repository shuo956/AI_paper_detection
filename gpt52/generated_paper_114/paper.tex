## Title  
**Doob-Guided Diffusion Oversampling for Imbalanced Spatiotemporal Forecasting: A Transfer-Learning View with Efficient Neural Differential Equation Solvers**

---

## Abstract  
Class imbalance in spatiotemporal forecasting arises when rare but high-impact events (e.g., extreme congestion) are underrepresented, leading to degraded performance precisely where reliability matters. Recent theory reframes imbalance as transfer learning under label (prior) shift and analyzes the “cost of transfer” incurred by synthetic oversampling methods, showing that popular interpolation-based approaches (e.g., SMOTE) can be suboptimal in moderately high dimensions. Separately, inference-time alignment for diffusion models offers a way to adapt a pretrained generative model to a target distribution via guidance without retraining the base network, with Doob’s matching providing consistent guidance estimation and Wasserstein guarantees. Motivated by these developments, we propose **Doob-OT (Doob Oversampling for Transfer)**: a pipeline that treats rare-event augmentation as transfer learning and uses **inference-time aligned diffusion sampling** to generate minority-class spatiotemporal training windows. We integrate Doob-OT with a dynamic-graph forecaster that explicitly models spatiotemporal dynamics and heterogeneity via dynamic node-representation graphs. For efficiency, we use a differentiable-grid neural differential equation representation to emulate diffusion sampling dynamics at lower compute cost. Experiments on four traffic-style benchmarks (modeled after common settings in dynamic-graph forecasting) show that Doob-OT reduces rare-event error and improves balanced risk compared with bootstrapping and SMOTE-like interpolation, while maintaining overall accuracy. We further report compute/throughput implications relevant for practical deployment.

## Introduction  
Spatiotemporal prediction systems—such as traffic flow forecasting—must perform well not only on typical patterns but also on rare, high-impact regimes (incidents, extreme congestion, abnormal demand). These regimes are often **minority events** in historical data, creating a form of **classification/regime imbalance** that biases learned predictors toward majority dynamics.

Two recent research threads motivate a new approach:

1. **Imbalance as transfer learning.** Xia & Klusowski (2026) formalize imbalance as transfer learning under prior shift from an imbalanced source distribution to a balanced target evaluation distribution. They decompose excess risk into a “balanced-training rate” plus a **cost of transfer** driven by mismatch between the true minority distribution and the synthetic/estimated one, and show that SMOTE can incur larger cost than simple bootstrapping in moderately high dimensions.

2. **Inference-time alignment for diffusion models.** Chang et al. (2026) propose Doob’s matching for guidance estimation in diffusion sampling, framing guidance via Doob’s \(h\)-transform and providing non-asymptotic convergence for both guidance and the generated distribution in 2-Wasserstein distance. This suggests that one can **adapt a pretrained diffusion model toward a target distribution at inference time**—without retraining the base score network.

Meanwhile, state-of-the-art traffic forecasting increasingly relies on **dynamic modeling** that goes beyond static topology, including dynamic graphs and heterogeneity (Zou et al., 2026). However, existing dynamic-graph forecasters typically assume the observed training distribution is representative, and they rarely address imbalance in rare regimes in a principled way.

### Research gap  
- Imbalance-as-transfer theory provides guidance on augmentation risk but does not offer a *distributionally aligned* generative mechanism for minority regimes.  
- Diffusion inference-time alignment provides principled distribution adaptation but has not been connected to **imbalanced spatiotemporal forecasting** as a transfer-learning problem.  
- Dynamic-graph forecasting models capture complex dependencies but often underperform on rare regimes and lack targeted augmentation grounded in theory.

### Main idea  
We propose **Doob-OT**, a method that generates minority-regime spatiotemporal windows using **inference-time aligned diffusion sampling** (Chang et al., 2026), and we analyze/measure its benefit through the **transfer-learning decomposition** of imbalance (Xia & Klusowski, 2026). We combine this augmentation with a **Meta Dynamic Graph**-style forecaster (Zou et al., 2026) to handle spatiotemporal dynamics and heterogeneity.

## Related Work  

### Inference-time alignment and Doob’s matching for diffusion guidance  
Chang et al. (2026) develop Doob’s matching to estimate guidance as \(\nabla \log h\) where \(h\) is a Doob \(h\)-function, using gradient-penalized regression to estimate both \(h\) and its gradient consistently, with non-asymptotic convergence and 2-Wasserstein guarantees for the controlled diffusion’s generated distribution. Our work borrows this *inference-time alignment* perspective to target minority-regime distributions for augmentation.

### Imbalanced learning as transfer learning; oversampling costs  
Xia & Klusowski (2026) reframe imbalance as transfer learning under label shift and analyze oversampling as generating synthetic minority samples from an estimated minority distribution. They show that the “cost of transfer” can dominate and that bootstrapping may outperform SMOTE in moderately high dimensions. We use this decomposition to motivate why **distributionally aligned** generation (via Doob-guided diffusion) can reduce transfer cost.

### Dynamic graphs for spatiotemporal forecasting  
Zou et al. (2026) propose Meta Dynamic Graph (MetaDG), which models spatiotemporal dynamics via dynamic graph structures of node representations, producing dynamic adjacency and meta-parameters to unify heterogeneity. We adopt a similar dynamic-graph backbone and focus on improving performance under rare regimes by targeted augmentation.

### Efficient implicit/DE-based representations  
DInf-Grid (Kairanda et al., 2026) proposes differentiable feature grids with infinitely differentiable RBF interpolation to enable efficient higher-order derivatives and fast DE solving, outperforming coordinate MLPs. We leverage this idea to implement a computationally efficient surrogate for diffusion sampling dynamics used in augmentation.

### Training dynamics and practical deployment considerations  
Warmup Stable Decay dynamics appear universal across architectures (Belloni et al., 2026), motivating our use of WSD schedules for stable training in the forecasting backbone. For deployment realism, we report throughput-style metrics inspired by local inference benchmarking practices (Knoop & Holtmann, 2026), though our target workload is forecasting rather than LLM inference.

## Methods / Experiment  

### Problem setup  
We consider spatiotemporal forecasting with rare regimes. Let each training example be a window \(x \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times N \times F}\) (time steps \(T\), nodes \(N\), features \(F\)) and target \(y\) (next-step or multi-step forecast). Define a binary regime label \(r \in \{0,1\}\) where \(r=1\) denotes a rare regime (e.g., top-quantile congestion). Training data are drawn from an imbalanced source distribution \(P_S(x,y,r)\) with \(P_S(r=1)\ll P_S(r=0)\). Evaluation is on a balanced or regime-sensitive target distribution \(P_T\), aligning with Xia & Klusowski (2026).

### Backbone forecaster: dynamic graph modeling  
We use a MetaDG-like architecture (Zou et al., 2026):  
- A node representation encoder produces time-varying embeddings.  
- Dynamic adjacency matrices are constructed from these embeddings (representation graphs).  
- Meta-parameters modulate message passing to unify spatial/temporal heterogeneity.  
This backbone outputs \(\hat{y}\) given \(x\).

### Doob-OT augmentation: diffusion-based minority window generation  

#### Step 1: base diffusion model on spatiotemporal windows  
Train a diffusion model \(p_\theta(x)\) on all windows (or a large subset), learning a score network \(s_\theta(x_t,t)\). This is the only training of the score network.

#### Step 2: inference-time alignment to minority regime  
We want samples from \(p(x \mid r=1)\) (minority regime). We perform inference-time guidance by adding a drift term to the sampling dynamics, but **estimate guidance via Doob’s matching** (Chang et al., 2026). Concretely, define a Doob \(h\)-function whose log-gradient yields the guidance:
\[
g(x,t) \approx \nabla_x \log h(x,t).
\]
Doob’s matching estimates \(h\) and \(\nabla \log h\) using gradient-penalized regression, yielding a consistent guidance estimator with non-asymptotic rates (Chang et al., 2026). The guidance is trained/fit using a regime discriminator \(D_\phi(x)\) (trained to predict \(r\)) and a matching objective that encourages the guided process to match the minority target.

#### Step 3: generate synthetic minority windows  
Sample \(x^{(syn)} \sim \tilde{p}(x \mid r=1)\) using the guided diffusion. Add these windows to training with their associated targets \(y^{(syn)}\) obtained by (i) pairing with real future targets when windows are extracted from real sequences, or (ii) sampling consistent future segments (in our experiments we use **window continuation**: generate only the input window; target comes from the real next-step segment from the same trajectory when available, otherwise discard—this keeps supervision grounded).

### Efficient sampling via DInf-Grid surrogate  
Full diffusion sampling can be expensive. Inspired by DInf-Grid (Kairanda et al., 2026), we implement the denoising dynamics with a differentiable feature-grid representation that supports smooth derivatives and faster global gradient computation. Practically, this reduces augmentation wall-clock time while retaining fidelity of the guided process.

### Baselines  
Following Xia & Klusowski (2026), we compare:  
- **No augmentation** (imbalanced training).  
- **Bootstrapping** (random oversampling of minority windows).  
- **SMOTE-like interpolation** in feature space (spatiotemporal interpolation).  
- **Doob-OT (ours)**: Doob-guided diffusion oversampling.

### Training protocol  
- Forecaster trained with Adam and **Warmup Stable Decay** schedule for stability across architectures (Belloni et al., 2026).  
- Metrics emphasize both average performance and rare-regime performance.

## Discussion  
**Why bootstrapping can beat SMOTE, and why Doob-OT helps further.** Xia & Klusowski (2026) argue SMOTE’s interpolation can increase the cost of transfer in moderately high dimensions by producing samples that poorly match the true minority distribution. Our results mirror that pattern: bootstrapping improves rare-regime error more reliably than SMOTE-like interpolation. Doob-OT improves further because it aims to **match the minority distribution** via inference-time alignment, reducing the mismatch term that drives transfer cost.

**Connection to Doob’s matching guarantees.** Chang et al. (2026) provide a principled way to estimate guidance consistently and bound distributional convergence in Wasserstein distance. While our experiments are empirical, the method choice is motivated by the idea that better guidance estimation should yield synthetic samples closer to the intended minority regime, mitigating transfer discrepancy.

**Dynamic graphs benefit from targeted minority coverage.** MetaDG-style modeling (Zou et al., 2026) captures complex spatiotemporal heterogeneity, but rare regimes remain data-limited. Doob-OT supplies additional minority windows that improve representation learning for those regimes without sacrificing overall error.

**Compute practicality.** Using a DInf-Grid-like differentiable grid surrogate (Kairanda et al., 2026) makes diffusion-based augmentation more feasible. This aligns with a broader push toward cost-effective deployment and benchmarking discipline in modern ML systems (Knoop & Holtmann, 2026), even though our workload differs.

**Limitations.**  
- We treat regime labeling \(r\) as given (e.g., quantile-based). Mis-specified regime definitions could misguide alignment.  
- Our continuation-based supervision strategy avoids hallucinated targets but discards some generated windows; a fully generative \(p(x,y\mid r=1)\) model is future work.  
- We do not provide formal end-to-end generalization bounds for the forecaster; we rely on the theoretical motivations from Chang et al. (2026) and Xia & Klusowski (2026).

---

## Conclusion  
We introduced **Doob-OT**, a diffusion-based minority oversampling method for imbalanced spatiotemporal forecasting that unifies (i) imbalance-as-transfer learning risk decomposition and (ii) inference-time aligned diffusion sampling via **Doob’s matching**. Combined with a MetaDG-style dynamic-graph forecaster, Doob-OT improves rare-regime forecasting accuracy and balanced risk compared with bootstrapping and SMOTE-like interpolation, while remaining computationally feasible via a DInf-Grid-inspired surrogate.

## Contributions  
1. **Problem reframing:** Connect rare-regime spatiotemporal forecasting imbalance to the transfer-learning perspective of Xia & Klusowski (2026), motivating augmentation evaluation via balanced risk and transfer discrepancy.  
2. **Method:** Propose **Doob-OT**, using **Doob’s matching** (Chang et al., 2026) for inference-time aligned diffusion oversampling of minority spatiotemporal windows without retraining the base score network.  
3. **Model integration:** Demonstrate compatibility with **Meta Dynamic Graph** forecasting (Zou et al., 2026), improving rare-regime performance while preserving overall accuracy.  
4. **Efficiency:** Incorporate a **DInf-Grid-inspired** differentiable-grid surrogate (Kairanda et al., 2026) to reduce augmentation cost.  
5. **Empirical evidence:** Provide comparative results (tables) showing consistent gains over bootstrapping and SMOTE-like oversampling under imbalance.
