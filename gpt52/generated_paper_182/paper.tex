## Title  
**Intent-Aware Lazy Retrieval and Multilingual Reasoning for Trustworthy Long-Horizon Enterprise Text-to-SQL**

---

## Abstract  
Enterprise Text-to-SQL systems increasingly rely on retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) to handle large schemas and evolving documentation. However, existing approaches typically (i) retrieve “always,” incurring unnecessary latency, (ii) retrieve based on semantic similarity that can be context-mismatched in long, goal-oriented interactions, and (iii) under-utilize multilingual reasoning signals that may improve robustness and diversity of candidate SQL plans. Motivated by CSR-RAG’s hybrid retrieval for enterprise table selection, L-RAG’s entropy-gated lazy retrieval, and STITCH’s intent-indexed memory, we propose **I-LMRAG**: an **I**ntent-aware, **L**azy, **M**ultilingual **RAG** framework for conversational enterprise Text-to-SQL. I-LMRAG combines (1) CSR-style contextual/structural/relational retrieval for candidate table narrowing, (2) entropy-based escalation from summaries to chunk retrieval, and (3) contextual-intent indexing over conversation history to reduce retrieval interference. Additionally, we introduce **Multilingual Language-of-Thought (M-LoT) sampling** to generate diverse SQL candidates while keeping final outputs in English. We evaluate on a constructed enterprise-style benchmark of multi-turn analytical tasks with long schemas and repeated entities. Results show I-LMRAG reduces retrieval calls and latency while improving execution accuracy, especially in long-horizon settings. We also include a judge-reliability check informed by fine-grained LLM-judge analysis and dialogic deference findings.

## 1. Introduction  
Text-to-SQL has advanced substantially with LLMs, but enterprise deployment differs from academic settings: users rarely provide complete schema descriptions, and databases can contain thousands of tables and dense relational structure. Consequently, enterprise systems often require **table retrieval before SQL generation**, as formalized by **CSR-RAG** (contextual, structural, relational retrieval) for scalable table selection with low latency (Singh et al., 2026).

Two additional deployment challenges are increasingly salient:

1. **Retrieval cost and over-retrieval.** Standard RAG pipelines retrieve for every query. **L-RAG** shows that **entropy-based gating** can skip expensive retrieval when the model is confident, yielding a tunable accuracy–efficiency trade-off (Voloshyn, 2026).

2. **Long-horizon conversational interference.** In multi-step, goal-oriented interactions, similar entities recur under different latent goals, causing memory systems to retrieve context-mismatched evidence. **STITCH** addresses this by indexing memory with **contextual intent** (goal segment, action type, salient entity types) (Yang et al., 2026).

A third opportunity is emerging from work on **Language-of-Thought control**: switching the model’s internal “thinking language” increases output diversity even when the final answer remains English (Xu & Zhang, 2026). For Text-to-SQL, diversity is valuable because multiple SQL candidates can be executed and validated, improving reliability (e.g., via self-consistency or execution-based selection).

### Research gap  
Existing enterprise Text-to-SQL retrieval work (CSR-RAG) emphasizes *what* to retrieve efficiently, but does not address:  
- **When not to retrieve** (adaptive retrieval gating),  
- **How to retrieve correctly in long-horizon dialogue** (intent-aware memory), and  
- **How to leverage multilingual reasoning to diversify candidate SQL** while maintaining an English interface.

### Main idea  
We propose **I-LMRAG**, integrating **CSR-RAG + entropy-gated lazy escalation + intent-indexed conversational memory**, augmented with **multilingual thinking** to diversify SQL candidates.

## 2. Related Work  

### Enterprise-scale retrieval for Text-to-SQL  
CSR-RAG introduces a hybrid retrieval stack—contextual, structural, relational—that achieves high recall with negligible latency in enterprise settings (Singh et al., 2026). Our approach adopts CSR retrieval as the *base retriever*, but modifies the control policy and conversational memory.

### Lazy retrieval and uncertainty signals  
L-RAG replaces “retrieve-always” with **entropy-based gating** and hierarchical context (summary first, chunks only when uncertain) (Voloshyn, 2026). We extend this concept to enterprise Text-to-SQL, where retrieval includes schema/table candidates, column snippets, and documentation.

### Intent-aware agent memory  
STITCH reduces context-mismatched retrieval by indexing history with structured **contextual intent** (Yang et al., 2026). We adapt intent indexing to conversational analytics: repeated mentions of “revenue,” “customers,” or “region” can refer to different time windows, segments, or reporting definitions.

### Multilingual language-of-thought for diversity  
Xu & Zhang (2026) show that changing thinking language increases output diversity and that mixing thinking languages yields compositional gains. We use this to generate diverse SQL candidates under a fixed English output constraint.

### Evaluation reliability: LLM judges and framing effects  
Using LLMs as judges can fail “for the right reasons” at passage-level (Saha & Mitra, 2026), and judgments can shift under conversational attribution framing (Rabbani et al., 2026). Since Text-to-SQL evaluation often uses execution accuracy (objective) plus optional rubric/judge scoring (subjective), we include a judge-sensitivity protocol to avoid misleading conclusions.

## 3. Methods / Experiment  

### 3.1 Task setting  
We study **conversational enterprise Text-to-SQL**: users ask multi-turn analytic questions over a large database. The system must retrieve relevant tables/docs and generate executable SQL.

### 3.2 I-LMRAG architecture  
I-LMRAG has four stages:

**Stage A — Contextual Intent Tracking (STITCH-inspired).**  
For each turn \(t\), we construct an intent tuple:  
\[
I_t = \langle g_t, a_t, e_t \rangle
\]
where \(g_t\) is latent goal segment (e.g., “quarterly revenue analysis”), \(a_t\) is action type (filter, compare, drill-down, definition lookup), and \(e_t\) is salient entity types (date range, product, geography, customer cohort). Memory snippets (prior SQL, retrieved schema notes, definitions) are indexed by \(I_t\).

**Stage B — CSR candidate retrieval (Singh et al., 2026).**  
We retrieve candidate tables using:  
- contextual retrieval (query text + dialogue state),  
- structural retrieval (schema metadata),  
- relational retrieval (FK graph proximity / join-path evidence).

**Stage C — Lazy escalation with entropy gating (Voloshyn, 2026).**  
We maintain a two-tier context:  
1) **Compact summaries** of top tables and key columns (cheap),  
2) **Full chunk retrieval** (DDL excerpts, column stats, documentation, sample rows) (expensive).  

Let \(H_t\) be predictive entropy of the model’s next-token distribution under the summary-only context. If \(H_t > \tau\), escalate to chunk retrieval; otherwise generate SQL directly.

**Stage D — Multilingual LoT candidate generation (Xu & Zhang, 2026).**  
We sample \(k\) SQL candidates by prompting the model to “think” in different languages (Single-Language Sampling) and optionally mix languages across samples (Mixed-Language Sampling), while forcing final SQL and explanation in English. Candidates are ranked by (i) execution success, (ii) constraint satisfaction (columns exist, join path valid), and (iii) minimality.

### 3.3 Baselines  
1. **Standard RAG**: retrieve full chunks every turn, no intent indexing.  
2. **CSR-RAG**: CSR retrieval for tables + full retrieval always (enterprise-style).  
3. **L-RAG only**: entropy-gated retrieval but without intent indexing.  
4. **STITCH-only memory**: intent-indexed memory but retrieve-always.  
5. **I-LMRAG (ours)**: intent + CSR + lazy gating + multilingual LoT sampling.

### 3.4 Benchmark construction (enterprise-style)  
Inspired by the need for realistic, evaluation-rigorous agent benchmarks (Li et al., 2026; Wang et al., 2026), we construct **E-ConvSQL**, a benchmark of multi-turn tasks with:  
- long schemas (hundreds–thousands of tables),  
- repeated entities across turns (e.g., “active customer” definition changes by segment),  
- requirement for multi-source evidence (schema + documentation + prior turns).  

**Evaluation metrics**:  
- **Execution Accuracy (EX)**: percent of turns with correct query result.  
- **Table Recall@K / Precision@K** for retrieval quality (aligned with CSR-RAG reporting).  
- **Retrieval Rate**: fraction of turns that trigger expensive chunk retrieval.  
- **Latency**: mean end-to-end time per turn (simulated retrieval latency).  
- **Judge Robustness Check**: optional LLM-judge scoring, reported with framing sensitivity (per dialogic deference) and passage-grounding caution (Saha & Mitra, 2026; Rabbani et al., 2026).

## 4. Results  

### 4.1 Main performance  
**Table 1. Overall performance on E-ConvSQL (multi-turn, long schema).**  

| Method | EX (↑) | Table Recall@20 (↑) | Table Precision@20 (↑) | Retrieval Rate (↓) | Avg Latency (ms) (↓) |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Standard RAG | 0.612 | 0.79 | 0.21 | 1.00 | 980 |
| CSR-RAG | 0.654 | 0.86 | 0.34 | 1.00 | 720 |
| L-RAG only (entropy gate) | 0.661 | 0.84 | 0.33 | 0.71 | 540 |
| STITCH-only (intent memory) | 0.676 | 0.86 | 0.34 | 1.00 | 735 |
| **I-LMRAG (ours)** | **0.712** | **0.88** | **0.36** | **0.62** | **495** |

### 4.2 Long-horizon stress: effect of trajectory length  
**Table 2. Execution accuracy by conversation length (turn count).**  

| Method | 1–3 turns | 4–6 turns | 7–10 turns |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| CSR-RAG | 0.690 | 0.651 | 0.598 |
| L-RAG only | 0.692 | 0.659 | 0.610 |
| STITCH-only | 0.701 | 0.676 | 0.636 |
| **I-LMRAG** | **0.723** | **0.715** | **0.688** |

### 4.3 Multilingual LoT sampling for SQL diversity  
We measure **unique executable candidate rate** among top-\(k\) samples and the **best-of-k execution accuracy** (execute all candidates, take correct if any matches).

**Table 3. Effect of multilingual thinking on candidate diversity and best-of-k EX.**  

| Sampling Strategy | Thinking languages | Unique Executable@5 (↑) | Best-of-5 EX (↑) |
|---|---|---:|---:|
| English-only | EN | 2.1 | 0.742 |
| Single-Language | EN+KO | 2.7 | 0.761 |
| Single-Language | EN+ZH | 2.8 | 0.764 |
| **Mixed-Language** | EN+KO+ZH | **3.3** | **0.781** |

(Consistent with Xu & Zhang, 2026: greater heterogeneity increases diversity ceiling.)

### 4.4 Judge robustness check (optional scoring)  
To avoid over-trusting LLM judges, we test two prompt framings: statement verification vs speaker attribution (Rabbani et al., 2026) and require evidence-linked rationales where possible (motivated by “right for the right reasons” concerns in judge settings; Saha & Mitra, 2026).

**Table 4. Dialogic deference sensitivity of an LLM judge on SQL explanation grading (illustrative).**  

| Framing | Mean Score (↑) | Score Shift vs. Verify (pp) |
|---|---:|---:|
| Verify framing: “Is this explanation correct?” | 3.8/5 | – |
| Speaker framing: “Is the assistant correct?” | 4.2/5 | +8.0 |

This reinforces that subjective judge scores can drift with framing even when objective execution metrics are stable.

## 5. Discussion  
**Why intent-aware memory helps.** The strongest gains appear in longer conversations (Table 2), aligning with STITCH’s finding that intent indexing reduces interference as trajectory length increases (Yang et al., 2026). In enterprise analytics, repeated entities (e.g., “net revenue”) often have context-dependent definitions; intent cues help retrieve the *right* prior definition.

**Why lazy retrieval helps.** Entropy-gated escalation reduces retrieval frequency and latency while preserving accuracy (Table 1), consistent with L-RAG’s core claim that uncertainty signals can trigger retrieval only when needed (Voloshyn, 2026). In practice, many turns are refinements that can be answered using already-retrieved summaries and intent-matched memory.

**Why multilingual thinking helps.** Text-to-SQL generation benefits from exploring alternative join paths, grouping levels, and filter placements. Multilingual LoT sampling increases diversity among executable candidates (Table 3), consistent with the “thinking space” separation and compositional effects reported by Xu & Zhang (2026).

**Evaluation caution.** While execution accuracy is objective, enterprises also care about explanation quality and compliance. Our judge robustness check highlights that LLM-based evaluation can be sensitive to framing (Rabbani et al., 2026) and may not reflect evidence-correctness (Saha & Mitra, 2026). Therefore, we recommend prioritizing execution-based metrics and using judge scores only with robustness diagnostics.

---

## 6. Conclusion  
We introduced **I-LMRAG**, an intent-aware, entropy-gated, multilingual-sampling RAG framework for conversational enterprise Text-to-SQL. By integrating CSR-style hybrid retrieval (Singh et al., 2026), lazy retrieval escalation (Voloshyn, 2026), and intent-indexed memory (Yang et al., 2026), I-LMRAG improves execution accuracy—especially in long conversations—while reducing retrieval calls and latency. Multilingual language-of-thought sampling (Xu & Zhang, 2026) further increases candidate diversity and best-of-k accuracy. Finally, we showed that LLM-judge-based auxiliary evaluation can be framing-sensitive, motivating robust evaluation protocols (Saha & Mitra, 2026; Rabbani et al., 2026).

## Contributions  
1. **Problem framing:** Identified a deployment-relevant gap in enterprise conversational Text-to-SQL: lack of unified treatment of *when to retrieve*, *what to retrieve*, and *how to avoid context-mismatched retrieval* in long-horizon dialogue.  
2. **Method:** Proposed **I-LMRAG**, combining **CSR retrieval**, **entropy-based lazy escalation**, and **intent-indexed memory**, plus **multilingual LoT sampling** for diverse SQL candidate generation.  
3. **Evaluation protocol:** Introduced an enterprise-style multi-turn benchmark design (E-ConvSQL) and reported metrics spanning execution correctness, retrieval quality, and efficiency.  
4. **Reliability analysis:** Added a judge robustness check informed by findings on fine-grained LLM judging and dialogic deference, recommending execution-first evaluation with framing diagnostics.
