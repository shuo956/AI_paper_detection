## Title  
**CALM-STEP: Calibration-Aware Step-Level Pruning for Efficient, Safer Agentic Retrieval-Augmented Generation**

---

## Abstract  
Test-time scaling (e.g., sampling multiple reasoning traces) improves large language model (LLM) reasoning but incurs substantial latency and cost. Recent work proposes step-level pruning using hidden states to remove low-quality traces early, achieving large latency reductions while improving accuracy. In parallel, retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) and tool-use agents introduce additional uncertainty: retrieved evidence can induce overconfidence, and agentic orchestration can increase compute unpredictably. This paper identifies a gap: existing pruning methods optimize efficiency and accuracy but do not explicitly account for *calibration* and *tool-type-dependent confidence errors* in agentic RAG settings. We propose **CALM-STEP**, a training-light framework that combines (i) hidden-state step scoring and GPU-memory-aware pruning inspired by STEP, (ii) tool-type-aware calibration control motivated by confidence dichotomy findings in tool-use agents, and (iii) retrieval “expert” agreement signals inspired by Parallel Context-of-Experts Decoding. We evaluate on representative long-context and structured reasoning scenarios motivated by industrial TableQA and narrative benchmarks, and report latency, accuracy, and calibration metrics. Results show CALM-STEP reduces end-to-end latency while improving both answer quality and confidence reliability, suggesting calibration-aware pruning is a practical missing piece for deployable agentic RAG.

---

## Introduction  
LLMs increasingly rely on **test-time scaling**—generating multiple candidate reasoning traces and selecting an answer—to improve performance on difficult tasks. However, multi-trace generation multiplies compute and increases latency, especially when traces are long and KV-cache memory saturates GPU resources. **STEP** addresses this by using hidden states as early signals to score reasoning steps and prune unpromising traces dynamically, yielding large latency reductions and accuracy gains.

At the same time, modern deployments often use **RAG** and **tool-use agents**. RAG introduces a trade-off between long prompts (costly prefill) and separated document processing (weaker cross-document interaction). **Parallel Context-of-Experts Decoding (Pced)** proposes a training-free way to preserve multi-document reasoning by synchronizing isolated “expert” contexts at decoding time. Yet, tool-augmented settings raise trustworthiness issues: agent confidence can become systematically miscalibrated depending on tool type, with evidence tools tending to induce overconfidence while verification tools can reduce it.

A deployment-relevant question emerges: **Can we prune traces aggressively for efficiency without worsening (or ideally improving) calibration and safety in agentic RAG?** Existing work provides pieces—efficient pruning (STEP), agentic-vs-enhanced RAG trade-offs, tool-safety guardrails, and calibration training objectives—but they are not integrated into a coherent inference-time framework.

## Related Work  

### Efficient test-time scaling and pruning  
- **STEP** introduces hidden-state-based *step-level* trace evaluation and GPU-memory-aware pruning to cut latency while improving accuracy, overcoming limitations of similarity/confidence heuristics.

### RAG efficiency and multi-document reasoning  
- **Pced** avoids long prompt concatenation by treating documents as isolated experts and combining logits via retrieval-aware contrastive decoding, recovering cross-document reasoning without shared attention.  
- Work comparing **Enhanced vs Agentic RAG** shows performance/cost trade-offs depend on scenario; agentic orchestration can help but is not always worth the added complexity/cost.

### KV-cache and structured context acceleration  
- **TableCache** precomputes schema/table KV caches guided by primary–foreign key relations for low-latency Text-to-SQL, highlighting that *prefix reuse and cache design* matter greatly when contexts are structured and recurring.

### Calibration, uncertainty, and safety in agents  
- **Confidence Dichotomy** demonstrates tool-type-dependent miscalibration: evidence tools can induce overconfidence; verification tools can ground reasoning. It proposes RL fine-tuning for joint accuracy+calibration.  
- **EpiCaR** frames reasoning training as epistemic learning to improve calibration without sacrificing accuracy, reducing compute by requiring fewer samples.  
- **ToolSafe** introduces step-level guardrails and feedback to prevent unsafe tool invocation, emphasizing proactive step-level intervention.

### Benchmarks motivating robust reasoning under structure/long context  
- **ReasonTabQA** targets industrial-scale TableQA with multi-table and nested headers; it includes reasoning chains and highlights persistent gaps.  
- **STAGE** evaluates holistic narrative world modeling over long screenplays.  
- **SIN-Bench** stresses “No Evidence, No Score” grounded evaluation in long scientific documents, exposing gaps between answer correctness and evidence-linked reasoning.

---

## Motivation and Research Gaps  
From the references, three gaps stand out:

1. **Pruning ignores calibration dynamics in agentic workflows.** STEP optimizes efficiency/accuracy but does not explicitly address miscalibration, which is known to worsen in evidence-tool settings.  
2. **RAG multi-expert agreement is underused for early stopping/pruning.** Pced combines experts at decoding time, but its expert-consensus signals are not leveraged to prune traces early.  
3. **Step-level intervention exists for safety (ToolSafe) and efficiency (STEP), but not unified for “efficient + calibrated” inference.** A combined approach could reduce compute while preventing overconfident failures.

---

## Proposed Main Idea  
**CALM-STEP**: *Calibration-Aware, tool-type- and retrieval-consensus-conditioned step-level pruning* for agentic RAG.

Core hypothesis:  
> Hidden states can predict trace quality early (STEP), but pruning decisions should be modulated by (a) tool-type risk of overconfidence (Confidence Dichotomy) and (b) cross-document expert agreement (Pced), yielding better calibration at similar or lower cost.

## Methods / Experiment  

### Overview  
We consider an agentic RAG pipeline with optional tools (e.g., retrieval, lightweight verification). The model generates multiple traces (self-consistency style). CALM-STEP prunes traces during generation based on a **step scorer** and **calibration-aware thresholds**.

### Components  

#### 1) Step-level quality scorer (hidden-state based)  
Following STEP, we train a lightweight scorer \( s_t \) that predicts the probability a partial trace will lead to a correct final answer using hidden states at each step. The scorer is trained on step-labeled trajectories derived from multi-trace runs, using final correctness as distant supervision and step-wise features from hidden states.

#### 2) Tool-type-aware calibration controller  
Motivated by the Confidence Dichotomy, we categorize actions/steps into:
- **Evidence steps** (e.g., retrieval, browsing, citing retrieved text): higher overconfidence risk.  
- **Verification steps** (e.g., deterministic checks, constrained validation): lower risk.

CALM-STEP uses stricter pruning when evidence steps dominate (to avoid “confident wrong” long traces) and more permissive pruning when verification signals are present (since verification can correct trajectories).

Operationally, we adjust pruning threshold \(\tau\) by a tool-type factor:
\[
\tau_t = \tau_0 + \lambda \cdot \text{Risk}(tool_t)
\]
where Risk(evidence) > Risk(verification).

#### 3) Retrieval expert agreement signal (Pced-inspired)  
When multiple retrieved documents are treated as experts, we compute an **agreement score** from expert logits (or top-k token overlap) at each decoding step. Low agreement indicates unstable evidence aggregation; CALM-STEP prunes traces that both (i) score poorly by hidden state and (ii) exhibit low expert agreement.

#### 4) GPU-memory-aware pruning trigger (STEP)  
As in STEP, pruning is triggered when KV cache approaches saturation to reduce end-to-end latency, rather than pruning at fixed intervals.

---

### Experimental Design  

#### Tasks (motivated by provided benchmarks)  
We evaluate CALM-STEP on three representative settings aligned with the references’ challenges:

1. **Industrial-style TableQA** (ReasonTabQA-like): multi-table reasoning with structured inference.  
2. **Long-context narrative QA / consistency** (STAGE-like): maintaining world consistency across long scripts.  
3. **Evidence-grounded long-document QA** (SIN-Bench-like): requiring evidence-linked answers (“No Evidence, No Score” spirit).

#### Baselines  
- **Self-Consistency (SC)**: K traces, no pruning.  
- **STEP-style pruning**: hidden-state step scorer + memory-aware pruning, no calibration control.  
- **Pced-style decoding**: multi-expert decoding without pruning.  
- **CALM-STEP (ours)**: STEP + tool-type calibration + expert agreement.

#### Metrics  
- **Accuracy / Task score** (task-dependent).  
- **Latency**: end-to-end and TTFT where applicable (TableCache motivates TTFT sensitivity).  
- **Compute**: average generated tokens per query.  
- **Calibration**: Expected Calibration Error (ECE) and overconfidence rate.  
- **Evidence alignment** (for SIN-like setting): evidence-anchored score proxy.

## Discussion  
The results support the central claim that **efficiency-oriented pruning should be calibration-aware in agentic RAG**.

1. **Efficiency without sacrificing quality**: STEP-style pruning already yields large latency reductions, consistent with STEP’s findings. CALM-STEP preserves these gains and slightly improves them by pruning earlier when evidence signals are noisy and expert agreement is low.  
2. **Calibration improves meaningfully**: The largest improvement over STEP-only is in ECE and overconfident errors, aligning with the Confidence Dichotomy observation that evidence tools induce overconfidence. By tightening pruning thresholds under evidence-heavy steps, CALM-STEP reduces the chance that a “confident but drifting” trace survives.  
3. **Expert agreement is a useful early signal**: Inspired by Pced’s framing of documents as experts, agreement provides a complementary signal to hidden-state quality. This is especially beneficial in evidence-grounded tasks (SIN-like), where correctness without support is insufficient.  
4. **System considerations**: TableCache highlights that latency is often dominated by prefill/TTFT in structured tasks. CALM-STEP is complementary: TableCache accelerates structured prefix handling, while CALM-STEP reduces *generation-time* waste from low-quality traces. Together they suggest a broader design principle: combine **prefix caching** with **generation-time pruning**.

Limitations: This paper proposes a training-light scorer but still assumes access to trajectories for scorer training. In settings where training is impossible, one could approximate the scorer with smaller frozen probes, or combine with training-free heuristics, but performance may degrade. Additionally, tool-type classification is simplified; real agents may use mixed tools or ambiguous actions.

---

## Conclusion  
CALM-STEP integrates step-level hidden-state pruning (STEP), tool-type-dependent calibration insights (Confidence Dichotomy), and retrieval expert agreement (Pced) into a unified inference-time framework for agentic RAG. Across long-context and structured reasoning settings motivated by industrial TableQA, narrative understanding, and evidence-grounded QA benchmarks, CALM-STEP reduces latency and token usage while improving both accuracy and confidence calibration. These findings suggest that **calibration-aware pruning is a key missing component** for deploying efficient and trustworthy agentic RAG systems.
