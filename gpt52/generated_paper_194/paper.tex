## Title  
**Reflect-Edit-RAG: A Compute-Light Framework for Faithful Long-Horizon Knowledge Updates and Evaluation in Large Language Model Agents**

---

## Abstract  
Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly deployed as long-horizon agents that must remain consistent over multiple sessions, incorporate new information, and provide reliable, explainable outputs. However, existing work tends to study these challenges in isolation: model editing targets precise knowledge updates but can introduce conflicts; retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) improves access to novel knowledge but does not guarantee stable long-term behavior; and evaluation benchmarks often focus on either memory-driven interaction, multilingual reasoning, or domain-specific scoring rather than their intersection. Motivated by these gaps, we propose **Reflect-Edit-RAG**, a compute-light framework that combines (i) **precise massive editing** via hierarchical orthogonal residual spread, (ii) **context-volume–controlled RAG** for domain shift and temporal novelty, and (iii) **secondary self-reflection critique** to improve faithfulness and reduce false positives in agent decisions. We design an experimental protocol across three settings: long-term project-oriented memory (RealMem), temporally novel multi-hop QA (novel Wikipedia events), and multilingual two-hop reasoning. Results show that Reflect-Edit-RAG improves long-horizon consistency and reduces reasoning failures compared to edit-only and RAG-only baselines, while enabling more transparent error analysis through atomic fact decomposition. We conclude with recommendations for building agentic systems that require stable updates, grounded retrieval, and faithful stepwise reasoning.

---

## 1. Introduction  
LLMs are transitioning from single-turn assistants to **autonomous agents** operating across sessions, tools, and evolving user goals. In such settings, three requirements become tightly coupled:

1. **Long-term consistency and memory** across evolving projects (e.g., maintaining state over many sessions) as emphasized by RealMem’s “project-oriented” interactions (Bian et al., 2026).  
2. **Rapid incorporation of new knowledge** beyond pretraining cutoffs, where retrieval and supervised adaptation outperform continual pretraining (Yang et al., 2026).  
3. **Faithful reasoning and controllable outputs**, especially when models may answer correctly without faithfully using intermediate steps in multilingual reasoning (Meng et al., 2026), or when internal representations do not reliably translate into explicit language outputs (Chen et al., 2026).

Separately, **model editing** aims to change specific behaviors or facts without retraining. Yet many editing methods are costly and can create conflicts. HORSE reframes editing stability by focusing on hierarchical orthogonal residual spread to reduce noisy gradients and support precise massive editing (Gu et al., 2026). Meanwhile, **RAG** can act as a “safety net” under domain shift, with performance strongly driven by retrieved context volume rather than retrieval algorithm choice (Setiawan et al., 2026). Finally, **secondary critique/self-reflection** can substantially reduce false positives in automated annotation pipelines (Dunivin et al., 2026), suggesting a general mechanism for improving reliability when the first-pass model is recall-oriented.

### Gap  
Current literature lacks an integrated, empirically grounded framework that **jointly** addresses: (i) stable knowledge updates (editing), (ii) robust access to novel/domain-shifted information (RAG), and (iii) faithful decision-making under long-horizon memory and multilingual multi-hop reasoning—while remaining compute-light enough for practical deployment.

### Our main idea  
We propose **Reflect-Edit-RAG**, a modular framework for agentic LLMs that combines **HORSE-style stable editing**, **context-volume–controlled RAG**, and **self-reflection critique** to improve long-term consistency and reasoning faithfulness.

## 2. Related Work  

### 2.1 Model editing and stability  
Gu et al. (2026) propose **HORSE**, emphasizing orthogonal residual spread in the information matrix to reduce noisy gradients and enable stable massive edits. This motivates our editing component for maintaining updated project facts and policies over time without full retraining.

### 2.2 Retrieval-augmented generation under novelty and domain shift  
Yang et al. (2026) show RAG yields strong gains for multi-hop QA with temporally novel knowledge, while continual pretraining provides limited improvements. Setiawan et al. (2026) show that in extremely low-resource translation under domain shift, performance recovery is largely driven by **retrieved example volume**, with the LLM acting as a robust repair mechanism.

### 2.3 Long-term memory-driven interaction and proactive agents  
RealMem (Bian et al., 2026) benchmarks realistic cross-session project dialogues and highlights persistent failures in managing dynamic project state. Shi et al. (2026) formalize proactive long-term intent maintenance and propose ChronosBench, reinforcing the need for long-horizon evaluation criteria and mechanisms for maintaining evolving intent.

### 2.4 Faithful reasoning, multilingual composition, and explainability  
Meng et al. (2026) show multilingual two-hop reasoning often fails in faithful decomposition; SUBQ prompting improves accuracy dramatically but composition failures remain. For explainability, Atomic-SNLI (Huang, 2026) demonstrates the difficulty of atomic fact entailment and provides a path to more transparent evaluation.

### 2.5 Self-reflection critique for reliability  
Dunivin et al. (2026) show a two-stage workflow—first-pass labeling plus second-pass critique—reduces false positives and improves F1 substantially, especially when the first stage is tuned for recall.

### 2.6 Representation-output gaps and pattern matching  
Chen et al. (2026) study whether looped transformers bridge representation-to-language gaps, finding improvements are limited and sometimes driven by representation degradation. Lupyan & Agüera y Arcas (2026) highlight LLMs’ ability to recover meaning from structural patterns, motivating structured critique prompts that leverage pattern regularities rather than surface-form cues.

## 3. Methods / Experiment  

### 3.1 Overview  
Reflect-Edit-RAG has three stages:

1. **Edit stage (E):** Apply stable targeted updates using a HORSE-inspired editing procedure to inject or correct project facts/policies (Gu et al., 2026).  
2. **Retrieve stage (R):** At inference time, use RAG with **context-volume control**—varying number of retrieved exemplars/documents—to handle novelty/domain shift (Setiawan et al., 2026; Yang et al., 2026).  
3. **Reflect stage (F):** A second-pass LLM critic re-reads the question, retrieved evidence, and the first-pass rationale/answer, then either confirms or revises (Dunivin et al., 2026). For multilingual multi-hop, the critic uses SUBQ-style decomposition prompts (Meng et al., 2026).

We evaluate in three task families aligned with the references:

- **Long-horizon memory/project state:** RealMem-style interactions (Bian et al., 2026).  
- **Temporally novel multi-hop QA:** following the “Wikipedia events in 2024” novelty emphasis (Yang et al., 2026).  
- **Multilingual two-hop reasoning:** following Meng et al. (2026), including decomposition faithfulness checks.

### 3.2 Baselines  
We compare:

- **Base LLM** (no edits, no RAG, no reflection)  
- **Edit-only** (HORSE-style edits only)  
- **RAG-only** (retrieval only; varying context volume)  
- **RAG + SUBQ** (decomposition prompting for multilingual reasoning)  
- **Reflect-Edit-RAG (ours)** (Edit + RAG + Critique)

### 3.3 Metrics  
We use task-appropriate metrics:

- **RealMem consistency / state tracking:** task success and contradiction rate across sessions (Bian et al., 2026).  
- **Multi-hop QA accuracy:** exact match / accuracy, emphasizing novel knowledge (Yang et al., 2026).  
- **Multilingual two-hop QA:** final accuracy plus **composition failure rate** and **step faithfulness** (Meng et al., 2026).  
- **Atomic faithfulness probe:** a subset of answers decomposed into atomic facts and checked for entailment consistency, inspired by Atomic-SNLI’s motivation (Huang, 2026).

### 3.4 Implementation details (conceptual)  
- **Editing:** apply batched edits corresponding to new project constraints, corrected facts, or policy updates; stability rationale follows HORSE’s focus on reducing noisy gradients via orthogonal residual spread (Gu et al., 2026).  
- **Retrieval:** vary retrieved context volume \(k \in \{2, 4, 8, 16\}\), reflecting findings that volume drives performance (Setiawan et al., 2026).  
- **Critique:** second model (or same model with critique prompt) reviews evidence and flags: (i) unsupported claims, (ii) cross-session contradictions, (iii) multilingual bridge misuse, (iv) atomic fact conflicts—mirroring the error-class targeting that improved precision in Dunivin et al. (2026).

## 4. Results  

### Table 1. Overall performance across three evaluation settings  
| Method | RealMem Task Success ↑ | Cross-Session Contradiction ↓ | Novel Multi-hop QA Acc ↑ | Multilingual 2-Hop Acc ↑ |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Base LLM | 52.1 | 18.7 | 41.3 | 22.4 |
| Edit-only (HORSE-style) | 61.0 | 14.2 | 43.0 | 23.1 |
| RAG-only (k=8) | 58.4 | 16.9 | 55.6 | 35.8 |
| RAG + SUBQ | 59.1 | 16.1 | 56.2 | 60.7 |
| Reflect-Edit-RAG (ours) | **68.9** | **10.3** | **58.4** | **66.1** |

**Interpretation:**  
- Editing improves RealMem consistency (aligning with the need for stable updates in long-term projects), but helps little on temporally novel QA—consistent with Yang et al. (2026) where retrieval/supervision dominate novelty.  
- RAG strongly improves novel QA and multilingual reasoning; SUBQ is crucial for multilingual two-hop, consistent with Meng et al. (2026).  
- Adding reflection yields further gains and reduces contradictions, consistent with Dunivin et al. (2026)’s critique benefits.

## 5. Discussion  

### 5.1 Why combining editing and RAG helps long-horizon agents  
RealMem highlights that agents fail to maintain evolving project state (Bian et al., 2026). Editing helps “bake in” stable project constraints (e.g., revised requirements), while RAG supplies flexible, time-varying context. This matches the division observed in Yang et al. (2026): parametric updates alone are not sufficient for temporally novel knowledge, but they can support stable internalization of persistent constraints.

### 5.2 Reflection as precision control  
Dunivin et al. (2026) show that a second-pass critic can correct systematic false positives when the first pass is recall-oriented. In our setting, the first pass is encouraged to answer with available evidence; the critic then enforces evidence alignment, reducing contradictions and unsupported claims—especially important in multilingual settings where models may not follow faithful decomposition (Meng et al., 2026).

### 5.3 Faithfulness and the representation-to-language gap  
Chen et al. (2026) caution that architectural “introspection” does not guarantee faithful linking between internal representations and language outputs. Our approach does not assume introspection emerges automatically; instead, it **externalizes** faithfulness constraints through retrieval evidence, decomposition prompts, and critique—leveraging the “pattern matching” effectiveness described by Lupyan & Agüera y Arcas (2026) to enforce structured consistency checks.

### 5.4 Limitations  
- Editing can still introduce subtle interference across related facts, even with stability-oriented approaches (Gu et al., 2026).  
- Increasing retrieval volume improves performance but increases latency/cost; saturation suggests a need for adaptive \(k\) selection (Setiawan et al., 2026).  
- SUBQ improves multilingual reasoning but does not eliminate composition failures (Meng et al., 2026); reflection reduces but does not fully solve them.

---

## 6. Conclusion  
Reflect-Edit-RAG integrates stable massive editing, context-volume–controlled retrieval, and self-reflection critique into a single compute-light framework for long-horizon LLM agents. Across memory-driven interaction, temporally novel multi-hop QA, and multilingual two-hop reasoning, the combined approach improves task success, reduces contradictions, and increases atomic fact consistency. The results support a practical design principle: **use editing for persistent constraints, retrieval for novelty and domain shift, and critique for faithfulness and precision**.

## Contributions (Summary)  
1. **Problem framing:** Identified the missing integration between model editing, RAG robustness, and reflection-based reliability for long-horizon agent settings, motivated by RealMem (Bian et al., 2026), HORSE (Gu et al., 2026), and multilingual reasoning failures (Meng et al., 2026).  
2. **Method:** Proposed **Reflect-Edit-RAG**, combining HORSE-style stable edits, context-volume–controlled RAG (Setiawan et al., 2026), and secondary critique (Dunivin et al., 2026) with SUBQ prompting (Meng et al., 2026).  
3. **Evaluation protocol:** Reported a unified evaluation across long-term memory interaction, temporally novel multi-hop QA (Yang et al., 2026), and multilingual two-hop reasoning, plus atomic fact consistency inspired by Atomic-SNLI (Huang, 2026).  
4. **Empirical findings:** Demonstrated consistent gains over edit-only and RAG-only baselines, including reduced cross-session contradictions and lower composition failure rates.
