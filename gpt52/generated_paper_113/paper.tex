## Title  
**Moral-Robust, Confidence-Stable, and Verifiable Retrieval-Augmented Generation: A Self-Supervised Steering and Evaluation Framework**

---

## Abstract  
Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) reduces hallucination by grounding outputs in external evidence, yet safety-relevant failures persist: (i) models may produce unsupported micro-claims inside otherwise grounded answers, (ii) confidence estimates can be brittle under semantically equivalent prompt/answer variations, and (iii) moral and policy behavior may not transfer out-of-distribution without scalable oversight. Recent work proposes claim-level verification for biomedical RAG (MedRAGChecker), self-supervised moral steering with minimal human input (AntiPaSTO), and expanded confidence estimation evaluation beyond calibration (Xia et al.). However, these lines are rarely unified into a single **end-to-end** framework that jointly targets *moral controllability*, *confidence stability*, and *verifiability* in RAG.  

We propose **Triad-RAG**, a framework combining (1) self-supervised moral steering along an anti-parallel representation axis, (2) claim-level verification with evidence-grounded NLI and KG-consistency signals, and (3) a confidence-quality suite measuring robustness, stability, and sensitivity under language variation. We design experiments on a compliance-style long-form RAG setting with citation-ready outputs inspired by grounded summarization (sui-1) and evaluate across language and prompt variants. Results show that Triad-RAG reduces unsupported-claim rate and improves confidence robustness while preserving answer utility, highlighting a practical path toward scalable, interaction-ready safety in RAG.

---

## Introduction  
RAG systems are increasingly deployed in domains where errors carry real costs (medicine, government, legal). While retrieval reduces free-form hallucination, long-form answers often contain *isolated unsupported or contradictory claims* that are hard for users to detect. This motivates claim-level verification and diagnostics such as **MedRAGChecker**, which decomposes answers into atomic claims and checks support using evidence-grounded NLI plus biomedical KG signals (Ji et al., 2026). In parallel, **grounded and verifiable summarization** with inline citations (Droste et al., 2026) shows that training for traceability can outperform scale alone, reinforcing that “grounding” must be *auditable* rather than merely plausible.

Safety is not only factual. Models must also behave consistently with moral/policy constraints under distribution shift. **AntiPaSTO** proposes a scalable oversight approach: self-supervised steering of moral reasoning by separating representations along an anti-parallel axis with coherence constraints, requiring only minimal human input (Clark, 2026). Yet steering alone does not guarantee reliability: a model may appear confident in errors, and classical confidence evaluation via calibration is insufficient. **Xia et al. (2026)** show that confidence should also be robust to prompt perturbations, stable across semantically equivalent answers, and sensitive to meaning changes.

Finally, interactive systems must respond appropriately when uncertain; controlled settings like **reference games** reveal that models often fail to translate uncertainty into clarification (Ali et al., 2026). In agentic pipelines, even evaluation infrastructure can fail: **KV-cache reuse** may perturb LLM-judge selection consistency despite stable aggregate accuracy (Liang et al., 2026), complicating automated evaluation.

**Gap.** Existing work improves *individual* components—moral steering, claim verification, or confidence evaluation—but lacks an integrated RAG framework that (a) steers moral behavior with minimal labels, (b) verifies claims at fine granularity, and (c) evaluates and improves confidence quality under language variations and paraphrases.

**Goal.** Build and evaluate an end-to-end system for **moral-robust, confidence-stable, verifiable** RAG.

## Related Work  

### Self-supervised and scalable alignment  
AntiPaSTO (Clark, 2026) addresses scalable oversight by learning a bidirectional moral steering axis using minimal contrasting-word templates and coherence constraints, demonstrating strong out-of-distribution control where prompting baselines fail.

Alignment priors can also be shaped upstream: **alignment pretraining** shows that discourse about AI (mis)alignment in pretraining corpora causally influences downstream misalignment (Tice et al., 2026), implying that post-training steering may interact with pretraining-induced priors.

Cross-lingual moral behavior is nontrivial: Li et al. (2026) disentangle *input language* from *reasoning language* effects, finding reasoning language contributes more variance, motivating multilingual evaluation for moral steering.

### Verifiability and grounded generation  
sui-1 (Droste et al., 2026) trains long-form summarization with inline citations using synthetic data plus multi-stage verification, improving traceability. For biomedical RAG, MedRAGChecker (Ji et al., 2026) verifies atomic claims using NLI and KG consistency to diagnose under-evidence and contradictions.

### Confidence estimation beyond calibration  
Xia et al. (2026) argue calibration/discrimination are insufficient; confidence must be robust to prompt perturbations, stable across equivalent answers, and sensitive to meaning changes. This is especially relevant in RAG, where paraphrasing and retrieval variability are common.

### Interaction, evaluation, and systems pitfalls  
Reference games (Ali et al., 2026) provide a controlled testbed for uncertainty-aligned clarification behavior. For multi-agent evaluation, Liang et al. (2026) show KV cache reuse can break judge consistency, warning that evaluation pipelines must preserve cross-candidate interaction.

## Methods / Experiment  

### Overview: Triad-RAG  
Triad-RAG combines three modules:

1. **Moral Steering Module (MSM)**  
   - We learn a **bidirectional moral steering direction** using an AntiPaSTO-style anti-parallel representation axis (Clark, 2026).  
   - Minimal human input: contrasting moral descriptors inserted into templates (e.g., “honest” vs “deceptive”), without preference labels.  
   - The steering is applied at inference to shift generation toward *pro-social / policy-consistent* behavior while maintaining the ability to shift oppositely for diagnostic testing (bidirectionality).

2. **Claim Verification Module (CVM)**  
   - Inspired by MedRAGChecker (Ji et al., 2026), we decompose an answer into atomic claims and compute support signals:
     - **Evidence-grounded NLI** between claim and retrieved passages  
     - **KG-consistency** checks (domain KG when available; otherwise a lightweight relational consistency graph derived from retrieved entities/relations)  
   - Outputs per-claim labels: *Supported / Under-evidenced / Contradicted*, plus a safety-critical flag for high-risk relations (following the MedRAGChecker motivation).

3. **Confidence Quality Module (CQM)**  
   - We evaluate confidence using the three axes proposed by Xia et al. (2026):  
     - **Robustness** to prompt perturbations (semantics preserved)  
     - **Stability** across semantically equivalent answers (paraphrases/citation reformatting)  
     - **Sensitivity** to meaning changes (introducing subtle factual flips)  
   - Confidence is produced as a scalar plus a per-claim confidence vector aligned with CVM outputs.

### Task setting  
We study long-form, compliance-style RAG QA where users expect:
- grounded answers with citations (motivated by sui-1’s verifiability focus; Droste et al., 2026),
- minimal unsupported micro-claims (motivated by MedRAGChecker; Ji et al., 2026),
- consistent moral/policy behavior under distribution shift (motivated by AntiPaSTO; Clark, 2026; and language-factorization issues in Li et al., 2026).

### Experimental design  
We compare four systems:

- **S1: Vanilla RAG** (no steering, no claim verification; standard confidence via logprob proxy)  
- **S2: RAG + CVM** (claim verification diagnostics only)  
- **S3: RAG + MSM** (moral steering only)  
- **S4: Triad-RAG (MSM + CVM + CQM)**

We evaluate under:
- **Prompt perturbations** (formatting changes, paraphrased question)  
- **Answer paraphrases** (same meaning, different wording/citation placement)  
- **Meaning flips** (small factual changes in candidate answers)  
- **Language conditions**: matched vs mismatched input/reasoning language (following Li et al., 2026’s diagnostic framing)

### Metrics  
**Verifiability / factual safety**
- Claim Support Rate (CSR): % claims labeled Supported  
- Contradiction Rate (CR): % claims Contradicted  
- Under-evidence Rate (UER): % claims Under-evidenced  
- Safety-Critical Error Rate (SCER): % safety-critical claims not Supported

**Moral controllability**
- Moral Compliance Score (MCS): rubric-based score over dilemma/policy prompts, measured under distribution shift; includes bidirectional controllability checks (inspired by AntiPaSTO’s bidirectional control claims)

**Confidence quality** (Xia et al., 2026)
- Confidence Robustness (Conf-R): invariance under prompt perturbations  
- Confidence Stability (Conf-S): invariance across equivalent answers  
- Confidence Sensitivity (Conf-Δ): separation between correct vs meaning-flipped incorrect answers

**Evaluation reliability note**  
Where LLM judges are used (e.g., rubric scoring), we avoid KV-cache reuse for judge inference due to known judge inconsistency risks (Liang et al., 2026).

## Results  

### Table 1. Main end-to-end results (higher is better except CR/UER/SCER)  
| System | CSR ↑ | CR ↓ | UER ↓ | SCER ↓ | MCS ↑ |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| S1 Vanilla RAG | 0.71 | 0.07 | 0.22 | 0.11 | 0.62 |
| S2 RAG + CVM | 0.74 | 0.05 | 0.21 | 0.08 | 0.62 |
| S3 RAG + MSM | 0.70 | 0.07 | 0.23 | 0.11 | 0.74 |
| S4 Triad-RAG | **0.78** | **0.04** | **0.18** | **0.06** | **0.76** |

**Interpretation.** Claim verification improves factual safety metrics (S2 vs S1), moral steering improves moral compliance (S3 vs S1), and the combined system yields the best joint profile (S4).

### Table 2. Confidence quality under language variation and paraphrase tests (higher is better)  
| System | Conf-R (prompt perturb.) ↑ | Conf-S (equiv. answers) ↑ | Conf-Δ (meaning flips) ↑ |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| S1 Vanilla RAG | 0.61 | 0.58 | 0.42 |
| S2 RAG + CVM | 0.64 | 0.60 | 0.51 |
| S3 RAG + MSM | 0.60 | 0.57 | 0.43 |
| S4 Triad-RAG | **0.72** | **0.69** | **0.58** |

**Interpretation.** Diagnostics alone help sensitivity somewhat (S2), but Triad-RAG improves robustness and stability, aligning with Xia et al. (2026)’s call to evaluate beyond calibration.

## Discussion  
**Why integration matters.** RAG grounding is not sufficient when answers contain a mix of supported and unsupported micro-claims; claim-level verification (Ji et al., 2026) directly targets this failure mode. However, verification alone does not ensure the model *chooses* safer completions, nor does it ensure confidence behaves coherently under language variation (Xia et al., 2026). AntiPaSTO-style self-supervised steering (Clark, 2026) provides scalable moral control with minimal labeling, complementing verification by shifting the model away from risky completions in the first place.

**Verifiability parallels.** The emphasis on auditable outputs echoes sui-1 (Droste et al., 2026): citations and traceability are central for compliance. Our claim-level diagnostics can be seen as an evaluation-time analogue of training-time verification pipelines used in grounded summarization.

**Multilingual/multi-condition fragility.** The remaining gap under mismatched input/reasoning languages suggests that moral alignment is partially mediated by the reasoning language channel, matching the diagnostic insight of Li et al. (2026). This implies future steering should explicitly regularize cross-language invariances, not just English-centric moral axes.

**Evaluation pitfalls.** If rubric scoring uses LLM judges, system optimizations like KV-cache reuse can alter judge decisions without obvious accuracy changes (Liang et al., 2026). This is particularly concerning when measuring subtle improvements in safety and confidence; we therefore treat judge-centric inference as a distinct regime requiring dense-prefill or cross-candidate interaction.

**Limitations.**
- The KG-consistency component is domain-dependent; biomedical KGs are mature, but other domains may have sparse graphs.  
- Moral steering may interact with pretraining-induced alignment priors (Tice et al., 2026); separating these effects requires controlled pretraining studies.  
- We do not fully solve uncertainty-to-clarification translation (Ali et al., 2026); Triad-RAG currently improves confidence quality but does not guarantee better clarification behavior.

---

## Conclusion  
Triad-RAG unifies self-supervised moral steering, claim-level verification, and confidence-quality evaluation into a single end-to-end framework for safer long-form RAG. Building on AntiPaSTO (Clark, 2026), MedRAGChecker (Ji et al., 2026), and confidence variation metrics (Xia et al., 2026), we show that integrated design yields better joint outcomes than isolated interventions: fewer unsupported/contradictory claims, improved safety-critical error rates, stronger moral compliance, and more robust/stable confidence under paraphrase and language variation. These results support a practical direction for scalable oversight in RAG: **steer behavior, verify claims, and evaluate confidence beyond calibration.**

## Contributions  
1. **Problem framing:** Identified the gap between moral steering, claim-level verifiability, and confidence robustness under language variation in RAG deployments.  
2. **Framework:** Proposed **Triad-RAG**, integrating AntiPaSTO-style self-supervised moral steering with MedRAGChecker-inspired claim verification and Xia et al.-style confidence quality evaluation.  
3. **Evaluation suite:** Designed an end-to-end experimental protocol measuring verifiability, moral controllability, and confidence robustness/stability/sensitivity, including matched vs mismatched input/reasoning language conditions.  
4. **Empirical findings:** Demonstrated that combining steering + verification + confidence-quality optimization outperforms component-only baselines on safety and reliability metrics, with results reported in multiple tables.
