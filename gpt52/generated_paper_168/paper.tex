## Title  
**Shift-Robust, Interpretable Multimodal Precision Psychiatry: Invariance Learning with Missing Outcomes and Conformal Uncertainty for Urinary Metabolomics–Clinical Time Series ADHD Screening**

---

## Abstract  
Objective diagnosis in psychiatry remains limited, motivating biology-based and robust machine-learning frameworks. Recent work shows urinary metabolomics combined with interpretable learning can diagnose ADHD with high accuracy using a small metabolite panel, but robustness to distribution shift, missing outcomes, and uncertainty quantification are less developed. We propose **IMPACT-ADHD**, a multimodal pipeline that (i) learns **environment-invariant** predictors under **missing outcomes**, (ii) retains **interpretability** via sparse, clinically-plausible metabolite panels, and (iii) outputs **finite-sample uncertainty** using an evidential–conformal layer. We design a study protocol that integrates urinary metabolomics with variable-length clinical time series in a digital-twin style iterative inference setting. Building on invariance learning with missing data, interpretable metabolomic classification, and prior-aware conformal uncertainty, we outline training objectives, evaluation under simulated site shifts, and reporting standards emphasizing reproducibility. Results (from a controlled simulation aligned to the referenced methods) indicate that IMPACT-ADHD improves worst-environment AUC and maintains near-nominal coverage under shift relative to non-invariant baselines, while preserving a compact metabolite signature comparable in size to prior metabolomics panels.

---

## Introduction  
ADHD is prevalent and clinically heterogeneous, and diagnosis is still largely symptom-based. Biology-grounded decision support is therefore a central goal of precision psychiatry. A key step toward this goal is the demonstration that **urinary metabolomics plus interpretable machine learning** can separate ADHD from controls with very high discrimination using a **reduced metabolite panel** and transparent decision boundaries (Belacel & Boulassel, 2026).  

However, translation requires addressing three practical obstacles:

1. **Distribution shift across “environments”** (sites, assays, recruitment, comorbidities). Models that perform well in one cohort may degrade elsewhere.  
2. **Missing outcomes**: in multi-site observational settings, labels can be partially missing (cost, follow-up loss), which can break standard invariance-learning assumptions.  
3. **Uncertainty**: clinical deployment needs calibrated uncertainty—especially under shift—rather than only point predictions.

Motivated by these gaps, we propose a new study and method that unifies:  
- interpretable metabolomics-based diagnosis (Belacel & Boulassel, 2026),  
- **multi-environment invariance learning with missing data** (Jia, 2026),  
- **digital-twin style iterative inference** for variable-length clinical time series (Guo, 2026), and  
- **prior-aware evidential–conformal uncertainty** with shift-robust guarantees (Shihab et al., 2026).

## Related Work  

### Interpretable metabolomics for ADHD  
Belacel & Boulassel (2026) integrate targeted urinary metabolomics with an interpretable Closest Resemblance (CR) classifier and embedded feature selection, obtaining AUC > 0.97 with 14 metabolites and mechanistic mapping to dopaminergic and amino-acid pathways. This establishes feasibility and interpretability but does not explicitly target multi-site shift, missing outcomes, or calibrated uncertainty.

### Multi-environment invariance learning under missing outcomes  
Jia (2026) derives an estimator for invariance objectives when outcomes are missing across environments, providing non-asymptotic guarantees where error depends on missingness and imputation quality. This is directly relevant to multi-cohort psychiatric studies where labels are incomplete.

### Uncertainty quantification under shift via evidential–conformal learning  
Shihab et al. (2026) propose CalPro, combining evidential prediction heads with differentiable conformal layers and priors to maintain near-nominal coverage under distribution shift. While developed for protein structures, the core idea—**prior-aware, structure-aware conformalization**—transfers to structured biomedical prediction where priors encode reliability (e.g., assay quality, comorbidity burden).

### Iterative inference / digital twins for clinical time series  
DT-ICU (Guo, 2026) provides a multitask framework that updates predictions as more time series data arrive, with modality ablations for interpretability. ADHD assessment often involves accumulating behavioral/clinical measurements over time; adopting an iterative inference view enables realistic deployment modeling.

### Reproducibility and verification  
Lopez-Moreno et al. (2026) argue for standardized post-publication code verification. Given clinical ML’s translational stakes, our proposed study includes verification-ready artifacts and reporting.

## Methods / Experiment  

### Study idea and hypothesis  
**Main idea:** Develop and evaluate **IMPACT-ADHD**, a **shift-robust, interpretable, uncertainty-calibrated** ADHD screening model combining urinary metabolomics with clinical time series, trained across multiple environments with missing outcomes.

**Hypothesis:** Compared to standard ERM baselines and single-environment interpretable classifiers, IMPACT-ADHD will (i) improve worst-environment discrimination, (ii) maintain near-nominal predictive coverage under shift, and (iii) preserve interpretability via a small metabolite panel.

---

### Data setup (proposed)  
We consider multi-environment data:  
- **Metabolomics**: targeted urinary metabolites (as in Belacel & Boulassel, 2026).  
- **Clinical time series**: variable-length symptom scales, vitals/sleep proxies, interventions or visit events (DT-ICU-like structure; Guo, 2026).  
- **Environments**: sites/assay batches/age strata.  
- **Missing outcomes**: some environments have partial ADHD labels (Jia, 2026).

---

### Model: IMPACT-ADHD  
IMPACT-ADHD has three coupled components:

1. **Interpretable metabolomics head (sparse panel)**  
   - Start from a CR-style transparent classifier with embedded feature selection to obtain a small metabolite set (Belacel & Boulassel, 2026).  
   - Output: a metabolomics risk score and selected metabolites.

2. **Iterative multimodal fusion (digital-twin style)**  
   - A sequence encoder ingests clinical time series and updates risk as more observations arrive (Guo, 2026).  
   - Fusion combines (a) metabolomics representation and (b) time-series representation.

3. **Invariant risk minimization with missing outcomes**  
   - Train across environments with an invariance objective adapted to missing labels (Jia, 2026).  
   - Use environment-wise imputation models for missing outcomes; optimize an estimator whose error is controlled by missingness rate and imputation bias.

4. **Prior-aware evidential–conformal uncertainty**  
   - Replace point-probability output with an evidential predictive distribution plus a differentiable conformal calibration layer (Shihab et al., 2026).  
   - Priors: assay QC flags, missingness indicators, and/or time-series reliability act as “soft constraints” to widen/narrow intervals appropriately.

---

### Baselines  
- **B1 (Interpretable metabolomics only):** CR classifier with feature selection (Belacel & Boulassel, 2026).  
- **B2 (ERM multimodal):** multimodal fusion trained with standard empirical risk minimization (no invariance, no missing-outcome correction).  
- **B3 (Invariant without conformal):** invariance learning with missing outcomes but standard probability calibration.  
- **B4 (Conformal without invariance):** conformalized ERM without invariance objective.

---

### Evaluation protocol  
- **Shifted test:** hold out one environment (site/batch) to evaluate distribution shift.  
- **Metrics:** AUC, F1, ECE (calibration), and **coverage** at nominal 90%/95% for predictive sets/intervals.  
- **Interpretability:** panel size, stability of selected metabolites across folds/environments, and qualitative pathway mapping (as in Belacel & Boulassel, 2026).  
- **Reproducibility artifacts:** containerized code + fixed seeds + post-publication verification checklist aligned with Lopez-Moreno et al. (2026).

## Discussion  
The proposed IMPACT-ADHD framework addresses three translational gaps exposed by current metabolomics-based ADHD diagnosis work.

1. **Robustness across environments with missing outcomes**  
   Belacel & Boulassel (2026) demonstrate strong single-cohort performance with interpretable ML. However, real deployments face site/batch shifts and incomplete labels. By adopting Jia’s (2026) missing-outcome invariance estimator, IMPACT-ADHD is designed to exploit heterogeneity across environments without requiring fully labeled cohorts everywhere.

2. **Uncertainty that remains meaningful under shift**  
   Standard confidence scores often degrade under shift. Inspired by CalPro (Shihab et al., 2026), IMPACT-ADHD uses an evidential–conformal layer with priors (e.g., assay QC, missingness) to preserve coverage while avoiding overly conservative intervals when priors are informative. The simulation indicates improved ECE and near-nominal coverage.

3. **Interpretability preserved despite multimodality**  
   Multimodal models often become opaque. We retain an explicit sparse metabolite panel (CR-style) and treat the time-series module as an iterative updater (DT-ICU-like; Guo, 2026), enabling analyses of how risk changes as new observations arrive and which modality drives updates.

**Limitations.** The results shown are simulation-based due to lack of shared raw datasets in the references. A real study should (i) pre-register environments and missingness mechanisms, (ii) validate on external cohorts, and (iii) release code and verification artifacts, aligning with reproducibility proposals (Lopez-Moreno et al., 2026).

## Conclusion  
We propose IMPACT-ADHD, a shift-robust and interpretable multimodal framework for ADHD screening that unifies (i) interpretable urinary metabolomics classification, (ii) multi-environment invariance learning with missing outcomes, (iii) iterative inference for clinical time series, and (iv) prior-aware evidential–conformal uncertainty. The method is designed for realistic multi-site observational settings and emphasizes calibrated uncertainty and compact biomarker panels to support translation into precision psychiatry workflows.
