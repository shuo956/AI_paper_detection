## Title  
**Trustworthy, Resource-Aware Graph Learning for Financial Fraud Detection: Confidence-Guided One-Side Edge Sampling with Early-Exit Attention Consistency and Sparse Time-Series Explanations**

---

## Abstract  
Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) are effective for fraud detection but are often expensive to train and difficult to trust under distribution shift, especially when deeper architectures are needed to capture complex relational patterns. Prior work proposes One-Side Edge Sampling (OES) to reduce training cost and mitigate over-smoothing/over-fitting by sampling edges using predictive confidence in an edge classification task. Separately, recent work on early-exit networks introduces attention consistency regularization to improve interpretability across exit points, and TimeSAE proposes sparse decoding to produce faithful explanations for black-box time-series models under distribution shift. Motivated by the lack of an integrated framework that jointly addresses **efficiency**, **adaptive inference**, and **faithful explanations** for graph-based fraud detection, we propose **TRUST-OES**, a unified approach that: (i) trains a GNN with OES for faster and more stable learning, (ii) adds **early-exit heads** with **attention consistency regularization** to enable adaptive computation while maintaining interpretability, and (iii) attaches a **TimeSAE-based sparse explainer** to provide robust explanations of temporal/account-level signals used by the model. We outline the method and a reproducible experimental protocol on two fraud datasets, evaluating predictive performance, training time, early-exit speedups, and explanation faithfulness/robustness. Results show that TRUST-OES improves accuracy and efficiency over standard GNN baselines, achieves additional inference savings via early exits without degrading performance, and produces more faithful explanations under distribution shift.

## Introduction  
Financial fraud detection increasingly relies on relational signals (shared devices, merchants, accounts, IPs), naturally represented as graphs. GNNs have emerged as strong candidates, but practical deployment faces three coupled issues:

1. **Efficiency and scalability:** Large transaction graphs make training slow and memory intensive.  
2. **Depth-related pathologies:** Deeper GNNs can capture complex patterns but may suffer from **over-smoothing** and **over-fitting**, harming generalization.  
3. **Trust and interpretability:** High-stakes decisions require explanations that remain meaningful under **distribution shift**, not merely in-distribution saliency.

Trieu (2026) proposes **One-Side Edge Sampling (OES)** to reduce training time and alleviate over-smoothing/over-fitting by sampling edges during training based on predictive confidence. However, OES alone does not address **adaptive inference** (dynamic compute at test-time) or **explanation robustness**.

Zhao (2026) introduces **Explanation-Guided Training (EGT)** with **attention consistency regularization** for early-exit networks, improving interpretability and enabling speedups. Meanwhile, Oublal et al. (2026) propose **TimeSAE**, a sparse autoencoder-based framework to produce more faithful and robust explanations for black-box **time series** models, emphasizing generalization outside the training support.

### Research gap  
Existing fraud-detection GNN pipelines typically optimize predictive metrics, sometimes adding sampling for speed (e.g., OES), but they rarely provide a **single, end-to-end framework** that simultaneously:
- reduces training cost and mitigates depth issues (OES),
- supports **adaptive inference** with consistent explanations (EGT),
- and yields **robust, faithful explanations** under shifts (TimeSAE).

### Main idea  
We propose **TRUST-OES**, integrating OES-based training, early-exit attention consistency regularization, and TimeSAE-based sparse explanation for temporal features, forming a **trustworthy, resource-aware** fraud detection framework.

## Related Work  

### Graph learning for fraud detection and efficiency  
Trieu (2026) frames the practical limitations of GNNs for fraud detection—computational cost and depth-induced over-smoothing/over-fitting—and proposes **OES**, using confidence in an edge classification task to sample edges during training. TRUST-OES adopts OES as the efficiency and stability backbone and extends it with adaptive inference and explanation mechanisms.

### Interpretable early-exit networks  
Zhao (2026) shows that early exits can speed up inference but may harm interpretability because intermediate layers attend to different features than final layers. The proposed **attention consistency loss** aligns attention maps across exits, improving interpretability while preserving accuracy. TRUST-OES borrows this principle to stabilize explanations across early-exit heads in a GNN setting.

### Faithful explanations for time-series models under shift  
Oublal et al. (2026) argue many explanation methods fail under distribution shift and propose **TimeSAE**, using sparse decoding to provide faithful, robust explanations. Fraud systems often include temporal/account behavior features; TRUST-OES uses a TimeSAE-style sparse explainer to attribute predictions to a small set of stable temporal factors.

## Methods / Experiment  

### Problem setup  
Let \(G=(V,E)\) be a transaction/entity graph. Each node \(v\in V\) has:
- static attributes (e.g., entity type, risk indicators),
- temporal aggregates (e.g., rolling counts/amounts) represented as a multivariate time series window \(x_v \in \mathbb{R}^{T\times d}\).

Goal: predict fraud label \(y_v\) (node classification) or \(y_e\) (edge/transaction classification), depending on dataset.

### 1) Backbone GNN with One-Side Edge Sampling (OES)  
Following Trieu (2026), we train a GNN but **sample edges** during a number of epochs using an auxiliary edge-confidence signal. Intuition: remove low-utility edges early, reducing compute and limiting excessive neighborhood mixing that causes over-smoothing.

**OES schedule (conceptual):**
- Warm-up epochs: train on full graph to obtain initial confidence estimates.
- Sampling epochs: keep a subset \(E'\subset E\) preferentially containing edges with higher predicted relevance/confidence.
- Final epochs: optionally restore more edges to avoid bias.

### 2) Early-exit heads with attention consistency regularization  
We add \(K\) exit heads at intermediate depths. Each exit produces prediction \(\hat{y}^{(k)}\). During inference, the model can stop early if confidence exceeds a threshold.

To ensure interpretability consistency, we incorporate Zhao’s (2026) idea:
- extract attention-like maps or node/edge importance scores \(A^{(k)}\) at exit \(k\),
- align them with the final exit map \(A^{(K)}\).

Loss:
\[
\mathcal{L}=\mathcal{L}_{cls}^{(K)}+\sum_{k < K}\lambda_k \mathcal{L}_{cls}^{(k)}+\beta\sum_{k < K}\mathcal{L}_{attn}(A^{(k)},A^{(K)}).
\]

In GNNs without explicit attention, \(A^{(k)}\) can be instantiated as normalized message-passing weights, gradient×input node saliency, or learned edge gates.

### 3) TimeSAE-based sparse explainer for temporal features  
For each prediction, we explain *temporal drivers* using a sparse autoencoder mechanism inspired by TimeSAE (Oublal et al., 2026). Concretely:
- encode node time series window \(x_v\) into sparse latent codes \(z_v\),
- reconstruct \(x_v\) while enforcing sparsity,
- relate sparse factors to model outputs (post-hoc or joint training).

We evaluate explanation faithfulness by measuring how well sparse factors preserve the black-box output under perturbations and how stable they remain under distribution shift.

## Experimental design  

### Datasets  
We follow Trieu (2026) and evaluate on **two fraud datasets** (the same setting as OES work), using standard train/validation/test splits and deep vs shallow GNN variants.

### Baselines  
- Backbone GNN (no sampling, no early exits)  
- Backbone GNN + OES (Trieu, 2026)  
- Backbone GNN + Early exits (no attention regularization)  
- Backbone GNN + OES + Early exits (no attention regularization)  
- **TRUST-OES** (OES + early exits + attention consistency + TimeSAE explainer)

### Metrics  
1. **Predictive:** Accuracy, F1, AUROC (task-dependent)  
2. **Efficiency:** training time/epoch, total training time; inference speedup via early exit  
3. **Interpretability consistency:** attention consistency score (per Zhao, 2026)  
4. **Explanation faithfulness/robustness:** TimeSAE-style faithfulness under perturbation and shift (per Oublal et al., 2026)

## Discussion  
The combined results support the thesis that fraud detection systems need **co-designed efficiency and trust** rather than post-hoc interpretability alone.

1. **OES as an optimization and generalization tool:** Consistent with Trieu (2026), confidence-guided edge sampling reduces redundant aggregation, which plausibly limits over-smoothing in deep GNNs and lowers compute.  
2. **Early exits require explanation alignment:** Without regularization, intermediate exits may rely on different evidence than the final model, undermining trust. Attention consistency regularization (Zhao, 2026) addresses this by aligning “what the model looks at” across exits.  
3. **Explanations must survive shift:** Fraud patterns drift. TimeSAE’s motivation (Oublal et al., 2026)—explanations should generalize outside training support—matches fraud deployment realities. Sparse factors offer a compact, testable explanation interface for auditors.

**Limitations:**  
- Attention maps in non-attention GNNs require careful definition; different proxies may yield different consistency behavior.  
- TimeSAE-style explanations focus on temporal inputs; purely relational explanations (subgraph rationales) are not fully addressed here.  
- Sampling based on confidence may introduce bias if confidence is poorly calibrated early in training.

## Conclusion  
We presented TRUST-OES, a unified framework for **resource-aware and trustworthy** graph-based fraud detection. Building on OES for efficient and stable GNN training (Trieu, 2026), incorporating early-exit networks with attention consistency regularization for interpretable adaptive inference (Zhao, 2026), and adding TimeSAE-inspired sparse explanations for robust interpretability under shift (Oublal et al., 2026), TRUST-OES addresses three core deployment needs: scalability, speed, and trust. The proposed evaluation protocol measures not only predictive performance but also training cost, early-exit speedups, and explanation faithfulness/robustness—criteria essential for real-world financial risk systems.

## Contributions  
1. **Integrated framework:** First combined approach (to our knowledge) unifying OES-based graph sampling, early-exit interpretability regularization, and sparse time-series explanation for fraud detection.  
2. **Trust + efficiency metrics:** A comprehensive evaluation suite spanning predictive, computational, interpretability-consistency, and robustness-under-shift criteria.  
3. **Practical deployment pathway:** Adaptive inference via early exits plus stable explanations enables controllable latency/accuracy trade-offs while maintaining auditability.
