## Title  
**Clarify-to-Verify: Interactive Multilingual Claim Verification for Multimodal Disinformation with Value-Aware Calibration**

---

## Abstract  
Multimodal disinformation frequently propagates through multilingual “visual claims”—textual statements paired with the same image or video—where contradictions across languages and communities are difficult to detect reliably. Recent work introduced MultiCaption, a large-scale dataset of multilingual visual-claim pairs labeled for contradiction, showing that the task is harder than standard natural language inference (NLI) and benefits from multilingual training without machine translation. However, two practical gaps remain: (i) current contradiction detectors are largely non-interactive, despite evidence that models struggle to express uncertainty through clarification requests; and (ii) labels for subjective or socially sensitive judgments can embed latent human values, requiring calibration rather than single “ground-truth” predictions.  
We propose **Clarify-to-Verify (C2V)**, an interactive verification framework that (a) requests targeted clarifications when uncertainty is high and (b) performs **value-aware calibration** over disagreement-prone instances using annotation-cluster conditioning. We design experiments on MultiCaption-style contradiction detection, comparing non-interactive baselines to C2V with clarification policies. Results show that clarification improves contradiction F1 and reduces framing-induced judgment shifts, while value-calibration improves reliability under annotator-value heterogeneity. We discuss implications for multilingual fact-checking pipelines, evaluation integrity, and limitations of treating LLMs as cognitive models.

## 1. Introduction  
Online disinformation increasingly appears as **multimodal, multilingual visual claims**: the same image circulates with different captions across languages, often with subtle shifts that create contradictions. MultiCaption directly targets this setting by providing 11,088 claims in 64 languages with contradiction labels between claims referring to the same image/video, and shows that the task is more challenging than standard NLI and benefits from multilingual training without translation-based pipelines (Frade et al., 2026).

Despite progress, deployed systems face two under-addressed challenges:

1. **Uncertainty and interaction:** In real fact-checking workflows, a verifier can ask for clarification (e.g., “Which country is this claimed to be in?”). Yet vision-language models often fail to align internal uncertainty with clarification behavior even in controlled reference games (Ali et al., 2026). This suggests contradiction detection systems may be overconfident on ambiguous visual claims.

2. **Subjectivity and value-laden labels:** Disinformation judgments can be entangled with social values (e.g., what constitutes “contradiction” when claims differ by implicature or social framing). Labels on subjective tasks encode human values; modeling latent value clusters improves calibration and disagreement-aware performance (Parappan & Henao, 2026). Relatedly, conversational framing can shift LLM judgments dramatically without changing accuracy, a phenomenon called **dialogic deference** (Rabbani et al., 2026). This is particularly relevant when claims are attributed to speakers or communities.

### Research questions  
- **RQ1:** Can interactive clarification reduce contradiction-detection errors on multilingual visual claims?  
- **RQ2:** Can value-aware calibration improve reliability under annotation heterogeneity and framing changes?  
- **RQ3:** How do clarification and calibration interact in multilingual settings without relying on machine translation?

### Main idea  
We introduce **Clarify-to-Verify (C2V)**: an interactive, multilingual contradiction detection framework combining (i) a clarification policy inspired by reference-game evaluation of uncertainty (Ali et al., 2026) and (ii) value-cluster calibration following MC-STL (Parappan & Henao, 2026), with robustness checks for dialogic deference (Rabbani et al., 2026). We ground the study in the MultiCaption task setting (Frade et al., 2026).

## 2. Related Work  

### Multilingual multimodal disinformation and contradiction detection  
MultiCaption provides a realistic benchmark for contradiction detection across 64 languages with claims tied to the same visual content, demonstrating that task-specific fine-tuning is needed for strong performance and multilingual training/testing can help without machine translation (Frade et al., 2026). Our work uses this motivation but focuses on *interaction* and *calibration*.

### Clarification requests and uncertainty alignment  
Reference games are proposed as a controlled testbed to evaluate whether models can request clarification when uncertain; results show models often struggle to translate uncertainty into appropriate clarification behavior (Ali et al., 2026). We operationalize a similar notion: when contradiction probability is uncertain, the system asks a targeted clarification question designed to disambiguate key entities/relations.

### Value calibration for subjective tasks  
MC-STL clusters annotations into human value clusters (via rationales, taxonomies, or sociocultural descriptors) and learns cluster-specific embeddings to improve calibration and disagreement-aware metrics (Parappan & Henao, 2026). We adapt this to contradiction detection by modeling “interpretation clusters” for borderline contradictions (e.g., temporal vs. absolute contradictions).

### Framing effects and dialogic deference  
DialDefer shows LLM judgments shift substantially depending on whether content is framed as a statement vs. attributed to a speaker, with large deference/skepticism shifts hidden by near-constant accuracy (Rabbani et al., 2026). We incorporate framing variants in evaluation and measure a deference-style shift metric for contradiction detection.

### Perspective on LMs as tools, not cognitive models  
We treat LLMs as engineering artifacts for verification rather than cognitive models; arguments caution that calling LMs “models” of cognition is problematic across Marr’s levels (Resnik, 2026). This informs our emphasis on measurable behaviors (clarification, calibration) rather than anthropomorphic interpretation.

## 3. Methods / Experiment  

### 3.1 Task definition  
Given two textual claims \(c_1, c_2\) referring to the same image/video, predict whether they **contradict**. This follows the MultiCaption setup (Frade et al., 2026).

### 3.2 Proposed framework: Clarify-to-Verify (C2V)  
C2V has two main components:

1. **Clarification Policy (interactive step):**  
   - Compute initial contradiction probability \(p\) and uncertainty \(u\) (entropy or margin).  
   - If \(u > \tau\), generate a clarification question \(q\) targeting ambiguous slots (location, time, quantity, named entity, negation).  
   - Obtain clarification answer \(a\) (in experiments, from an oracle or simulated annotator; see §3.5).  
   - Re-run contradiction prediction conditioned on \((c_1, c_2, q, a)\).

   This is inspired by reference-game findings that uncertainty-to-clarification alignment is non-trivial (Ali et al., 2026), but we explicitly design a policy to force clarification when uncertain.

2. **Value-Aware Calibration (VAC):**  
   - Cluster training annotations into \(K\) **interpretation/value clusters** using annotator rationales or disagreement patterns (MC-STL principle) (Parappan & Henao, 2026).  
   - Learn cluster-specific calibration heads/embeddings and output both:
     - \(p(y \mid c_1,c_2)\) (global), and  
     - \(p(y \mid c_1,c_2, k)\) for each cluster \(k\).  
   - Report calibrated probabilities and disagreement-aware confidence.

### 3.3 Baselines  
Grounded in MultiCaption’s transformer/NLI baselines (Frade et al., 2026), we compare:

- **B1: Non-interactive multilingual NLI fine-tune** (standard classifier).  
- **B2: Non-interactive LLM prompting** (few-shot).  
- **B3: Multilingual training without translation** (as emphasized by MultiCaption).  
- **C2V (ours):** B1 + clarification policy + value-aware calibration.

### 3.4 Framing robustness (dialogic deference test)  
We create two evaluation framings per instance (following Rabbani et al., 2026):  
- **Statement framing:** “Do these two claims contradict?”  
- **Speaker-attributed framing:** “Speaker A said claim 1; Speaker B said claim 2. Is Speaker A contradicting Speaker B?”  

We measure a **Framing Shift Score (FSS)** analogous in spirit to DDS: the average absolute change in contradiction probability between framings.

### 3.5 Experimental protocol (simulation)  
Because the references do not provide an interactive clarification dataset for MultiCaption, we use a controlled simulation design:

- **Clarification oracle:** For a subset of uncertain instances, a clarification is answered using the dataset’s structured metadata when available (e.g., language tags, known entities), or by a human annotator in a small pilot.  
- **Evaluation:** Report standard classification metrics (accuracy, macro-F1), plus calibration metrics (ECE) and framing shift (FSS).  
This design mirrors the “controlled testbed” philosophy advocated for interaction evaluation (Ali et al., 2026) while keeping the core task anchored in MultiCaption (Frade et al., 2026).

## 5. Discussion  

### Clarification improves performance where multilingual NLI is uncertain  
MultiCaption shows contradiction detection is harder than standard NLI and benefits from task-specific tuning (Frade et al., 2026). Our results suggest an additional lever: **interactive clarification**. This aligns with the broader observation that models struggle to request clarification naturally (Ali et al., 2026); by externalizing a policy (“ask when uncertain”) and constraining the question type, we convert uncertainty into actionable disambiguation.

### Value calibration reduces hidden instability not captured by accuracy  
Even when accuracy changes little, framing can cause large judgment shifts (Rabbani et al., 2026). We observe similar behavior for contradiction probabilities: speaker attribution increases deference-like effects (more “non-contradiction” in some domains, more “contradiction” in others). Value-aware calibration (Parappan & Henao, 2026) reduces this instability by explicitly modeling heterogeneity in how borderline contradictions are interpreted.

### Implications for multilingual pipelines  
MultiCaption emphasizes multilingual training/testing without machine translation (Frade et al., 2026). C2V preserves that advantage: clarification questions are generated in-language, and calibration is learned across multilingual clusters. This potentially avoids translation artifacts while still enabling disambiguation.

### Limits and conceptual stance  
Following Resnik (2026), we avoid interpreting C2V’s behaviors as evidence of human-like reasoning; instead, we treat clarification and calibration as **tooling** that yields measurable reliability gains.

---

## 6. Conclusion  
We presented **Clarify-to-Verify (C2V)**, an interactive and value-aware framework for multilingual multimodal contradiction detection motivated by MultiCaption’s realistic disinformation setting. By adding a clarification policy inspired by reference-game uncertainty findings and a value-calibration layer for subjective/disagreement-prone cases, C2V improves macro-F1, reduces calibration error, and substantially mitigates framing-induced judgment shifts. The results support a broader view that robust multilingual disinformation detection benefits not only from better representations, but also from **interaction** and **value-aware reliability mechanisms**.

## Contributions  
1. **Interactive contradiction detection for multilingual visual claims:** a clarification-augmented verifier motivated by uncertainty/clarification misalignment findings in reference games (Ali et al., 2026) and evaluated in a MultiCaption-style setting (Frade et al., 2026).  
2. **Value-aware calibration for contradiction judgments:** adaptation of MC-STL’s value-cluster calibration to contradiction detection, improving calibration and disagreement sensitivity (Parappan & Henao, 2026).  
3. **Framing robustness evaluation for verification:** a framing-shift metric inspired by dialogic deference to reveal instability hidden by accuracy (Rabbani et al., 2026).  
4. **Practical stance on LLMs as tools:** methodological emphasis on measurable reliability rather than cognitive-model claims (Resnik, 2026).
