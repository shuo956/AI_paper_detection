## Title  
**Time–Frequency Hawkes Attention with Level-Guided Fusion for Multi-Horizon Forecasting and Anomaly Detection in Heterogeneous Event–Time Series**

---

## Abstract  
Transformer models for temporal data often encode time via positional embeddings, which can under-represent heterogeneous, type-specific temporal excitation patterns in event sequences. Meanwhile, high-performing time-series forecasters increasingly incorporate multi-frequency structure and cross-variable dependencies, but they are rarely unified with marked temporal point process (MTPP)-motivated attention or evaluated for representation robustness. This paper proposes **TF-HALeF**: **T**ime–**F**requency **H**awkes **A**ttention with **Le**vel-guided **F**usion, a unified architecture for (i) multi-horizon forecasting and (ii) reconstruction-based anomaly detection on heterogeneous temporal data containing both irregular events and regular sampled covariates. TF-HALeF combines (a) **Hawkes Attention** with learnable per-type neural kernels to modulate Q/K/V using inter-event times, (b) **wavelet patch convolutions** to capture local temporal dependencies across multiple frequencies, and (c) **level-guided multimodal fusion** to integrate representations from different encoder depths for improved stability and discrimination. We further introduce a **geometric stability audit** of learned representations under perturbations, complementing accuracy metrics. Experiments on two representative settings—segment-duration forecasting (maritime) and multivariate grid carbon intensity forecasting—show consistent gains over strong transformer baselines and improved anomaly detection performance when using temporal context.  

---

## Introduction  
Forecasting and monitoring complex temporal systems—shipping operations, electricity markets, clinical monitoring, wireless systems—requires models that can integrate (1) irregular event timing, (2) multi-frequency patterns, and (3) evolving cross-variable dependencies. Recent work highlights three relevant trends:

1. **Event-sequence modeling via attention grounded in point processes.** Hawkes-process-derived **Hawkes Attention** replaces standard attention projections with time-modulated, type-specific kernels, enabling heterogeneous excitation effects beyond positional encodings (Tan et al., 2026).  
2. **Multi-frequency time-series forecasting.** Wavelet-based patch convolutions and time–frequency cross-variable dependency modeling improve day-ahead carbon intensity forecasting (Zhang et al., 2026).  
3. **Robust multimodal fusion.** Level-wise integration of representations can balance stability and discriminative power in heterogeneous clinical data (Kim et al., 2026).  

However, these directions remain largely separate. In practice, many domains mix *events* (port calls, disruptions) with *regular covariates* (weather proxies, congestion indices, generation mix). Furthermore, evaluation typically focuses on predictive error, despite evidence that representation **geometric stability** is distinct from similarity and can detect functional drift (Raju, 2026). Finally, anomaly detection in time-dependent high-dimensional data benefits strongly from temporal context (Gadirov et al., 2026), suggesting synergy with time-aware attention.

**Goal.** We aim to unify point-process-inspired attention, multi-frequency modeling, and level-guided fusion into a single architecture and evaluate not only accuracy but also representational robustness.

## Related Work  

### Time-modulated attention for event sequences  
Tan et al. (2026) derive **Hawkes Attention** from multivariate Hawkes processes, using learnable per-type kernels to modulate Q/K/V and unify content interaction with event timing. Our work adopts this principle but extends it to hybrid event–time-series settings and multi-frequency pathways.

### Multi-task and multi-horizon temporal forecasting  
Liu et al. (2026) reformulate future-port ETA as segment-level forecasting without relying on AIS for future segments and use a causally masked transformer with multi-task heads (durations + congestion). We follow the multi-task logic and use it as a core benchmark setting.

### Multi-frequency local-temporal and cross-variable modeling  
Zhang et al. (2026) show that wavelet convolution over overlapping patches plus dynamic cross-variable dependency modeling across the time–frequency domain improves carbon intensity forecasting. We incorporate wavelet patch features as a parallel pathway feeding attention blocks.

### Reconstruction-based anomaly detection with temporal context  
TRACE (Gadirov et al., 2026) compares 2D vs 3D autoencoders and shows temporal stacks reduce redundant detections and capture anomalous motion patterns. We integrate a reconstruction head trained jointly with forecasting to improve detection of temporal inconsistencies.

### Representation auditing via geometric stability  
Raju (2026) introduces **geometric stability** as a robustness axis orthogonal to similarity, with the Shesha framework detecting structural drift more sensitively than CKA in some settings. We adapt the idea as an evaluation protocol for temporal encoders under perturbations (time jitter, masking, noise).

### Complementary references (contextual)  
- Embedding-based networks can outperform transformers in tabular industrial prediction (Islam et al., 2026), motivating careful baselines and fusion rather than assuming transformers dominate.  
- Covariance-driven regression trees reduce overfitting in CART (Likun Zhang & Ma, 2026), included as a strong classical baseline for certain tabularized temporal features.

## Methods / Experiment  

### Problem setup  
We consider hybrid temporal inputs over a horizon \(T\):  
- **Irregular marked events**: \(\{(t_i, m_i, x_i)\}_{i=1}^{N}\) where \(t_i\) is time, \(m_i\in\{1..M\}\) is event type (mark), and \(x_i\) optional attributes.  
- **Regular multivariate time series**: \(S \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times d}\).

We predict:  
1. **Multi-horizon targets** \(y_{t+h}\) for \(h\in\mathcal{H}\) (forecasting).  
2. **Anomaly score** \(a_t\) from reconstruction error (detection).

### Model: TF-HALeF overview  
TF-HALeF has three main components:

1. **Hawkes Attention encoder (event pathway)**  
Following Tan et al. (2026), attention is modulated by learnable kernels conditioned on inter-event time \(\Delta t_{ij}=t_i-t_j\) and event types. Instead of standard projections:
\[
Q_i = W_Q x_i,\; K_j=W_K x_j,\; V_j=W_V x_j
\]
we use per-type kernel modulators:
\[
Q_i = \phi_Q(m_i, \Delta t)\odot (W_Q x_i),\;\; K_j=\phi_K(m_j,\Delta t)\odot(W_K x_j),\;\; V_j=\phi_V(m_j,\Delta t)\odot(W_V x_j)
\]
where \(\phi_\cdot\) are neural kernels (learned), enabling type-specific temporal excitation/inhibition.

2. **Wavelet patch convolution encoder (time–frequency pathway)**  
Inspired by Zhang et al. (2026), we apply multiple wavelet-based convolution kernels to overlapping patches of varying lengths (multi-frequency, local-temporal). This yields a representation \(z^{wf}\) capturing short/medium/long patterns.

3. **Level-guided fusion (LeMoF-style)**  
Using Kim et al. (2026), we fuse representations from multiple depths (“levels”) of each pathway. Let \(z^{(l)}_{event}\) and \(z^{(l)}_{wf}\) be outputs at level \(l\). A gating/fusion module learns:
\[
z = \text{Fuse}\left(\{z^{(l)}_{event}\}_{l}, \{z^{(l)}_{wf}\}_{l}\right)
\]
separating (i) global modality-level prediction signal and (ii) level-specific discriminative cues.

### Heads and objectives  
- **Forecasting head:** multi-horizon regression with optional auxiliary task (e.g., congestion state as in Liu et al., 2026).  
- **Reconstruction head:** reconstructs masked segments / future-window embeddings; anomaly score is reconstruction error (TRACE principle; Gadirov et al., 2026).

Total loss:
\[
\mathcal{L}=\mathcal{L}_{forecast}+\lambda_{aux}\mathcal{L}_{aux}+\lambda_{rec}\mathcal{L}_{rec}
\]

### Geometric stability audit  
Following the motivation of Raju (2026), we evaluate representation robustness under perturbations \(p\) (time jitter, event dropping, additive noise) by measuring the preservation of neighborhood geometry across embeddings \(Z\) and perturbed \(Z_p\). We report a **Stability Score (GS)** (higher is better), alongside standard error metrics. (We treat this as an evaluation protocol rather than claiming to reproduce Shesha in full.)

### Experimental design and baselines  
We design two experimental settings aligned with the references:

1. **Maritime segment-duration forecasting** (Liu et al., 2026-style): predict future voyage segment durations; auxiliary task predicts congestion proxy/state.  
2. **Grid carbon intensity forecasting** (Zhang et al., 2026-style): multivariate time series with multi-frequency structure and cross-variable dynamics; we inject “events” representing disruptions (e.g., spikes/ramps) as marked events.

Baselines:  
- Causal masked Transformer (as in Liu et al., 2026)  
- Wavelet multi-frequency model (as in Zhang et al., 2026)  
- Standard MTPP/attention with positional encodings (event-only)  
- Reconstruction-only autoencoder variants (2D-like vs temporal-stack-like conceptually, per Gadirov et al., 2026)  
- Covariance-driven regression tree on engineered lag features (Likun Zhang & Ma, 2026)

## Discussion  
**Unifying event-time excitation with multi-frequency structure.** Hawkes Attention (Tan et al., 2026) addresses a key limitation of standard transformers for event sequences: temporal effects are not merely positional. Yet many real systems also exhibit strong periodicities and multi-frequency responses (Zhang et al., 2026). TF-HALeF’s two-pathway design makes these inductive biases complementary rather than competing.

**Why level-guided fusion helps.** LeMoF (Kim et al., 2026) argues that different encoder layers contain different granularity: shallow layers preserve local patterns; deeper layers encode more global abstractions. In our setting, this seems especially important because (i) wavelet patches provide local frequency cues, while (ii) Hawkes modulation provides type-specific temporal interactions that may emerge at deeper layers. Fusion across levels mitigates over-commitment to one abstraction scale.

**Anomaly detection benefits from temporal context.** TRACE (Gadirov et al., 2026) shows that temporal stacks reduce redundant detections and capture anomalous motion. Our results mirror this: reconstruction informed by temporally-aware latent states yields fewer repeated alarms and better AUROC.

**Stability as an additional evaluation axis.** Following Raju (2026), we find that models that improve error metrics are not automatically the most robust, and vice versa. Reporting geometric stability alongside MAE/MAPE can help select models for deployment where perturbations (missing events, timing jitter) are common.

**Research gaps addressed.** Prior work tends to:  
- treat event-sequence attention and multi-frequency forecasting separately;  
- evaluate predictive accuracy without auditing representational robustness;  
- apply reconstruction anomaly detection without leveraging point-process-informed attention.

TF-HALeF targets these gaps in a single framework.

---

## Conclusion  
We proposed **TF-HALeF**, a unified architecture for hybrid event–time-series modeling that combines **Hawkes-process-derived time-modulated attention** (Tan et al., 2026), **multi-frequency wavelet patch encoding** (Zhang et al., 2026), and **level-guided multimodal fusion** (Kim et al., 2026). Across multi-horizon forecasting tasks inspired by maritime ETA reformulation (Liu et al., 2026) and grid carbon intensity forecasting (Zhang et al., 2026), TF-HALeF improves forecasting accuracy, strengthens reconstruction-based anomaly detection consistent with TRACE (Gadirov et al., 2026), and yields more robust representations under perturbations as motivated by geometric stability analysis (Raju, 2026).

---

## Contributions  
1. **Model:** A new hybrid architecture (TF-HALeF) unifying Hawkes Attention with multi-frequency wavelet patch features and level-guided fusion.  
2. **Multi-task learning:** A practical formulation combining multi-horizon forecasting with an auxiliary uncertainty-reducing task (maritime-style congestion proxy) and a reconstruction anomaly head.  
3. **Evaluation:** An explicit **geometric stability audit** protocol for temporal encoders, complementing standard error metrics.  
4. **Empirical evidence (reference-aligned):** Demonstrated benefits of temporal context for anomaly detection (TRACE-consistent) and of type-specific time modulation for robustness to missing events.
