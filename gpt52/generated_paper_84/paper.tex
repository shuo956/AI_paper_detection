## Title  
**CALM-Teach: Value-Calibrated, Evidence-Anchored Multi-Label Teaching Feedback with Adaptive Judging and Episodic Memory**

---

## Abstract  
Open-ended student feedback contains actionable signals about teaching quality, but existing automated approaches often collapse comments into coarse sentiment or single scores, limiting diagnostic usefulness. Recent work proposes multi-label, dimension-anchored evaluation with graph-based evidence modeling (TeachPro), yet two practical gaps remain: (i) subjective labels reflect heterogeneous human values and annotator norms, and (ii) long-horizon course feedback requires structured memory to preserve provenance and coherence across weeks. We propose **CALM-Teach**, a framework that extends multi-label qualitative teaching evaluation with **value calibration** over annotator clusters and **structured episodic event memory** for longitudinal evidence aggregation. CALM-Teach combines (1) a **Dimension-Anchored Evidence Encoder** and cross-view syntactic–semantic graph representations inspired by TeachPro, (2) **MC-STL-style value clustering** to produce value-conditional calibrated predictions, (3) an **AdaJudge-style adaptive pooling/judging head** to dynamically aggregate evidence for each dimension, and (4) **SEEM-inspired episodic memory** to maintain traceable teaching “episodes” with provenance pointers. We design an evaluation protocol including multi-label accuracy/F1, calibration, disagreement-aware metrics, and interpretability measures aligned with fine-grained evidence practices (InFi-Check). Experiments on a newly constructed longitudinal teaching-feedback benchmark demonstrate improved macro-F1, better value-specific calibration, and higher evidence faithfulness compared to strong baselines.

---

## Introduction  
Student evaluations of teaching (SET) are widely used but suffer from restricted response formats, distortion, and low reliability. Mining open-ended comments can improve actionable insight, but many methods reduce feedback to binary sentiment, failing to isolate pedagogically meaningful dimensions such as clarity, feedback timeliness, or classroom experience. **TeachPro** addresses this by introducing multi-label qualitative evaluation across five teaching dimensions with (i) **dimension-anchored evidence encoding** and (ii) **cross-view syntactic/semantic graph synergy** for robust representation learning.

However, two underexplored issues limit real deployment:

1. **Subjectivity and value pluralism in labels.** Teaching quality judgments encode implicit norms (e.g., “strict grading is good/bad”), and annotators/students disagree systematically. **MC-STL** shows that subjective-task labels can be decomposed into **human value clusters** and that calibrating predictions per cluster improves both discrimination and disagreement-aware performance.

2. **Longitudinal coherence and provenance.** Teaching feedback accumulates over weeks; isolated comment-level predictions miss narrative structure (“improved after week 4”, “feedback got slower”). Static RAG-like retrieval can be scattered and non-structural. **SEEM** demonstrates that hierarchical **episodic event memory** with provenance pointers improves coherence and reasoning over long interactions.

Motivated by these gaps, we propose **CALM-Teach**, integrating value calibration and episodic memory into a TeachPro-like multi-label evaluation pipeline, while using **adaptive evidence aggregation** inspired by **AdaJudge** to better match task-dependent preference signals.

## Related Work  

### Multi-label qualitative teaching evaluation  
TeachPro reframes SET mining as **multi-label** prediction over five pedagogical dimensions, aligning comment evidence to learnable semantic anchors and fusing **syntactic** dependencies with **semantic** similarity graphs. CALM-Teach adopts this core framing and extends it to value-conditional and longitudinal settings.

### Value calibration for subjective NLP tasks  
MC-STL argues that labels embed human values; clustering annotator rationales/taxonomies enables **cluster-specific embeddings** and improves calibration and disagreement-aware metrics. CALM-Teach uses value clustering to model heterogeneous student/annotator norms in teaching evaluation.

### Adaptive judging and evidence aggregation  
AdaJudge shows that static pooling is a mismatch for preference/reward modeling; adaptive multi-view pooling and discrimination-oriented representation refinement improve judging. CALM-Teach adapts this idea to teaching dimensions: different dimensions require different evidence routing (e.g., “professional expertise” may rely on content-specific statements; “classroom experience” on affective and interaction cues).

### Structured memory for long-horizon reasoning  
SEEM introduces hierarchical graph + episodic memory with provenance pointers and reverse provenance expansion to reconstruct coherent contexts. CALM-Teach uses episodic frames to capture week-by-week “teaching events” and link predictions to traceable evidence.

### Interpretable fine-grained verification  
InFi-Check emphasizes evidence, fine-grained error types, and corrections for factuality evaluation. While teaching evaluation is not pure fact-checking, CALM-Teach borrows the **evidence-first** principle: predictions should be accompanied by extractive evidence spans and provenance, and evaluated for faithfulness.

## Methods / Experiment  

### Task definition  
Given a set of student comments for an instructor/course over time, predict a **multi-label vector** over five dimensions (as in TeachPro):  
1) Professional expertise, 2) Instructional behavior, 3) Pedagogical efficacy, 4) Classroom experience, 5) Other.

Additionally, CALM-Teach predicts **value-cluster–conditional** dimension scores and stores evidence in an episodic memory.

### Dataset construction (new benchmark)  
We construct **LongTeach-FG** (Longitudinal Teaching Feedback—Fine-Grained), designed to stress (i) value disagreement and (ii) longitudinal coherence.

- **Inputs:** weekly batches of open-ended comments (week 1…N).  
- **Annotations:** multi-label dimension tags + evidence spans; annotator IDs and short rationales to enable value clustering (MC-STL-style).  
- **Provenance:** each evidence span is linked to comment ID and week.

We follow TeachPro’s emphasis on expert qualitative annotations, and incorporate evidence-centric annotation practices inspired by InFi-Check (explicit evidence + justification).

### Model: CALM-Teach  
CALM-Teach has four main components:

**(A) Dimension-Anchored Evidence Encoder (DAEE)** (TeachPro-inspired)  
- Text encoder produces contextual token embeddings.  
- Learnable **dimension anchors** represent the five dimensions.  
- Cross-attention aligns evidence tokens to anchors, producing dimension-specific evidence representations.

**(B) Cross-view graph synergy encoder** (TeachPro-inspired)  
- **Syntactic branch:** dependency-parse graph over tokens.  
- **Semantic branch:** similarity graph (e.g., BERT cosine edges).  
- BiAffine fusion + disentanglement regularizer to encourage complementary views.

**(C) Value-calibrated prediction head (MC-STL-inspired)**  
- Cluster annotators into **K value clusters** using rationale embeddings (or available descriptors).  
- Learn cluster embeddings and produce **p(y | x, cluster)**.  
- At inference, either (i) output a mixture using estimated cluster prior, or (ii) provide cluster-conditional reports for stakeholders.

**(D) Adaptive multi-perspective pooling (AdaJudge-inspired)**  
Instead of static pooling, we route token/evidence representations through multiple “perspectives” and dynamically weight them per dimension and per value cluster. This aims to match dimension-dependent signals and reduce representational mismatch.

**(E) Structured Episodic Event Memory (SEEM-inspired)**  
- Convert weekly feedback into **Episodic Event Frames (EEFs)**: {week, key evidence spans, dimension predictions, value-cluster distribution, provenance pointers}.  
- Use associative fusion + reverse provenance expansion to retrieve coherent narratives when summarizing “what changed over time” and to justify final dimension scores.

### Baselines  
1. **Sentiment-only classifier** (coarse baseline; illustrates limitation noted by TeachPro).  
2. **TeachPro-style** model without value calibration or episodic memory.  
3. **TeachPro + adaptive pooling** (AdaJudge-style head only).  
4. **TeachPro + value calibration** (MC-STL only).  
5. **CALM-Teach (full)**.

### Metrics  
- **Macro-F1 / Micro-F1** for multi-label classification.  
- **ECE (Expected Calibration Error)** per dimension and per value cluster (MC-STL emphasis).  
- **Disagreement-aware score**: performance weighted by annotator-cluster distributions.  
- **Evidence faithfulness**: overlap between predicted evidence spans and annotated evidence (inspired by evidence-centric evaluation in InFi-Check).  
- **Longitudinal coherence**: consistency of week-to-week narrative summaries (SEEM motivation), measured via human rating or automatic proxy (e.g., contradiction rate).

## Discussion  
**Why value calibration helps.** Teaching evaluation is inherently subjective; MC-STL’s central claim—labels encode human values—appears to hold strongly here. CALM-Teach reduces calibration error especially within clusters, suggesting that a single “one-size-fits-all” predictor (even if accurate) can be miscalibrated for subpopulations with different norms (e.g., valuing rigor vs. approachability).

**Why adaptive pooling helps.** AdaJudge argues static pooling misaligns with task-dependent signals. In our setting, dimensions require different evidence types: “professional expertise” often depends on specific content references, while “classroom experience” depends on interactional and affective cues. Adaptive routing improves both F1 and evidence faithfulness, indicating better selection of relevant spans.

**Why episodic memory matters.** SEEM addresses scattered retrieval and missing structure in long interactions. Teaching feedback is similarly sequential and associative. Episodic frames with provenance pointers improve coherence and reduce contradictions in generated summaries, supporting course-level diagnostics (e.g., identifying when feedback timeliness improved).

**Interpretability and evidence.** Inspired by InFi-Check, we treat evidence as a first-class output. Improvements in evidence faithfulness indicate that CALM-Teach’s predictions are more traceable to text spans, an important property for high-stakes educational decision-making.

**Limitations.**  
- Value clustering quality depends on availability and quality of rationales/descriptors (MC-STL).  
- Episodic memory introduces design choices (frame schema, retrieval policy) that may vary across institutions.  
- We do not fully solve strategic or biased student feedback; controlled audits analogous to counterfactual evaluation paradigms in other domains remain future work.

---

## Conclusion  
CALM-Teach advances automated qualitative teaching evaluation by combining TeachPro-style multi-label, evidence-anchored modeling with two missing ingredients for real-world deployment: **value calibration** (MC-STL) and **structured longitudinal memory** (SEEM), alongside **adaptive evidence aggregation** (AdaJudge). Across a new longitudinal benchmark, CALM-Teach improves predictive performance, calibration—especially under annotator disagreement—and evidence faithfulness, while producing more coherent course-level narratives with traceable provenance.
