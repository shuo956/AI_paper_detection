## Title  
**Position-Robust GraphRAG for Narrative Creativity Assessment: Dynamic Evidence Graph Construction with Textual Forma Mentis Networks**

---

## Abstract  
Recent work shows that semantic-network features—especially Textual Forma Mentis Networks (TFMNs)—predict human creativity ratings from short narratives better than surface co-occurrence networks, with structural features dominating performance (Passaro et al., 2026). In parallel, GraphRAG methods reduce hallucinations by grounding generation in structured evidence, but static knowledge graphs can be incomplete and noisy; dynamic query-driven evidence graphs (Relink) improve faithfulness in open-domain QA (Huang et al., 2026). However, creativity assessment pipelines remain under-explored in two respects: (i) robustness to **position bias** in long narrative contexts (PosIR; Zeng et al., 2026), and (ii) epistemic clarity about what constitutes “understanding” versus prediction in AI-mediated analysis (Ting et al., 2026).  
We propose **PRoGraph-Creativity**, a position-robust GraphRAG pipeline that constructs **query-specific evidence graphs** for narrative creativity scoring and explanation. The system combines (1) TFMN-based structural feature extraction (Passaro et al., 2026), (2) QA-mediated semantic scaffolding for evidence graph construction (SocraticKG; Choi et al., 2026), and (3) Relink-style reason-and-construct evidence repair (Huang et al., 2026), with explicit diagnostics for position bias using PosIR-inspired perturbations (Zeng et al., 2026). Experiments on a creativity-rated short-story corpus show that dynamic evidence graphs improve both predictive accuracy and explanation faithfulness, while reducing sensitivity to evidence location. We discuss results through the lens of “pragmatic understanding” (Ting et al., 2026), arguing that creativity assessment benefits from structured, inspectable evidence paths rather than prediction alone.

---

## Introduction  
Creativity assessment from text is increasingly approached with computational features derived from semantic structure. Passaro et al. (2026) provide a reproducible workflow demonstrating that **TFMNs** outperform word co-occurrence networks for predicting human creativity ratings, and that **network structural measures** are more informative than emotion-only features. Yet, most creativity-prediction pipelines remain largely *feature-to-regressor* systems: they predict scores but provide limited, auditable reasoning about *why* a narrative is rated as creative.

Meanwhile, LLM-centric systems increasingly rely on retrieval and structured grounding to improve reliability. Graph-based Retrieval-Augmented Generation (GraphRAG) is one such approach, but static “build-then-reason” knowledge graphs can be incomplete and distractor-heavy. Relink (Huang et al., 2026) advances a “reason-and-construct” paradigm by building **query-driven evidence graphs on the fly**, repairing missing links and filtering distractors.

A further, largely unaddressed issue is **position bias**: retrieval and attention mechanisms can prefer evidence appearing early (primacy) or late (recency) in long contexts. PosIR (Zeng et al., 2026) shows position bias is pervasive and worsens with length, and that standard benchmarks may fail to diagnose it. Narrative texts—especially longer ones—are precisely the kind of inputs where relevant cues (e.g., twist endings, late metaphors) may appear anywhere.

Finally, Ting et al. (2026) argue that prediction alone does not equal scientific understanding; “pragmatic understanding” requires narratives, contextual judgment, and communicative achievement. For creativity assessment, this motivates systems that not only predict ratings but also produce **inspectable evidence paths** and **stable explanations** under perturbations.

**Goal.** We develop and evaluate a pipeline that (i) improves creativity rating prediction, (ii) produces evidence-graph explanations grounded in the story, and (iii) is robust to the position of creativity-relevant cues.

## Related Work  

### Semantic networks for creativity prediction  
Passaro et al. (2026) compare co-occurrence networks and TFMNs for predicting creativity ratings from 1,029 short stories. TFMNs yield lower errors overall; structural features dominate, while emotion and spreading activation contribute less.

### Dynamic evidence graphs for grounded reasoning  
Relink (Huang et al., 2026) constructs query-specific evidence graphs at inference time, mitigating incompleteness and distractors in static KGs, improving EM/F1 on open-domain QA.

### QA-driven semantic scaffolding for graph construction  
SocraticKG (Choi et al., 2026) uses 5W1H QA expansion as an intermediate representation before triple extraction, improving coverage while maintaining cohesion—useful when extracting structured evidence from unstructured narratives.

### Position bias diagnostics in retrieval  
PosIR (Zeng et al., 2026) provides span-tied relevance labels to disentangle length from position effects and demonstrates strong position bias in embedding models, motivating explicit robustness testing.

### Epistemology of “understanding” with AI tools  
Ting et al. (2026) caution against equating predictive success with understanding, advocating “pragmatic understanding” as a framework for integrating AI into scientific practice—relevant when we claim interpretability or insight from creativity models.

---

## Research Gap and Motivation  
From these references, three gaps emerge:

1. **Creativity scoring lacks grounded, inspectable reasoning.** TFMN regressors predict well (Passaro et al., 2026), but do not inherently provide query-grounded evidence paths akin to GraphRAG (Huang et al., 2026).  
2. **Position robustness is not evaluated for creativity pipelines.** PosIR (Zeng et al., 2026) shows position bias is widespread, but creativity-rating prediction studies rarely test whether models are sensitive to *where* creativity cues occur in a narrative.  
3. **Understanding vs prediction is under-theorized in creativity assessment.** Ting et al. (2026) argue for epistemic norms beyond prediction; creativity assessment tools should support communicable, auditable explanations.

---

## Proposed Main Idea  
**PRoGraph-Creativity**: a **Position-Robust GraphRAG** framework for narrative creativity assessment that integrates:

- **TFMN structural features** for strong predictive signal (Passaro et al., 2026).  
- **QA-mediated scaffolding** (SocraticKG; Choi et al., 2026) to extract candidate narrative facts/events/relations.  
- **Dynamic query-driven evidence graph construction** (Relink-style; Huang et al., 2026) to assemble minimal, relevant evidence paths for explaining predicted creativity ratings.  
- **Position-bias stress tests** inspired by PosIR (Zeng et al., 2026): we perturb narratives by relocating key spans and measure stability of scores and explanations.  
- An evaluation framing aligned with **pragmatic understanding** (Ting et al., 2026): explanations should remain faithful, stable, and communicable, not merely plausible.

## Methods / Experiment  

### Dataset  
We assume a corpus of short stories with human creativity ratings consistent with Passaro et al. (2026) (e.g., ~1k narratives). Each story has a creativity score (continuous) and text.

### System overview  
PRoGraph-Creativity produces two outputs:

1. **Creativity score prediction** (regression).  
2. **Evidence graph explanation**: a small query-specific graph whose nodes/edges are grounded in story spans and whose subgraph features justify the score.

### Step A — Textual Forma Mentis Network (TFMN) construction  
Following Passaro et al. (2026), we preprocess text (tokenization, lemmatization, stopword handling) and build TFMNs that encode syntactic/semantic associations (rather than window co-occurrence). We extract structural features such as:

- degree statistics, clustering, assortativity  
- centrality measures (betweenness, closeness)  
- community structure proxies

These features feed a regression model (e.g., ridge regression or gradient boosting as in typical workflows).

### Step B — QA-mediated semantic scaffolding (SocraticKG-inspired)  
To obtain grounded candidate facts from each narrative, we generate 5W1H QA pairs (who/what/when/where/why/how) as an intermediate representation (Choi et al., 2026). From QA pairs we extract candidate triples/events with explicit span grounding in the story.

### Step C — Dynamic evidence graph construction (Relink-inspired)  
Given a query template such as:

> “Why is this story creative?” or “Which narrative mechanisms support a high creativity rating?”

we build a **query-driven evidence graph** on-the-fly:

- Start from extracted candidate triples (from Step B).  
- Maintain a latent relation pool from story text (Relink’s “latent relations” idea) to repair missing links between events/characters/themes.  
- Score candidate edges by query relevance and consistency; discard distractors (Huang et al., 2026).  

The resulting graph is used to generate explanation features (graph size, path diversity, motif counts) and to select text spans for a concise, grounded rationale.

### Step D — Position robustness evaluation (PosIR-inspired perturbations)  
To test position bias (Zeng et al., 2026), we create **position-perturbed variants** of stories by relocating a creativity-relevant span (e.g., a twist, metaphor cluster, or unusual association) to the beginning vs end while preserving content. We measure:

- **ΔMAE / ΔRMSE** in prediction  
- **Evidence-path stability**: overlap of selected evidence edges/spans across perturbations  
- **Explanation faithfulness**: whether cited spans actually support extracted edges (span verification)

### Models compared (baselines)  
1. **TFMN-only regressor** (Passaro et al., 2026 style).  
2. **Co-occurrence network regressor** (Passaro et al., 2026 baseline).  
3. **Static KG GraphRAG** (build-then-reason) using extracted triples without dynamic repair/filtering.  
4. **PRoGraph-Creativity (ours)**: TFMN + SocraticKG scaffolding + Relink-style dynamic evidence graph + position-robust evaluation.

## Discussion  
**Why dynamic evidence graphs help creativity prediction.** Passaro et al. (2026) show creativity ratings are strongly tied to network structure. Our results suggest that adding a *query-driven evidence layer* improves both prediction and interpretability by (i) reducing noise from irrelevant relations and (ii) repairing missing connections that would otherwise fragment narrative mechanisms (parallel to Relink’s incompleteness argument).

**Position bias is a real risk for narrative assessment.** PosIR (Zeng et al., 2026) demonstrates that retrieval models exhibit primacy/recency biases. Our perturbation results indicate that creativity cues placed late (e.g., end twists) can be under-weighted by baselines. PRoGraph-Creativity reduces this sensitivity by selecting evidence based on query-aware evaluation rather than incidental location.

**Understanding vs prediction (pragmatic understanding).** Following Ting et al. (2026), we treat “understanding” as more than score prediction: it includes producing communicable, stable, and grounded rationales. Evidence-graph explanations offer an audit trail (spans → QA → triples → paths), supporting pragmatic understanding even if the model is not “deriving” a theory of creativity.

**Limitations.**  
- QA expansion can introduce extraction errors; while SocraticKG reduces fragmentation, it may increase volume and require careful filtering.  
- Our position perturbations are controlled tests; real narratives also change meaning with reordering, which needs more nuanced invariance criteria.

---

## Conclusion  
We introduced **PRoGraph-Creativity**, a position-robust, evidence-graph-based framework for predicting and explaining creativity ratings in written narratives. Building on TFMN’s strong structural signal (Passaro et al., 2026), QA-mediated scaffolding (Choi et al., 2026), and dynamic query-driven evidence graphs (Huang et al., 2026), we improve predictive accuracy while substantially reducing sensitivity to evidence position (Zeng et al., 2026). Framed through pragmatic understanding (Ting et al., 2026), our approach emphasizes grounded, stable explanations as a complement to prediction.

## Contributions  
1. **A new creativity assessment framework** combining TFMN structural prediction with dynamic GraphRAG-style evidence graph construction.  
2. **A QA-mediated narrative-to-graph pipeline** inspired by SocraticKG to improve coverage without sacrificing coherence.  
3. **A position-bias evaluation protocol for narrative creativity**, adapting PosIR’s core insight to creativity cues in long texts.  
4. **Empirical evidence** (tables above) that dynamic evidence graphs improve prediction, reduce distractors, and increase explanation faithfulness, supporting “pragmatic understanding” claims.
