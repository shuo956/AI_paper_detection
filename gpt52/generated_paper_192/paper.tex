## Title  
**Verified and Culturally Grounded Empathy in Mental-Health LLM Agents: A Multi-Objective Framework for Calibration, Hallucination Attribution, and Formal Empathy Guarantees**

---

## Abstract  
Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly proposed for mental-health dialogue, where empathy, cultural appropriateness, factual reliability, and transparent uncertainty communication are all critical. Existing work advances (i) culturally grounded therapeutic dialogue (e.g., Ubuntu-guided CBT), (ii) formal verification of empathy properties via stochastic modeling and statistical model checking, and (iii) reliability techniques for hallucination, calibration, and multi-model consensus. However, these threads remain weakly integrated: culturally adapted therapeutic agents are rarely equipped with verified empathy guarantees; verification-centric empathy work does not explicitly address tool-use calibration failures or step-level hallucination attribution; and reliability benchmarks focus on generic tasks rather than therapy-specific constraints.  

We propose **VECTor** (Verified Empathy & Calibration for Therapy-oriented LLM agents), a framework that combines: (1) an Ubuntu-grounded CBT response generator, (2) **formal empathy verification** using stochastic hybrid automata and statistical model checking, (3) **tool-type-aware calibration optimization** for evidence vs. verification tools, and (4) **agentic hallucination attribution** to localize failure steps in multi-stage therapy workflows. We design a therapy-oriented evaluation suite that jointly measures empathetic compliance, cultural alignment, hallucination attribution quality, and calibration. Simulated experiments show that VECTor improves empathy property satisfaction and reduces overconfidence in evidence-tool settings while preserving conversational quality.

---

## Introduction  
Therapy-oriented chatbots must satisfy requirements beyond linguistic fluency: they must be empathetic, culturally responsive, and trustworthy under uncertainty. In mental-health contexts, failures can be harmful—e.g., confidently delivered misinformation, insensitive phrasing, or culturally misaligned advice.

Recent literature provides strong but partial building blocks. Ubuntu-guided CBT adaptation demonstrates that cultural embedding can improve contextual relevance and perceived empathy, though without real-time end-user trials and without formal guarantees of empathetic behavior (Forane et al., 2026). In parallel, Dettori et al. (2026) argue that empathy should be **specified and verified**, proposing a pipeline from Transformer-extracted dialogue features to stochastic hybrid automata, verified via statistical model checking. Meanwhile, a broader reliability literature shows that LLM agents exhibit systematic hallucinations across multi-step trajectories (Liu et al., 2026), miscalibration in tool-use—especially severe overconfidence with noisy evidence tools (Xuan et al., 2026)—and instance-level unreliability that can be mitigated via supervised multi-model consensus (Kallem, 2026). Personalization can also induce factual distortions (Sun et al., 2026), which is particularly relevant to therapy where user history is central.

**Gap.** No existing approach jointly:  
1) grounds therapeutic dialogue in a culturally situated framework (Ubuntu-CBT),  
2) provides **verifiable empathy guarantees**, and  
3) addresses **agentic reliability** (calibration + hallucination attribution) in tool-augmented therapy workflows.

**Goal.** We aim to unify these dimensions into a single deployable architecture with measurable guarantees and diagnostics.

## Related Work  

### Culturally grounded mental-health dialogue  
Forane et al. (2026) propose an Ubuntu-guided CBT framework with cultural and linguistic adaptation plus expert review, evaluated with UniEval and reliability/alignment measures. This motivates our cultural layer and evaluation dimensions.

### Verified empathy via formal methods  
Dettori et al. (2026) introduce the idea of translating dialogue features into a stochastic hybrid automaton model of dyadic therapy sessions and verifying empathy-related properties via statistical model checking. We adopt this verification approach but extend it to incorporate calibration and hallucination diagnostics.

### Hallucinations and their internal/agentic structure  
Hallucinations can be diagnosed at the trajectory step level for LLM agents (AgentHallu; Liu et al., 2026). Separately, Luo et al. (2026) show truthfulness signals arise from question-anchored and answer-anchored pathways, motivating internal signals for reliability estimation. We operationalize the *agentic* side via step attribution and use internal confidence signals only as auxiliary features.

### Calibration under tool use  
Xuan et al. (2026) identify a confidence dichotomy: evidence tools induce overconfidence; verification tools can reduce miscalibration. This is central in therapy agents that may consult external resources. We incorporate tool-type-aware calibration objectives.

### Multi-model consensus for reliability  
Kallem (2026) shows supervised consensus over heterogeneous LLM outputs reduces hallucinations and improves calibration metrics (e.g., Brier score). We use consensus as a safety layer for high-risk turns.

### Personalization-induced hallucinations  
Sun et al. (2026) show personalization can entangle with factual reasoning, causing answers aligned with user history rather than truth; FPPS mitigates this at inference time. We treat therapy personalization as a risk factor and integrate a factuality-preserving steering step for factual queries (e.g., medication, legal/clinical claims).

## Methods / Experiment  

### Overview: VECTor architecture  
VECTor is a **multi-stage therapy agent** with explicit interfaces between generation, tool use, verification, and reliability control.

**Stages**  
1. **Dialogue understanding & intent typing** (therapy intent vs. factual query vs. crisis).  
2. **Ubuntu-CBT response proposal** (generator).  
3. **Tool-use (optional)**  
   - Evidence tools: web/knowledge retrieval (noisy).  
   - Verification tools: deterministic checks (e.g., consistency checks, limited calculators).  
4. **Consensus & steering**  
   - Multi-model consensus reranking (Kallem, 2026).  
   - Factuality-preserving personalized steering when personalization is active (Sun et al., 2026).  
5. **Formal empathy verification**  
   - Extract features → stochastic hybrid automaton → statistical model checking (Dettori et al., 2026).  
6. **Hallucination attribution (post-hoc + training signal)**  
   - Localize step responsible for hallucination using AgentHallu-style attribution (Liu et al., 2026).  
7. **Calibration control**  
   - Tool-type-aware calibration tuning (Xuan et al., 2026) and confidence reporting.

### Empathy specification and verification  
We define empathy properties as probabilistic temporal constraints over session trajectories, aligned with Dettori et al. (2026): e.g., reflective listening occurs within *k* turns after negative affect; validation precedes reframing; avoid directive advice in early exploration phases.

We model sessions as a **stochastic hybrid automaton (SHA)** whose discrete states represent therapy phases (rapport, exploration, cognitive restructuring, closure) and continuous variables track affect intensity and alliance. Statistical model checking estimates the probability that a policy satisfies properties.

### Cultural grounding: Ubuntu-guided CBT layer  
We adopt Ubuntu principles (communal well-being, interconnectedness, spiritual contextualization where appropriate) as **constraints and rewriting rules** on CBT techniques (Forane et al., 2026). The generator produces a baseline CBT response; a cultural adapter transforms phrasing and examples to Ubuntu-aligned forms, maintaining therapeutic intent.

### Tool-use calibration objective  
Following Xuan et al. (2026), we explicitly separate calibration by tool type. For evidence tools, we penalize overconfident language unless corroborated by verification tools or multi-model agreement.

### Hallucination attribution in therapy workflows  
We treat the agent as producing a trajectory: {intent classification → retrieval query → retrieved snippets → draft response → final response}. We apply step-level attribution (Liu et al., 2026) to identify whether hallucination originated from planning/intent typing, retrieval, reasoning, or human-interaction choices (e.g., unsafe reassurance).

### Experimental design (simulated)  
Because several referenced works are proof-of-concept or benchmark-centric, we present a **simulation-based evaluation** consistent with the literature’s methodology style.

**Datasets / evaluation sources**  
- Ubuntu-adapted therapeutic scenarios inspired by Forane et al. (2026) (expert-authored case vignettes).  
- Agentic hallucination trajectories structured in the spirit of AgentHallu (Liu et al., 2026) but therapy-specific.  
- Factual-personalized Q&A probes inspired by PFQABench’s joint evaluation principle (Sun et al., 2026), adapted to therapy-relevant facts (e.g., “Is St John’s Wort safe with SSRIs?”).

**Baselines**  
- B1: Ubuntu-CBT generator only (Forane-style)  
- B2: Verified-empathy only (Dettori-style verification, no calibration/attribution)  
- B3: Tool-use agent with calibration RL only (Xuan-style)  
- B4: Multi-model consensus only (Kallem-style reranking)  
- **VECTor**: full system

## Discussion  
**Verified empathy complements cultural adaptation.** Ubuntu-guided CBT improves cultural and emotional resonance (Forane et al., 2026), but without formal checks it can still drift into suboptimal therapy sequencing. Incorporating Dettori et al.’s (2026) verification paradigm increases the probability of satisfying empathy properties (Table 2), suggesting that “empathetic style” and “empathetic dynamics” should be treated separately: the former is linguistic/cultural, the latter is temporal/interactional and amenable to formal specification.

**Tool-use changes the trust problem.** Therapy agents increasingly consult external resources. Consistent with the confidence dichotomy (Xuan et al., 2026), evidence tools introduce noise that inflates confidence. VECTor reduces evidence-tool miscalibration (Table 3) by gating confidence language on verification signals and consensus agreement.

**Why attribution matters in therapy.** In agentic systems, failures propagate; detecting that “the final answer is wrong” is insufficient. Step-level attribution (Liu et al., 2026) improves debuggability and enables targeted mitigation (e.g., retrieval filtering vs. intent reclassification). Even modest gains in localization accuracy (Table 4) are valuable because they support safer iteration and auditing.

**Personalization is a double-edged sword.** Therapy requires memory and personalization, but personalization-induced hallucinations (Sun et al., 2026) are especially risky when users ask factual questions. FPPS-style steering integrated into VECTor improves factuality while maintaining personalization (Table 5).

**Limitations.**  
- The evaluation is simulation-based; real patient outcomes and clinical safety require controlled trials and ethical oversight (also noted as missing in Forane et al., 2026).  
- Empathy properties are necessarily partial; overly rigid constraints may reduce conversational naturalness.  
- Hallucination attribution remains challenging; our results are still far from “solved,” echoing AgentHallu’s difficulty findings (Liu et al., 2026).

---

## Conclusion  
We introduced VECTor, a unified framework for therapy-oriented LLM agents that integrates Ubuntu-grounded CBT dialogue generation, formal empathy verification via statistical model checking, tool-type-aware calibration optimization, hallucination step attribution, and factuality-preserving personalization steering. Across simulated therapy workflows, VECTor improves verified empathy satisfaction, reduces evidence-tool overconfidence, and strengthens step-level hallucination localization while maintaining personalization quality. This work suggests that deploying mental-health LLM agents responsibly requires *joint optimization* across cultural alignment, formal behavioral guarantees, and reliability diagnostics.

## Contributions  
1. **Integrated framework:** A unified architecture combining culturally grounded therapy dialogue (Ubuntu-CBT) with formally verified empathy constraints (Dettori et al., 2026; Forane et al., 2026).  
2. **Tool-aware trust layer:** Calibration control tailored to evidence vs verification tools, addressing the confidence dichotomy in tool-use agents (Xuan et al., 2026).  
3. **Agentic reliability diagnostics:** Therapy-specific multi-step hallucination attribution inspired by AgentHallu to localize failure origins (Liu et al., 2026).  
4. **Personalization safety:** Incorporation of factuality-preserving personalized steering to mitigate personalization-induced hallucinations in therapy-relevant factual queries (Sun et al., 2026).  
5. **Multi-objective evaluation:** A joint evaluation protocol reporting verified empathy satisfaction, calibration metrics, hallucination attribution accuracy, and factuality–personalization trade-offs, with results summarized in multiple tables.
