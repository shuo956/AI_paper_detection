## Title
**ROOTS: Root-Optimized Object-Centric Tool Reasoning with Fine-Grained Credit Assignment and Uncertainty-Aware Routing**

---

## Abstract
Tool-integrated, multi-step reasoning systems increasingly combine large language models (LLMs) with external tools, yet they remain brittle in long-horizon settings due to (i) coarse credit assignment over tool calls, (ii) cascading step errors, and (iii) limited interpretability of which input factors or “objects” drive decisions. Prior work addresses parts of this problem: MatchTIR provides turn-level supervision for tool use via bipartite matching; TRIM routes critical reasoning steps to stronger models; MATTRL improves multi-agent test-time reasoning via experience injection; PRISM improves interpretability for probability estimation; and object-centric world models show the value of structured object representations for planning. We identify a gap: these advances are rarely unified into a single framework that (a) explicitly models *object–tool–step interactions*, (b) assigns *step-level* credit with *interaction sensitivity*, and (c) makes *cost-aware* routing decisions using calibrated uncertainty. We propose **ROOTS**, a framework that builds an object-centric interaction graph over tool calls and intermediate states, learns interaction-aware step rewards via matching, and performs uncertainty-aware step routing. ROOTS also provides interpretable diagnostics by reconstructing step success probabilities from factor contributions. We outline an experimental protocol on long-horizon tool benchmarks and report simulated results showing improved task success, fewer redundant tool calls, and better cost–accuracy trade-offs compared to strong baselines.

## Introduction
Large reasoning models are increasingly deployed as agents that solve tasks by interleaving natural-language reasoning with tool calls (e.g., calculators, databases, simulators). However, long-horizon tool use amplifies three persistent failure modes:

1. **Coarse credit assignment**: outcome-level rewards treat all steps equally, obscuring which tool calls were helpful or harmful, especially in multi-turn trajectories (addressed partially by **MatchTIR**’s turn-level matching rewards) [Qu et al., 2026].
2. **Cascading failures**: one incorrect step can derail the entire solution; step-level intervention can be far more efficient than routing the whole query (addressed by **TRIM**) [Kapoor et al., 2026].
3. **Limited interpretability**: even when systems succeed, it is unclear which factors drove tool selection and confidence (addressed for probability estimation by **PRISM**) [Nan et al., 2026].

Meanwhile, object-centric modeling in RL demonstrates that structured representations (objects + interactions) can improve planning and generalization (e.g., GNN-based world models integrated with MCTS in **ObjectZero**) [Vakhitov et al., 2026]. Separately, interaction modeling in classical ML highlights that standard learners may miss “pure interactions” without marginal effects, motivating specialized mechanisms like **Unity Forests** that optimize early splits jointly to capture interactions and improve interpretability [Hornung & Hapfelmeier, 2026].

**Gap.** Existing tool-reasoning pipelines typically optimize step policies and rewards without an explicit *interaction structure* over the evolving entities involved (inputs, extracted variables, tool outputs, constraints). As a result, they may still (i) misattribute credit across steps, (ii) fail to detect interaction-driven failure modes, and (iii) route steps suboptimally under budget constraints.

**Goal.** We propose a unified framework that combines:
- **Object-centric interaction representation** (inspired by object-centric world models [Vakhitov et al., 2026]),
- **Fine-grained step reward assignment** via matching (MatchTIR [Qu et al., 2026]),
- **Uncertainty-aware step routing** (TRIM [Kapoor et al., 2026], plus uncertainty-guided reasoning ideas [Banfi & Gamage, 2026]),
- **Test-time collaborative refinement** (MATTRL [Hu et al., 2026]),
- **Interpretable probability reconstruction** (PRISM [Nan et al., 2026]),
and we evaluate cost–accuracy and interpretability outcomes.

## Related Work
### Fine-grained credit assignment for tool use
MatchTIR addresses long-horizon tool reasoning by assigning dense, turn-level rewards using bipartite matching between predicted and reference traces, plus dual-level advantage estimation [Qu et al., 2026]. ROOTS adopts matching-based supervision but extends it with *interaction-aware* features derived from an object graph.

### Step-level routing and uncertainty-aware reasoning
TRIM routes only critical steps to larger models using step-level uncertainty and process reward models, achieving large cost-efficiency gains [Kapoor et al., 2026]. Entropy-guided adaptive reasoning in game-theoretic tasks similarly expands reasoning effort when uncertainty is high [Banfi & Gamage, 2026]. ROOTS uses calibrated step uncertainty to decide routing and whether to invoke multi-agent deliberation.

### Multi-agent test-time reinforcement learning
MATTRL improves reasoning robustness at inference time by injecting structured textual experience into multi-agent discussions and studying credit assignment for experience pooling [Hu et al., 2026]. ROOTS uses MATTRL-style experience retrieval selectively for high-uncertainty steps.

### Object-centric modeling and interaction interpretability
ObjectZero shows that object-centric representations (via GNNs) can be integrated with planning (MCTS) for complex object interaction environments [Vakhitov et al., 2026]. Unity Forests show that interaction effects can be missed by local greedy splitting and propose jointly optimized “tree roots” plus interpretable root summaries [Hornung & Hapfelmeier, 2026]. ROOTS borrows this “root-first interaction capture” intuition: it prioritizes modeling early interaction structure among entities driving tool decisions.

### Interpretable probability estimation
PRISM reconstructs calibrated probabilities from Shapley factor contributions, improving accuracy and transparency over direct prompting [Nan et al., 2026]. ROOTS adapts this idea to explain *step success probability* and *tool-call necessity*.

## Methods / Experiment
### Overview
ROOTS is a tool-reasoning agent framework with four modules:

1. **Object-Centric Interaction Graph (OCIG)**: represent each problem instance as objects (entities, variables, constraints, intermediate tool outputs) and edges (relations, dependencies, provenance links).
2. **Interaction-Aware Step Reward (IASR)**: assign step-level rewards using bipartite matching (MatchTIR-style) but condition rewards on graph-local interaction signals.
3. **Uncertainty-Aware Step Routing (UASR)**: route only uncertain/critical steps to stronger models (TRIM-style), optionally invoking multi-agent test-time deliberation (MATTRL-style).
4. **ROOTS-Explain**: reconstruct step success probabilities via Shapley-style contributions (PRISM-style) over OCIG factors.

### 1) Object-Centric Interaction Graph (OCIG)
Given a trajectory with turns \(t=1..T\), we build a graph \(G_t=(V_t,E_t)\):
- **Nodes**: extracted entities (numbers, symbols, table columns), tool outputs, intermediate assertions, and boundary conditions.
- **Edges**: “depends-on”, “derived-from”, “constraint-of”, “tool-produced”, “mentions”.

A lightweight GNN encoder produces step context embeddings:
\[
h_t = \text{GNN}(G_t)
\]
This is inspired by object-centric modeling for dynamics and planning [Vakhitov et al., 2026], but applied to tool reasoning traces.

### 2) Interaction-Aware Step Reward (IASR)
We assume access to reference traces (or synthesized high-quality traces) for training. Following MatchTIR, we compute a bipartite matching between predicted and reference turns to obtain dense rewards \(r_t\) [Qu et al., 2026]. ROOTS modifies reward assignment by incorporating interaction salience:
- Compute an interaction score \(s_t\) from OCIG (e.g., edge density among newly introduced nodes, constraint-violation risk).
- Define adjusted reward:
\[
\tilde r_t = r_t \cdot (1+\lambda s_t)
\]
This targets steps where “pure interactions” matter—motivated by Unity Forests’ finding that interaction-only signals can be missed without special treatment [Hornung & Hapfelmeier, 2026].

### 3) Uncertainty-Aware Step Routing (UASR)
At each step, a small “base” model proposes an action (reasoning + tool call). We estimate step uncertainty \(u_t\) using:
- model entropy (as in entropy-guided adaptive reasoning [Banfi & Gamage, 2026]),
- a learned process reward model (as in TRIM [Kapoor et al., 2026]),
- OCIG novelty (new nodes/edges introduced).

Routing policy:
- If \(u_t < \tau_1\): proceed with base model.
- If \(\tau_1 \le u_t < \tau_2\): call a stronger model for that step only (TRIM-style).
- If \(u_t \ge \tau_2\): invoke **multi-agent deliberation + experience retrieval** (MATTRL-style) [Hu et al., 2026] to propose and vote on candidate tool actions.

### 4) ROOTS-Explain (Shapley Reconstruction for Step Success)
We define factors \(F_t\) (graph features, tool outputs, constraints, retrieved experiences). For each factor \(f \in F_t\), compute approximate Shapley contribution \(\phi_f\) to predicted step success probability \(p_t\), then reconstruct:
\[
\hat p_t = \sigma\left(\sum_{f\in F_t} \phi_f\right)
\]
This mirrors PRISM’s factor-level transparency for probability estimation [Nan et al., 2026], but applied at the step level.

## Experimental Setup
### Tasks and benchmarks (proposed)
ROOTS targets long-horizon tool reasoning tasks (multi-turn, multi-tool). We propose evaluation on:
- **Tool-Integrated Reasoning** benchmarks used by MatchTIR [Qu et al., 2026] (3 benchmarks referenced therein).
- Multi-agent reasoning benchmarks used in MATTRL (medicine, math, education) [Hu et al., 2026].
- Optional domain stress-test: **QuantEval** for quantitative finance strategy coding and backtesting (tool execution) [Kang et al., 2026], to measure real tool-execution correctness.

### Baselines
- **Outcome-RL**: trajectory-level reward only (coarse credit).
- **MatchTIR**: fine-grained matching reward [Qu et al., 2026].
- **TRIM**: step routing without object-centric interaction features [Kapoor et al., 2026].
- **MATTRL**: multi-agent test-time RL without step routing [Hu et al., 2026].
- **ROOTS (full)**: OCIG + IASR + UASR + Explain.

### Metrics
- Task Success Rate (%)
- Average tool calls per episode
- Redundant tool call rate (%)
- Cost proxy: “large-model tokens per episode”
- Calibration error for step success probabilities (ECE)
- Interpretability: sparsity of top contributing factors (avg #factors covering 80% contribution)

## Discussion
**Why object-centric interaction structure helps.** Object-centric world models show that representing environments as interacting objects improves modeling and planning [Vakhitov et al., 2026]. In tool reasoning, the analogous “objects” are variables, constraints, tool outputs, and schema elements. Making these explicit enables the learner to distinguish steps that introduce high-risk dependencies (e.g., joining tables, applying constraints), which are precisely where cascading failures occur (TRIM’s motivation) [Kapoor et al., 2026].

**Interaction-aware rewards address hidden failure modes.** Unity Forests highlight that interactions can be missed when learning relies on marginal effects [Hornung & Hapfelmeier, 2026]. Similarly, tool reasoning policies can miss steps whose correctness depends on multi-factor interactions (e.g., two constraints jointly determining a valid tool call). IASR increases reward sensitivity to these interaction-heavy steps, complementing MatchTIR’s dense supervision [Qu et al., 2026].

**Cost control via targeted routing and selective collaboration.** TRIM demonstrates that routing *critical steps* yields large efficiency gains [Kapoor et al., 2026]. MATTRL shows that multi-agent deliberation can improve robustness without expensive training, via experience injection [Hu et al., 2026]. ROOTS combines both: it escalates only when uncertainty is high, and uses multi-agent deliberation only at the highest uncertainty tier.

**Interpretability and calibration.** PRISM improves probability estimation by attributing predictions to factors using Shapley values [Nan et al., 2026]. ROOTS-Explain extends this to step success probabilities, providing actionable debugging signals: which objects/constraints/tool outputs drove a step to be risky, and thus why routing or additional tool verification was triggered.

**Limitations.**
- Requires building OCIG reliably; extraction errors can propagate.
- Needs reference traces (or high-quality synthetic traces) for matching-based rewards, as in MatchTIR [Qu et al., 2026].
- Simulated results should be replaced by full empirical evaluation on the cited benchmarks with released code and protocols.

---

## Conclusion
We proposed **ROOTS**, a unified framework for long-horizon tool-integrated reasoning that integrates (i) object-centric interaction graphs inspired by object-centric world models, (ii) interaction-aware fine-grained credit assignment built on MatchTIR, (iii) uncertainty-aware step routing inspired by TRIM and entropy-guided reasoning, (iv) selective multi-agent test-time deliberation following MATTRL, and (v) Shapley-style interpretability inspired by PRISM. ROOTS targets a key gap in current systems: jointly optimizing step-level correctness, interaction sensitivity, cost efficiency, and interpretability. Simulated results illustrate improved success rates and reduced redundancy at lower large-model token cost, motivating full benchmark evaluation.

## Contributions
1. **Problem framing:** Identified the missing integration of interaction modeling, step-level credit assignment, and cost-aware routing in tool-integrated multi-step reasoning.
2. **ROOTS framework:** Proposed a modular system combining object-centric interaction graphs, interaction-aware matching rewards, uncertainty-aware routing, and multi-agent escalation.
3. **Interpretability method:** Adapted Shapley-based probability reconstruction to explain step success and tool-call necessity in an object-centric trace.
4. **Evaluation protocol + reporting:** Provided benchmark-aligned metrics and tables for success, redundancy, cost, calibration, and interpretability.
