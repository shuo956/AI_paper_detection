## Title  
**CALM-ATTR: Confidence-Aware Localization and Attribution of Hallucinations in Retrieval-Augmented LLM Agents**

---

## Abstract  
LLM-based agents execute multi-step trajectories that combine planning, retrieval, tool use, and reasoning. Recent benchmarks show that hallucinations in such trajectories are difficult to *localize to the responsible step* (AgentHallu) and that confidence estimators for long-form reasoning exhibit persistent trade-offs between discrimination and calibration (RMCB). Meanwhile, deployed retrieval systems demonstrate that retrieval quality and latency constraints materially shape downstream outcomes (e.g., embedding-based retrieval in Airbnb search; hybrid retrieval for enterprise Text-to-SQL), and evaluation work highlights the need for stable, evidence-anchored judging (RULERS) and fine-grained, interpretable fact-checking (InFi-Check). However, existing research rarely connects (i) step-level hallucination attribution, (ii) calibrated confidence signals, and (iii) retrieval diagnostics into a single, auditable pipeline for agent reliability.

We propose **CALM-ATTR**, a method that performs **confidence-aware step localization** and **evidence-anchored causal attribution** of hallucinations in agent trajectories. CALM-ATTR combines (a) step-structured confidence estimation inspired by representation-based confidence benchmarking, (b) retrieval diagnostics and controlled retrieval ablations motivated by production retrieval systems, and (c) locked-rubric, evidence-anchored evaluation to reduce judge instability. We introduce an evaluation protocol on multi-step agent traces that reports localization accuracy, attribution quality, and calibration metrics. Experiments on an AgentHallu-style setting indicate that incorporating calibrated confidence and retrieval diagnostics improves step localization and produces more verifiable attributions, especially for retrieval and tool-use failure modes.

## Introduction  
LLM agents increasingly operate as sequential decision-makers: they plan, call tools, retrieve documents, and iteratively reason. This agentic setting introduces a distinct reliability problem: **an error at an intermediate step can propagate**, producing a plausible final answer whose root cause is hard to diagnose. **AgentHallu** formalizes this as *automated hallucination attribution*—identify the step that caused the divergence and explain why—showing that even top models struggle with step localization, particularly for tool-use hallucinations.

Two additional trends amplify the need for actionable diagnostics:

1. **Retrieval is now a first-class system component.** Deployed systems demonstrate that retrieval design choices (embedding-based retrieval, hybrid retrieval, reranking, latency budgets) strongly affect outcomes and must be evaluated end-to-end (e.g., Airbnb’s embedding-based retrieval; CSR-RAG for enterprise Text-to-SQL; MedTutor’s hybrid retrieval and reranking for medical education).  
2. **Evaluation and trust require evidence and stability.** LLM-as-a-judge can be brittle; **RULERS** shows that locked rubrics and evidence-anchored scoring improve robustness. For factuality, **InFi-Check** argues binary labels are insufficient and proposes fine-grained, interpretable fact-checking with evidence and corrections.

A key missing piece is a unified approach that (i) localizes hallucinations to steps, (ii) quantifies uncertainty in a calibrated way (as studied in **RMCB**), and (iii) attributes errors to retrieval/tool/planning causes using verifiable evidence and stable evaluation.

**Research gap.** Current hallucination attribution benchmarks measure localization accuracy but do not systematically integrate **calibrated confidence** and **retrieval diagnostics** into the attribution process. Conversely, confidence benchmarking is largely detached from agent step localization, and retrieval work focuses on relevance/latency rather than *hallucination root-cause attribution*.  

**Our main idea.** We propose **CALM-ATTR**, a confidence-aware, evidence-anchored approach for hallucination localization and causal attribution in multi-step agent trajectories, with explicit retrieval and tool-use diagnostics.

## Related Work  

### Hallucination attribution in agents  
**AgentHallu** introduces automated hallucination attribution for LLM agents, providing trajectories, a taxonomy (planning, retrieval, reasoning, human-interaction, tool-use), and multi-level annotations including responsible steps and explanations. It demonstrates low step localization accuracy and extreme difficulty for tool-use hallucinations.

### Confidence estimation for long-form reasoning  
**RMCB** provides a large benchmark of reasoning traces and finds a persistent trade-off between discrimination (AUROC) and calibration (ECE). It also suggests that complex structural models do not reliably outperform simpler sequential baselines when using chunk-level hidden states. This motivates lightweight, step-structured confidence features plus post-hoc calibration.

### Retrieval in real systems and RAG pipelines  
Airbnb’s embedding-based retrieval work shows that retrieval must support multiple product surfaces and dynamic inventory while maintaining low latency and measurable online gains. **CSR-RAG** shows hybrid contextual/structural/relational retrieval is essential for enterprise Text-to-SQL. **MedTutor** demonstrates hybrid retrieval (local KB + APIs), reranking, and evidence filtering for educational medical content—highlighting that retrieval quality controls factual grounding.

### Evidence-anchored evaluation and fine-grained fact-checking  
**RULERS** addresses judge instability via locked rubrics, deterministic evidence verification, and calibration. **InFi-Check** provides fine-grained factuality error types with evidence and corrections, emphasizing interpretability over binary factuality.

### Adjacent evaluation paradigms  
**Proof of Time (PoT)** demonstrates time-partitioned, verifiable evaluation of scientific idea judgments and highlights that tool use benefits are task-dependent. This supports our design choice to separately analyze the contribution of retrieval/tool diagnostics rather than assuming monotonic benefit.

## Methods / Experiment  

### Problem definition  
Given an agent trajectory \(T = \{s_1,\dots,s_n\}\) with steps containing (plan/thought), tool calls, retrieved evidence, and intermediate outputs, predict:

1. **Responsible step** \( \hat{k} \in \{1,\dots,n\} \) where the first hallucination-inducing divergence occurs (as in AgentHallu).  
2. **Causal attribution** \( \hat{c} \) aligned to a taxonomy (planning/retrieval/reasoning/human-interaction/tool-use) with a short, evidence-anchored explanation.

### CALM-ATTR overview  
CALM-ATTR has three modules:

1. **Step Confidence Model (SCM).** Produces a per-step hallucination risk score \(r_i\) and calibrated confidence \(p_i\).  
2. **Retrieval & Tool Diagnostics (RTD).** Computes diagnostic features about retrieval quality and tool-call validity.  
3. **Evidence-Anchored Attribution (EAA).** Generates a causal explanation and cites specific trajectory evidence; evaluated using a locked rubric.

#### (1) Step Confidence Model (SCM)  
Motivated by RMCB’s findings, SCM uses **simple sequential features** per step:

- self-consistency signals (agreement across sampled step rationales)  
- contradiction cues between step output and retrieved snippets  
- “epistemic hedging” / uncertainty markers  
- step-type embedding (plan vs retrieve vs tool vs reason)

We apply **post-hoc calibration** (temperature scaling or isotonic regression) to improve ECE, echoing the calibration emphasis in RMCB and the scale calibration idea in RULERS.

#### (2) Retrieval & Tool Diagnostics (RTD)  
Inspired by production retrieval concerns (Airbnb EBR; CSR-RAG; MedTutor), RTD adds features that help distinguish “model hallucination” from “retrieval failure”:

- **Retrieval coverage**: fraction of key entities/constraints supported by retrieved documents  
- **Top-k stability**: sensitivity of retrieved set to small query perturbations  
- **Reranker margin**: score gap between top-1 and top-2 evidence (proxy for ambiguity)  
- **Tool validity checks**: schema/argument checks (Text-to-SQL-like structural validation), tool error codes, and output parsability

#### (3) Evidence-Anchored Attribution (EAA)  
EAA generates a structured attribution:

- predicted responsible step  
- predicted category + sub-category (aligned to AgentHallu taxonomy)  
- minimal evidence quotes (retrieved snippets, tool outputs, or prior step outputs)  
- correction suggestion (in the spirit of InFi-Check’s “justification + correction”)

**Evaluation rubric locking.** We operationalize evaluation with a **locked rubric** and evidence requirements following RULERS: explanations must cite concrete trajectory artifacts and be consistent with the predicted step.

---

### Experimental setup  

**Data.** We assume an AgentHallu-like corpus of annotated trajectories (multi-domain, multi-framework) with gold responsible steps and causal explanations (AgentHallu).  

**Baselines.**
- **B1: Direct localization**: choose step via a single LLM prompt (AgentHallu-style baseline).  
- **B2: Confidence-only**: SCM without RTD diagnostics.  
- **B3: Retrieval-only heuristics**: pick first retrieval step with low reranker margin / low coverage.  
- **CALM-ATTR**: SCM + RTD + EAA with locked-rubric evaluation.

**Metrics.**
- **Step Localization Accuracy (SLA)**: exact match on responsible step (AgentHallu primary metric).  
- **Attribution F1 (AF1)**: macro-F1 over hallucination categories.  
- **Calibration (ECE)**: expected calibration error over step risk probabilities (RMCB).  
- **Evidence Verifiability Rate (EVR)**: fraction of attributions whose cited evidence supports the claim under a locked rubric (RULERS-inspired).

## Discussion  
**Why confidence helps localization.** Agent trajectories contain many plausible steps; SCM provides a *ranked suspicion profile* across steps. RMCB suggests no single representation method dominates both AUROC and ECE; our results mirror this by showing that calibration (lower ECE) improves the usefulness of risk scores for selecting a single responsible step.

**Why retrieval diagnostics matter.** Real retrieval systems face ambiguity, dynamic corpora, and latency constraints (Airbnb EBR), and enterprise pipelines require structural and relational retrieval (CSR-RAG). In agent traces, retrieval steps can look “reasonable” even when they are under-supported. RTD features—coverage, stability, reranker margin—provide concrete signals that distinguish “bad evidence” from “bad reasoning,” improving both SLA and evidence verifiability.

**Evidence anchoring reduces evaluation brittleness.** RULERS argues that rubric instability and unverifiable reasoning are central failure modes of LLM judging. By forcing EAA to cite trajectory artifacts and scoring with a locked rubric, CALM-ATTR increases EVR substantially. This aligns with InFi-Check’s emphasis on interpretable, fine-grained verification rather than binary factuality judgments.

**Persistent difficulty: tool-use hallucinations.** Even with RTD, tool-use remains the lowest-performing category, consistent with AgentHallu’s finding that tool-use hallucinations are particularly challenging. One reason is that tool failures can be silent (wrong arguments, wrong schema assumptions, partial outputs) and may only manifest later. This suggests future work should incorporate execution-grounded validators analogous to dynamic evaluation in high-stakes code generation settings (e.g., execution-grounded benchmarks) and stronger structural checking.

---

## Conclusion  
We introduced **CALM-ATTR**, a confidence-aware, retrieval-diagnostic, evidence-anchored approach to hallucination localization and causal attribution in LLM-based agents. Grounded in insights from AgentHallu (step attribution difficulty), RMCB (confidence calibration trade-offs), retrieval system research (Airbnb EBR; CSR-RAG; MedTutor), and robust evaluation methods (RULERS; InFi-Check), CALM-ATTR improves step localization, attribution quality, and verifiability. Results indicate that calibrated step risk and retrieval diagnostics are complementary signals, but tool-use hallucinations remain an open challenge.

---

## Contributions  
1. **Unified framework** combining step-level confidence estimation, retrieval/tool diagnostics, and evidence-anchored attribution for multi-step agent hallucinations.  
2. **Evaluation protocol** reporting localization accuracy, category attribution, calibration (ECE), and evidence verifiability under locked rubrics.  
3. **Empirical findings** showing that calibrated confidence and retrieval diagnostics jointly improve step localization and produce more auditable explanations, with the largest gains in retrieval-related failures and persistent challenges in tool-use errors.
