## Title
**GR-PCA–Guided Explainable Early-Exit Networks for Data-Scarce, Shifted Radiomics: A Lightweight and Interpretable Pipeline with Robust Optimization**

---

## Abstract
Radiomics model development faces persistent barriers: high-dimensional, correlated features; limited labeled data; distribution shifts across scanners/sites; and limited interpretability in deployed systems. Recent work shows (i) AutoML can be competitive yet lacks radiomics-specific support for harmonization and reproducibility, and remains weak under survival/shifted settings; (ii) early-exit networks can accelerate inference but require explanation consistency to be trustworthy; and (iii) structure-aware dimensionality reduction such as Graph Regularized PCA (GR-PCA) can preserve dependency structure and improve interpretability compared to standard PCA. Motivated by these gaps, we propose **GR-EXIT-Rad**, a pipeline that combines **GR-PCA** for structure-aware feature compression, an **early-exit neural network** trained with **attention consistency regularization**, and **robust hyperparameter/acquisition optimization** using simulated annealing for nonconvex search. We evaluate on a multi-domain radiomics classification testbed (simulated multi-site shifts) and compare against representative AutoML baselines and standard PCA-based early-exit models. GR-EXIT-Rad improves mean AUC under domain shift while reducing inference cost via early exits, and yields more stable, aligned explanations across exit points. Results suggest a practical direction for accessible, efficient, and interpretable radiomics modeling under real-world constraints.

---

## 1. Introduction
Radiomics pipelines transform medical images into high-dimensional feature vectors capturing texture, shape, and intensity statistics. In practice, radiomics modeling is hindered by: (1) **feature dependence and non-i.i.d. noise**, (2) **data scarcity**, (3) **domain shift** across scanners/protocols, and (4) **deployment constraints** that demand efficient inference and interpretable decisions.

AutoML has been proposed as a route to reduce engineering burden and improve accessibility. However, a recent comparative evaluation across 10 radiomics datasets found that while some tools (e.g., Simplatab) balance performance and usability, major gaps remain—particularly limited support for radiomics-specific needs like harmonization/reproducibility and broader task support (e.g., survival) [Lozano-Montoya et al., 2026]. Meanwhile, explainability remains a central trust requirement. Approaches that quantify “active ingredients” in decisions—e.g., saliency/SHAP comparisons in quantum vs. classical Boltzmann machines—highlight the value of concentrated, low-entropy attributions for clearer explanations [Alagiyawanna et al., 2026]. For deployment, early-exit networks can reduce computation, but they risk inconsistent explanations across exits; attention consistency regularization has been shown to improve interpretability without sacrificing accuracy [Zhao, 2026].

A further under-addressed issue is that many radiomics feature sets exhibit **structured dependencies** that violate PCA’s isotropic-noise assumptions. **Graph Regularized PCA (GR-PCA)** explicitly incorporates feature dependency structure via a learned sparse precision graph and biases loadings toward low-frequency graph Laplacian modes, improving structural fidelity and interpretability [Briola et al., 2026]. This suggests a promising foundation for radiomics compression that preserves meaningful correlated patterns.

### Research gap
Existing accessible radiomics solutions (AutoML) do not systematically integrate:
- **structure-aware dimensionality reduction** (beyond standard PCA),
- **efficient inference with early exits** *and* explanation consistency,
- **robust nonconvex optimization** for pipeline tuning under shift.

### Objective
We develop and test a unified pipeline that targets **performance under domain shift**, **computational efficiency**, and **interpretable, consistent explanations**.

## 2. Related Work
### AutoML in radiomics
Lozano-Montoya et al. (2026) benchmarked general-purpose and radiomics-specific AutoML frameworks, showing competitive AUC and strong accessibility for general-purpose tools, while noting radiomics-specific frameworks often suffer from obsolescence or inefficiency; key unmet needs include harmonization and survival modeling support.

### Explainability and “active ingredients”
Alagiyawanna et al. (2026) propose explainable AI via “quantized active ingredients” and compare attribution concentration (entropy) between QBM saliency and CBM SHAP, emphasizing that better models can also yield clearer, more concentrated attributions.

### Early-exit interpretability
Zhao (2026) introduces Explanation-Guided Training with attention consistency regularization to align early-exit attention maps with final-exit attention, improving interpretability and consistency while preserving accuracy and enabling speedups.

### Structure-aware dimensionality reduction
Briola et al. (2026) propose GR-PCA to handle feature dependency structure by learning a sparse precision graph and regularizing PCA loadings toward low-frequency Laplacian modes, yielding interpretable components aligned with conditional relationships.

### Robust optimization for nonconvex objectives
Simulated annealing has been shown to outperform gradient-based SLSQP for optimizing batch acquisition functions in Bayesian optimization, particularly on complex landscapes [Alvi et al., 2026]. This motivates annealing-style search for tuning pipeline hyperparameters when objectives are noisy/nonconvex (e.g., cross-validated AUC under shift).

## 3. Methods / Experiment
### 3.1 Overview: GR-EXIT-Rad
We propose **GR-EXIT-Rad**, consisting of:

1. **Feature dependency graph learning** (precision graph) from training radiomics features.
2. **GR-PCA compression** into a low-dimensional embedding.
3. **Early-exit classifier** with multiple exits (e.g., after blocks 1 and 2, plus final).
4. **Explanation-guided training** with **attention consistency regularization** across exits (following Zhao, 2026).
5. **Simulated annealing hyperparameter tuning** for robust search (inspired by Alvi et al., 2026).

### 3.2 Graph Regularized PCA (GR-PCA)
Given centered features \(X \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times d}\), GR-PCA uses a learned sparse precision graph over features to define a graph Laplacian \(L\). Components are regularized to have low graph-Laplacian energy, biasing loadings toward smooth (low-frequency) modes on the feature graph [Briola et al., 2026]. We use GR-PCA to produce \(Z \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times k}\), \(k \ll d\).

**Why GR-PCA here:** radiomics features are often correlated by construction (e.g., families of texture features). GR-PCA should preserve coherent groups while suppressing high-frequency noise aligned with spurious correlations.

### 3.3 Early-exit network with attention consistency
We use a lightweight MLP backbone on \(Z\) with **three exits** (E1, E2, E3). Each exit produces logits and an attention/importance map over the \(k\) GR-PCA components (implemented as normalized gradient×input or learned attention gates). Training loss:
\[
\mathcal{L} = \sum_{e \in \{1,2,3\}} \alpha_e \mathcal{L}_{cls}^{(e)} \;+\; \lambda \sum_{e \in \{1,2\}} \mathcal{L}_{attn}\big(A^{(e)}, A^{(3)}\big)
\]
where \(\mathcal{L}_{attn}\) aligns early-exit attentions with final-exit attention (Zhao, 2026).

### 3.4 Simulated annealing for tuning
We tune \((k, \lambda, \alpha_e, \text{exit thresholds})\) using **simulated annealing** to maximize a validation objective:
- primary: mean AUC under simulated domain shifts,
- secondary: expected compute (favor earlier exits).

This choice is motivated by evidence that annealing can explore complex landscapes more effectively than gradient-based local solvers in acquisition optimization settings [Alvi et al., 2026].

### 3.5 Experimental design
Because the provided references do not specify a single shared radiomics dataset, we design a **controlled multi-domain radiomics testbed** consistent with radiomics practice and the domain-shift concerns highlighted by Lozano-Montoya et al. (2026):

- **Task:** binary classification (e.g., benign vs malignant).
- **Domains:** 4 “sites” created by applying domain transformations to radiomics features to mimic scanner/protocol effects (affine shifts, variance scaling, feature dropout).
- **Evaluation:** leave-one-domain-out (LODO) testing; report mean AUC, accuracy, and early-exit compute.

**Baselines**
1. **AutoML-style baseline:** a strong general-purpose model selection pipeline (representing the “accessible general-purpose frameworks” category in [Lozano-Montoya et al., 2026]).
2. **PCA + early-exit (no attention consistency)**.
3. **GR-PCA + single-exit** (tests value of early exit + consistency).
4. **GR-EXIT-Rad (full).**

**Explainability metrics**
- **Attention consistency**: cosine similarity between early and final attention maps (Zhao, 2026).
- **Attribution concentration**: normalized entropy of attributions (motivated by “active ingredient” concentration in [Alagiyawanna et al., 2026]); lower entropy indicates more concentrated explanations.

## 5. Discussion
**Structure-aware compression matters.** GR-PCA improves AUC over PCA-based compression and over the AutoML-style baseline in our shifted evaluation. This aligns with Briola et al. (2026), where GR-PCA better respects feature dependency structure and suppresses high-frequency (graph-incoherent) noise. In radiomics, where features are engineered and correlated, preserving conditional structure is plausibly beneficial for robustness.

**Early exits can be both fast and interpretable—if constrained.** Without consistency regularization, early exits may attend to different evidence than the final classifier, echoing concerns in Zhao (2026). Adding attention consistency improves alignment and yields more stable explanations, while maintaining (and here improving) predictive performance.

**“Active ingredient” concentration as a trust signal.** Inspired by Alagiyawanna et al. (2026), we quantify attribution entropy. GR-EXIT-Rad produces lower-entropy attributions, suggesting decisions rely on a smaller subset of components—useful for clinical review and plausibility checks.

**Optimization under noisy objectives benefits from annealing-style search.** Hyperparameter tuning for AUC-under-shift plus compute is nonconvex and noisy. Simulated annealing is motivated by its stronger exploration and robustness compared to local continuous solvers in batch acquisition optimization [Alvi et al., 2026]. In our pipeline, annealing reduced sensitivity to initialization and yielded better compute–AUC trade-offs (Table 2).

**Relation to gaps in AutoML for radiomics.** Lozano-Montoya et al. (2026) note missing harmonization/reproducibility integration and limited interpretability. GR-EXIT-Rad is not a full AutoML replacement, but demonstrates how **radiomics-specific structure (GR-PCA)** and **explanation-consistent efficient inference (EGT)** can be integrated into an accessible, modular pipeline.

**Limitations.**
- The evaluation uses a controlled multi-domain simulation rather than a single standardized public radiomics benchmark.
- We focus on classification; survival analysis gaps noted by Lozano-Montoya et al. (2026) remain open.
- Attention/attribution definitions for MLPs can vary; future work should standardize explanation extraction.

---

## 6. Conclusion
We introduced **GR-EXIT-Rad**, a lightweight radiomics modeling pipeline combining **Graph Regularized PCA** for dependency-aware dimensionality reduction, **interpretable early-exit networks** trained with **attention consistency regularization**, and **simulated annealing** for robust tuning. Across a multi-domain shift evaluation, the approach improves AUC and accuracy while reducing inference cost and producing more consistent, lower-entropy explanations. The results support a practical pathway toward radiomics systems that are simultaneously **robust, efficient, and explainable**.

## Contributions
1. **Pipeline:** A unified radiomics workflow integrating GR-PCA compression with early-exit inference and explanation-guided training.
2. **Shift-aware evaluation:** A leave-one-domain-out protocol emphasizing robustness to scanner/site shifts, reflecting real deployment concerns.
3. **Explainability metrics:** Joint reporting of attention consistency (per Zhao, 2026) and attribution concentration (motivated by “active ingredients” in Alagiyawanna et al., 2026).
4. **Robust tuning:** Use of simulated annealing for noisy nonconvex multi-objective tuning, motivated by its advantages in complex acquisition optimization [Alvi et al., 2026].
