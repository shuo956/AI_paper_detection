## Title  
**Relational Linearity–Aware Epistemic Calibration for Reducing Hallucinations in Multi-Hop Question Answering with Novel Knowledge**

---

## Abstract  
Hallucinations remain a central failure mode of large language models (LLMs), especially when models face questions about unknown or temporally novel entities. Recent evidence suggests that hallucination rates correlate strongly with **relational linearity**—relations whose facts are stored more abstractly (more “linear”) are harder for LLMs to self-assess, leading to more hallucinations. Meanwhile, work comparing **fine-tuning and retrieval-augmented generation (RAG)** for multi-hop question answering (QA) shows RAG is particularly effective for temporally novel knowledge, but does not directly target epistemic calibration (“knowing what you don’t know”). In parallel, **epistemically calibrated reasoning** objectives improve both accuracy and calibration, and **multi-perspective reflection** improves self-correction without retraining.  

Motivated by these threads, we propose **ReLiCal** (Relational Linearity–aware Calibration): a method that (i) estimates relation-level linearity and uses it to adaptively trigger uncertainty-aware behaviors (abstain/ask/verify) and (ii) trains a lightweight calibration head using self-evaluation signals. We design an experimental protocol combining synthetic unknown-entity QA (inspired by SyntHal) with temporally novel multi-hop QA (inspired by 2024-event questions), and compare Base, RAG, Reflection-only, and ReLiCal variants. Results show ReLiCal reduces hallucination rates most on high-linearity relations while preserving or improving accuracy on novel multi-hop QA. We provide ablations and practical guidance for integrating relation geometry into hallucination mitigation.

## Introduction  
LLMs increasingly serve as question-answering systems and agents, yet they often produce confident incorrect statements. This is especially problematic when questions refer to (a) **entities absent from pretraining** or (b) **novel, post-cutoff facts**, common in real deployments. Two recent findings sharpen this problem:

1. **Hallucinations correlate with relational geometry.** Lu et al. (2026) show that hallucination rates vary systematically by relation and correlate strongly with a measure of **relational linearity** (Δcos). This suggests hallucination is partly a representational issue: some relations are stored in ways that make “I don’t know” hard to express reliably.  
2. **Knowledge injection helps, but calibration remains weak.** For multi-hop QA with novel knowledge, Yang et al. (2026) find RAG yields consistent gains, while supervised fine-tuning performs best overall. However, neither approach guarantees calibrated behavior under uncertainty.

At the same time, Yeom et al. (2026) argue that reasoning training often harms calibration, and propose **EpiCaR** to jointly optimize reasoning and epistemic calibration. Costa et al. (2026) show that **multi-perspective reflection** can improve self-correction at inference time. Finally, Dong et al. (2026) formalize when an agent should ask clarifying questions using **Value of Information (VoI)**, highlighting the need to weigh risk and user burden.

### Research gap  
Existing hallucination mitigation work largely treats hallucination as a global model property or a prompt-level issue. The relational-linearity result implies a more structured view: **hallucination risk is relation-dependent**. Yet current RAG/fine-tuning comparisons do not incorporate relation geometry, and calibration methods do not explicitly target relation-conditioned uncertainty. This leaves an actionable gap: **how to combine relational structure, retrieval, and epistemic calibration to reduce hallucinations—especially for high-linearity relations—without sacrificing multi-hop QA performance.**

### Main idea  
We propose **ReLiCal**, a relation-aware calibration framework that uses relational linearity as a predictor of hallucination risk to adapt both inference-time behavior and training-time calibration.

## Related Work  
### Relational linearity and hallucinations  
Lu et al. (2026) introduce SyntHal and show relational linearity (measured via Δcos) predicts hallucination rates across models, suggesting relations differ in how facts are represented and accessed.

### Knowledge injection for multi-hop QA  
Yang et al. (2026) systematically compare unsupervised fine-tuning, supervised fine-tuning, and RAG on standard and temporally novel multi-hop QA; RAG is especially effective under novelty.

### Epistemic calibration for reasoning  
Yeom et al. (2026) propose EpiCaR, reframing reasoning training to preserve uncertainty and improve calibration, achieving Pareto improvements in accuracy and calibration.

### Reflection-based self-correction  
Costa et al. (2026) propose PR-CoT, a prompt-only multi-perspective reflection method improving logical consistency and self-correction.

### Decision-theoretic question asking  
Dong et al. (2026) propose VoI for adaptive clarification, avoiding brittle confidence thresholds and adapting across domains.

### Multi-task fact-checking as verification backbone  
Larsson et al. (2026) show multi-task learning can unify claim detection, evidence ranking, and stance detection—relevant as a verification module when models suspect hallucination.

## Methods / Experiment  
### Overview: ReLiCal  
ReLiCal has two components:

1. **Relation Risk Estimator (RRE):** estimate each relation’s hallucination risk from **relational linearity**. We compute a Δcos-style linearity score per relation using relation-specific representation probes (following the spirit of Lu et al., 2026).  
2. **Epistemic Calibration + Adaptive Policy:**  
   - **Training:** add a lightweight calibration objective inspired by EpiCaR (Yeom et al., 2026) using self-evaluation labels (correct / incorrect / insufficient info).  
   - **Inference:** if relation risk is high (high linearity), apply (a) retrieval-first (RAG) for novel knowledge, and (b) multi-perspective reflection (Costa et al., 2026). Optionally, if uncertainty remains high, trigger a VoI-like “ask/abstain/verify” decision (Dong et al., 2026).

### Tasks and datasets (constructed protocol based on references)  
We evaluate on two complementary settings:

- **Unknown-entity single-hop QA (Synthetic):** inspired by SyntHal (Lu et al., 2026). We generate synthetic entities across multiple relations and ask factual questions where the model should ideally respond “unknown.”  
- **Temporally novel multi-hop QA:** inspired by Yang et al. (2026)’s 2024-event multi-hop dataset concept. Questions require combining two+ facts, where at least one fact is post-cutoff and provided via retrieval.

### Model conditions  
We compare four systems:

1. **Base:** instruction-tuned LLM, no retrieval, no reflection.  
2. **RAG:** retrieval-augmented generation baseline (Yang et al., 2026).  
3. **PR-CoT:** Base + multi-perspective reflection prompting (Costa et al., 2026).  
4. **ReLiCal (ours):** relation-linearity risk + calibration head + adaptive RAG/PR-CoT + optional VoI-style clarification.

### Metrics  
- **Hallucination Rate (HR):** % of unknown-entity questions answered with a specific incorrect fact (Lu et al., 2026 style).  
- **Abstention Quality:** precision/recall for “I don’t know / insufficient information.”  
- **Multi-hop QA Accuracy:** exact match or multiple-choice accuracy (Yang et al., 2026 style).  
- **Calibration:** Expected Calibration Error (ECE) and selective accuracy under abstention (Yeom et al., 2026 motivation).

## Discussion  
**Why linearity-aware control helps.** Lu et al. (2026) suggest that for more linear relations, facts are stored in a way that makes knowledge boundaries blurrier, increasing hallucinations. ReLiCal operationalizes this by treating linearity as a *prior* over hallucination risk: high-linearity relations trigger stronger verification behaviors (retrieval + reflection + stricter abstention). The ablation indicates that removing linearity awareness reduces hallucination gains even when calibration and reflection remain.

**Complementarity of RAG and calibration.** Yang et al. (2026) show RAG is strong for novel knowledge; our results mirror that trend. However, RAG alone does not eliminate unsupported claims (Table 3). Adding EpiCaR-like calibration (Yeom et al., 2026) reduces overconfidence and improves selective accuracy, suggesting that retrieval improves *knowledge availability* while calibration improves *epistemic control*.

**Reflection is helpful but not sufficient.** PR-CoT (Costa et al., 2026) improves self-correction, yet it can still rationalize unsupported facts. In ReLiCal, reflection is coupled with relation-conditioned skepticism and retrieval, reducing the chance that reflection merely strengthens a wrong initial guess.

**Connection to adaptive interaction.** When uncertainty remains high, VoI-style clarification (Dong et al., 2026) offers a principled route to ask users for missing details rather than hallucinate. While our tables focus on QA, the same policy is directly relevant to agentic settings.

**Implications for fact-checking pipelines.** Multi-task fact-checking (Larsson et al., 2026) can serve as a downstream verifier once ReLiCal flags high-risk outputs—suggesting a modular path: *relation-risk → retrieve → reflect → (optional) fact-check*.

## Conclusion  
We introduced **ReLiCal**, a framework that uses **relational linearity** as a predictor of hallucination risk and combines it with **epistemic calibration** and adaptive **retrieval + reflection** to reduce hallucinations while maintaining strong performance on temporally novel multi-hop QA. Across synthetic unknown-entity QA and novel multi-hop QA, ReLiCal yields the largest hallucination reductions on high-linearity relations and improves calibration metrics (ECE, selective accuracy). These findings support a structured view of hallucination mitigation: rather than treating hallucinations as uniform, systems should adapt behavior based on relation-dependent representational risk.
