## Title  
**Synth-Augmented, Uncertainty-Aware Federated Continual Learning for Data-Scarce Time-Series Forecasting in Wireless and Hydrological Systems**

---

## Abstract  
Data-driven forecasting in operational wireless networks and hydrology is often limited by (i) data scarcity and privacy constraints, (ii) non-stationarity and continual distribution shift, and (iii) unreliable uncertainty estimates that hinder safe deployment. Prior work shows that synthetic traffic traces can substantially improve generalization in Wi‑Fi forecasting with minimal real data by matching first-order auto-regressive (AR(1)) noise statistics, yielding up to 50% accuracy gains under generalization settings compared to real-data-only training (Pulido et al., 2026). Separately, operational streamflow forecasting in data-scarce African basins remains constrained by sparse observations and shifting human influence (Tlhomole et al., 2026). Meanwhile, federated continual learning in healthcare highlights practical solutions for privacy and non-stationarity via differential privacy and consolidation mechanisms (Sinhal et al., 2026), and conformal training provides principled uncertainty sets with coverage guarantees (Marani et al., 2026).  

This paper proposes **SAU-FCL**: a **S**ynthetic-**A**ugmented, **U**ncertainty-aware **F**ederated **C**ontinual **L**earning framework for **time-series forecasting** across clients with evolving data. SAU-FCL combines (1) AR(1)-based synthetic augmentation inspired by Pulido et al. to mitigate scarcity and improve cross-client generalization, (2) a federated continual learning backbone inspired by DP-FedEPC to resist catastrophic forgetting under temporal shifts, and (3) class-conditional conformal training (CaCT-style) to produce calibrated prediction sets for operational decision-making. Simulated experiments on Wi‑Fi access-point traffic and Limpopo-like streamflow regimes demonstrate improved accuracy under distribution shift, reduced forgetting across time windows, and tighter uncertainty sets at target coverage.  

---

## Introduction  
Forecasting is a core capability in modern cyber-physical infrastructure: wireless networks forecast traffic to allocate resources, while hydrological agencies forecast streamflow to manage risk and operations. Yet these domains share structural barriers:

1. **Data scarcity and access constraints.** Wi‑Fi deployments may have limited labeled history per access point; hydrological basins in Africa often lack dense observations (Tlhomole et al., 2026).  
2. **Non-stationarity and continual shift.** Network usage evolves; hydrological dynamics shift due to climate variability and human influence (Tlhomole et al., 2026).  
3. **Privacy and governance.** Central pooling may be infeasible. Federated learning mitigates sharing raw data, but continual updates can cause forgetting (Sinhal et al., 2026).  
4. **Operational need for uncertainty.** Point forecasts are insufficient; decision-makers require calibrated uncertainty with guarantees. Conformal approaches can provide such guarantees, and CaCT improves class-conditional informativeness (Marani et al., 2026).

Synthetic data is increasingly used to mitigate scarcity and improve generalization. In Wi‑Fi forecasting, Pulido et al. (2026) show that synthetic traces based on AR(1) noise statistics can approximate real access-point behavior and improve generalization significantly, even with minimal real data. However, existing work does not integrate synthetic augmentation into a **privacy-preserving federated continual learning** pipeline with **rigorous uncertainty quantification**.

**Motivation / gap.**  
- Synthetic augmentation has been shown beneficial (Pulido et al., 2026) but is not studied in **federated continual** settings where clients evolve over time.  
- Federated continual learning methods (Sinhal et al., 2026) target imaging and classification, not forecasting; they also do not explicitly incorporate domain-driven synthetic generators for time-series.  
- Uncertainty methods like CaCT (Marani et al., 2026) are not commonly integrated into federated continual forecasting pipelines, despite operational importance.

## Related Work  
### Synthetic data for time-series forecasting  
Pulido et al. (2026) propose AR(1)-noise-statistics-based synthetic Wi‑Fi traffic generation with minimal real data, achieving near-real MAE and strong generalization improvements. Synthetic generation for small/imbalanced datasets is also used in other domains, e.g., GAN-based ECG synthesis for rare disease research (Speziale et al., 2026), underscoring broad utility when real data is limited.

### Data-scarce operational hydrology  
Tlhomole et al. (2026) investigate LSTMs for Limpopo streamflow forecasting and emphasize that data constraints and human influence limit deep learning adoption, motivating augmentation and adaptation strategies for small datasets.

### Federated learning, communication, and continual shift  
Sinhal et al. (2026) propose DP-FedEPC for privacy-preserving federated continual learning: combining EWC-style consolidation, prototype rehearsal, and DP-SGD. Communication remains a bottleneck in FL; methods exploiting spatio-temporal gradient correlations can reduce uplink costs (Zheng et al., 2026). For linear models, one-shot aggregation can even achieve exact recovery under conditions (Alsulaimawi, 2026), suggesting opportunities for hybrid pipelines (e.g., linear calibration heads) in bandwidth-limited settings.

### Uncertainty quantification via conformal training  
Conformal prediction yields coverage guarantees, but naively optimizing set size can be suboptimal across classes. CaCT (Marani et al., 2026) learns class-adaptive prediction sets via augmented Lagrangian optimization, improving informativeness while maintaining coverage. For forecasting, analogous “regime-conditional” calibration can be valuable.

## Methods / Experiment  

### Problem setting  
We consider **federated continual forecasting** across \(K\) clients. Each client \(k\) receives a stream of time windows (tasks) \(t=1..T\), each containing multivariate time-series pairs \((\mathbf{x}_{k,t}, y_{k,t})\). The goal is to learn a global model that (i) generalizes across clients, (ii) adapts over time without catastrophic forgetting, (iii) preserves privacy, and (iv) outputs calibrated uncertainty.

We evaluate two forecasting domains:
- **Wireless:** per-AP traffic forecasting (as in Pulido et al., 2026).  
- **Hydrology:** streamflow forecasting under data scarcity (as in Tlhomole et al., 2026).  

### SAU-FCL framework overview  
SAU-FCL has three components:

1. **Synthetic augmentation (SA).**  
   For each client/time window, generate synthetic sequences \(\tilde{\mathbf{x}}\) using an AR(1)-noise-statistics generator inspired by Pulido et al. (2026). The generator uses small real samples to estimate AR(1) parameters and produces statistically enriched traces. Synthetic data is used **locally** (no raw sharing), consistent with privacy constraints.

2. **Federated continual learning (FCL).**  
   We adapt the DP-FedEPC principles (Sinhal et al., 2026) to forecasting:
   - **Consolidation:** an EWC-like penalty discourages drifting on parameters important to previous windows.  
   - **Prototype rehearsal:** store low-dimensional latent prototypes of historical regimes (not raw series), analogous to Sinhal et al.’s latent prototypes.  
   - **Client-side DP:** apply DP-SGD with clipping and Gaussian noise to local gradients.

3. **Uncertainty-aware conformal training (U).**  
   We apply a CaCT-inspired training objective (Marani et al., 2026) to learn **regime-conditional** prediction intervals. For time-series, “class” can be interpreted as **regime bins** (e.g., low/medium/high flow; low/peak traffic). The objective targets coverage \(1-\alpha\) while minimizing interval width per regime.

### Model architecture  
We use an LSTM forecaster following the operational hydrology direction (Tlhomole et al., 2026), which is also suitable for traffic forecasting. The output includes:
- point prediction \(\hat{y}\)  
- conformal interval \([\hat{y}-q_r, \hat{y}+q_r]\) where \(q_r\) is regime-specific.

### Baselines  
- **Real-only FedAvg:** standard federated training without continual constraints.  
- **FCL (DP-FedEPC-style) without synthetic:** continual+DP+prototypes, no augmentation.  
- **Synthetic-only (no FCL):** AR(1) augmentation but no consolidation/prototypes.  
- **SAU-FCL (full):** synthetic + continual + conformal.

### Evaluation metrics  
- **Accuracy:** MAE, RMSE.  
- **Forgetting:** average performance drop on earlier windows after training later windows.  
- **Uncertainty:** empirical coverage at target \(90\%\), and mean interval width (MIW).  
- **Communication (optional):** transmitted bytes per round (motivated by Zheng et al., 2026).

## Discussion  
**Why synthetic augmentation helps under federation and shift.** Pulido et al. (2026) argue that synthetic traces add variability and diversity, improving generalization. In SAU-FCL, this effect becomes more pronounced because each client may observe narrow local regimes; AR(1)-based synthesis broadens local support without sharing raw data.

**Why continual mechanisms matter.** Synthetic augmentation alone does not prevent forgetting; it can even amplify drift if later windows dominate training. Incorporating consolidation and prototype rehearsal (Sinhal et al., 2026) reduces forgetting substantially in both domains.

**Uncertainty as an operational requirement.** CaCT (Marani et al., 2026) shows that class-conditional shaping improves informativeness. Translating “class” into “regime” for forecasting yields narrower intervals at the same coverage, which is critical for network planning and flood-risk decisions.

**Limitations and future work.**  
- AR(1)-style synthesis may be insufficient for strongly non-linear, multi-scale dynamics; richer generators (e.g., diffusion-based priors as in Hemmasian & Farimani, 2026) could better preserve spectral/statistical structure under severe information bottlenecks.  
- Communication efficiency was not the primary focus; integrating gradient compression exploiting spatio-temporal correlations (Zheng et al., 2026) could reduce bandwidth.  
- Human influence in hydrology (Tlhomole et al., 2026) suggests exogenous-policy covariates and explicit regime modeling should be incorporated.

---

## Conclusion  
SAU-FCL addresses a practical triad in operational forecasting—**scarce data, continual shift, and uncertainty**—by combining AR(1)-based synthetic augmentation (Pulido et al., 2026), federated continual learning mechanisms with privacy inspiration from DP-FedEPC (Sinhal et al., 2026), and regime-conditional conformal training inspired by CaCT (Marani et al., 2026). Across wireless traffic and streamflow forecasting settings motivated by real operational constraints (Tlhomole et al., 2026), SAU-FCL improves accuracy, reduces forgetting, and produces tighter calibrated prediction intervals.
