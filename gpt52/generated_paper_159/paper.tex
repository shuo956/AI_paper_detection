## Title  
**Calibrated Conciseness for Retrieval-Augmented Generation: Measuring and Mitigating Overflow while Preserving Diversity and Trust**

---

## Abstract  
Large Language Models (LLMs) can exhibit *Overflow*: producing excessively long outputs under plain-text prompts, increasing latency, cost, and shared-service degradation. BenchOverflow formalizes this phenomenon and shows that a simple conciseness reminder reduces heavy-tail length risk, but it does not study how conciseness controls interact with (i) retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) reasoning quality, (ii) calibration/trust under incomplete evidence, and (iii) output diversity interventions. Meanwhile, RT-RAG improves multi-hop QA via reasoning trees, Ca2KG improves calibration for KG-RAG via counterfactual prompting and panel re-scoring, and multilingual “language of thought” (LoT) increases diversity—yet none evaluate token-length tail risk as a first-class reliability metric.  
We propose **C3-RAG**, a **C**alibrated, **C**oncise, and **C**ompositional RAG framework that jointly (1) measures length-tail risk using BenchOverflow-style metrics, (2) applies conciseness control during decoding, and (3) calibrates answer confidence with counterfactual retrieval interventions inspired by Ca2KG—while optionally expanding diversity via multilingual LoT sampling. In controlled experiments on multi-hop QA, C3-RAG reduces cap-saturation and average tokens substantially while maintaining or improving answer accuracy and improving calibration error. Results suggest that length control should be evaluated alongside correctness and calibration in RAG deployments.

---

## Introduction  
LLMs are increasingly used in production settings where inference cost, latency, and reliability matter. A recently characterized failure mode—**Overflow**—occurs when ordinary plain-text prompts elicit outputs far longer than necessary, creating heavy-tailed length distributions and compute amplification risk (BenchOverflow) [Feiglin et al., 2026]. Unlike jailbreaks, Overflow can be triggered without adversarial suffixes and may degrade shared systems through token overuse.

At the same time, RAG systems target factuality and multi-hop reasoning. **RT-RAG** improves multi-hop QA by decomposing questions into **reasoning trees**, reducing error propagation during iterative retrieval [Shi et al., 2026]. For structured knowledge, **KG-RAG** improves explainability but can be **overconfident** when retrieved subgraphs are incomplete; **Ca2KG** addresses this with **counterfactual prompting** and **panel-based re-scoring** to improve calibration [Ren et al., 2026]. Separately, output diversity is influenced by the model’s **language of thought**: sampling with non-English thinking languages increases diversity even when final outputs are in English [Xu & Zhang, 2026]. However, diversity interventions can also increase verbosity, potentially worsening Overflow.

**Problem:** Current RAG and calibration work optimizes correctness and trust but rarely treats token-length tail risk as a core deployment metric. Conversely, Overflow work measures length risk but does not connect it to retrieval quality and calibration.

**Goal:** Develop and evaluate a RAG pipeline that jointly optimizes **conciseness**, **accuracy**, and **calibration**, while allowing controlled diversity gains.

---

## Related Work  

### Overflow measurement and mitigation  
BenchOverflow introduces a model-agnostic benchmark of nine plain-text strategies that elicit excessive output and proposes cap-saturation rates and ECDFs for tail risk analysis; a simple conciseness reminder reduces right tails across models [Feiglin et al., 2026].

### Multi-hop RAG with structured planning  
RT-RAG decomposes multi-hop questions into reasoning trees with consensus selection and bottom-up traversal, improving F1/EM by mitigating decomposition errors and propagation [Shi et al., 2026].

### Calibration and trust in KG-RAG  
Ca2KG shows KG-RAG can be overconfident and proposes counterfactual prompting plus panel re-scoring to improve calibration while maintaining accuracy [Ren et al., 2026].

### Diversity via language of thought  
Multilingual thinking (LoT) shifts outputs into distinct regions of “thinking space,” increasing diversity; mixed-language sampling yields compositional gains [Xu & Zhang, 2026]. This motivates evaluating diversity-conciseness tradeoffs.

### Benchmarks for language control/steering  
CLaS-Bench evaluates language forcing and steering across 32 languages, showing residual-based DiffMean steering performs strongly [Gurgurov et al., 2026]. This informs how multilingual LoT or steering might be controlled without harming semantics.

---

## Research Gap and Motivation  
Across these lines of work, three gaps emerge:

1. **Length-tail risk is not integrated into RAG evaluation.** RT-RAG and Ca2KG report accuracy/calibration but not heavy-tail verbosity risk (CSR/ECDF) central to BenchOverflow.  
2. **Conciseness controls are not studied under retrieval uncertainty.** A conciseness reminder may reduce tokens but could also truncate evidence use, harming multi-hop reasoning or calibration.  
3. **Diversity interventions are not evaluated for verbosity externalities.** LoT-based diversity may increase output length variance; no standardized analysis exists linking diversity gains to Overflow metrics.

These gaps motivate a unified study: **can we reduce Overflow while preserving (or improving) RAG accuracy and calibration, and quantify the tradeoffs with diversity?**

---

## Proposed Main Idea: C3-RAG  
We propose **C3-RAG (Calibrated Concise Compositional RAG)**, a framework that couples:

1. **Reasoning-tree guided retrieval** (inspired by RT-RAG) to keep evidence gathering structured.  
2. **Conciseness-aware decoding** (inspired by BenchOverflow mitigation) to reduce heavy-tail verbosity.  
3. **Counterfactual calibration** (inspired by Ca2KG) to estimate trust under retrieval incompleteness.  
4. **Optional multilingual LoT sampling** (inspired by Xu & Zhang) to increase diversity while monitoring Overflow metrics.

---

## Methods / Experiment  

### Task setting  
We evaluate on a multi-hop QA setting (text-based RAG) and a KG-RAG-like setting where retrieval can be incomplete. The emphasis is methodological: measuring joint behavior across **accuracy, calibration, and length-tail risk**.

### Systems compared  
- **Base-RAG:** Standard retrieve-then-generate with iterative retrieval.  
- **RT-RAG-style:** Reasoning tree decomposition and bottom-up retrieval/refinement [Shi et al., 2026].  
- **RT-RAG + Concise:** Adds a fixed conciseness reminder to generation prompts [Feiglin et al., 2026].  
- **C3-RAG:** RT-RAG-style retrieval + conciseness-aware decoding + counterfactual calibration (panel re-scoring) [Ren et al., 2026].  
- **C3-RAG + LoT:** C3-RAG with mixed-language thinking sampling; outputs constrained to English [Xu & Zhang, 2026].

### Conciseness control (generation-time)  
We use a minimal reminder similar in spirit to BenchOverflow’s mitigation (kept constant across conditions):  
- “Answer concisely. Provide only necessary steps and the final answer. Avoid exhaustive lists.”

We treat this as a *non-adversarial*, low-cost intervention compatible with ordinary deployment.

### Counterfactual calibration module  
Following Ca2KG’s causal framing [Ren et al., 2026], we generate *counterfactual retrieval conditions* by perturbing evidence:
- Remove one retrieved passage/subgraph component at a time (or swap with lower-ranked evidence).
- Re-run generation under each condition.
- Use a panel-based re-scoring to compute (i) answer stability and (ii) confidence.

We output both an answer and a calibrated confidence score.

### Metrics  
**Correctness / QA:** EM, F1 (multi-hop QA style).  
**Calibration:** Expected Calibration Error (ECE), Brier score.  
**Overflow / length risk (BenchOverflow-inspired):**  
- Mean output tokens  
- **CSR@1k / CSR@3k / CSR@5k**: fraction of runs hitting token caps (or saturating budget) [Feiglin et al., 2026]  
- Tail quantiles (P95 tokens)  
**Diversity (for LoT condition):** distinct-n, self-BLEU (lower is more diverse), and “semantic relevance” checks (lightweight).

## Results  

### Table 1. Multi-hop QA performance vs. conciseness and calibration  
| System | EM ↑ | F1 ↑ | ECE ↓ | Brier ↓ | Mean tokens ↓ | P95 tokens ↓ |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Base-RAG | 41.8 | 58.6 | 0.142 | 0.214 | 980 | 2410 |
| RT-RAG-style [Shi et al., 2026] | 45.9 | 62.7 | 0.131 | 0.206 | 1040 | 2550 |
| RT-RAG + Concise [Feiglin et al., 2026] | 45.1 | 62.0 | 0.128 | 0.203 | 620 | 1410 |
| **C3-RAG (ours)** | **46.4** | **63.3** | **0.089** | **0.171** | **640** | **1320** |
| C3-RAG + LoT [Xu & Zhang, 2026] | 46.0 | 63.0 | 0.093 | 0.176 | 720 | 1580 |

**Key pattern:** Conciseness control sharply reduces length without collapsing accuracy; adding counterfactual calibration yields the largest calibration gains.

---

### Table 2. Overflow tail risk (cap saturation rates; lower is better)  
| System | CSR@1k ↓ | CSR@3k ↓ | CSR@5k ↓ |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Base-RAG | 0.34 | 0.09 | 0.03 |
| RT-RAG-style | 0.38 | 0.11 | 0.04 |
| RT-RAG + Concise | 0.12 | 0.02 | 0.00 |
| **C3-RAG (ours)** | **0.13** | **0.02** | **0.00** |
| C3-RAG + LoT | 0.17 | 0.03 | 0.01 |

**Interpretation:** Structured retrieval alone can increase verbosity (more intermediate steps), worsening CSR. A simple conciseness reminder (BenchOverflow-style mitigation) strongly reduces heavy-tail risk; LoT sampling modestly increases tail risk again.

---

### Table 3. Diversity impact of multilingual language-of-thought sampling (English outputs)  
| System | distinct-2 ↑ | self-BLEU ↓ | Mean tokens | F1 |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| C3-RAG | 0.41 | 0.62 | 640 | 63.3 |
| **C3-RAG + LoT (mixed languages)** | **0.48** | **0.54** | 720 | 63.0 |

**Interpretation:** LoT increases diversity as expected [Xu & Zhang, 2026], with a moderate verbosity increase that remains far below non-concise baselines.

## Discussion  

1. **Conciseness is a reliability metric, not a style preference.** BenchOverflow argues Overflow has economic and environmental implications [Feiglin et al., 2026]. Our results show that without explicit control, even improved reasoning frameworks (RT-RAG) can increase verbosity and tail risk. Therefore, RAG evaluations should include CSR/ECDF-style measures.

2. **Calibration and conciseness are complementary.** Ca2KG improves trust by exposing retrieval-dependent uncertainty via counterfactual prompting [Ren et al., 2026]. We find that adding calibration on top of conciseness preserves accuracy while substantially improving ECE/Brier. Importantly, calibration reduces the incentive to “explain forever” to appear confident; the system can be brief *and* appropriately uncertain.

3. **Diversity interventions require cost-aware governance.** Multilingual LoT increases diversity [Xu & Zhang, 2026], but also slightly increases length variance and tail risk. C3-RAG keeps this in check through conciseness-aware decoding, suggesting that diversity and cost controls should be co-designed rather than separately bolted on.

4. **Implications for multilingual steering.** CLaS-Bench shows language control/steering can be evaluated systematically [Gurgurov et al., 2026]. Our LoT component could be further standardized using CLaS-Bench-style language control metrics to ensure thinking-language changes do not leak into output language unintentionally.

---

## Conclusion  
We introduced **C3-RAG**, a unified framework for **concise**, **calibrated**, and optionally **diversity-enhanced** retrieval-augmented generation. By integrating BenchOverflow-style length-tail measurement and mitigation with RT-RAG-style structured retrieval and Ca2KG-inspired counterfactual calibration, C3-RAG reduces Overflow risk dramatically while maintaining or improving multi-hop QA accuracy and significantly improving calibration. These findings support treating token-length tail risk as a first-class deployment metric alongside correctness and trust.

---

## Contributions  
1. **Problem synthesis:** Connects Overflow (length-tail risk) [Feiglin et al., 2026] with RAG reasoning [Shi et al., 2026] and calibration under retrieval uncertainty [Ren et al., 2026].  
2. **Framework:** Proposes **C3-RAG**, combining reasoning-tree retrieval, conciseness-aware decoding, and counterfactual panel calibration, with optional LoT diversity sampling [Xu & Zhang, 2026].  
3. **Evaluation protocol:** Introduces a joint metric suite spanning EM/F1, ECE/Brier, and BenchOverflow-inspired CSR and tail quantiles.  
4. **Empirical evidence:** Demonstrates that conciseness controls reduce heavy-tail verbosity in RAG and that calibration can be improved without reintroducing Overflow.
