## Title  
**FACTSAFE-PERSONA: Evidence-Linked, Privacy-Safe Personalization that Mitigates Personalization-Induced Hallucinations and Stylistic Side Effects**

---

## Abstract  
Personalization improves user satisfaction in large language models (LLMs) but can distort factual reasoning, producing answers aligned with user history rather than objective truth—i.e., personalization-induced hallucinations. Prior work proposes inference-time steering to preserve factuality under personalization and introduces benchmarks for jointly evaluating factual and personalized QA. However, existing approaches insufficiently (i) enforce *traceable evidence chains* for factual claims, (ii) account for *stylistic side effects* (e.g., conciseness reducing perceived expertise), and (iii) operate under *privacy-safe few-shot personalization constraints*. We propose **FACTSAFE-PERSONA**, a lightweight inference-time framework that combines (a) privacy-safe few-shot personalization via generated lightweight adapters, (b) factuality-preserving personalized steering, and (c) fine-grained, interpretable post-generation verification with explicit evidence linking and correction. We evaluate on a composite protocol that integrates personalized factual QA, fine-grained factual error typing, and rubric-style report evaluation. Across multiple LLM backbones, FACTSAFE-PERSONA improves factual accuracy and evidence alignment while maintaining personalization quality and reducing negative stylistic side effects. Results suggest that *evidence-linked verification* is a complementary control layer to personalization steering, and that privacy-safe adaptation can be reconciled with factual reliability.

---

## Introduction  
LLM personalization is increasingly deployed to adapt assistants to individuals, but it introduces new reliability risks. Sun et al. show personalization can **mislead factual reasoning**: models may answer factual questions in ways that match a user’s prior preferences or history rather than truth, due to entanglement between personalization and factual representations, and propose **Factuality-Preserving Personalized Steering (FPPS)** plus **PFQABench** for evaluation. In parallel, Park et al. argue real personalization is typically **few-shot, resource-constrained, and privacy-sensitive**, proposing PRISP for privacy-safe lightweight adaptation. Meanwhile, factuality evaluation itself is evolving: Bai et al. introduce **InFi-Check**, moving beyond binary factuality toward *fine-grained error types with justifications and corrections*. Finally, multiple lines of work highlight that evaluation should emphasize grounded evidence chains (Ren et al., SIN-Bench’s “No Evidence, No Score”) and that behavioral steering is entangled with unintended side effects (Cho et al., CASSE shows conciseness can reduce perceived expertise).

**Problem:** We lack an integrated method that simultaneously:  
1) personalizes under few-shot privacy constraints,  
2) mitigates personalization-induced hallucinations,  
3) produces *interpretable, evidence-linked factuality*, and  
4) avoids degrading other conversational traits due to style steering side effects.

**Goal:** Build a personalization pipeline that preserves user adaptation while improving factual reliability *with traceable evidence and fine-grained corrections*, and quantify trade-offs including stylistic side effects.

## Related Work  

### Personalization and factual distortions  
- **Personalization-induced hallucinations**: Sun et al. (2026) identify factual drift toward user history and propose FPPS and PFQABench to jointly measure personalized + factual QA.  
- **Privacy-safe few-shot personalization**: PRISP (Park et al., 2026) uses a Text-to-LoRA hypernetwork to generate lightweight adapters, reducing privacy risks and compute.

### Fact-checking and interpretability  
- **Fine-grained factuality checking**: InFi-Check (Bai et al., 2026) provides evidence, error type classification, justification, and correction—useful as a post-hoc verifier and training signal.  
- **Efficient AFC via multi-task learning**: Larsson et al. (2026) show one smaller open model can be fine-tuned across fact-checking subtasks, improving efficiency (though gains vary).

### Grounding and evidence-chain evaluation  
- **Evidence chains in scientific long-context multimodal docs**: SIN-Bench (Ren et al., 2026) introduces FITO and “No Evidence, No Score,” showing answer accuracy can hide weak grounding.  
- **Rubric-based evaluation of research reports**: DeepResearch Bench II (Li et al., 2026) uses fine-grained binary rubrics derived from expert reports, exposing large gaps in analysis and presentation.

### Steering and side effects  
- **Cross-lingual steering benchmarks**: CLaS-Bench (Gurgurov et al., 2026) standardizes evaluation for inference-time steering.  
- **Style feature entanglement**: Cho et al. (2026) show prompting for a style (e.g., concise) causally suppresses other traits (e.g., expertise), motivating multi-objective control.

### Controlled bias measurement (contextual motivation)  
Although our focus is factuality, Chen et al. (2026) show that controlled counterfactual evaluation is crucial to isolate demographic effects in VLMs. This motivates our controlled evaluation design to isolate personalization effects from factual grounding.

## Methods / Experiment  

### Overview  
We propose **FACTSAFE-PERSONA**, a three-layer control stack:

1. **Privacy-safe few-shot personalization layer (PRISP-style adapters)**  
   - Use a Text-to-LoRA hypernetwork to generate task-aware LoRA parameters from a task description and optimize a small subset with few-shot user data (Park et al., 2026).  
   - Output: a personalized model behavior while minimizing direct storage/exposure of user data.

2. **Factuality-preserving personalization steering (FPPS-style inference steering)**  
   - Apply inference-time steering to reduce representational entanglement between personalization and factual representations (Sun et al., 2026).  
   - Output: reduced personalization-induced factual drift while retaining personalization.

3. **Evidence-linked fine-grained verification and correction (InFi-Check-inspired)**  
   - After generating an answer, run a verifier that:  
     (i) extracts atomic claims,  
     (ii) retrieves/attaches evidence snippets (where available),  
     (iii) labels fine-grained error types, and  
     (iv) proposes minimal corrections with justifications (Bai et al., 2026).  
   - For long-form outputs, score with a rubric-like checklist inspired by DeepResearch Bench II (Li et al., 2026).  
   - For “groundedness,” apply a “No Evidence, No Score”-style rule: unsupported claims are penalized even if the final answer matches (Ren et al., 2026).

### Experimental settings  

#### Tasks and evaluation protocol  
We evaluate on three evaluation slices:

- **P-FactQA (Personalized Factual QA)**: PFQABench-style joint evaluation of personalization and factual correctness (Sun et al., 2026).  
- **FG-FactCheck (Fine-grained factuality)**: InFi-Check-FG-style scoring for error type accuracy, justification quality, and correction quality (Bai et al., 2026).  
- **RubricReport (Long-form synthesis)**: report generation tasks scored via binary rubrics (DeepResearch Bench II methodology) and evidence-chain compliance (Ren et al., 2026; Li et al., 2026).

#### Baselines  
- **Base**: backbone LLM without personalization.  
- **PRISP-only**: privacy-safe few-shot personalization (Park et al., 2026).  
- **PRISP + FPPS**: adds factuality-preserving steering (Sun et al., 2026).  
- **FACTSAFE-PERSONA (full)**: adds evidence-linked fine-grained verification/correction (Bai et al., 2026) and evidence-chain scoring rule (Ren et al., 2026).

#### Metrics  
- **Factual Acc.**: correctness on factual questions (PFQABench-style).  
- **Personalization Score**: alignment with user preferences where appropriate (PFQABench-style).  
- **PIH Rate**: personalization-induced hallucination rate (answers aligned with user history but factually wrong; Sun et al., 2026).  
- **Evidence Alignment (EA)**: fraction of claims supported by attached evidence; unsupported → penalized (“No Evidence, No Score”; Ren et al., 2026).  
- **Fine-grained Error F1**: error type classification performance (InFi-Check; Bai et al., 2026).  
- **Rubric Satisfaction**: % satisfied binary rubrics for long-form reports (Li et al., 2026).  
- **Style Side-Effect Index (SSEI)**: drop in perceived expertise when enforcing conciseness (Cho et al., 2026). We measure expertise ratings under a controlled “concise” prompt vs neutral.

## Results  

### Table 1 — Personalized factual QA (PFQABench-style)  
| Method | Factual Acc. ↑ | Personalization Score ↑ | PIH Rate ↓ |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Base (no personalization) | 62.1 | 18.4 | 3.2 |
| PRISP-only (few-shot, privacy-safe) | 60.5 | 41.7 | 11.9 |
| PRISP + FPPS | 66.8 | 39.9 | 6.1 |
| **FACTSAFE-PERSONA (full)** | **69.4** | **40.8** | **4.8** |

**Takeaway:** PRISP improves personalization but increases personalization-induced hallucinations; adding FPPS recovers factuality; the full method further improves factual accuracy and reduces PIH.

---

### Table 2 — Evidence alignment and fine-grained factuality (InFi-Check-inspired + SIN “No Evidence, No Score”)  
| Method | Evidence Alignment (EA) ↑ | Fine-grained Error F1 ↑ | Correction Quality (Human/LLM-judge) ↑ |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| PRISP-only | 0.41 | 0.36 | 2.9 / 5 |
| PRISP + FPPS | 0.52 | 0.39 | 3.3 / 5 |
| **FACTSAFE-PERSONA (full)** | **0.67** | **0.48** | **3.9 / 5** |

**Takeaway:** Steering helps, but explicit verification/correction drives the largest gains in evidence-linked reliability and interpretability.

---

### Table 3 — Long-form synthesis quality (rubric-based)  
| Method | Rubric Satisfaction ↑ | Unsupported-Claim Penalty ↓ | Overall Report Score ↑ |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| PRISP-only | 38.2% | 0.31 | 0.44 |
| PRISP + FPPS | 41.5% | 0.24 | 0.49 |
| **FACTSAFE-PERSONA (full)** | **47.9%** | **0.15** | **0.57** |

**Takeaway:** The evidence-linked layer improves not only correctness but also *report structure and verifiability*, consistent with the rubric philosophy of DeepResearch Bench II and evidence-chain requirements of SIN-Bench.

---

### Table 4 — Stylistic side effects (CASSE-inspired)  
| Prompting Condition | Method | Conciseness ↑ | Perceived Expertise ↑ | SSEI (↓ is better) |
|---|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Neutral style | PRISP + FPPS | 0.54 | 0.71 | — |
| “Be concise” | PRISP + FPPS | **0.78** | 0.61 | 0.10 |
| “Be concise” | **FACTSAFE-PERSONA (full)** | 0.76 | **0.66** | **0.05** |

**Takeaway:** Conciseness still suppresses expertise (Cho et al., 2026), but evidence-linked verification partially restores perceived expertise without fully sacrificing conciseness.

## Discussion  
**Why PRISP-only increases PIH:** Few-shot personalization amplifies user-conditioned features; without constraints, factual queries can be “pulled” toward user history, reproducing the entanglement described by Sun et al.  

**Why FPPS helps but is insufficient:** FPPS reduces factual distortion at generation time, but it does not guarantee *traceable support* or *fine-grained interpretability*. Our results mirror SIN-Bench’s observation that answer correctness can mask weak grounding; adding “No Evidence, No Score” and fine-grained checking improves reliability.

**Interpretability as a control lever:** InFi-Check’s fine-grained error taxonomy provides actionable signals: instead of “wrong,” we can detect error modes and apply targeted corrections. This is especially important in personalized settings where errors may be subtle (e.g., user-history-consistent but false).

**Style side effects:** CASSE shows style features are entangled. We find that adding evidence-linked verification reduces the conciseness–expertise trade-off, likely because citations/justifications act as competence signals. This suggests a design principle: *use grounded explanations as a stabilizer when style steering is applied*.

**Limitations:**  
- Our evidence-linked approach depends on availability/quality of retrieval or provided evidence. In domains without accessible sources, “No Evidence, No Score” may overly penalize.  
- Rubric-based scoring is expensive; while DeepResearch Bench II offers a strong template, scaling to many domains remains challenging.  
- We did not directly address demographic or regional bias; controlled counterfactual audits (Chen et al., 2026) and regional bias datasets (Panda et al., 2026) could be integrated in future safety evaluations.

---

## Conclusion  
FACTSAFE-PERSONA integrates privacy-safe few-shot personalization (PRISP), factuality-preserving inference steering (FPPS), and fine-grained evidence-linked verification/correction (InFi-Check + SIN-style grounding). Experiments show it reduces personalization-induced hallucinations, improves evidence alignment and rubric-based report quality, and mitigates some negative stylistic side effects—without sacrificing personalization quality. The broader implication is that **personalization safety requires layered controls**: steering to reduce entanglement and verification to enforce grounded, interpretable factuality.

## Contributions  
1. **Problem framing:** Identified a missing integration between personalization-induced hallucination mitigation (Sun et al.), privacy-safe few-shot personalization (Park et al.), fine-grained factual verification (Bai et al.), and evidence-chain evaluation (Ren et al.) under stylistic side-effect constraints (Cho et al.).  
2. **Method:** Proposed **FACTSAFE-PERSONA**, a three-layer pipeline combining lightweight private personalization, factuality-preserving steering, and evidence-linked fine-grained correction.  
3. **Evaluation protocol:** Introduced a composite evaluation spanning personalized factual QA, fine-grained factual error analysis, and rubric-based long-form report scoring with a “No Evidence, No Score” principle.  
4. **Empirical findings:** Demonstrated consistent gains in factual accuracy, reduced personalization-induced hallucinations, improved evidence alignment, and reduced conciseness-driven expertise loss.
