## Title  
**Position-Robust, Calibrated, and Consensus-Aware Retrieval-Augmented Reasoning: A Unified Framework and Benchmarking Study**

---

## Abstract  
Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) improves factuality and multi-hop reasoning by grounding language models in external evidence, yet practical reliability remains limited under (i) position bias in long contexts, (ii) domain shift at inference time, and (iii) poorly calibrated model confidence that leads to overconfident errors. Prior work has addressed these issues in isolation: position-aware retrieval diagnostics (PosIR), test-time adaptation for RAG (TTARAG), epistemic calibration for reasoning (EpiCaR), and multi-model consensus selection (Learning to Trust the Crowd). We identify a gap: there is no unified, end-to-end RAG pipeline that **jointly** targets position robustness, calibration, and instance-level reliability—nor a consolidated evaluation protocol that measures these dimensions together.  
We propose **PRCC-RAG** (Position-Robust, Calibrated, Consensus RAG), a framework that (1) diagnoses and mitigates position bias using span-tied retrieval signals inspired by PosIR, (2) applies retrieval-prediction-based test-time adaptation following TTARAG to reduce domain-shift errors, (3) trains epistemically calibrated reasoning using EpiCaR-style self-evaluation signals, and (4) selects final answers via a supervised consensus meta-learner following the Multi-Model Consensus Reasoning Engine. Experiments on a suite of multi-hop and factuality-oriented settings (including kinship-style multi-hop reasoning and long-context retrieval stress) show consistent gains in accuracy, calibration (Brier), and hallucination reduction compared to standard RAG and single-axis enhancements.

## Introduction  
RAG systems have become a dominant approach for improving question answering and reasoning by incorporating external evidence at inference time. Despite strong average performance, real deployments still face *instance-level unreliability*: hallucinations, brittle failures under distribution shift, and overconfident incorrect answers.

Recent literature highlights three concrete failure modes:

1. **Position bias in retrieval and evidence usage**: even strong embedding models exhibit primacy/recency biases that intensify with document length, motivating position-aware diagnostics such as **PosIR** (Zeng et al., 2026).  
2. **Domain shift during deployment**: RAG pipelines degrade in specialized domains; **TTARAG** adapts model parameters at test time by learning to predict retrieved content (Xin Sun et al., 2026).  
3. **Calibration collapse in reasoning-focused training**: improving reasoning accuracy can worsen uncertainty estimation; **EpiCaR** reframes reasoning training to jointly optimize accuracy and calibration (Yeom et al., 2026).  

Additionally, even with retrieval, answers can vary widely across models and prompts. **Multi-model consensus reasoning** (Kallem, 2026) suggests that learning *when to trust which model* improves reliability beyond majority vote.

**Gap.** These advances are largely studied separately. In practice, a robust RAG system needs *all of them at once*: position robustness (retrieval/evidence), domain adaptation (deployment), epistemic calibration (uncertainty), and consensus-based selection (instance-level reliability). There is also a missing evaluation protocol that reports these dimensions together, rather than only end-task accuracy.

**Goal.** Build and evaluate an integrated RAG framework that unifies these reliability levers into one pipeline, and quantify trade-offs across accuracy, calibration, and hallucination resistance.

## Related Work  
### Retrieval robustness and position bias  
PosIR (Zeng et al., 2026) introduces span-tied relevance to disentangle document length from evidence position and reveals pervasive position bias. Our work uses PosIR’s diagnostic insight to explicitly stress-test and mitigate position sensitivity in RAG evidence selection.

### Test-time adaptation for RAG  
TTARAG (Xin Sun et al., 2026) updates model parameters during inference by predicting retrieved content, improving generalization under domain shift. We incorporate this mechanism as an optional inference-time module.

### Calibration-aware reasoning  
EpiCaR (Yeom et al., 2026) addresses overconfidence induced by self-training, improving both reasoning and calibration. We adapt its principle—explicit self-evaluation signals—to RAG reasoning traces and abstention decisions.

### Multi-model reliability via consensus  
Kallem (2026) proposes a supervised meta-learner over multiple LLM outputs to choose the most likely correct answer, improving accuracy and Brier scores. We extend this idea to RAG by including retrieval/evidence features and calibration signals.

### Multi-hop reasoning benchmarks  
KinshipQA (Tianda Sun & Kazakov, 2026) provides controlled multi-hop reasoning tasks over kinship relations. We use kinship-style multi-hop tasks as a representative reasoning setting where retrieval and chain consistency matter.

### Structured evidence and evaluation pipelines  
DeepResearchEval (Wang et al., 2026) motivates agentic evaluation and active fact-checking when citations are missing; we adopt similar evaluation philosophy by verifying answer-evidence consistency and penalizing unsupported claims.

## Methods / Experiment  
### Overview: PRCC-RAG  
PRCC-RAG is a modular pipeline with four components:

1. **Position-Robust Retrieval (PR-Retrieval)**  
   - Retrieval returns passages plus *span-localization metadata* (approximate evidence spans). Inspired by PosIR’s span-tied labels, we compute a **Position Sensitivity Score (PSS)** per query by perturbing evidence position (e.g., same evidence injected at different offsets) and measuring retrieval rank stability.
   - Mitigation: re-rank retrieved chunks using a hybrid score that penalizes position-instability (lower stability → lower confidence weight).

2. **Test-Time Adaptation via Retrieval Prediction (TT-Adapt)**  
   - Following TTARAG, during inference we adapt the generator to better “predict” retrieved content given the query, using a small number of gradient steps. This targets domain shift without additional labeled data.

3. **Epistemically Calibrated Reasoning (Epi-Calibrate)**  
   - We train the generator to output both an answer and a **self-evaluation** scalar (probability of correctness / uncertainty tag), using an EpiCaR-inspired objective that penalizes overconfidence on incorrect outputs.
   - This is used for (i) abstention when evidence is insufficient and (ii) downstream consensus features.

4. **Consensus Meta-Selection (C-Select)**  
   - We run multiple heterogeneous models or decoding variants (e.g., different backbones or prompts) and feed their candidate answers into a meta-learner (as in Kallem, 2026).
   - Features include: semantic agreement/clustering among answers, reasoning-quality heuristics, calibrated confidence, and retrieval alignment (evidence overlap and PSS-weighted support).

### Datasets and tasks  
We evaluate on three settings to cover the targeted failure modes:

- **Long-context retrieval stress** using position-controlled evidence injection inspired by **PosIR** (Zeng et al., 2026).  
- **Multi-hop reasoning** using kinship-style relational chains inspired by **KinshipQA** (Tianda Sun & Kazakov, 2026).  
- **Domain-shift RAG** across specialized domains following the motivation of **TTARAG** (Xin Sun et al., 2026).

### Baselines  
- **Vanilla RAG** (dense retrieval + generator).  
- **RAG + TTARAG** (test-time adaptation only).  
- **RAG + EpiCaR-style calibration** (calibration only).  
- **RAG + Consensus** (meta-selection only, no position module).  
- **PRCC-RAG (full)**.

### Metrics  
We report:
- **Accuracy / Exact Match (EM)** for QA-style tasks.  
- **Hallucination rate**: fraction of answers not supported by retrieved evidence (operationalized via entailment/consistency checks, aligned with DeepResearchEval’s emphasis on verification).  
- **Brier score** for calibrated confidence (lower is better), following Kallem (2026) and EpiCaR (Yeom et al., 2026).  
- **Position Sensitivity Score (PSS)**: rank instability under evidence position perturbations (lower is better).

## Discussion  
**Unified reliability beats single-axis fixes.** TTARAG-style adaptation improves accuracy under domain shift but does not fundamentally address overconfidence or position bias. EpiCaR-style calibration substantially improves Brier but only modestly improves accuracy. Consensus selection improves both accuracy and hallucination rate, consistent with Kallem (2026), but still inherits retrieval position failures unless retrieval is made position-robust. PRCC-RAG’s gains suggest these components are complementary rather than redundant.

**Position bias is not just a retrieval issue—it becomes a generation reliability issue.** PosIR (Zeng et al., 2026) shows embedding sensitivity to evidence location; our results indicate that this sensitivity propagates into answer instability and hallucinations when evidence is missed or underweighted.

**Calibration enables safer abstention and better consensus.** EpiCaR (Yeom et al., 2026) argues that reasoning improvements can harm calibration; our findings align: removing calibration increases hallucinations and worsens Brier even when EM stays similar. Moreover, calibrated confidence is a strong feature for meta-selection, improving consensus quality.

**Limitations.**  
- PRCC-RAG assumes access to multiple models or decoding variants for consensus; this may be costly.  
- The span-localization and position perturbation protocol is inspired by PosIR but not a full replacement for PosIR-scale benchmarking (310 datasets).  
- Test-time adaptation introduces latency; practical deployment may require conditional triggering (e.g., only when PSS is high or confidence is low).

---

## Conclusion  
We presented PRCC-RAG, a unified framework for reliable RAG that integrates position-robust retrieval (motivated by PosIR), test-time adaptation (TTARAG), epistemic calibration (EpiCaR), and supervised multi-model consensus (Learning to Trust the Crowd). Across long-context and multi-hop reasoning settings, PRCC-RAG improves accuracy while simultaneously reducing hallucinations and improving calibration, addressing a key gap in prior work that typically optimizes only one reliability dimension at a time.
