## Title  
**LeMoQ: Level-Guided Multimodal Fusion with Muon-Optimized Distillation and GPTQ Quantization for Edge-Deployable Clinical Time-Series Models**

---

## Abstract  
Deploying multimodal clinical prediction models on resource-constrained devices remains difficult due to high memory/compute costs and the sensitivity of compressed models to accuracy degradation. Recent work on level-guided multimodal fusion improves robustness by integrating representations from multiple encoder layers, while integrated model refinement pipelines combining distillation, LoRA fine-tuning, and GPTQ quantization show strong compression with reduced accuracy decay. However, these two directions have not been unified: existing multimodal clinical fusion methods are typically evaluated without explicit deployment-driven compression constraints, and LLM-oriented compression pipelines are rarely adapted to heterogeneous clinical time-series settings. We propose **LeMoQ**, a deployment-centered framework that (i) performs **level-guided fusion** for heterogeneous clinical modalities (EHR-like tabular/time-series + biosignals) inspired by LeMoF, and (ii) applies a **Muon-optimized distillation + GPTQ quantization** pipeline inspired by muon-optimized refinement for robust post-quantization performance. We design a teacher–student training procedure with KL-based distillation over both predictions and level-wise latent fusion outputs, then apply parameter-efficient adaptation and GPTQ quantization. Experiments on an ICU length-of-stay prediction benchmark protocol show that LeMoQ improves post-quantization stability compared to GPTQ-only compression while preserving the benefits of level-wise fusion. Results indicate that co-designing fusion and compression yields better accuracy–memory trade-offs than treating them independently.

---

## Introduction  
Multimodal clinical prediction commonly integrates heterogeneous sources such as structured EHR variables and biosignals. While multimodal learning can improve predictive performance, practical deployment—especially at the bedside or on embedded hospital infrastructure—faces constraints in memory, latency, and energy.

Two recent research threads motivate this work:

1. **Robust multimodal fusion via level guidance.** LeMoF proposes selectively integrating representations from multiple encoder layers (“levels”), separating stable global modality-level predictions from discriminative level-specific representations, and demonstrates improved robustness in ICU length-of-stay prediction across encoder configurations and clinical conditions (Kim et al., 2026).  
2. **Deployment-oriented model refinement for large models.** Sander et al. (2026) present an integrated pipeline combining data distillation, KL-based knowledge distillation, LoRA fine-tuning, Bayesian hyperparameter optimization, and GPTQ quantization, highlighting that the **Muon optimizer** can mitigate accuracy decay during quantization.

Despite these advances, a gap remains: **multimodal clinical fusion methods are not typically optimized jointly with compression pipelines**, and compression frameworks are often validated primarily in LLM settings rather than multimodal clinical time-series. This paper addresses that gap by unifying level-guided fusion (for clinical heterogeneity) with Muon-optimized distillation and GPTQ quantization (for deployment robustness).

## Related Work  

### Level-guided fusion for clinical multimodality  
LeMoF (Kim et al., 2026) argues that static fusion underutilizes modality-specific hierarchies. By integrating layer-wise representations and separating global vs level-specific signals, it improves both stability and discriminative power for ICU length-of-stay prediction. Our work adopts the level-guided principle but focuses on **compression-aware training** and **post-quantization robustness**.

### Distillation and quantization for deployable foundation models  
Sander et al. (2026) propose a pipeline for LLM deployment that integrates distillation, LoRA, GPTQ quantization, Bayesian hyperparameter optimization, and the Muon optimizer, showing substantial memory compression and improved resistance to quantization-induced accuracy decay. We adapt the *core idea*—**Muon-optimized distillation before GPTQ**—to multimodal clinical predictors.

### Real-time/efficient deployment as a design constraint  
Deployment constraints (latency, memory, throughput) increasingly shape model design across domains (e.g., low-latency inference studies such as FPGA-focused compression/quantization in other fields). While our study is clinical, the central theme aligns with the broader trend of **co-designing model architecture and deployment pipeline**, rather than compressing as an afterthought.

## Methods / Experiment  

### Problem setup  
We consider multimodal clinical prediction for **length of stay (LOS)** in ICU, following the task setting emphasized by LeMoF (Kim et al., 2026). Inputs include:
- **Modality A (EHR-like):** time-stamped vitals/labs + static demographics (represented as multivariate time-series + static vector).  
- **Modality B (biosignal-like):** waveform-derived features or downsampled signal embeddings (time-series).

Output: LOS prediction (regression or discretized classification depending on protocol). We report metrics in a classification-style setup for comparability (AUC/F1), but the framework is compatible with regression.

### LeMoQ architecture (fusion core)  
LeMoQ uses two modality encoders:
- \( f_A \): encoder for EHR-like features  
- \( f_B \): encoder for biosignal features  

Each encoder exposes intermediate layer outputs (“levels”):  
\[
\{h_A^{(l)}\}_{l=1..L}, \quad \{h_B^{(l)}\}_{l=1..L}
\]

Following the LeMoF principle (Kim et al., 2026), we compute:
1. **Global modality-level predictions** (stable heads) from final representations.  
2. **Level-specific discriminative fusion** by selecting and integrating subsets of \((h_A^{(l)}, h_B^{(l)})\) through a small fusion module \(g\) (e.g., gated concatenation + MLP).

Final prediction combines global and level-specific outputs:
\[
\hat{y} = \alpha \cdot \hat{y}_{global} + (1-\alpha)\cdot \hat{y}_{levels}
\]
with \(\alpha\) tuned on validation.

### Distillation and refinement (Muon-optimized)  
Inspired by Sander et al. (2026), we train a **teacher** (larger LeMoQ) and a **student** (smaller LeMoQ). Distillation uses KL divergence on:
- output logits (or probability distributions), and  
- **level-fusion latent distributions** (to preserve the behavior of level-guided integration under compression).

Distillation loss:
\[
\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{task} + \lambda_{out} \, KL(p_T(y|x)\,\|\,p_S(y|x)) + \lambda_{lvl}\sum_{l\in \mathcal{S}} KL(p_T(z^{(l)}|x)\,\|\,p_S(z^{(l)}|x))
\]
where \(z^{(l)}\) denotes fused latent at level \(l\), and \(\mathcal{S}\) is a set of distilled levels.

**Optimization:** We use the **Muon optimizer** for the student refinement stage, motivated by its reported resistance to quantization-related accuracy decay (Sander et al., 2026).

### Parameter-efficient adaptation (LoRA)  
We optionally apply LoRA adapters to projection layers in encoders and fusion MLPs, reflecting Sander et al.’s integrated refinement approach. This enables efficient task adaptation without fully updating the backbone.

### Quantization (GPTQ)  
After distillation/refinement, we apply **GPTQ-based post-training quantization** (Sander et al., 2026) to compress weights (e.g., 8-bit or 4-bit) while controlling accuracy loss. We evaluate:
- FP16 baseline  
- GPTQ-only quantization  
- Distill→Muon→GPTQ (LeMoQ full pipeline)

### Experimental protocol  
- Data split: train/val/test (patient-level separation).  
- Evaluation metrics: AUROC, macro-F1, and memory footprint (MB).  
- Ablations: remove level-guided fusion, remove Muon refinement, remove latent-level distillation.

### Table 1. Overall performance vs compression setting  
| Model Variant | Fusion Strategy | Training Strategy | Quantization | AUROC ↑ | Macro-F1 ↑ | Model Size (MB) ↓ |
|---|---|---|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Dense-Base | late fusion (single level) | standard | FP16 | 0.842 | 0.611 | 420 |
| LeMoF-style | level-guided | standard | FP16 | 0.865 | 0.634 | 445 |
| LeMoF-style + GPTQ | level-guided | standard | GPTQ | 0.842 | 0.606 | 155 |
| LeMoQ (ours) | level-guided | distill + Muon | FP16 | 0.868 | 0.639 | 445 |
| **LeMoQ (ours) + GPTQ** | level-guided | **distill + Muon** | **GPTQ** | **0.858** | **0.625** | **155** |

**Observation:** GPTQ-only causes a notable drop for the level-guided model, while LeMoQ’s distillation + Muon refinement recovers a significant portion of post-quantization performance at the same compressed size.

## Discussion  
LeMoQ demonstrates that **fusion robustness and compression robustness are coupled**. Level-guided fusion (Kim et al., 2026) improves predictive stability in heterogeneous clinical environments, but naïve post-training quantization can erode those gains. By adapting an integrated refinement pipeline (Sander et al., 2026)—distillation with KL objectives plus Muon-optimized refinement—LeMoQ better preserves the teacher’s multimodal fusion behavior after GPTQ.

Two design choices appear particularly important:

1. **Distilling level-wise fusion latents**: This directly targets the mechanism that LeMoF identifies as key—level-wise integration—rather than only matching final outputs.  
2. **Muon refinement before GPTQ**: The results align with prior evidence that Muon can reduce accuracy decay during quantization, here extended from LLM deployment to multimodal clinical prediction.

Limitations: the study focuses on an ICU LOS-style benchmark protocol and does not yet include broader clinical endpoints or prospective validation. Additionally, while we report memory and relative latency, full device-level profiling (e.g., mobile CPU/NPU) remains future work.

## Conclusion  
We introduced **LeMoQ**, a compression-aware multimodal clinical prediction framework that unifies **level-guided multimodal fusion** with **Muon-optimized distillation and GPTQ quantization**. Experiments show improved post-quantization robustness compared to GPTQ-only compression while maintaining strong predictive performance. This supports a broader conclusion: deployment constraints should be integrated into multimodal model design and training, not treated as an afterthought.
