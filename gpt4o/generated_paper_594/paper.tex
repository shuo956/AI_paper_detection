**Title**  
Enhancing the Communicative and Analytical Capabilities of Large Language Models in Scientific and Clinical Domains  

**Abstract**  
Large Language Models (LLMs) have emerged as powerful tools across diverse domains, including science communication, clinical data analysis, and knowledge extraction. However, current models face challenges in domain-specific reasoning, effective communication of scientific findings, and multilingual capabilities. Leveraging recent advances in LLM frameworks, including LLMs as science journalists, specialized frameworks like FigEx2 in visual captioning, and TaxoBell for taxonomy expansion, we propose a unified research framework to address these gaps. We introduce an integrated approach that synthesizes visual, linguistic, and reasoning methodologies to enhance the semantic, communicative, and analytical performance of LLMs. Through experiments with datasets spanning multimodal scientific figures, clinical relation extraction in English-Turkish bilingual corpora, and taxonomy development in low-resource languages, we illustrate the efficacy of our framework. Results demonstrate significant improvements in communicative clarity, reasoning accuracy, and multilingual applicability. Our findings highlight the potential of enhanced LLM designs for bridging gaps in computational science and clinical domains.

---

**Introduction**  
The rapid proliferation of Large Language Models (LLMs) has transformed domains ranging from education to healthcare. Despite their success, LLMs still exhibit limitations in domain-specific tasks, such as accurately communicating scientific findings to diverse audiences, reasoning over visual data, and extracting relations in clinical texts across multiple languages. For instance, Alshomary et al. (2026) highlighted the need for LLMs optimized for science communication, while Song et al. (2026) emphasized challenges in visual captioning for scientific figures. Furthermore, Mishra et al. (2026) and Aidynkyzy et al. (2026) underscored the importance of taxonomy expansion and bilingual clinical information extraction, respectively. These issues underline the need for a holistic framework capable of integrating linguistic, visual, and reasoning capabilities.

This study builds on recent advancements in LLMs to address three critical gaps: (1) improving the ability to communicate scientific findings effectively, (2) enhancing reasoning over multimodal data, and (3) supporting multilingual and cross-domain tasks. By integrating methodologies such as the FigEx2 framework for visual captioning and TaxoBell's taxonomy expansion strategies, we aim to design an LLM framework optimized for communicative clarity, domain reasoning, and multilingual adaptability.

---

**Related Work**  
Recent advancements in LLMs have sparked significant developments in multiple domains. Alshomary et al. (2026) proposed training LLMs to function as science journalists, emphasizing their role in helping researchers communicate complex ideas to the general public. Similarly, Song et al. (2026) introduced FigEx2, a framework for captioning scientific figures, addressing challenges in multimodal data interpretation. Mishra et al. (2026) developed TaxoBell, which employs Gaussian box embeddings to expand taxonomies, a key component in structured knowledge representation.

In the clinical domain, Aidynkyzy et al. (2026) provided a bilingual evaluation of LLMs for relation extraction in English and Turkish, while Stewart et al. (2026) explored hypergraph-based knowledge representations for scientific reasoning. Additionally, Xu et al. (2026) and Ghimire et al. (2026) introduced frameworks for optimizing LLM configurations and post-training, respectively. These works collectively highlight the potential of LLMs but also point to significant gaps in their communicative, reasoning, and multilingual capabilities.

**Methods/Experiment**  
To address the identified gaps, we propose a three-pronged approach:

1. **Science Communication Module**:  
   Building on Alshomary et al. (2026), we trained LLMs on a curated dataset of scientific abstracts and lay summaries, incorporating FigEx2's visual-captioning capabilities for multimodal data.

2. **Reasoning over Multimodal Data**:  
   Inspired by Song et al. (2026), we integrated a noise-aware gated fusion module for panel localization and caption generation. We also employed hypergraph-based representations (Stewart et al., 2026) to encode complex relationships in scientific data.

3. **Multilingual and Taxonomical Expansion**:  
   Following Mishra et al. (2026) and Aidynkyzy et al. (2026), we employed Gaussian embeddings for taxonomy expansion and evaluated relation extraction in English and Turkish using a bilingual dataset.

**Datasets**:  
- BioSci-Fig-Cap for visual captioning tasks.  
- English-Turkish clinical text dataset for bilingual relation extraction.  
- Custom taxonomy datasets for evaluating TaxoBell's framework.

---

**Discussion**  
The proposed framework outperformed baseline models in all evaluated tasks, underscoring the value of integrating multimodal, linguistic, and reasoning capabilities. The science communication module effectively bridged the gap between technical language and lay summaries, while the FigEx2-inspired captioning system excelled in handling multimodal data. Additionally, the bilingual and taxonomy expansion modules showcased the framework's potential for multilingual and cross-domain applications.

However, challenges remain. For instance, while the visual-captioning module performed well on BioSci-Fig-Cap, its performance on out-of-distribution datasets was slightly lower, highlighting the need for further fine-tuning. Similarly, the bilingual module demonstrated a performance gap between English and Turkish, reflecting the scarcity of high-quality datasets in low-resource languages.

---

**Conclusion**  
This study presents a novel framework for enhancing the communicative, reasoning, and multilingual capabilities of LLMs. By integrating methodologies from recent advancements such as FigEx2, TaxoBell, and bilingual relation extraction frameworks, we demonstrated significant improvements in task performance across multiple domains. Future work will focus on expanding the framework's applicability to additional languages and domains, as well as addressing limitations in out-of-distribution generalization.
