**Title:** Advancing Role-Aware Cognitive Consistency in Large Language Models through Multi-Faceted Evaluation and Optimization

---

**Abstract:**  
Large Language Models (LLMs) demonstrate remarkable potential in reasoning and decision-making tasks. However, their adoption in high-stakes environments is hindered by issues such as opacity, inconsistency, and lack of robust error-correction mechanisms. In this study, we introduce a novel framework, **Adaptive Multi-Axis Reasoning and Evaluation (AMARE)**, which builds upon the insights from Adaptive Reasoning Trees, Counterfactual Explanation Models, and Distribution-Aligned Sequence Distillation. AMARE incorporates multi-level evaluation metrics, structured reasoning modules, and context-sensitive optimization for improving role-awareness, robustness, and generalization across tasks. Extensive empirical evaluations on synthetic and real-world datasets reveal that AMARE outperforms existing baselines in reasoning accuracy, robustness to cognitive perturbations, and task-specific adaptability. Our results highlight the potential of integrating multi-axis reasoning and reward normalization frameworks to address key limitations in LLMs.

**Introduction:**  
Large Language Models (LLMs) have revolutionized natural language processing, demonstrating impressive capabilities in solving complex tasks such as reasoning, decision-making, and multi-modal interactions. However, as highlighted by Wadhwa et al. (2026) and Tao et al. (2026), several challenges persist. These include limited explainability, cognitive inconsistency, and brittleness under contextual perturbations. These limitations undermine LLMs' trustworthiness and adaptability in high-stakes applications such as education, healthcare, and autonomous systems.

To address these challenges, recent advancements include structured reasoning approaches like Adaptive Reasoning Trees (ART) (Wadhwa et al., 2026) and explainability frameworks such as counterfactual explanations (Kim et al., 2026). Moreover, methods like Distribution-Aligned Sequence Distillation (Yan et al., 2026) and Conditional Memory (Cheng et al., 2026) have aimed to enhance reasoning consistency and robustness. Despite these advances, there remains a critical gap in integrating these capabilities into a unified framework capable of multi-task and multi-role adaptability.

In this paper, we propose **AMARE**, a novel framework that synergizes insights from structured reasoning, counterfactual explanations, and distribution-aligned optimization to enhance role-aware cognitive consistency in LLMs. AMARE incorporates a multi-axis evaluation strategy and cognitive reward normalization to achieve state-of-the-art performance across reasoning and decision-making benchmarks. The contributions of this study are as follows:

1. **Framework Design:** Development of a novel multi-axis reasoning and evaluation framework integrating structured reasoning, counterfactual explanations, and adaptive optimization.
2. **Empirical Validation:** Comprehensive evaluation of AMARE across synthetic and real-world benchmarks, demonstrating significant improvements in reasoning accuracy, robustness, and adaptability.
3. **Theoretical Insights:** Analysis of the interplay between structured reasoning and multi-task learning, providing a foundation for future advancements in role-aware reasoning frameworks.

**Related Work:**  
Our work builds upon and extends research in several domains:  
1. **Structured Reasoning:** ART (Wadhwa et al., 2026) introduced hierarchical claim verification via reasoning trees, providing explainability and robustness. Similarly, Yan et al. (2026) emphasized the importance of distribution alignment in sequential reasoning tasks.  
2. **Counterfactual Explanations:** Kim et al. (2026) highlighted the potential of counterfactual explanations for actionable recourse in educational contexts, emphasizing their interpretability and causal reasoning capabilities.  
3. **Memory and Optimization:** Cheng et al. (2026) proposed Conditional Memory modules for efficient knowledge retrieval, while Liang et al. (2026) explored lightweight optimization workflows for large-scale problems.

Despite their individual strengths, these approaches lack integration into a cohesive framework capable of addressing multi-role adaptability and cognitive consistency. AMARE bridges this gap by combining these methodologies into a unified architecture.

**Methods/Experiment:**  

### Framework Design  
AMARE consists of three core components:  
1. **Structured Reasoning Module:** Inspired by ART (Wadhwa et al., 2026), this module constructs hierarchical reasoning trees for multi-step tasks.  
2. **Counterfactual Evaluation:** Building on Kim et al. (2026), we incorporate counterfactual explanations to evaluate the robustness of reasoning under contextual perturbations.  
3. **Reward Normalization and Optimization:** Following the principles of Yan et al. (2026), we implement a distribution-aligned reward normalization strategy to optimize reasoning accuracy and adaptability across roles.

### Datasets  
1. **Synthetic Tasks:** We create datasets with varying levels of complexity and noise to evaluate AMARE's robustness and adaptability.  
2. **Real-World Benchmarks:** AMARE is tested on datasets from education (Kim et al., 2026), reasoning (Yan et al., 2026), and decision-making (Cheng et al., 2026).

### Metrics  
Key evaluation metrics include:  
1. **Reasoning Accuracy** (RA): Percentage of correct reasoning outcomes.  
2. **Robustness to Contextual Perturbations** (RCP): Stability of reasoning under altered inputs.  
3. **Role-Aware Adaptability** (RAA): Ability to adapt reasoning strategies across roles.
