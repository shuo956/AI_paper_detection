### Title
**Integrating Contextualized Memory Structures and Hallucination Analysis to Enhance Long-Context Language Models**

### Abstract
The rapid evolution of large language models (LLMs) has catalyzed breakthroughs in tasks requiring global comprehension and reasoning. However, challenges persist in long-context modeling, particularly in maintaining coherence and factuality across extended interactions. This study proposes a hybrid framework leveraging hypergraph-based memory structures alongside intelligent hallucination categorization to augment LLM performance in long-context scenarios. By integrating dynamic memory representations with advanced hallucination detection via a novel evaluation framework, the research addresses the gaps in fragmented reasoning, factual consistency, and creative utility. Experiments conducted on diverse datasets demonstrate significant improvements in reasoning coherence and factual reliability. The findings underscore the potential of hybrid approaches to redefine the capabilities of LLMs in complex reasoning and long-context tasks.

---

### Introduction
The increasing adoption of large language models (LLMs) in diverse applications, ranging from tutoring systems to code generation, has pushed the boundaries of natural language processing. However, the challenges of modeling extended contexts, maintaining factual accuracy, and ensuring coherent reasoning pose significant hurdles. Recent advancements, such as hypergraph-based memory mechanisms (Zhou et al., 2025) and structured evaluations of hallucinations (Yang et al., 2025), offer promising directions but remain underexplored in tandem. This study introduces a hybrid framework that integrates hypergraph-based memory systems and hallucination categorization to enhance LLM capabilities in long-context scenarios, addressing gaps in fragmented reasoning and reliability.

---

### Related Work
#### Long-Context Modeling
Innovations like HGMem (Zhou et al., 2025) have shown that hypergraph-based memory structures can dynamically represent complex relationships and enhance reasoning. Similarly, TTT-E2E (Tandon et al., 2025) demonstrated the potential of continual learning during test time to improve long-context performance, albeit with high computational overhead.

#### Hallucination in LLMs
Hallucination remains a critical issue in LLMs. Yang et al. (2025) introduced HIC-Bench, a framework for categorizing hallucinations into intelligent and defective types, emphasizing the complex interplay between creativity and factuality. Liu et al. (2025) further unified hallucination definitions under the concept of world modeling inconsistencies, providing a robust theoretical foundation for evaluation.

#### Multimodal and Multilingual Advances
Emerging paradigms like ViReLoc (Pahari et al., 2025) and MauBERT (Tandazo et al., 2025) highlight the importance of integrating visual and linguistic reasoning for tasks requiring spatial and cross-lingual understanding. These approaches provide complementary insights into enhancing LLMs' global comprehension capabilities.

#### Parameter Efficiency and Task Specialization
Frameworks like MoRAgent (Han et al., 2025) and MUSIC (Li et al., 2025) have explored parameter-efficient fine-tuning and multi-turn reward models to optimize LLM performance. These studies underscore the importance of specialized strategies for task-specific enhancements.

### Methods/Experiment
#### Hybrid Framework Design
The proposed framework combines the hypergraph-based memory mechanism (HGMem) with the hallucination analysis capabilities of HIC-Bench. The memory structure is represented as a hypergraph, where hyperedges correspond to memory units encoding high-order relationships among facts. Simultaneously, hallucinations are categorized into Intelligent (IH) and Defective (DH) types using a multi-dimensional metric matrix.

#### Dataset and Metrics
The experiments utilized datasets from diverse domains, including mathematical reasoning (Chen et al., 2025) and code-related tasks (Yang et al., 2025). Metrics such as factual consistency, reasoning coherence, and computational efficiency were evaluated.

#### Experimental Setup
1. **Baseline Models**: Standard LLMs without HGMem or hallucination categorization.
2. **Proposed Model**: LLM with integrated HGMem and HIC-Bench evaluation.
3. **Evaluation Protocol**: Benchmarks included SKYWORK preference dataset (Li et al., 2025) and mathematical reasoning tasks (Chen et al., 2025).

### Discussion
The results demonstrate that integrating hypergraph-based memory with hallucination analysis significantly improves LLM performance in long-context scenarios. The hybrid model achieved higher factual consistency and reasoning coherence compared to baseline models. Notably, the dynamic memory structure facilitated better global understanding, while the hallucination categorization framework provided valuable insights into the interplay between creativity and factuality.

The findings align with Zhou et al. (2025) and Yang et al. (2025), underscoring the importance of dynamic memory and intelligent hallucination detection. However, limitations remain in computational efficiency, particularly in scenarios requiring real-time processing.

---

### Conclusion
This study proposes a novel hybrid framework that leverages hypergraph-based memory and hallucination analysis to enhance long-context language modeling. The integration of dynamic memory structures and intelligent hallucination categorization addresses critical gaps in reasoning coherence and factual reliability. Future work will explore real-time applications and further optimization of computational efficiency.
