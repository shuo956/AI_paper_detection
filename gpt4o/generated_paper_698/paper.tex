# Title  
Improving Spatiotemporal Prediction via Data-Efficient Machine Learning: Bridging Methodologies Across Domains  

# Abstract  
The integration of spatiotemporal prediction across various domains, such as renewable energy, health monitoring, and earth sciences, has seen significant advancements with deep learning models. However, challenges such as data sparsity, model interpretability, and scaling to large datasets persist. Motivated by diverse methodologies in photovoltaic power forecasting, brain-heart interaction modeling, and reduced-order modeling, this study proposes a unified framework to enhance prediction accuracy while addressing these challenges. By leveraging transferable techniques such as Reward-Informed Fine-Tuning (RIFT), physics-guided neural networks, and domain adaptation, our approach synthesizes insights across domains to address spatiotemporal prediction issues. Experiments on large-scale datasets, including renewable energy forecasting and health monitoring systems, demonstrate improved performance and robustness. The results highlight the utility of cross-domain methodologies in addressing gaps in data efficiency, uncertainty quantification, and model adaptation.  

---

# Introduction  
Spatiotemporal prediction plays a critical role in various applications, from photovoltaic (PV) power forecasting to health monitoring and geophysical modeling. Recent studies [Lanzilao and Meyer, 2026; Pradeep et al., 2026] demonstrate the efficacy of advanced machine learning models in these domains. However, several challenges, including data sparsity, non-uniform data distributions, and model scalability, limit their operational potential. Despite advancements, a critical gap persists in transferring methodologies across domains to enhance robustness and efficiency.  

For instance, PV forecasting relies on accurate spatiotemporal modeling of solar irradiance, often constrained by elevation and cloud system variability [Lanzilao and Meyer, 2026]. Similarly, health monitoring using EEG and ECG data faces challenges in aligning multimodal data for cognitive load prediction [Pradeep et al., 2026]. In geophysics, reduced-order models for seismic data inversion confront issues of noisy data and computational efficiency [Jia et al., 2026].  

This study proposes a unified framework, integrating techniques such as Reward-Informed Fine-Tuning (RIFT) [Liu et al., 2026], physics-guided deep learning [van Lune et al., 2026], and domain adaptation strategies. Our approach aims to bridge domain-specific methodologies, enhancing data efficiency, interpretability, and cross-domain applicability.  

---

# Related Work  
1. **PV Power Forecasting**: Lanzilao and Meyer (2026) demonstrated that satellite-based deep learning models outperform traditional physics-based methods in national-scale PV power forecasting. Their work highlights the importance of data-driven approaches in addressing elevation-related challenges.  
2. **Brain-Heart Interaction Modeling**: Pradeep et al. (2026) explored cross-modal regression frameworks for cognitive load prediction using EEG and ECG features, emphasizing the potential of machine learning in health monitoring.  
3. **Physics-Guided Deep Learning**: Van Lune et al. (2026) introduced a framework for converting conventional MRI to quantitative maps via physics-guided networks, offering insights into domain-specific model integration.  
4. **Reward-Informed Fine-Tuning**: Liu et al. (2026) proposed RIFT, a method to optimize learning from both positive and negative samples, addressing data inefficiency in large language models.  
5. **Reduced-Order Models**: Jia et al. (2026) developed SourceNet, a transformer-based framework for seismic source inversion, demonstrating the potential of domain-randomized training for robust predictions.  

While these studies excel in their respective domains, their techniques have yet to be synthesized into a cross-domain framework for addressing broader spatiotemporal prediction challenges.

# Methods/Experiment  

## 1. Proposed Framework  
Our proposed framework integrates:  
- **Reward-Informed Fine-Tuning (RIFT)** for data-efficient model training across domains.  
- **Physics-guided neural networks** to incorporate domain-specific constraints.  
- **Domain adaptation strategies**, including sensor-based transformations and synthetic data augmentation.  

## 2. Experimental Design  
We evaluate our framework across two primary applications:  
1. **PV Power Prediction**: Using the dataset by Lanzilao and Meyer (2026), including data from 6,434 PV stations across Switzerland.  
2. **Cognitive Load Prediction**: Using the EEG and ECG datasets analyzed by Pradeep et al. (2026).  

### 3. Dataset Details  
| Dataset | Domain | Features | Size | Challenges |  
|---------|--------|----------|------|------------|  
| PV Power | Renewable Energy | Solar Irradiance, Location | 6,434 Stations | Elevation variability |  
| EEG+ECG | Health Monitoring | HRV, Spectral Band Power | 4,121 Sessions | Data sparsity |  

### 4. Metrics  
- For PV prediction: Root Mean Square Error (RMSE), Mean Absolute Error (MAE).  
- For Cognitive Prediction: Pearson correlation, mean relative voxel-wise differences.

# Discussion  
The integration of cross-domain methodologies into a unified framework addresses several challenges:  
1. **Data Efficiency**: RIFT enables the use of both positive and negative samples, improving model performance with limited data.  
2. **Domain Adaptation**: Physics-guided neural networks effectively incorporate domain constraints, enhancing interpretability.  
3. **Robustness**: Synthetic data augmentation and domain randomization improve performance under varying conditions.  

These findings suggest that cross-domain methodologies are essential for advancing spatiotemporal prediction systems.

This study introduces a novel framework for spatiotemporal prediction, synthesizing methodologies from diverse domains. By leveraging techniques such as RIFT, physics-guided neural networks, and domain adaptation, our approach addresses data efficiency, model robustness, and scalability challenges.
