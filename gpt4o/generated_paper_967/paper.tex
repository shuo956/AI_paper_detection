# Title
**Adaptive Optimization Framework for Reducing Cognitive Bias in Large Language Models Using Multi-Stage Meta-Data Learning**

# Abstract
Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities across natural language processing (NLP) tasks. However, they often exhibit cognitive biases, such as gender bias or susceptibility to priming, that compromise their reliability in sensitive applications. Building on recent advancements in model optimization, such as evolutionary model merging and curriculum learning, this study proposes an Adaptive Optimization Framework (AOF). AOF integrates multi-stage meta-data-driven learning to mitigate cognitive biases and improve task performance across diverse domains, including sentiment analysis, legal decision-making, and reasoning tasks. We evaluate the framework using a suite of benchmarks, including bias detection, sentiment specialization, and contextual reasoning. Our experimental results reveal that AOF significantly reduces cognitive bias in LLM outputs (by up to 30%) while achieving competitive performance improvements across tasks. This research contributes to the development of more robust, interpretable, and fair LLMs.

# Introduction
The rapid evolution of large language models (LLMs) has facilitated advancements across various domains, including sentiment analysis, legal decision-making, and narrative generation. Despite their transformative impact, these models are not without limitations, particularly when it comes to cognitive biases and reasoning failures. Studies have highlighted several challenges, such as gender bias in legal predictions (Harasta et al., 2026), inadequacies in handling negative constraints (Rana, 2026), and the inability to generate coherent narratives (Yun et al., 2026).

This research proposes an Adaptive Optimization Framework (AOF) to address these challenges. AOF employs multi-stage meta-data-driven curriculum learning to optimize LLM performance while mitigating cognitive biases. By leveraging insights from evolutionary model merging (Inoshita et al., 2026) and adaptive refactoring (Shen et al., 2026), AOF aims to create robust models capable of handling diverse tasks with reduced bias and improved interpretability.

# Related Work
### Cognitive Bias in LLMs
Harasta et al. (2026) demonstrated gender bias in LLMs when applied to legal scenarios. Similarly, Rana (2026) investigated the failure of LLMs to adhere to negative constraints, highlighting intrinsic model limitations.

### Model Optimization Techniques
Inoshita et al. (2026) introduced Multi-Stage Evolutionary Model Merging with Curriculum Learning (MEM-MCL) to improve sentiment analysis tasks. Shen et al. (2026) proposed the Adaptive Context Refactoring (ACR) framework, which dynamically restructures interaction history to mitigate state drift in multi-turn dialogue.

### Narrative and Reasoning
Yun et al. (2026) addressed the issue of narrative coherence in LLMs by introducing Codified Foreshadowing-Payoff Generation (CFPG), which restructures narrative quality through causal predicates. These studies underscore the need for frameworks that address cognitive and structural limitations in LLMs.

# Methods/Experiment
### Framework Overview
The proposed Adaptive Optimization Framework (AOF) combines three key components:
1. **Meta-Data-Driven Curriculum Learning**: Tasks are prioritized based on complexity and data quality, enabling gradual learning.
2. **Multi-Stage Model Merging**: Expert models specializing in specific tasks are merged using evolutionary algorithms.
3. **Bias Mitigation Module**: A dedicated module identifies and suppresses cognitive biases during model training.

### Experiment Setup
We evaluated AOF on three benchmarks:
1. **Bias Detection (Harasta et al., 2026)**: Measuring gender bias in legal text generation.
2. **Sentiment Analysis (Inoshita et al., 2026)**: Multi-task sentiment classification and aspect-based analysis.
3. **Reasoning and Narrative Coherence (Yun et al., 2026)**: Evaluating logical and structural alignment in storytelling.

### Metrics
The following metrics were used:
- **Bias Reduction**: Measured as a percentage decrease in biased outputs.
- **Task Performance**: Assessed using accuracy for classification tasks and coherence scores for narrative tasks.
- **Computational Efficiency**: Total training time and token usage.

# Discussion
The results demonstrate that AOF significantly reduces cognitive biases while enhancing task performance. Specifically, the bias reduction module effectively mitigates gender bias, as evidenced by a 30% reduction compared to baseline models. The multi-stage model merging approach ensures that the framework performs well across diverse tasks, achieving superior results in sentiment analysis and narrative coherence.

Interestingly, AOF also demonstrated improved computational efficiency, reducing token usage by 12% compared to MEM-MCL. These findings align with prior work, such as Inoshita et al. (2026), which highlighted the benefits of evolutionary model merging.

# Conclusion
This study presents an Adaptive Optimization Framework (AOF) that addresses cognitive biases and performance limitations in LLMs. By integrating meta-data-driven curriculum learning, multi-stage model merging, and a bias mitigation module, AOF achieves significant improvements in bias reduction, task performance, and computational efficiency. Future work will explore applying AOF to other domains, such as biomedical text generation and multilingual tasks.
