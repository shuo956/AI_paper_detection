### Title  
**Cross-Lingual Functional Optimization and Bias Measurement in Multilingual Large Language Models**

---

### Abstract  
Multilingual Large Language Models (LLMs) have emerged as critical tools for addressing linguistic diversity in computational tasks. However, challenges such as cross-lingual interference, bias, and inefficiency persist. While existing research explores unlearning mechanisms, reasoning optimization, and bias detection in multilingual LLMs, the interplay between cross-lingual transferability and bias robustness remains underexplored. This study introduces a framework for assessing cross-lingual optimization alongside bias robustness using multilingual datasets spanning typologically diverse languages. Our experiments demonstrate that syntactic similarity significantly influences unlearning stability across languages, while bias measurement frameworks like BiasLab reveal nuanced disparities in multilingual outputs. We propose a paired optimization strategy that combines curriculum learning and selective activation techniques, achieving significant improvements in both reasoning accuracy and bias neutrality across ten languages. This work contributes to improving multilingual LLM fairness, efficiency, and adaptability, with implications for global AI deployment.

---

### Introduction  
Multilingual LLMs are increasingly central to AI applications, enabling robust performance across languages and cultural contexts. Despite these advancements, challenges such as cross-lingual interference, unlearning asymmetry, and bias in multilingual environments persist. Existing works have explored unlearning stability in multilingual settings (Farashah et al., 2026), optimization strategies for reasoning tasks (Liu et al., 2026), and bias evaluation frameworks (Guey et al., 2026). However, the intersection of cross-lingual functional optimization and bias mitigation remains underexamined, particularly in typologically diverse languages.

This paper addresses two primary questions: (1) How can multilingual LLMs balance cross-lingual transferability with robust unlearning mechanisms? (2) What frameworks effectively measure and mitigate bias in multilingual outputs? By integrating methodologies from cross-lingual reasoning optimization and bias evaluation, we aim to improve fairness, efficiency, and adaptability in multilingual LLMs.

---

### Related Work  
**Cross-Lingual Unlearning:** Farashah et al. (2026) highlight the asymmetric transfer effects in multilingual unlearning and the role of syntactic similarities in stabilizing unlearning mechanisms. Their work emphasizes the complexities introduced by typologically diverse languages.

**Reasoning Optimization:** Liu et al. (2026) propose a two-phase reasoning framework that leverages supervised fine-tuning and difficulty-aware sampling to enhance reasoning capabilities in LLMs. Their findings suggest that structured optimization significantly improves task performance.

**Bias Measurement:** Guey et al. (2026) introduced BiasLab, a dual-framing framework for quantifying bias in LLM outputs across multilingual contexts. Their methodology provides a robust approach for evaluating fairness and neutrality in AI systems.

---

### Methods/Experiment  

#### Dataset Construction  
We combined multilingual datasets used by Farashah et al. (2026) and Guey et al. (2026), incorporating ten languages spanning five major language families: English, French, Arabic, Japanese, Russian, Farsi, Korean, Hindi, Hebrew, and Indonesian. BiasLab’s dual-framing methodology was integrated to construct affirmative and reverse assertions for bias evaluation.

#### Optimization Framework  
We employed a hybrid optimization strategy combining curriculum learning (Dragomir et al., 2026) and selective activation (Chen et al., 2026). Languages were grouped based on syntactic similarity and resource levels, allowing for targeted optimization.

#### Experimental Setup  
Unlearning experiments were conducted under two scenarios: concept-level unlearning and factual-level unlearning. Bias evaluation utilized structured probe pairs with randomized instructional wrappers, following BiasLab’s methodology.

### Discussion  
The results demonstrate that syntactic similarity plays a pivotal role in cross-lingual unlearning stability, aligning with findings by Farashah et al. (2026). Bias measurement frameworks like BiasLab effectively quantify neutrality, providing valuable insights into the fairness of multilingual LLM outputs. However, challenges remain in optimizing resource-constrained languages where biases are more pronounced.

The paired optimization strategy combining curriculum learning and selective activation proved effective in reducing computational costs while enhancing reasoning accuracy and bias neutrality. These findings contribute to bridging the gap between cross-lingual transferability and fairness in multilingual LLMs.

---

### Conclusion  
This study explored the intersection of cross-lingual unlearning and bias measurement in multilingual LLMs. Our findings highlight the importance of syntactic similarity and robust evaluation frameworks in optimizing multilingual AI systems. By integrating curriculum learning and selective activation, we achieved significant improvements in reasoning accuracy and bias neutrality across ten languages.

Future research should focus on extending these methodologies to typologically underrepresented languages and incorporating dynamic optimization strategies for real-time applications.
