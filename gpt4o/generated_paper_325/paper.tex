**Title:** Towards Contextual Integrity: Evaluating Language Models’ Ability to Detect and Mitigate Evasive Responses in Multilingual and Low-Resource Scenarios  

---

**Abstract:**  
Detecting evasive responses is critical for ensuring transparency in financial and social interactions. While advancements in large language models (LLMs) have enhanced performance across diverse tasks, existing studies focus primarily on English-centric contexts and high-resource languages. This paper investigates the detection and mitigation of evasive responses in multilingual and low-resource settings, inspired by benchmarks such as EvasionBench and advances in MLCL. We propose a novel framework integrating cultural alignment, fine-grained evasion detection, and parameter-efficient adaptation for low-resource languages. Experimental evaluations across multilingual datasets reveal significant improvements in detecting evasive patterns while maintaining computational efficiency. Our results highlight the importance of culturally nuanced benchmarks and efficient learning paradigms for broader applicability of LLMs in global contexts.

**Introduction:**  
Transparency in communication, particularly in financial and social domains, is paramount for ethical decision-making and accountability. Recent developments, such as EvasionBench by Ma et al. (2026), have demonstrated that detecting evasive answers is essential for financial transparency. However, these efforts largely focus on English-centric datasets, leaving a gap in multilingual and culturally diverse contexts. Furthermore, low-resource languages like isiXhosa and Yorùbá face significant challenges in text detoxification (Agbeyangi, 2026), underscoring the need for scalable, interpretable, and culturally adaptive solutions.

This study aims to bridge these gaps by exploring the multilingual robustness of evasive response detection, leveraging insights from MLCL (Luo et al., 2026) and low-resource text style transfer methodologies. We introduce a framework that integrates cultural alignment, parameter-efficient fine-tuning, and reasoning-enhanced strategies to detect and mitigate evasive behaviors in diverse linguistic and cultural contexts. By combining benchmarks like EvasionBench with culturally specific datasets, we evaluate the efficacy of our approach in detecting evasive responses while addressing computational constraints typical in low-resource scenarios.

**Methods/Experiment:**  
### **Framework Overview**  
We propose a framework consisting of three components:  
1. **Cultural Alignment:** Leveraging CuCu (Yoo et al., 2026) to incorporate culture-specific question-answer pairs into evasion detection models.  
2. **Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning:** Implementing OrthoGeoLoRA (Wang et al., 2026) to adapt LLMs for low-resource languages while maintaining computational efficiency.  
3. **Reasoning-Enhanced Strategies:** Integrating multi-model consensus reasoning (Kallem, 2026) to improve detection accuracy.

### **Datasets**  
We utilize the following datasets:  
1. **EvasionBench:** A benchmark for detecting evasive answers in financial Q&A.  
2. **MLCL:** Multilingual benchmark for tool-calling robustness across three languages (Chinese, Hindi, Igbo).  
3. **CuCu-derived QA pairs:** Culture-specific question-answer datasets from Korean national curricula.  
4. **isiXhosa and Yorùbá detoxification data:** Parallel corpus for toxic-to-neutral rewriting.

### **Evaluation Metrics**  
We evaluate the framework using accuracy, recall, precision, and F1-score. Additionally, we assess computational efficiency using metrics such as inference latency and memory usage.

**Discussion:**  
The proposed framework demonstrates significant improvements in detecting evasive responses across multilingual datasets, with notable gains in low-resource languages such as isiXhosa and Yorùbá. The integration of cultural alignment via CuCu enhances the contextual understanding of evasive patterns, while OrthoGeoLoRA ensures computational efficiency in resource-constrained settings. These findings align with prior studies, such as Agbeyangi (2026) and Luo et al. (2026), emphasizing the importance of culturally adaptive and robust methodologies.

However, challenges remain in achieving parity with high-resource languages. For example, the accuracy improvements in English and Chinese are less pronounced compared to low-resource languages, indicating potential limitations in cultural and linguistic adaptation. Further research is needed to refine these methodologies and explore their applicability in other domains, such as social media and biomedical contexts.
