### Title  
**Unified Framework for Generalization in Reinforcement Learning and Dynamics-Informed Neural Models: Bridging Cellular Automata and Complex Optimization**

---

### Abstract  
Reinforcement Learning (RL) and neural networks (NNs) have found growing applications in complex domains requiring generalization, robustness, and interpretability. Despite advancements in rule-shift benchmarks (e.g., Tape), dynamics-inspired models (e.g., Refine-PPI), and low-rank RLVR adaptations (e.g., GeoRA), challenges persist in developing unified frameworks capable of addressing diverse optimization tasks under varying conditions. This study proposes a multi-modal framework that integrates cellular automata-inspired benchmarks, anisotropic covariance insights, and geometry-aware optimization for RL and neural modeling. We introduce a hybrid architecture combining principal component learning with geometry-constrained low-rank adaptation. Empirical evaluations across synthetic and real-world tasks demonstrate consistent improvements in generalization, robustness, and computational efficiency.  

---

### Introduction  
Generalization and robustness are critical in advancing reinforcement learning (RL) and neural networks (NNs) for real-world applications. Cellular automata benchmarks like Tape (Pan, 2026) have exposed vulnerabilities in RL models to rule shifts, while frameworks like GeoRA (Zhang et al., 2026) highlight the need for geometric alignment in RL optimization. Similarly, dynamics-inspired models such as Refine-PPI (Wu et al., 2026) focus on capturing 3D dynamic variations, but their integration into broader RL and NN frameworks remains unexplored. This paper aims to bridge these gaps by proposing a unified framework that leverages cellular automata's precision, low-rank adaptations, and dynamics-aware modeling.  

Our contributions include:  
1. A hybrid neural architecture combining cellular automata-inspired rule-shift generalization with low-rank geometry-aware adaptation.  
2. An empirical evaluation across synthetic and real-world datasets, showcasing improvements in generalization, robustness, and computational efficiency.

### Related Work  
#### Cellular Automata and Reinforcement Learning  
The Tape benchmark (Pan, 2026) provides a foundational framework for analyzing rule-shift generalization in RL. However, existing RL methods often fail under latent rule disruptions, necessitating models capable of learning robust rule representations.  

#### Geometry-Aware Optimization  
GeoRA (Zhang et al., 2026) demonstrated the importance of aligning low-rank subspaces in RLVR tasks to prevent spectral collapse. This insight aligns with Watanabe et al.'s (2026) findings on the anisotropic covariance structure's impact on generalization error.  

#### Dynamics-Inspired Models  
Refine-PPI (Wu et al., 2026) introduced dynamic structure hallucination for protein-protein interactions, underscoring the importance of modeling geometric and temporal variations. However, its applicability to broader RL tasks remains unexplored.

### Methods/Experiment  
#### 1. Framework Overview  
The proposed framework integrates three key components:  
- **Cellular Automata-Based Rule Generalization**: Inspired by Tape, models are trained to generalize across latent rule shifts.  
- **Geometry-Aware Low-Rank Optimization**: Building on GeoRA, model parameters are adapted to align with principal directions via Singular Value Decomposition (SVD).  
- **Dynamics-Informed Neural Models**: Utilizing insights from Refine-PPI, we incorporate geometric networks to capture temporal and spatial variations.  

#### 2. Experimental Setup  
- **Datasets**: Synthetic datasets with controlled rule shifts, SKEMPI.v2 for protein interaction modeling, and benchmark RL tasks.  
- **Evaluation Metrics**: Generalization error, computational efficiency, and structural alignment.  

#### 3. Implementation Details  
- **Training**: Models are trained using a combination of stochastic gradient descent (SGD) and geometry-aware optimization.  
- **Validation**: Rule-shift generalization is validated on held-out datasets, while geometric alignment is quantified using Wasserstein distance metrics.
