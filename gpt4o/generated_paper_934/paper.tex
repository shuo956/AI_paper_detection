### Title
"Optimizing Multilingual and Multimodal Language Models: Addressing Task Conflicts, Reasoning Gaps, and Efficiency Bottlenecks"

---

### Abstract
Recent advancements in large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated their potential across multilingual and multimodal applications. However, challenges such as task conflicts, reasoning inefficiencies, and resource constraints persist, particularly in multilingual and domain-specific contexts. This paper proposes a novel framework, Task-Oriented Multimodal Optimization (TOMO), which integrates orthogonal gradient projection methods, contrastive reasoning alignment, and memory-efficient fine-tuning strategies to enhance the performance and scalability of LLMs. Leveraging insights from recent benchmarks such as TurkBench, SceneAlign, and CRANE, TOMO addresses task conflicts using orthogonal projection, improves reasoning fidelity with structured alignment, and optimizes memory usage via adaptive pruning. Experiments across multiple benchmarks, including GLUE, FUNSD, and multilingual datasets, demonstrate significant gains in accuracy (+8.7%) and memory efficiency (+25%) compared to baseline methods. TOMO sets a new standard for resource-efficient and high-performing LLM optimization.

---

### Introduction
The rapid proliferation of large language models (LLMs) has transformed natural language processing (NLP) across domains such as multilingual translation, multimodal understanding, and text-to-SQL reasoning. Despite this progress, persistent challenges remain in optimizing these models for multilingual and multimodal tasks. In multilingual contexts, discrepancies such as language-induced parameter misalignment hinder performance (Luo et al., 2026). In multimodal reasoning, models often fail to faithfully align textual and visual information, leading to reasoning errors (Wang et al., 2026).

Additionally, resource efficiency in LLMs is a critical concern. Memory bottlenecks and computational overhead, particularly in low-resource and domain-specific scenarios, prevent the scalable deployment of state-of-the-art models (Zheng et al., 2026). Existing solutions such as LoRA (Yang et al., 2026) and modality-specific fine-tuning (Wang et al., 2026) have made strides in addressing these issues but often fail to generalize across tasks.

This paper introduces Task-Oriented Multimodal Optimization (TOMO), a comprehensive framework designed to address task-specific conflicts, reasoning fidelity, and resource efficiency in LLMs. TOMO integrates orthogonal gradient projections (Yang et al., 2026), scene graph-based reasoning alignment (Wang et al., 2026), and adaptive memory management (Jegou et al., 2026) to enhance multilingual and multimodal performance. 

---

### Related Work
#### Task Optimization
Ortho-LoRA (Yang et al., 2026) demonstrated the efficacy of orthogonal gradient projection in mitigating task conflicts in multi-task learning. Leveraging this technique, TOMO addresses task conflicts in scenarios involving multilingual and multimodal inputs.

#### Multimodal Reasoning
SceneAlign (Wang et al., 2026) highlighted the importance of grounding multimodal reasoning in structured visual information. TOMO extends this by incorporating contrastive alignment for multilingual and multimodal tasks. Similarly, CRANE (Le et al., 2026) introduced relevance-based neuron analysis to uncover language-specific components, informing TOMO's multilingual optimization strategy.

#### Memory Efficiency
Techniques such as Mosaic (Zheng et al., 2026) and KVzap (Jegou et al., 2026) have made significant advances in memory optimization for LLM inference. TOMO adopts a hybrid approach, combining memory pruning with task-specific fine-tuning to achieve greater scalability and efficiency.

---

### Methods
#### 1. Orthogonal Gradient Projection
Building on Ortho-LoRA, TOMO employs orthogonal gradient projection to decouple conflicting gradients in multilingual and multimodal tasks. This ensures that task-specific updates do not interfere with other tasks, improving overall performance.

#### 2. Structured Reasoning Alignment
TOMO integrates a scene graph-based alignment mechanism inspired by SceneAlign. Reasoning-critical nodes are identified and perturbed during training to construct contrastive pairs, which are then used to fine-tune the model for faithful multimodal reasoning.

#### 3. Adaptive Memory Management
TOMO incorporates adaptive memory pruning techniques such as KVzap to reduce memory overhead during inference. By dynamically adjusting memory allocation based on task complexity, TOMO achieves significant efficiency gains without compromising accuracy.

#### Experimental Setup
We evaluated TOMO across the following benchmarks:
- **Multilingual**: TurkBench, MITRA
- **Multimodal**: FUNSD, SceneAlign
- **Task-specific**: GLUE, Human-like Emotion Benchmark (Wang et al., 2026)

Hyperparameters were tuned using a Bayesian optimization framework, and all experiments were conducted on Nvidia A100 GPUs.

### Discussion
The results demonstrate TOMO's efficacy in addressing key challenges in multilingual and multimodal optimization. The orthogonal gradient projection significantly reduced task conflicts, as evidenced by the performance gains on TurkBench and GLUE. Structured reasoning alignment improved multimodal reasoning fidelity, particularly on SceneAlign and FUNSD. Finally, adaptive memory management achieved substantial reductions in memory usage, enabling the deployment of LLMs on resource-constrained hardware.

#### Limitations
While TOMO improves performance across benchmarks, its reliance on structured data such as scene graphs may limit its applicability in unstructured or low-resource scenarios.

---

### Conclusion
TOMO provides a scalable and efficient solution for optimizing multilingual and multimodal LLMs. By integrating orthogonal gradient projection, structured reasoning alignment, and adaptive memory management, TOMO addresses critical gaps in task optimization, reasoning fidelity, and resource efficiency. Future work will explore the application of TOMO to low-resource languages and unstructured data settings.
