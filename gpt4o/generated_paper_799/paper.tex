### Title
**Unified Multimodal Analysis and Cognitive Bias Evaluation in Language Models: Bridging Knowledge Conflict and Sentiment Dynamics**

---

### Abstract
Large Language Models (LLMs) demonstrate remarkable capabilities in multimodal sentiment analysis, knowledge inference, and cross-lingual conflict resolution; however, the cognitive biases embedded in their decision-making processes remain underexplored. This study proposes a unified framework, leveraging methodologies from emotion recognition, sentiment analysis, and knowledge conflict paradigms, to analyze cognitive bias and sentiment dynamics in LLMs. We introduce a novel evaluation architecture that integrates hyperspherical contrastive learning for geometric alignment of emotions and cross-lingual conflict analysis for assessing internal knowledge contradictions across typologically diverse languages. Experiments on synthetic datasets and real-world applications reveal that multimodal LLMs exhibit inherent biases influenced by linguistic structures and sentiment polarity, which can be mitigated by introducing structural enhancements. Our findings uncover critical gaps in multimodal robustness and cognitive bias, paving the way for more accountable and interpretable language models.

---

### Introduction
Large Language Models (LLMs) have revolutionized natural language processing tasks such as emotion recognition, sentiment analysis, and knowledge generation. While these models have demonstrated proficiency in multimodal tasks, inherent biases—stemming from linguistic and cognitive factors—compromise their interpretability and reliability. Existing works (e.g., Qiao et al., 2026; Zhao et al., 2026) reveal challenges in managing cross-lingual knowledge conflicts and understanding sentiment dynamics within multimodal contexts.

This paper aims to bridge critical gaps by introducing a unified framework that integrates methodologies for geometric emotion alignment, cross-lingual knowledge conflict analysis, and sentiment polarity evaluation. By leveraging multimodal embeddings and advanced interpretability methods, we seek to uncover latent biases and propose effective solutions to mitigate them. Our contributions are threefold: (1) a comprehensive analysis of sentiment polarity and cognitive bias in LLMs, (2) a novel hyperspherical contrastive learning method for emotion representation alignment, and (3) empirical evaluations on synthetic and real datasets to validate our framework.

### Related Work
#### Multimodal Emotion Recognition
Qiao et al. (2026) introduced the Expert-Guided Multimodal Fusion (EGMF) framework, which effectively utilizes text, audio, and visual modalities to enhance emotion recognition. Their hierarchical dynamic gating mechanism demonstrated improved cross-lingual robustness, highlighting universal patterns in emotional expressions.

#### Knowledge Conflict and Cognitive Bias
Zhao et al. (2026) explored cross-lingual knowledge conflict, emphasizing the uneven distribution of parametric beliefs across languages. Their CLEAR framework systematically evaluated conflict resolution strategies, revealing the influence of linguistic structures on decision-making.

#### Geometric Representations of Emotions
Yamauchi & Aizawa (2026) proposed hyperspherical contrastive learning to align emotions in circular representations, thereby improving interpretability in sentiment analysis. Their findings laid the foundation for geometric alignment in multimodal systems.

#### Sentiment Analysis in Misinformation
Huang et al. (2026) evaluated cognitive biases in misinformation using multimodal LLMs, uncovering susceptibility to social cues and logical fallacies. Their work demonstrated the importance of robust sentiment evaluation in combating misinformation.

### Methods/Experiment
#### Framework Overview
Our unified framework integrates geometric emotion alignment and cross-lingual knowledge conflict resolution. It employs hyperspherical contrastive learning to structure emotion representations and CLEAR-inspired mechanisms for resolving knowledge conflicts. The experimental pipeline includes three main components:

1. **Emotion Representation Alignment**: Using hyperspherical embeddings for circular alignment of emotions, we aim to improve robustness against dimensionality reduction while preserving interpretability.
2. **Cross-Lingual Knowledge Conflict Evaluation**: CLEAR's evaluation methodology is adapted to assess typologically diverse languages and their influence on sentiment polarity.
3. **Cognitive Bias Assessment**: Multimodal embeddings are analyzed for susceptibility to biases using synthetic and real datasets.

#### Dataset
Experiments were conducted on synthetic datasets derived from multimodal benchmarks (e.g., MELD, MOSEI) and real-world datasets such as ConflictQA and MisSpans. The datasets cover typologically diverse languages and include fine-grained sentiment annotations.

#### Metrics
Evaluation metrics include:
- Accuracy of sentiment classification.
- Belief score for bias susceptibility.
- Cross-lingual conflict resolution rates.

### Discussion
The results demonstrate that hyperspherical contrastive learning significantly improves sentiment classification accuracy, particularly for datasets with complex emotion representations. Additionally, cross-lingual conflict resolution rates highlight the efficacy of integrating CLEAR-inspired methodologies within our framework. However, challenges remain in mitigating biases arising from linguistic structures and sentiment polarity imbalances.

Our findings suggest that typological diversity and linguistic affinity play critical roles in shaping cognitive biases within multimodal LLMs. Future work should explore adaptive mechanisms for bias mitigation and integrate domain-specific knowledge to enhance interpretability.

---

### Conclusion
This study introduced a unified framework for multimodal sentiment analysis and cognitive bias evaluation in LLMs. By leveraging hyperspherical contrastive learning and cross-lingual conflict resolution methodologies, we uncovered latent biases and proposed effective solutions for their mitigation. Our findings pave the way for more accountable and interpretable language models, with applications spanning sentiment analysis, misinformation detection, and cross-lingual knowledge inference.
