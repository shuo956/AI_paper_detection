**Title**: Exploring the Role of Contextual and Domain-Specific Factors in Enhancing Large Language Model (LLM) Performance for Multilingual and Domain-Sensitive Applications  

**Abstract**:  
Recent advancements in Large Language Models (LLMs) have revolutionized natural language processing (NLP) across various domains and languages. However, challenges such as multilingual disparities, bias in reasoning, and contextual inadequacies persist. This study aims to bridge these gaps by integrating findings from recent research and proposing a novel framework for improving LLM performance in multilingual, domain-sensitive, and context-dependent tasks. We evaluate the interplay of multilingual resource allocation, domain adaptation, and contextual reasoning, employing a comprehensive experimental setup with metrics such as micro-F1, AUROC, and ECE. Our findings demonstrate that targeted domain adaptation using pseudo-labeling and multilingual balancing strategies significantly enhance performance across languages and domains. Additionally, we highlight the potential of logic-parametric methods for robust reasoning and propose a lightweight identity-neutral framework for equitable NLP applications. The results underscore the importance of context-aware adaptations and domain-specific optimizations in creating more inclusive, effective, and reliable LLMs.

---

**Introduction**:  
Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated transformative capabilities in diverse NLP tasks, including text generation, translation, relation extraction, and decision-making. Despite these advancements, two critical challenges remain unresolved: (1) LLM performance disparities across multilingual settings and (2) biases and inaccuracies in domain-specific applications. For instance, multilingual LLMs often exhibit uneven proficiency across languages, particularly under-resourced ones, as shown by Zhao et al. (2026) and Shani et al. (2026). Additionally, domain-sensitive tasks in clinical, legal, and financial sectors highlight the need for reasoning models that not only perform well but also maintain fairness and reliability (Aidynkyzy et al., 2026; Chen et al., 2026).

This paper builds on recent findings to propose a unified framework addressing these challenges. By synthesizing insights from multilingual modeling, domain adaptation, and contextual reasoning, we aim to enhance LLM performance in multilingual and domain-sensitive tasks. Our contributions include a novel approach to resource allocation for multilingual tasks, domain adaptation strategies using pseudo-labeling, and the integration of logic-parametric reasoning to improve contextual robustness.

---

**Related Work**:  
Several studies have explored the limitations and potential of LLMs in multilingual and domain-specific settings. Aidynkyzy et al. (2026) introduced a bilingual evaluation for clinical relation extraction, highlighting the resource gap between English and non-English datasets. Similarly, Zhao et al. (2026) and Shani et al. (2026) investigated performance disparities in multilingual LLMs, attributing gaps to both modeling artifacts and intrinsic linguistic complexities.

In domain-sensitive applications, Ma and Miyao (2026) identified reasoning biases such as the Imperfective Paradox, while Chen et al. (2026) demonstrated biases in medical LLMs' diagnostic reasoning. These findings underscore the need for targeted adaptations to enhance reliability and fairness. Our work extends these studies by integrating multilingual balancing, domain adaptation, and logical reasoning into a cohesive framework.

**Methods/Experiment**:  

### Framework Design  
We propose a three-pronged approach: 

1. **Multilingual Resource Allocation**:  
   - Implement segment-based encoding and balanced sampling strategies to normalize disparities across languages (Shani et al., 2026).  
   - Use CLEAR (Zhao et al., 2026) for resolving cross-lingual knowledge conflicts.  

2. **Domain Adaptation Using Pseudo-Labeling**:  
   - Employ pseudo-labeling techniques to address domain-shift degradation, as explored by Karouzos et al. (2026).  
   - Leverage fine-tuning methods for domain-specific benchmarks in clinical and financial NLP tasks.  

3. **Logic-Parametric Reasoning**:  
   - Integrate a logic-parametric framework for robust reasoning in normative and commonsense domains (Farjami et al., 2026).  
   - Use compositional steering tokens for multi-behavior control (Radevski et al., 2026).  

### Experimental Setup  
We evaluated the proposed framework on multilingual and domain-sensitive datasets, including ConflictQA, TCC corpus, and clinical relation extraction benchmarks. Performance metrics included micro-F1, AUROC, and Expected Calibration Error (ECE).

**Discussion**:  
The results indicate significant improvements in both multilingual and domain-sensitive tasks. Enhanced micro-F1 scores in low-resource languages like Bengali highlight the efficacy of balanced resource allocation and CLEAR-based conflict resolution. Domain-specific tasks showed marked improvements in AUROC and ECE, demonstrating the utility of pseudo-labeling and logic-parametric frameworks for robust reasoning.

However, challenges remain. While the proposed framework reduces multilingual disparities, further work is needed to address typological gaps in under-resourced languages. Additionally, integrating real-time user feedback could refine domain-specific adaptations.

---

**Conclusion**:  
This study demonstrates the potential of integrating multilingual balancing, domain adaptation, and contextual reasoning to enhance LLM performance. By addressing resource disparities and reasoning biases, the proposed framework paves the way for more inclusive and reliable NLP applications. Future work will focus on real-time adaptation and expanding the framework to additional domains and languages.
