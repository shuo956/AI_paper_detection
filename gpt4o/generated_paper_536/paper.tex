**Title:** Enhancing Large Language Models Through Adaptive Computational Frameworks: Bridging Gaps in Reasoning, Retrieval, and Personalization  

---

**Abstract**  
Recent advancements in large language models (LLMs) have led to applications ranging from reasoning tasks to specialized domains such as finance, healthcare, and historical knowledge. However, challenges such as inefficiencies in reasoning, retrieval bottlenecks, lack of personalization, and domain-specific risks still persist. This study proposes a novel computational framework leveraging adaptive reasoning, retrieval augmentation, and personalized memory structures to address these gaps. We integrate methodologies such as epistemically calibrated reasoning, adaptive tree-based decoding, citation-context dataset discovery, and hierarchical taxonomy construction within multi-agent systems. Experimental results demonstrate significant improvements in reasoning accuracy, retrieval efficiency, and personalized dialogue consistency across diverse datasets. Our findings pave the way for scalable, efficient, and culturally aligned LLM applications.  

---

**Introduction**  

Large language models (LLMs) have revolutionized natural language processing by achieving exceptional performance across tasks such as reasoning, retrieval, and personalized dialogue. Despite their success, several limitations hinder their full potential:

1. **Reasoning Inefficiency:** Current methods like Chain-of-Thought (CoT) often lead to overconfidence and neglect nuanced reasoning paths, reducing calibration accuracy (Yeom et al., 2026).
2. **Retrieval Bottlenecks:** Keyword-based search engines fail to capture the semantic intent of research queries, limiting dataset discovery and retrieval performance (Tan & Duan, 2026).
3. **Persona Inconsistency:** Long-term personalized dialogue systems struggle with memory noise accumulation and reasoning degradation during extended interactions (Zhao et al., 2026).
4. **Domain-Specific Risks:** Specialized applications in domains like finance and healthcare face unique safety risks from implicit harmful prompts (Zheng et al., 2026).

To address these challenges, this paper proposes an adaptive computational framework combining epistemically calibrated reasoning, hierarchical retrieval augmentation, and structured memory evolution to enhance LLM capabilities.  

---

**Related Work**  

**Reasoning Frameworks:**  
Several efforts have focused on improving reasoning within LLMs. Structured Reasoning (Han et al., 2026) employs Generate-Verify-Revise paradigms to reduce unnecessary reasoning steps, while epistemically calibrated reasoning (Yeom et al., 2026) optimizes models for both accuracy and calibrated uncertainty.

**Retrieval Systems:**  
Citation-context mining (Tan & Duan, 2026) provides a foundation for dataset discovery based on actual research usage rather than metadata. CSR-RAG (Singh et al., 2026) introduces relational retrieval for text-to-SQL tasks, improving precision and recall.

**Personalization and Memory Systems:**  
PersonaTree (Zhao et al., 2026) enables structured growth in personalized dialogue systems, addressing noise accumulation and persona inconsistency.

**Domain-Specific Models:**  
RiskAtlas (Zheng et al., 2026) highlights implicit harmful prompts in specialized domains, identifying challenges in transforming domain knowledge into actionable constraints.

---

**Methods/Experiment**  

**1. Adaptive Reasoning Framework:**  
We implemented epistemically calibrated reasoning (EpiCaR) to enhance reasoning accuracy and calibration. Models were trained using explicit self-evaluation signals combined with iterative supervised fine-tuning.

**2. Semantic Retrieval Augmentation:**  
A hybrid retrieval system was developed by integrating citation-context mining and CSR-RAG techniques. The system dynamically maps queries to a universal latent space for scalable dataset discovery.

**3. PersonaTree Evolution:**  
We utilized PersonaTree as a user-centric profiling carrier. A lightweight MemListener model guided memory operations (\{ADD, UPDATE, DELETE\}), enabling dynamic memory evolution.

**4. Risk Assessment in Specialized Domains:**  
RiskAtlas methodology was adapted to construct implicit harmful prompts, leveraging dual-path obfuscation rewriting for enhanced evaluation in domain-specific contexts.

**Experimental Setup:**  
Datasets included reasoning benchmarks (GSM8K, MBPP), retrieval tasks, and personalized dialogue scenarios. Models were evaluated on accuracy, calibration, retrieval efficiency, and persona consistency.

**Discussion**  

Our experiment results highlight the transformative impact of integrating adaptive computational frameworks into LLMs:

1. **Improved Reasoning:** The EpiCaR framework significantly enhanced reasoning accuracy while reducing calibration error, confirming its effectiveness in addressing overconfidence in reasoning paths.
2. **Efficient Retrieval:** The hybrid retrieval system achieved remarkable precision and recall in dataset discovery, surpassing traditional keyword-based methods.
3. **Persona Consistency:** PersonaTree demonstrated superior memory compression and persona consistency, outperforming conventional systems.
4. **Domain Safety:** RiskAtlas effectively exposed implicit harmful prompts, emphasizing the need for robust frameworks in specialized domains.

These findings underscore the scalability and reliability of the proposed methodologies in diverse applications.

---

**Conclusion**  

This study introduces a comprehensive adaptive computational framework for enhancing reasoning, retrieval, and personalization in LLMs. By addressing inefficiencies in reasoning, retrieval bottlenecks, and persona inconsistency, we provide practical solutions for scalable and domain-specific applications. Future directions include integrating cultural alignment mechanisms and expanding the scope of implicit risk assessments.
