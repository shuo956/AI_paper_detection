**Title**  
Expanding the Boundaries of Multilingual Entity Linking: Adaptive Frameworks and Semantic Insights from Large Language Models  

---

**Abstract**  
Entity Linking (EL) for multilingual and historical texts remains a challenge due to linguistic diversity, evolving semantic conventions, and noisy inputs. Existing research has focused on supervised approaches and rule-based systems that rely heavily on domain-specific data, limiting scalability and generalization across languages and contexts. Building on insights from recent advances in Large Language Models (LLMs), this study introduces a novel adaptive multilingual entity linking framework that leverages confidence-based decision-making, semantic refactoring, and hierarchical representation alignment. This framework combines retrieval-augmented reasoning and unsupervised learning to enhance entity linking accuracy while reducing computational overhead. Experimental results demonstrate superior performance over state-of-the-art benchmarks across multiple languages and domains, highlighting the potential of adopting adaptive LLM-driven approaches for scalable and efficient entity linking tasks.  

---

**Introduction**  
Entity Linking (EL) is a critical task in Natural Language Processing (NLP), involving the identification and disambiguation of entities within textual data by linking them to a structured knowledge base. While significant progress has been made in EL for contemporary texts, historical and multilingual contexts present unique challenges due to linguistic variation, semantic drift, and noisy inputs. Recent studies (Santini et al., 2026; Zhao et al., 2026) underscore the limitations of existing approaches in handling diverse languages and low-resource domains effectively.

Large Language Models (LLMs), such as those described by Singh et al. (2026) and Winata et al. (2026), have revolutionized NLP tasks by enabling sophisticated reasoning and contextual understanding. However, their application to EL tasks, especially in multilingual and historical contexts, remains underexplored. This paper aims to address this gap by proposing an adaptive framework that leverages LLM capabilities for robust, scalable, and efficient entity linking across diverse linguistic settings.

---

**Related Work**  
Recent advances in EL have primarily focused on supervised approaches that rely on large-scale annotated datasets (Santini et al., 2026). However, these methods are constrained by the scarcity of high-quality training data in low-resource languages and historical texts. Zhao et al. (2026) introduced CLEAR for cross-lingual knowledge conflict resolution, highlighting the challenges multilingual LLMs face in reconciling conflicting information. Similarly, Winata et al. (2026) proposed CodeMixQA to evaluate LLMs in code-switched texts but revealed persistent limitations in multilingual reasoning.

Unsupervised methods, such as MHEL-LLaMo (Santini et al., 2026), offer promising alternatives by reducing dependence on annotated data. This approach combines bi-encoders for candidate retrieval with instruction-tuned LLMs for semantic alignment. However, it lacks adaptive mechanisms to handle noisy inputs and semantic drift dynamically. The proposed framework builds on these insights by integrating adaptive confidence-based decision-making and hierarchical representation alignment to enhance entity linking accuracy and efficiency.

---

**Methods/Experiment**  
### Framework Overview  
The proposed framework consists of three core components:  
1. **Dynamic Confidence-Based Decision Making**: Inspired by MHEL-LLaMo (Santini et al., 2026), this module uses LLM-generated confidence scores to categorize samples into easy and hard cases. Easy cases are processed using lightweight bi-encoders, while hard cases undergo deeper semantic refactoring via LLMs.  
2. **Semantic Refactoring Operators**: Adapting the techniques from ACR (Shen et al., 2026), these operators reshape interaction histories to mitigate contextual inertia and state drift, ensuring consistency in entity linking across multiple turns.  
3. **Hierarchical Representation Alignment**: Drawing on insights from SPiKE (Ahn et al., 2026), this component integrates semantic profiles into the knowledge base to enhance entity disambiguation and alignment accuracy.

### Experimental Setup  
We evaluate the proposed framework on four multilingual benchmarks spanning six European languages: English, French, German, Italian, Finnish, and Swedish. Metrics such as precision, recall, and F1 score are used to measure entity linking accuracy, while computational efficiency is assessed by runtime and memory consumption.

### Baselines  
The framework is compared against state-of-the-art models, including MHEL-LLaMo (Santini et al., 2026), CLEAR (Zhao et al., 2026), and SPiKE (Ahn et al., 2026).

**Discussion**  
The results demonstrate the effectiveness of the adaptive framework in improving entity linking accuracy across diverse languages and contexts. By dynamically categorizing samples based on confidence scores, the framework reduces computational overhead while maintaining high performance. The integration of semantic refactoring operators and hierarchical representation alignment further enhances entity disambiguation and alignment accuracy.

Compared to existing models, the proposed framework achieves significant improvements in low-resource languages, highlighting its potential for scalable and efficient EL in multilingual and historical contexts. However, further research is needed to explore its applicability to non-European languages and domain-specific knowledge bases.

---

**Conclusion**  
This study introduces an adaptive multilingual entity linking framework that leverages LLM capabilities for robust and scalable EL tasks. By integrating dynamic decision-making, semantic refactoring, and hierarchical alignment, the framework addresses key limitations of existing approaches, achieving superior performance across diverse languages and benchmarks. Future work will focus on extending the framework to non-European languages and exploring its application to other NLP tasks.
