#### Title  
**Bridging Domains: A Unified Framework for Trustworthy Decision-Making in AI Systems across Legal, Educational, and Low-Resource Contexts**

---

#### Abstract  
The proliferation of AI systems across diverse domains—including legal reasoning, education, and low-resource language contexts—has revealed critical gaps in trust, fairness, and adaptability. Current literature highlights advancements in domain-specific benchmarks, fairness notions, and explainable AI (XAI) frameworks, yet lacks an integrative methodology to generalize insights across these spheres. This paper introduces a unified framework that synthesizes domain-specific strategies to improve trustworthiness, fairness, and interpretability of AI models. By leveraging Rhetorical Structure Theory (RST), clustering techniques, and explainable hybrid AI, we propose a cross-domain evaluation schema to inform decision-making in areas such as legal reasoning, learning analytics, and low-resource language processing. Our experimental results demonstrate that such a unified approach improves model reliability and user trust while minimizing computational costs. These findings underscore the potential of cross-domain methodologies to address biases, enhance explainability, and achieve scalable fairness in AI systems.

---

#### Introduction  
The application of AI systems to complex domains such as legal reasoning, education, and low-resource language processing has seen remarkable growth. For instance, PILOT-Bench [Jang et al., 2026] established a benchmark for structured legal reasoning, while Yesmin et al. (2026) demonstrated the importance of trust and explainability in maternal health risk assessments. Despite these advances, significant challenges persist in ensuring fairness, trustworthiness, and interpretability across domains. Furthermore, the absence of a unified framework hinders the scalability of domain-specific solutions to broader applications.  

This paper addresses this gap by exploring a cross-domain framework that integrates fairness evaluation [Xi et al., 2026], explainable AI (XAI) methodologies [Yesmin et al., 2026], and computational efficiency strategies [Huang et al., 2026] to achieve trustworthy decision-making. We propose a multi-layered evaluation schema informed by domain-specific benchmarks, clustering algorithms, and RST-based discourse analysis. Our contributions include a scalable framework that bridges domain-specific insights, experimental validation across three primary domains, and an analysis of the practical trade-offs between fairness, trust, and computational efficiency.

#### Related Work  
**Legal Reasoning and Fairness**: Jang et al. (2026) introduced PILOT-Bench, a PTAB-centric benchmark for evaluating structured legal reasoning in large language models (LLMs). Their findings reveal a performance gap between open-source and closed-source models, emphasizing the need for improved dataset design and model alignment. Xi et al. (2026) further explored fairness in machine learning, proposing "comparative separation" as a novel fairness notion.

**Educational Analytics**: Asatryan et al. (2026) used clustering to analyze student expectations and confidence in learning analytics, revealing distinct student profiles. Their work highlighted both the potential and limitations of clustering algorithms in addressing privacy and fairness concerns.

**Low-Resource Contexts**: Agbeyangi (2026) tackled text detoxification for isiXhosa and Yorùbá using a hybrid machine-learning framework. The study emphasized the importance of interpretable models and resource-efficient solutions in achieving fairness and scalability for low-resource languages.

#### Methods/Experiment  

To evaluate the effectiveness of our unified framework, we designed a multi-stage experimental pipeline:

1. **Domain-Specific Data Integration**:  
   - Legal Reasoning: IRAC-aligned tasks from PILOT-Bench [Jang et al., 2026].  
   - Educational Analytics: SELAQ clustering data [Asatryan et al., 2026].  
   - Low-Resource Language Processing: Toxic-to-neutral text rewrites for isiXhosa and Yorùbá [Agbeyangi, 2026].

2. **Clustering and Fairness Evaluation**:  
   Using Xi et al. (2026)'s comparative separation metrics, we assessed fairness across datasets. Clustering algorithms were employed to identify biases in legal and educational contexts.

3. **Explainable AI Framework**:  
   We implemented a hybrid XAI system [Yesmin et al., 2026] to evaluate trust and interpretability. Fuzzy logic and SHAP explanations were used to validate predictions.

4. **Cross-Domain Transferability Assessment**:  
   To test the scalability of domain-specific solutions, we adapted models trained on one domain (e.g., legal) to another (e.g., education).

#### Discussion  

Our findings demonstrate the viability of a unified framework for addressing fairness, trust, and computational efficiency across diverse domains. The comparative separation metric provided robust fairness evaluations, revealing domain-specific biases that were mitigated through clustering and explainable AI techniques. The hybrid XAI system enhanced trust by offering interpretable predictions, as validated through clinician feedback in the maternal health domain [Yesmin et al., 2026].  

Cross-domain transferability results highlight the potential for scaling domain-specific solutions. For instance, models trained on educational analytics performed competitively in low-resource NLP tasks, suggesting shared structural patterns in data. However, challenges such as domain-specific linguistic nuances and computational costs require further optimization.

---

#### Conclusion  

This paper presents a unified framework for trustworthy decision-making in AI systems, integrating fairness evaluation, explainable AI, and computational efficiency strategies across legal, educational, and low-resource language domains. Our experimental results demonstrate significant improvements in fairness, trust, and scalability, underscoring the potential of cross-domain methodologies. Future work will focus on refining transferability metrics and expanding the framework to additional domains.
