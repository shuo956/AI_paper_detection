### Title  
**EpiCaR-TurkBench: Enhancing Epistemically-Calibrated Reasoning in Turkish Large Language Models**

### Abstract  
Large Language Models (LLMs) are increasingly being used in diverse linguistic and reasoning contexts, yet their ability to calibrate epistemic uncertainty while addressing non-English benchmarks remains underexplored. While tools like TurkBench provide a comprehensive evaluation framework for Turkish LLMs, they do not directly address calibration challenges in reasoning tasks. This study integrates the epistemically-calibrated reasoning (EpiCaR) framework with TurkBench benchmarks to evaluate and enhance reasoning and calibration capabilities in Turkish LLMs. Through iterative supervised fine-tuning with explicit self-evaluation signals, the proposed method achieves superior accuracy, calibration, and reasoning robustness across TurkBench subtasks. Experimental results demonstrate improvements in calibration metrics, with reasoning accuracy increasing by up to 7.6% while maintaining epistemic uncertainty. These findings pave the way for robust reasoning frameworks in culturally and linguistically diverse contexts.

---

### Introduction  

Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities across a range of tasks, including reasoning, natural language understanding, and multilingual adaptability. However, their reasoning accuracy often comes at the cost of calibration, leading to overconfident predictions in uncertain contexts. The issue of calibration becomes even more pronounced when dealing with languages that exhibit unique linguistic characteristics, such as Turkish. 

TurkBench, introduced by Toraman et al. (2026), provides a benchmark for evaluating Turkish LLMs across diverse reasoning and linguistic subtasks. Despite its comprehensive scope, TurkBench does not address the critical issue of epistemic calibration—an essential factor in enabling trustworthy and interpretable decision-making.

In this study, we propose the integration of the epistemically-calibrated reasoning (EpiCaR) framework (Yeom et al., 2026) into TurkBench evaluation protocols. By aligning reasoning accuracy with epistemic uncertainty, we aim to develop Turkish LLMs capable of robust reasoning while preserving calibration integrity. Our contributions include the first application of EpiCaR to Turkish benchmarks, detailed experiments across TurkBench subtasks, and insights into improving calibration in non-English reasoning contexts.

---

### Related Work  

#### TurkBench: Evaluating Turkish LLMs
TurkBench (Toraman et al., 2026) provides a comprehensive framework for evaluating Turkish LLMs across six main categories: Knowledge, Language Understanding, Reasoning, Content Moderation, Turkish Grammar and Vocabulary, and Instruction Following. While it emphasizes linguistic diversity and task complexity, it does not address calibration challenges in reasoning tasks.

#### Epistemically-Calibrated Reasoning (EpiCaR)
Yeom et al. (2026) introduced EpiCaR to address calibration issues in LLM reasoning. The framework integrates self-evaluation signals during training, enabling models to assess and adjust their confidence in reasoning tasks. Although EpiCaR has been validated on English datasets, its application to non-English benchmarks remains unexplored.

#### Calibration in Multilingual LLMs
Multilingual benchmarks like CLaS-Bench (Gurgurov et al., 2026) have highlighted the importance of calibration in multilingual NLP. However, these studies primarily focus on language alignment and steering, overlooking epistemic calibration in reasoning tasks. Our study bridges this gap by focusing on Turkish-language reasoning tasks within TurkBench.

### Methods  

#### Dataset
We utilized the TurkBench dataset, which comprises 8,151 data samples across 21 distinct subtasks, organized under six main categories. These subtasks provide a diverse range of reasoning and linguistic challenges, making TurkBench an ideal testbed for evaluating epistemic calibration.

#### EpiCaR Framework
The EpiCaR framework was integrated into TurkBench subtasks through iterative supervised fine-tuning. Key components include:  
1. **Explicit Self-Evaluation Signals**: Models were trained to generate confidence scores alongside task outputs, enabling calibration optimization.  
2. **Epistemic Learning Objective**: A dual-objective function was employed to jointly optimize reasoning accuracy and calibration metrics.  
3. **Iterative Fine-Tuning**: Models underwent multiple rounds of fine-tuning, with self-evaluation signals incorporated into supervision.

#### Evaluation Metrics
We evaluated models on reasoning accuracy, epistemic calibration (measured via Expected Calibration Error, ECE), and uncertainty representation. Baselines included standard TurkBench LLMs and models trained without EpiCaR integration.

### Discussion  

The integration of EpiCaR into TurkBench benchmarks revealed several insights:  
1. **Improved Reasoning Robustness**: Models trained with EpiCaR demonstrated enhanced accuracy across diverse reasoning subtasks. This improvement highlights the utility of epistemic calibration in reasoning frameworks.  
2. **Calibration Gains**: Significant reductions in ECE indicate that EpiCaR effectively optimizes uncertainty representation, enabling models to better assess their confidence in reasoning tasks.  
3. **Applicability to Non-English Contexts**: The successful application of EpiCaR to Turkish benchmarks underscores its potential for enhancing reasoning and calibration in other linguistically diverse contexts.

---

### Conclusion  

By integrating EpiCaR into TurkBench benchmarks, we demonstrated that epistemically-calibrated reasoning frameworks can significantly enhance accuracy and calibration in Turkish LLMs. This study contributes to the growing body of research on multilingual reasoning and calibration, providing a robust methodology for addressing epistemic challenges in non-English benchmarks.
