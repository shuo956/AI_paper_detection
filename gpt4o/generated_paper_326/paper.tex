**Title:** Interpretable Multi-Modal Learning Frameworks for Complex Scientific Applications: A Unified Perspective

---

**Abstract**  
Recent advancements in multi-modal learning frameworks have enabled breakthroughs across scientific domains, including medical diagnostics, environmental monitoring, and physical modeling. However, significant gaps remain in scalability, interpretability, and robustness across diverse datasets, particularly in small-sample and challenging noise conditions. Building upon foundational work in tensor decomposition, contrastive learning, and domain-specific frameworks (e.g., OceanSAR-2 and SyntheFluor-RL), this study introduces a unified multi-modal learning framework that integrates contextual discrepancy-aware contrastive learning, graph-based embeddings, and stochastic optimization. The proposed methodology demonstrates enhanced interpretability, scalability, and domain applicability across experimental settings, including lunar terrain reconstruction and EEG signal analysis. This paper presents empirical results highlighting significant improvements in predictive accuracy, computational efficiency, and cross-domain transferability, paving the way for robust, interpretable solutions in real-world applications.

---

**Introduction**  
Multi-modal learning frameworks have emerged as pivotal tools in addressing complex scientific problems characterized by high-dimensional, noisy datasets. Applications span environmental monitoring (OceanSAR-2), medical diagnostics (CoDAC), and physical modeling (SourceNet). Despite significant advancements, challenges such as data scarcity, interpretability, and scalability limit adoption in real-world settings. For example, tensor decomposition techniques like SymGCP struggle with symmetry constraints in complex data, while contrastive learning frameworks often underperform in small-sample scenarios or fail to capture cross-domain dynamics.

This study seeks to bridge these gaps by introducing an interpretable, scalable multi-modal learning framework that combines contextual discrepancy-aware methods, graph-based embeddings, and robust optimization techniques. Leveraging existing literature on EEG analysis, lunar terrain reconstruction, and stochastic physical modeling, we evaluate our methodology across diverse datasets, aiming to establish a unified approach for tackling scientific challenges.

---

**Related Work**  
1. **Tensor Decompositions**  
SymGCP decomposition has proven effective for modeling symmetry in tensor data but lacks scalability in large datasets. Mulrooney & Hong (2026) proposed stochastic approximations to address computational bottlenecks, which remain limited in multi-modal settings.

2. **Contrastive Learning for Medical Time Series**  
CoDAC, proposed by Tanaka et al. (2026), integrates contextual discrepancy estimation for robust medical diagnostics. While effective in small-sample settings, the framework does not address domain transferability.

3. **Environmental Monitoring**  
OceanSAR-2 (Tuel et al., 2026) introduced self-supervised learning for ocean observation, achieving high transfer performance across geophysical tasks. However, challenges remain in adapting models to temporal and spatial variability.

4. **Physical Modeling**  
SourceNet (Jia et al., 2026) employed Transformers for earthquake source inversion, leveraging domain randomization for Sim-to-Real transfer. Despite its success, interpretability remains a challenge in practical deployments.

**Methods/Experiment**  
The proposed framework integrates the following components:  
1. **Contextual Discrepancy Estimation (CDE):** A Transformer-based module quantifies abnormalities using dynamic anomaly scores.  
2. **Graph-Based Embeddings:** Leveraging spectral graph convolutional methods (Tengler et al., 2026), embeddings are computed to capture spatial dependencies.  
3. **Stochastic Optimization:** A cubic stochastic differential equation model (Gee & Ravela, 2026) is adopted for robust simulation across diverse datasets.
