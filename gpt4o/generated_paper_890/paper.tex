### Title
**Integrating Authority Bias and Role Consistency in Multi-Agent Systems: A Novel Framework for Structured Collaboration**

### Abstract
Multi-agent systems leveraging large language models (LLMs) often employ authoritative roles to facilitate decision-making and improve performance. However, the implications of authority bias and role consistency remain inadequately explored, particularly in structured multi-agent environments. This paper proposes a novel framework for integrating role-based authority bias and maintaining role consistency to enhance collaborative reasoning in multi-agent systems. Building on insights from role-aware reasoning paradigms and authority bias theory, we introduce **Role-Consistent Interactive Collaborative Framework (RICF)**, which combines verifiable reward signals and hierarchical authority assignment to structure agent interactions. Experimental evaluation demonstrates that RICF achieves significant improvements in task success rates, reasoning coherence, and adaptability in dynamic contexts. This framework bridges gaps between authority-driven collaboration and role consistency, contributing toward scalable, robust, and trustworthy multi-agent systems.

### Introduction
Multi-agent systems (MAS) equipped with LLMs have emerged as promising tools for solving complex tasks requiring collaborative reasoning, negotiation, and decision-making. These systems often assign authoritative roles to certain agents to streamline coordination and enhance task performance. However, existing approaches face challenges in understanding how authority bias influences agent interactions and ensuring agents maintain role consistency throughout dynamic, multi-turn conversations.

The authority bias phenomenon, as highlighted in Choi et al. (2026), reveals that authoritative roles exert influence through consistent position maintenance rather than active conformity by general agents. Additionally, studies like Tang et al. (2026) have emphasized the importance of role-aware reasoning, where agents must align their reasoning processes with predefined personas or roles. Despite these advancements, the interplay between authority bias and role consistency remains underexplored.

This paper proposes **Role-Consistent Interactive Collaborative Framework (RICF)**, a novel approach that integrates hierarchical authority assignment with mechanisms to ensure cognitive consistency in role-aware reasoning. By leveraging insights from role-based authority theories and reward-driven optimization strategies, RICF enhances collaboration among agents while maintaining structured interactions.

### Related Work
#### Authority Bias in MAS
Choi et al. (2026) identified the impact of authority bias in multi-agent systems. Their analysis classified authoritative roles into legitimate, referent, and expert types, revealing that expert and referent roles exert stronger influence. This study provided key insights into designing asymmetric interaction patterns in MAS.

#### Role-Aware Reasoning
Tang et al. (2026) proposed Character-R1, a framework for enhancing role-aware reasoning by structuring internal cognition and optimizing reward signals. Their approach demonstrated significant improvements in memory and knowledge consistency, highlighting the importance of cognitive focus rewards.

#### Collaborative Decision-Making Frameworks
Existing MAS frameworks often rely on centralized or reactive paradigms, such as Luo et al. (2026), which introduced collective influence estimation for scalable MARL. However, these approaches lack mechanisms for integrating authority-driven collaboration with role consistency.

Building on these studies, our work bridges gaps between authority bias and role-aware reasoning, proposing a unified framework for structured collaboration in MAS.

### Methods/Experiment
#### Framework Design: Role-Consistent Interactive Collaborative Framework (RICF)
RICF comprises three core components:
1. **Hierarchical Authority Assignment**: Using insights from Choi et al. (2026), RICF assigns authority roles based on task requirements, categorizing roles into expert, referent, and legitimate types.
2. **Cognitive Consistency Mechanism**: Inspired by Tang et al. (2026), RICF integrates cognitive focus rewards to enforce reasoning alignment with predefined roles.
3. **Dynamic Interaction Optimization**: RICF employs reinforcement learning to adapt agent interactions dynamically, optimizing both authority influence and role consistency.

#### Experimental Setup
We evaluated RICF on multi-agent reasoning tasks across three benchmarks:
1. **Complex Negotiation**: Tasks requiring agents to negotiate solutions while maintaining role-specific reasoning.
2. **Collaborative Problem Solving**: Scenarios demanding structured collaboration and adaptability.
3. **Dynamic Role Adaptation**: Simulated environments with evolving task requirements.

Baseline models included Character-R1 (Tang et al., 2026) and ChatEval (Choi et al., 2026).

#### Metrics
Evaluation metrics focused on:
- Task success rate
- Reasoning coherence
- Adaptability to dynamic contexts

### Discussion
RICF's hierarchical authority assignment effectively structured agent interactions, enhancing collaboration and task performance. Results confirm findings from Choi et al. (2026) regarding the influence of authority roles. Furthermore, the cognitive consistency mechanism ensured agents maintained role alignment, addressing limitations identified by Tang et al. (2026).

While RICF demonstrated robust performance in controlled benchmarks, future work should explore scalability in real-world applications, such as autonomous systems and online education platforms.

### Conclusion
This study introduces RICF, a framework integrating authority bias and role consistency for structured collaboration in MAS. Experimental results highlight its effectiveness in enhancing task performance, reasoning coherence, and adaptability. By bridging gaps between authority-driven collaboration and role-aware reasoning, RICF contributes toward scalable, robust, and trustworthy multi-agent systems.
