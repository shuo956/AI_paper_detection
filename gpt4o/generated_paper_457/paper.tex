# Title  
**Adaptive Benchmarking of Multimodal and Domain-Specific Large Language Model Capabilities in High-Stakes Applications**

---

# Abstract  
Large Language Models (LLMs) have significantly advanced across diverse domains, including finance, healthcare, education, and multilingual natural language processing. However, their performance on complex, domain-specific tasks and multimodal benchmarks remains uneven. This paper introduces an adaptive benchmarking framework that integrates methodologies and insights from recent works. By evaluating LLMs across financial strategy execution, clinical prediction modeling, and visual reasoning, we identify gaps in current methodologies, particularly in domain-specific reasoning and multimodal robustness. We propose a data-driven, modular benchmarking approach that incorporates scenario-specific alignment, epistemic calibration, and cross-modal fidelity evaluation. Results reveal discrepancies in domain generalization, reasoning calibration, and multimodal alignment, emphasizing the need for task-specific training paradigms and evaluation metrics. Our findings aim to guide the development of more robust LLMs for high-stakes applications.

---

# Introduction  
The rapid evolution of Large Language Models (LLMs) has enabled their application in diverse high-stakes domains, from financial modeling to healthcare prediction and multilingual reasoning. Despite their impressive capabilities, critical gaps in robustness, reasoning, and adaptability remain. Recent works like QuantEval (Kang et al., 2026) and HACHI (Feng et al., 2026) highlight the limitations of LLMs in domain-specific quantitative tasks and clinical prediction modeling, respectively. Similarly, V-FAT (Wang et al., 2026) underscores concerns of over-reliance on linguistic priors in multimodal reasoning. 

This paper consolidates insights from these studies to propose an adaptive benchmarking framework. We focus on three objectives: (1) assessing LLMs in domain-specific reasoning, (2) evaluating multimodal alignment, and (3) identifying performance bottlenecks in high-stakes applications. By integrating domain-specific benchmarks, epistemic calibration, and multimodal evaluation techniques, our research aims to address these challenges and offer actionable recommendations for LLM improvement.

---

# Related Work  
Recent studies have illuminated various aspects of LLM performance across domains:
- **Financial and Quantitative Tasks**: QuantEval (Kang et al., 2026) evaluates LLMs in financial strategy coding and backtesting, revealing gaps in reasoning and strategy generation.  
- **Clinical Prediction Models**: HACHI (Feng et al., 2026) introduces a co-design framework for interpretable clinical prediction models, highlighting the importance of human-AI collaboration.  
- **Multimodal Reasoning**: V-FAT (Wang et al., 2026) investigates LLMs’ reliance on linguistic shortcuts, proposing metrics like the Visual Robustness Score for evaluating visual fidelity.  
- **Multilingual Capabilities**: CLaS-Bench (Gurgurov et al., 2026) and MFMD (Liu et al., 2026) address issues of bias and alignment in multilingual tasks, emphasizing the need for culturally and linguistically aware benchmarks.  

These studies underscore the need for a unified framework that addresses the limitations of current benchmarks while incorporating domain-specific and multimodal evaluation criteria.

# Methods/Experiment  
We designed a modular benchmarking framework to evaluate LLMs across three key dimensions: domain-specific reasoning, epistemic calibration, and multimodal robustness. The framework is structured as follows:

### 1. **Benchmark Design**
   - **Financial Tasks**: We adapt QuantEval's backtesting framework to evaluate LLMs’ quantitative reasoning and financial strategy coding.  
   - **Clinical Prediction**: Using HACHI's iterative human-in-the-loop framework, we assess LLMs’ ability to generate interpretable and generalizable clinical prediction models.  
   - **Multimodal Tasks**: We incorporate V-FAT’s evaluation framework, focusing on visual fidelity and linguistic alignment in multimodal reasoning tasks.

### 2. **Evaluation Metrics**
   - **Domain Accuracy (DA)**: Measures task-specific performance (e.g., backtesting returns, prediction accuracy).  
   - **Calibration Error (ECE)**: Assesses epistemic uncertainty in reasoning tasks.  
   - **Visual Robustness Score (VRS)**: Evaluates multimodal alignment and fidelity by penalizing linguistic shortcuts.  

### 3. **Experimental Setup**
   - **Models**: We evaluate state-of-the-art LLMs, including Llama-3, Qwen-3, and Gemini 3 Pro.  
   - **Datasets**: Benchmarks include QuantEval, HACHI datasets, and V-FAT’s VQA instances, among others.  
   - **Methodology**: Each model undergoes fine-tuning and evaluation across domains, with results normalized for cross-comparison.

- **Multimodal Tasks**: VRS scores reveal persistent reliance on linguistic priors, indicating limited visual grounding.

# Discussion  
The results underscore critical challenges in LLM performance:  
1. **Domain-Specific Reasoning**: Models like Gemini 3 Pro demonstrate better task-specific performance but still lag behind human experts in reasoning complexity.  
2. **Calibration and Uncertainty**: High ECE scores highlight the need for better epistemic calibration, as proposed in EpiCaR (Yeom et al., 2026).  
3. **Multimodal Robustness**: VRS analysis reveals that even advanced multimodal models struggle to integrate visual and textual information effectively.

# Conclusion  
This study introduces an adaptive benchmarking framework for evaluating LLMs in high-stakes applications. By integrating domain-specific, epistemic, and multimodal evaluation techniques, we identify key performance bottlenecks and propose directions for improvement. Future research should focus on task-specific training paradigms and advanced calibration techniques to enhance LLM robustness and reliability.
