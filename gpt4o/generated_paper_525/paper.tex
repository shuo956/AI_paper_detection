Title: **Advancing Multimodal Predictive Frameworks through Hybrid Optimization and Interpretable Machine Learning**

---

Abstract:  
The integration of multimodal data, efficient optimization algorithms, and interpretable machine learning models has emerged as a cornerstone of advancements in predictive systems. This paper presents a novel hybrid optimization framework inspired by recent developments in Split Federated Learning (SFL) and interpretable neural architectures. By addressing computational constraints on edge devices and leveraging scalable optimization methodologies, the proposed framework bridges the current gaps in balancing client-side resource efficiency with model accuracy. Furthermore, we introduce a domain-specific application for forecasting and classification tasks, utilizing interpretable neural additive models to ensure intelligibility and robust uncertainty estimation. Experiments across diverse datasets underscore the efficacy of the framework, demonstrating improved predictive accuracy, reduced computational strain, and enhanced interpretability. This work contributes to the development of scalable, resource-efficient, and transparent machine learning systems applicable to real-world scenarios, such as patient monitoring in critical care and energy forecasting.

---

### Introduction  
The exponential growth of data and computational resources has fostered significant progress in machine learning (ML). However, resource-constrained environments, such as edge devices and embedded systems, pose unique challenges, including limited memory, computational power, and latency requirements. Simultaneously, the demand for interpretable and robust models has increased across domains like healthcare, energy forecasting, and environmental monitoring. Although recent advancements in federated learning, Gaussian processes, and neural additive models have tackled aspects of these challenges, the field lacks a unified approach combining resource efficiency, predictive accuracy, and interpretability.

Our study builds upon recent contributions in Split Federated Learning (Kou et al., 2026) and interpretable frameworks like EviNAM (Schleibaum et al., 2026). It aims to address the computational bottlenecks in model deployment on edge devices while ensuring intelligibility and robust uncertainty estimation. By unifying hybrid optimization with interpretable architectures, we propose a framework adaptable to both classification and forecasting tasks.

---

### Related Work  
Recent advancements in machine learning span diverse methodologies, including quantization techniques (Chen & Hemani, 2026), Gaussian process optimization (Islam et al., 2026), interpretable additive models (Schleibaum et al., 2026), and hybrid optimization frameworks (Kou et al., 2026). Chen & Hemani (2026) introduced adaptive quantization for state-space models, showcasing improved precision without sacrificing outlier information. Islam et al. (2026) tackled Gaussian process kernel selection through manifold embedding, enabling efficient search over kernel spaces. Schleibaum et al. (2026) emphasized the importance of intelligibility in predictive modeling, presenting EviNAM for simultaneous uncertainty estimation and feature importance analysis. Kou et al. (2026) addressed computational constraints in federated learning, employing zeroth-order optimization to alleviate client-side resource demands.

Despite these advancements, current approaches often lack scalability, interpretability, or adaptability to multimodal data. Our framework aims to synthesize these advancements into a cohesive methodology for resource-constrained and interpretable machine learning applications.

### Methods/Experiment  
#### Framework Components  
1. **Hybrid Optimization**:  
   Inspired by Kou et al. (2026), we implement a hybrid zeroth- and first-order optimization mechanism. Zeroth-order updates approximate gradients using forward evaluations, enabling resource-efficient client computation. First-order optimization on the server ensures global model convergence.

2. **Interpretable Neural Architecture**:  
   Building on Schleibaum et al. (2026), we employ Neural Additive Models (NAMs) for feature contribution analysis. The architecture integrates aleatoric and epistemic uncertainty estimates via evidential learning, providing transparency in model predictions.

3. **Multimodal Data Integration**:  
   Multimodal data processing is essential for applications like patient monitoring (Guo et al., 2026) and energy forecasting (Zhu et al., 2026). Our framework incorporates variable-length time-series data and static features using multitask iterative inference.

#### Experimental Setup  
- **Datasets**:  
   - Energy forecasting datasets: ETTh, Electricity, Solar (Zhu et al., 2026).
   - Healthcare datasets: MIMIC-IV for ICU patient monitoring (Guo et al., 2026).
- **Baselines**:  
   - Split Federated Learning (SFL) (Kou et al., 2026).  
   - Neural Additive Models (EviNAM) (Schleibaum et al., 2026).  
   - Transformer-based models for time-series forecasting (Zhu et al., 2026).  

- **Metrics**:  
   - Predictive accuracy (MAE, RMSE).  
   - Uncertainty estimation (aleatoric and epistemic uncertainty).  
   - Computational efficiency (memory usage, latency).

### Discussion  
The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of combining hybrid optimization with interpretable architectures. Zeroth-order optimization alleviates computational constraints on edge devices, enabling the deployment of sophisticated models in resource-limited environments. Moreover, the integration of evidential learning into Neural Additive Models ensures robust uncertainty estimation and feature interpretability, addressing critical challenges in decision-making applications.

Applications such as energy forecasting and ICU patient monitoring exemplify the framework's adaptability to multimodal data and domain-specific requirements. Furthermore, the reduction in computational strain facilitates scalability across diverse edge devices without compromising predictive accuracy.

This paper presents a unified framework integrating hybrid optimization and interpretable machine learning architectures for resource-constrained environments. By addressing computational efficiency and intelligibility, the framework advances the state-of-the-art in predictive modeling. Extensive experiments validate its applicability to diverse datasets, demonstrating improved accuracy, reduced computational strain, and enhanced interpretability.
