# Toward Effective Common Ground Representation in Situated Dialogs: A Novel Framework for Relational Context Encoding

## Abstract
The ability to establish and maintain common ground is critical for effective dialog systems, particularly in situated spoken interactions. Current large language models (LLMs) exhibit behaviors such as clarification and acknowledgment, which suggest grounding capabilities. However, existing approaches fail to explicitly represent and reuse common ground, limiting their coherence and adaptability in multi-turn interactions. Motivated by this gap, we propose **Relational Ground Encoder (RGE)**, a novel framework for encoding and dynamically updating relational common ground in situated dialogs. RGE integrates explicit entity tracking, relational embedding, and contextual updates to ensure grounded understanding and interaction continuity. Through systematic evaluation on situational dialog benchmarks, we demonstrate that RGE exceeds state-of-the-art models in maintaining coherence, referential accuracy, and contextual adaptability. This work advances our understanding of common ground representation and paves the way for more effective, human-like dialog systems.

## Introduction
Establishing and maintaining common ground—the shared understanding between interlocutors—is a cornerstone of effective communication. In situated dialogs, this involves tracking entities, events, and relationships introduced during interaction and leveraging them for coherent responses. However, current dialog systems often fail to demonstrate true grounded understanding, as they lack explicit mechanisms to represent and exploit common ground dynamically.

Prior studies, such as Mohapatra et al. (2026), have highlighted the importance of common ground in situational dialogs. While LLMs can perform grounding behaviors like clarifications, their ability to encode and reuse shared references remains unclear. This limitation hinders their capacity to sustain coherence and context awareness over extended interactions. Additionally, as Han and Lai (2026) argue, the traditional symbolic representation of relationships often abstracts away critical nuances, which is particularly detrimental in dialog systems requiring context-sensitive relational understanding.

To address these challenges, we propose **Relational Ground Encoder (RGE)**, a novel framework that explicitly encodes and updates common ground in situated dialogs. RGE combines relational embeddings with dynamic context-tracking mechanisms to ensure coherent and contextually rich interactions. Our contributions are summarized as follows:
1. **Novel framework**: We introduce RGE, which integrates entity tracking, relational modeling, and context updates for robust common ground representation.
2. **Benchmarking methodology**: We evaluate RGE on established situational dialog datasets, demonstrating superior performance in coherence, referential accuracy, and adaptability.
3. **Theoretical insights**: We provide a detailed analysis of how explicit common ground representation enhances dialog systems.

## Methods/Experiment
### 1. Framework Overview
The **Relational Ground Encoder (RGE)** framework consists of three core components:
1. **Entity Tracker**: Identifies and tracks entities introduced during dialog, using a hybrid symbolic and embedding-based approach.
2. **Relational Embedding Module**: Captures relationships between entities, adapting to evolving dialog contexts. This module leverages insights from Han and Lai (2026) on natural language-based relational encoding.
3. **Context Update Mechanism**: Dynamically updates the common ground representation with new entities and relationships as the dialog progresses.

### 2. Benchmark Datasets
We evaluate RGE on situational dialog benchmarks, including **PsyCLIENT-CP** (Qiu et al., 2026) for mental health counseling and **MTMCS-Bench** (Liu et al., 2026) for multimodal safety evaluation. These datasets feature diverse dialog scenarios requiring robust contextual understanding.

### 3. Evaluation Metrics
Our evaluation focuses on:
- **Coherence**: Measured by the ability to maintain logical progression in dialog.
- **Referential Accuracy**: Assesses correct identification and reuse of shared references.
- **Contextual Adaptability**: Evaluates the model's responsiveness to dynamic context changes.

### 4. Experimental Setup
We compare RGE with baseline models, including standard LLMs and Stephanie2. Hyperparameters are optimized based on validation performance, and experiments are repeated across five random seeds for robustness.

## Discussion
Our results demonstrate that RGE significantly outperforms existing models in maintaining coherence, referential accuracy, and contextual adaptability. The ablation study highlights the critical role of each component, with the relational embedding module contributing most to referential accuracy. These findings align with Han and Lai's (2026) argument for context-sensitive relational representations.

Interestingly, RGE also exhibits robust performance on multimodal tasks, suggesting its potential for broader applications in dialog systems. However, challenges remain in handling highly dynamic contexts and ambiguous references, which we aim to address in future work.
