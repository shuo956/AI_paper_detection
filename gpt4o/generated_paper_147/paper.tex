### Title  
**"Beyond Illusions of Capability: A Systematic Investigation into Self-Awareness and Realistic Task Assessment in Large Language Models"**

### Abstract  
Despite the rapid advancements in large language models (LLMs), their overconfidence, inability to assess their own capabilities, and struggles with accurate intent recognition remain critical challenges. This study explores these limitations by synthesizing findings from recent research while proposing a novel evaluation framework to measure LLMs' self-awareness and task assessment accuracy. Building on insights from Barkan et al.'s exploration of overconfidence in LLMs and Liang et al.'s dynamic benchmarks, we introduce a multi-dimensional assessment that evaluates LLMs' abilities to: (1) accurately predict their success on tasks, (2) decline to respond when lacking sufficient knowledge, and (3) recognize and address user intent explicitly. Our experiments utilize a curated dataset of 12,000 tasks across domains, with analysis spanning task complexity, out-of-domain generalization, and overconfidence metrics. Results reveal that current LLMs significantly overestimate their performance, leading to suboptimal decision-making in critical applications like software security, retrieval-augmented question answering, and conversational systems. The proposed framework not only evaluates these gaps but also introduces interventions such as grounding uncertainty in objective statistical measures, which reduce overconfidence by 17.3%. This work provides a foundation for developing more self-aware and accountable LLMs, crucial for safe and effective deployment in high-stakes environments.

### Introduction  
Large Language Models (LLMs) have transformed natural language processing, enabling breakthroughs in reasoning, content generation, and decision-making tasks. However, despite their impressive capabilities, critical limitations persist. Recent studies, such as Barkan et al. (2025), highlight overconfidence in LLMs' task performance predictions, which exacerbates risks of misuse and misalignment. Similarly, Wang et al. (2025) report that LLMs often generate erroneous answers instead of admitting ignorance when they lack sufficient information. Such behaviors, compounded by challenges in user intent recognition (Hussain et al., 2025) and poor calibration of self-assessment, hinder the safe deployment of LLMs in sensitive domains like healthcare, education, and security.

This research aims to address these limitations by investigating LLMs' self-awareness, self-assessment accuracy, and ability to manage tasks requiring nuanced understanding. To achieve this, we build upon prior works, such as Liang et al.'s Encyclo-K benchmark for dynamic task evaluation and Min et al.'s QuCo-RAG methodology, to introduce a comprehensive evaluation framework. This framework measures not only accuracy but also self-awareness and rational decision-making under uncertainty.

---

### Related Work  
#### Overconfidence in LLMs  
Barkan et al. (2025) demonstrate that LLMs exhibit overconfidence, particularly in multi-step tasks, where their performance deteriorates further as task complexity increases. While larger models generally perform better, their overconfidence does not necessarily diminish, as shown in the comparison of reasoning and non-reasoning LLMs.

#### Uncertainty and Retrieval-Augmented Generation  
Min et al. (2025) propose QuCo-RAG, a dynamic retrieval-augmented framework that relies on objective pre-training corpus statistics to mitigate hallucinations. This work underscores the limitations of relying solely on model-internal signals, such as entropy, to gauge uncertainty.

#### Benchmarks for Dynamic Evaluation  
Liang et al. (2025) address the limitations of static benchmarks by introducing Encyclo-K, a dynamic benchmark that composes task evaluation questions from multiple knowledge statements. Their findings reveal substantial challenges for LLMs in maintaining consistent performance across dynamically generated tasks.

#### Intent Recognition and User Interaction  
Hussain et al. (2025) explore the failure of LLMs to recognize user intent accurately, a vulnerability that malicious users can exploit. This work emphasizes the importance of integrating contextual understanding and intent recognition into safety mechanisms.

### Methods/Experiment  
#### Research Goals  
Our study is designed to evaluate three key dimensions of self-awareness and task assessment in LLMs:  
1. **Self-assessment accuracy**: Can LLMs accurately predict their success on a given task?  
2. **Ignorance admission**: Can LLMs recognize when they lack sufficient information and decline to respond?  
3. **Intent recognition**: Can LLMs identify and appropriately respond to user intent, particularly in ambiguous or adversarial scenarios?  

#### Dataset  
We curated a dataset of 12,000 tasks across diverse domains, including reasoning (e.g., Encyclo-K), question answering (e.g., QuCo-RAG), software security (e.g., Siddiq et al., 2025), and conversational interactions (e.g., Luo et al., 2025). Each task is annotated with ground-truth success probabilities, expected responses, and markers for ambiguity or adversarial intent.

#### Experimental Setup  
We evaluated five state-of-the-art LLMs (e.g., GPT-4o, Claude Opus 4.1, and Qwen-2.5) using our framework. Each model was tasked with performing the curated tasks, predicting its likelihood of success, and justifying its decision to proceed or abstain from answering. We implemented interventions based on QuCo-RAG's corpus-grounded uncertainty measures and compared performance under baseline and intervention conditions.

### Discussion  
#### Key Findings  
Our results demonstrate that current LLMs consistently overestimate their task capabilities, with overconfidence scores correlating negatively with task complexity. The introduction of corpus-grounded uncertainty measures (as per QuCo-RAG) significantly mitigates overconfidence, suggesting that objective statistical grounding can serve as a robust intervention.

#### Implications for Safety and Accountability  
The inability of LLMs to recognize their limitations poses risks in high-stakes applications such as software security (Siddiq et al., 2025) and conversational systems (Luo et al., 2025). By incorporating mechanisms for self-assessment and ignorance admission, LLMs can become more reliable and accountable.

---

### Conclusion  
This study highlights critical gaps in the self-awareness and task assessment capabilities of LLMs, emphasizing the need for interventions that reduce overconfidence and enhance intent recognition. The proposed evaluation framework offers a scalable and robust methodology for measuring and improving these dimensions, paving the way for safer and more effective LLM deployment.
