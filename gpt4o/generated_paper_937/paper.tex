### Title:  
Unifying Multi-Domain Optimization and Learning: A Data-Driven Framework for Generalization Across Diverse Computational Problems  

### Abstract:  
The existing literature in computational optimization and learning spans diverse domains, from healthcare to fluid-structure interaction, yet a unifying framework that bridges disparate methodologies is missing. This paper proposes a comprehensive data-driven approach that integrates multi-preconditioned optimization, domain-adaptive machine learning, and interpretable models to solve multi-domain problems. By leveraging techniques such as compositional neural operators, differential privacy in federated settings, and hierarchical adaptive mechanisms, our framework enables robust generalization across domains while reducing computational complexity. We validate our approach on applications ranging from medical image segmentation to portfolio optimization, achieving state-of-the-art performance with improved scalability. Our results highlight the transformative potential of cross-domain learning in advancing computational science and engineering.

---

### Introduction:  
The rapid growth in computational models has catalyzed advancements across diverse domains, including physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) for differential equations, federated learning for healthcare, and clustering algorithms for high-dimensional data. Despite these developments, current methodologies often operate in isolation, limiting their capacity to generalize across tasks or domains. This fragmentation results in inefficiencies and missed opportunities for knowledge transfer.

The motivation for this paper arises from the need to unify these disparate approaches under a single, scalable framework. Inspired by the recent contributions in multi-preconditioned optimization (Salvadó-Benasco et al., 2026), adaptive few-shot learning for evolving environments (Jia et al., 2026), and interpretable clustering for heterogeneous data (Güttel & Roy, 2026), we propose a data-driven framework that leverages state-of-the-art techniques to address key challenges in multi-domain optimization and learning.

Our contributions include:  
1. Developing a unified framework that integrates domain-specific methodologies into a cohesive system.  
2. Addressing issues of scalability, interpretability, and domain adaptation through advanced algorithms.  
3. Validating the framework across diverse applications, demonstrating its versatility and efficacy.

### Related Work:  
The landscape of computational optimization and machine learning is rich and varied. Salvadó-Benasco et al. (2026) introduced the Multi-Preconditioned LBFGS algorithm, which enhances the training of finite-basis PINNs through domain-decomposition-inspired architectures. Similarly, Jia et al. (2026) tackled the challenge of adaptive few-shot learning in dynamic manufacturing environments, demonstrating robust generalization with minimal data. In the realm of healthcare, Sinhal et al. (2026) proposed a federated learning approach that integrates differential privacy with elastic prototype consolidation to enable privacy-preserving medical image classification.  

Meanwhile, clustering methods have evolved to address the complexities of high-dimensional data. Güttel & Roy (2026) extended the CLASSIX algorithm to incorporate diverse distance metrics, achieving faster and more accurate clustering. In the context of medical imaging, Xu et al. (2026) leveraged self-supervised features for robust few-shot segmentation, overcoming domain-specific challenges in medical datasets.  

Despite these advancements, a gap persists in the integration of these techniques for solving multi-domain problems, a challenge this paper aims to address.

### Methods/Experiment:  

#### Framework Overview:  
Our framework integrates three core components:  
1. **Multi-Preconditioned Optimization**: Extending the work of Salvadó-Benasco et al. (2026), we employ MP-LBFGS for efficient optimization across subdomains.  
2. **Adaptive Learning Mechanisms**: Inspired by Jia et al. (2026), we incorporate hypothesis testing for novelty detection and adaptive learning for evolving environments.  
3. **Interpretable Machine Learning Models**: Building on Güttel & Roy (2026), we utilize explainable clustering techniques to enhance model transparency.

#### Experimental Setup:  
We validate our framework across three case studies:  
1. **Medical Image Segmentation**: Utilizing the DINO-AugSeg framework (Xu et al., 2026), we test our model on MRI and CT datasets.  
2. **Portfolio Optimization**: We apply deep learning techniques for multi-asset strategies, following the methodology of Luo & Skufca (2026).  
3. **Fluid-Structure Interaction**: The HGATSolver (Zhang et al., 2026) serves as the basis for testing our approach on multi-physics systems.

#### Metrics:  
Performance is evaluated using:  
- Accuracy and Area Under the Curve (AUC) for classification tasks.  
- Relative L2 error for regression.  
- Computational efficiency in terms of run time and memory usage.
