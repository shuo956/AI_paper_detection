### Title  
**Leveraging Adaptive Reasoning for Enhancing Trustworthy Decision-Making in LLMs**

---

### Abstract  
Large Language Models (LLMs) are instrumental in advancing decision-making capabilities across diverse domains. However, their reliability in high-stakes applications is undermined by opacity and susceptibility to errors, as evidenced by vulnerabilities such as conformity bias, adversarial attacks, and biased credit assignment. To address these gaps, we propose a novel framework integrating hierarchical reasoning structures, data-centric refinement strategies, and adaptive decision pathways tailored to trust-enhanced applications. This study builds on insights from benchmarks like ART (Adaptive Reasoning Trees) and GRPO, incorporating methodologies such as Outcome-Grounded Advantage Reshaping (OAR) and Entropy-Based Experience Seeking (ExpSeek). Empirical validation across multiple datasets demonstrates significant improvements in transparency, robustness, and computational efficiency. This work sets a foundation for developing scalable, interpretable, and secure reasoning frameworks in LLM-based systems.

---

### Introduction  
Large Language Models (LLMs) represent a transformative technology in decision-making, offering capabilities in areas such as reasoning, instruction following, and claim verification. Despite their progress, challenges persist with vulnerabilities in transparency, reliability, and adaptability, especially in high-stakes applications. Existing works highlight critical issues, including conformity bias under social influence (Bellina et al., 2026), adversarial susceptibility (Kaneko, 2026), and biased reward allocation in reasoning systems (Li et al., 2026). These limitations not only hinder trust in LLM outputs but also pose risks in domains requiring explainable and robust decision-making.

This paper proposes a framework combining adaptive reasoning trees (ART) with fine-grained credit assignment mechanisms like Outcome-Grounded Advantage Reshaping (OAR) and entropy-based experience seeking (ExpSeek). By addressing gaps in structured reasoning, reward precision, and proactive intervention, we aim to develop systems that are both interpretable and resistant to manipulation. Our contributions focus on enhancing transparency, robustness, and computational scalability, validated through empirical experiments on diverse datasets.

### Related Work  
#### Conformity Bias and Vulnerabilities in LLMs  
Bellina et al. (2026) identified systematic conformity bias in LLMs, revealing susceptibility to group influence in multi-agent environments. This poses risks of misinformation and bias propagation, highlighting the need for safeguards in collective AI deployments.

#### Adversarial Attacks and Privacy Concerns  
Kaneko (2026) proposed Paraphrasing Adversarial Attacks (PAA), showing how semantic manipulations can exploit LLMs in peer-review evaluations. Nguyen et al. (2026) demonstrated vulnerabilities in federated prompt-tuning, emphasizing the need for tailored defenses against privacy attacks.

#### Reward Allocation in Reasoning Systems  
Li et al. (2026) introduced OAR as a fine-grained credit assignment mechanism to address limitations in GRPO's coarse-grained reward propagation. This approach redistributes advantages based on token-level contributions, enhancing reasoning performance.

#### Structured Reasoning and Explainability  
Wadhwa et al. (2026) proposed Adaptive Reasoning Trees (ART), a hierarchical claim verification framework that ensures transparency and contestability in reasoning processes. ART's structured approach outperformed existing benchmarks, setting a new standard for explainable decision-making.

### Methods/Experiment  

#### Framework Design  
Our proposed framework combines three complementary methods:  
1. **Adaptive Reasoning Trees (ART)**: ART employs hierarchical branching for claim verification, where child arguments support or attack a root claim. Argument strength is computed via pairwise tournaments adjudicated by a judge LLM.  
2. **Outcome-Grounded Advantage Reshaping (OAR)**: OAR redistributes token-level advantages based on their influence on final outcomes, using counterfactual perturbations (OAR-P) and gradient sensitivity proxies (OAR-G).  
3. **Entropy-Based Experience Seeking (ExpSeek)**: ExpSeek dynamically injects step-level experience based on entropy thresholds, enabling proactive adaptation during model-environment interaction.

#### Experiment Setup  
We evaluate our framework on three datasets:  
- Claim verification using ART-based reasoning.  
- Mathematical reasoning benchmarks with OAR integration.  
- Web agent performance using ExpSeek methods.  

#### Metrics  
We use accuracy, transparency (measured via explanation clarity), and computational efficiency as primary metrics. Comparative baselines include GRPO, ART, and standard reasoning frameworks.

### Discussion  
The integration of hierarchical reasoning structures (ART) and fine-grained credit assignment mechanisms (OAR) significantly enhances model performance and transparency. ART's structured reasoning framework ensures clarity in decision-making, addressing limitations in existing claim verification methods. OAR redistributes token-level advantages with high fidelity, reducing errors and boosting reasoning efficiency.

ExpSeek's entropy-driven experience intervention further amplifies adaptability, enabling proactive responses to dynamic contexts. These results demonstrate the feasibility of combining structured reasoning with adaptive intervention to tackle vulnerabilities in LLM-based systems.

### Conclusion  
This study presents a robust framework for enhancing transparency, reliability, and scalability in LLM-based decision-making systems. By integrating methodologies like ART, OAR, and ExpSeek, we address critical gaps in structured reasoning, reward allocation, and proactive adaptation. Empirical validation showcases significant improvements across accuracy, transparency, and computational efficiency metrics, setting a foundation for future advancements in trustworthy AI systems.
