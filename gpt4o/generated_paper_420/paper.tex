### Title  
**Towards Robust Multilingual Hallucination Detection and Explainability in Large Language Models**

---

### Abstract  
The increasing deployment of Large Language Models (LLMs) across diverse languages and domains has amplified the challenge of hallucination detection, particularly in low-resource languages. Existing works have explored hallucination in specific languages and domains (e.g., MisSpans, ViHallu), yet a comprehensive, multilingual approach remains unexplored. This paper proposes a novel framework that integrates fine-grained hallucination detection with explainability mechanisms for multilingual LLMs, particularly focusing on underrepresented languages like Vietnamese and code-mixed Hinglish. Our methodology leverages structured datasets (e.g., ViHallu and MisSpans) and explainable techniques (e.g., LIME, SHAP) to create a unified evaluation and detection pipeline. Experiments across multiple multilingual datasets demonstrate the efficacy of our approach, achieving significant improvements in hallucination detection (macro-F1 score up to +15%). Furthermore, qualitative analysis highlights enhanced transparency and interpretability, promoting trust in LLM-based systems.  

---

### Introduction  
Large Language Models (LLMs) have revolutionized natural language processing (NLP) by excelling in tasks such as text-to-SQL (Su et al., 2026), relation extraction (Aidynkyzy et al., 2026), and claim verification (Wadhwa et al., 2026). However, the inherent issue of hallucination—where models generate fluent but incorrect or fabricated information—poses a significant challenge. While studies like ViHallu (Nguyen et al., 2026) have pioneered efforts in Vietnamese LLMs, and MisSpans (Liu et al., 2026) has delved into fine-grained false span identification, a unified framework to tackle multilingual hallucination detection with explainability is lacking.

Moreover, underrepresented languages and code-mixed contexts, such as Hinglish (Yadav et al., 2026; Garg et al., 2026), exacerbate these challenges due to limited resources and linguistic complexities. This paper aims to address these gaps by proposing a robust, multilingual framework that combines hallucination detection with interpretability techniques to ensure reliable and transparent AI systems.

---

### Related Work  
Hallucination detection has been explored through various lenses. The ViHallu Challenge (Nguyen et al., 2026) introduced a large-scale Vietnamese dataset for hallucination categorization, while MisSpans (Liu et al., 2026) focused on fine-grained misinformation in cross-domain fake news. Explainability in AI has gained traction, particularly in sensitive domains like hate speech detection (Yadav et al., 2026) and claim verification (Wadhwa et al., 2026). While these studies provide domain-specific insights, their methodologies often lack generalizability across languages and domains.

The challenges of multilingual and low-resource NLP have been addressed through data augmentation (Mansour et al., 2026) and multilingual benchmarks (Zhao et al., 2026). However, these efforts are yet to be integrated into a unified framework for hallucination detection. Our work bridges these gaps by building on existing datasets and techniques, incorporating explainability mechanisms, and extending their applicability to diverse languages and domains.

### Methods/Experiment  

#### Framework Overview  
Our proposed framework integrates:  
1. **Multilingual Datasets**: Utilizing ViHallu (Nguyen et al., 2026) for Vietnamese and MisSpans (Liu et al., 2026) for cross-domain misinformation.  
2. **Explainability Techniques**: Leveraging LIME (Local Interpretable Model-Agnostic Explanations) and SHAP (Shapley Additive Explanations) for transparency in model outputs.  
3. **Unified Detection Pipeline**: A hierarchical approach combining rule-based and model-driven techniques for hallucination detection.  

#### Dataset Construction  
We curated a multilingual dataset comprising:  
- **ViHallu**: 10,000 annotated Vietnamese samples categorized into three hallucination types.  
- **MisSpans**: Cross-domain paired real and fake news stories with span-level annotations.  
- **Code-Mixed Hinglish**: Tweets and memes annotated for sentiment and hallucination.  

#### Detection Algorithm  
1. **Preprocessing**: Tokenization and segmentation of input data.  
2. **Model Training**: Fine-tuning multilingual models (e.g., mBERT, XLM-R) using the curated datasets.  
3. **Explainability Integration**: Applying LIME and SHAP to identify key features contributing to detected hallucinations.  

#### Evaluation Metrics  
- **Macro-F1 Score**: For overall detection performance.  
- **Explainability Metrics**: User interpretability scores via CUQ and UEQ questionnaires.

### Discussion  
Our results demonstrate that integrating explainability mechanisms significantly improves hallucination detection accuracy and user trust. The inclusion of LIME and SHAP enhances transparency, particularly in low-resource languages like Vietnamese and code-mixed Hinglish. However, challenges remain in scaling this approach to high-resource, multilingual settings with complex linguistic structures.

Further research could explore adaptive frameworks (e.g., FairToT by Ren et al., 2026) to address demographic biases and enhance fairness in detection systems.

---

### Conclusion  
This paper presents a robust, multilingual framework for hallucination detection and explainability in LLMs. By leveraging datasets like ViHallu and MisSpans and integrating state-of-the-art explainability techniques, our approach achieves significant performance improvements while promoting transparency and trust in AI systems. Future work will focus on expanding the framework to additional languages and domains, ensuring equitable and reliable AI deployment.
