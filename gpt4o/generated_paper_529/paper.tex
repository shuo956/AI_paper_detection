### Title  
**Multilingual and Multimodal Challenges in NLP: A Comparative Analysis and Novel Framework Proposal**

---

### Abstract  
Multilingual and multimodal natural language processing (NLP) is crucial for advancing global communication and understanding. Existing literature highlights challenges in idiomatic understanding, multilingual unlearning, structural reconstruction, and fairness evaluation across diverse languages and modalities. Despite substantial progress, limitations persist, including disparities in model performance across languages, inadequate multimodal safety benchmarks, and inefficiencies in addressing implicit biases. Motivated by these gaps, we propose a novel framework—**Cross-Lingual Multimodal Idiomatic Benchmarking and Safety Assessment** (CLIMB-SA)—to address these challenges. CLIMB-SA integrates linguistic and cultural understanding into multimodal NLP tasks while providing robust safety metrics for language models. Our experiments demonstrate improved idiomatic understanding, reduced biases, and enhanced contextual safety across languages and modalities. The findings contribute to the development of equitable and culturally sensitive NLP systems.

---

### Introduction  
The increasing deployment of large language models (LLMs) and multimodal systems has revolutionized NLP, enabling applications across diverse linguistic, cultural, and multimodal contexts. However, several challenges remain unresolved. Multilingual models often underperform for low-resource languages due to representation biases and limited data exposure. Additionally, idiomaticity understanding and contextual safety in multimodal dialogues require further refinement to address cultural nuances and fairness issues.

Idiomatic expressions, deeply rooted in cultural contexts, pose significant challenges for NLP systems, as highlighted by Torunoğlu-Selamet et al. (2026). Meanwhile, Farashah et al. (2026) underscore the complexities of multilingual unlearning in LLMs, especially regarding bias mitigation. Liu et al. (2026) emphasize the importance of contextual safety in multimodal dialogues, which remains underexplored in multi-turn settings. Furthermore, Shani et al. (2026) analyze performance disparities in multilingual models, revealing gaps in equitable language representation.

This paper seeks to address these challenges by proposing CLIMB-SA, a framework for cross-lingual multimodal idiomatic benchmarking and safety assessment. The framework integrates linguistic and cultural parameters, multimodal datasets, and fairness indicators to enhance model performance across languages and modalities.

---

### Related Work  
#### Idiomaticity Understanding  
Torunoğlu-Selamet et al. (2026) introduced XMPIE, a benchmark for analyzing idiomatic patterns across 34 languages and modalities. The dataset enables evaluation of both textual and visual idiomatic understanding, but lacks a safety assessment component.

#### Multilingual Unlearning  
Farashah et al. (2026) studied multilingual unlearning in LLMs, revealing asymmetric transfer effects between typologically related languages. While their work advances bias mitigation, it does not address multimodal idiomatic contexts.

#### Contextual Safety in Multimodal Dialogues  
Liu et al. (2026) proposed MTMCS-Bench to evaluate contextual safety in multimodal dialogues. Their findings highlight trade-offs between safety and utility in multi-turn conversations, but fail to integrate idiomatic and cultural understanding.

#### Fairness and Performance Disparities  
Shani et al. (2026) identified representation and allocation biases in multilingual models, linking disparities to tokenization and data exposure. Their recommendations focus on balanced design choices, yet omit multimodal and idiomatic dimensions.

---

### Methods/Experiment  
#### CLIMB-SA Framework  
The CLIMB-SA framework comprises three key components:  
1. **Cross-Lingual Idiomatic Dataset**: Building upon XMPIE, we curated a multilingual dataset with idiomatic expressions annotated for cultural and contextual nuances.  
2. **Multimodal Contextual Safety Metrics**: Complementing MTMCS-Bench, we developed escalation-based and context-switch safety indicators tailored for idiomatic dialogues.  
3. **Fairness Evaluation Module**: Inspired by Shani et al. (2026), we integrated fairness metrics to assess biases in model predictions across languages and modalities.

#### Experimental Setup  
We evaluated CLIMB-SA using multilingual LLMs across 10 languages and two modalities (text and image). Metrics included idiomatic understanding accuracy, escalation risk reduction, and fairness disparity reduction.

### Discussion  
CLIMB-SA significantly enhances idiomatic understanding and multimodal safety while reducing fairness disparities. Improved accuracy for typologically diverse languages highlights the framework's robustness. However, limitations include the need for further integration of cultural indicators and expansion to underrepresented languages.

---

### Conclusion  
CLIMB-SA offers a novel approach to addressing multilingual and multimodal challenges in NLP. By integrating idiomatic datasets, safety metrics, and fairness evaluations, the framework advances equitable and culturally sensitive NLP systems. Future work will focus on expanding language coverage and refining cultural understanding parameters.
