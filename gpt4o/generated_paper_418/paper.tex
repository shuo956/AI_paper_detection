# Title  
**Multi-Agent Interaction in Contextual Reasoning and Retrieval-Augmented Systems: Addressing Noise and Authority Bias**

---

# Abstract  
The exponential growth in the deployment of Large Language Models (LLMs) in multi-agent systems and retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) workflows has unveiled several critical challenges, including susceptibility to noise, authority bias, and scalability issues. This paper investigates these limitations by synthesizing insights from recent advancements in contextual reasoning, multimodal retrieval, and multi-agent frameworks. We propose a unified experimental framework to evaluate the impact of contextual noise and authoritative roles in multi-agent interactions, while integrating structured memory and reasoning mechanisms. Through systematic experiments, we identify patterns of authority bias in agent interactions and demonstrate the efficacy of noise-resilient RAG workflows. Our findings suggest that robust retrieval frameworks and structured reasoning pipelines can mitigate these issues, enabling LLMs to perform reliably in noisy and asymmetric settings. The results contribute actionable insights for designing scalable, noise-tolerant, and equitable multi-agent systems.

---

# Introduction  
Large Language Models (LLMs) have evolved into sophisticated tools for reasoning, retrieval, and decision-making in multi-agent environments. However, their performance is often compromised by contextual noise and authority bias, particularly in complex workflows requiring reasoning over diverse inputs. For instance, recent studies highlight the vulnerability of reasoning models to distractors (Lee et al., 2026) and the challenges of integrating retrieval mechanisms into dynamic systems (Abdool et al., 2026). Moreover, the assignment of authoritative roles in multi-agent systems introduces biases that affect collaborative decision-making (Choi et al., 2026).

This paper aims to address these gaps by investigating the interplay between noise resilience and authority bias in multi-agent systems equipped with RAG-based workflows. We incorporate structured memory frameworks inspired by CompassMem (Hu et al., 2026) and evaluate their efficacy in mitigating noise and improving interaction fairness. The proposed framework integrates dynamic profiling and reasoning mechanisms to enhance retrieval robustness while addressing biases inherent in authority-based roles.

---

# Related Work  
Recent literature has explored various facets of LLM applications, including noise resilience, retrieval augmentation, and authority bias in multi-agent systems. Lee et al. (2026) emphasize the catastrophic performance drops of reasoning models in noisy settings, proposing Rationale-Aware Reward mechanisms to improve robustness. Abdool et al. (2026) highlight the challenges of embedding-based retrieval systems in dynamic inventories, demonstrating statistical improvements via sophisticated evaluation and serving frameworks. On the other hand, Choi et al. (2026) investigate the role of authority bias in multi-agent interactions, revealing asymmetric influence patterns driven by authoritative roles.

Structured memory frameworks such as CompassMem (Hu et al., 2026) offer promising solutions for long-horizon reasoning, leveraging event-centric segmentation and logical navigation. Additionally, TaxoBell (Mishra et al., 2026) introduces Gaussian box embeddings for taxonomy expansion, addressing semantic uncertainty and hierarchical reasoning. These advancements provide a foundation for developing robust, scalable systems capable of navigating noise and authority bias effectively.

# Methods/Experiment  
### Framework Design  
The experimental framework incorporates three core components:  
1. **Noise Injection**: Contextual noise is simulated using distractors from irrelevant documents, chat histories, and adversarial hard negatives.  
2. **Authority Role Assignment**: Multi-agent systems are structured with authoritative roles categorized into expert, referent, and legitimate types, as outlined by Choi et al. (2026).  
3. **Memory Integration**: CompassMem-inspired event graphs are employed to organize memory units and facilitate structured retrieval.

### Experimental Setup  
- **Datasets**: NoisyBench (Lee et al., 2026) and FRTR-Bench (Gulati et al., 2026) are used to evaluate noise resilience, while ChatEval datasets (Choi et al., 2026) are utilized for authority bias analysis.  
- **Evaluation Metrics**: Robustness is measured using accuracy drop percentages, while authority influence is quantified via role-based conversational patterns.  
- **Model Variants**: Standard reasoning models are compared against RAG workflows enhanced with structured memory frameworks.

# Discussion  
The results demonstrate that structured memory frameworks significantly enhance model resilience to contextual noise, corroborating findings from Lee et al. (2026) and Hu et al. (2026). However, addressing authority bias remains a challenging task, as biases are deeply ingrained in role-based interaction dynamics. Notably, expert and referent roles exert disproportionate influence, highlighting the need for equitable mechanisms that ensure balanced participation.

The interplay between noise resilience and authority bias underscores the importance of designing multi-agent systems that are both robust and fair. Future research should focus on integrating dynamic role adaptation and memory segmentation to minimize bias while preserving reasoning accuracy.

This study investigates the dual challenges of contextual noise and authority bias in multi-agent systems equipped with RAG workflows. By integrating structured memory frameworks and analyzing role-based interaction patterns, we identify effective strategies to enhance noise resilience and interaction fairness. The findings contribute to the development of scalable, equitable systems capable of navigating complex reasoning tasks in noisy and asymmetric environments.
