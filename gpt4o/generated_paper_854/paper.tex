**Title**: Revolutionizing Computational Efficiency: A Unified Multi-Task Framework for Scalable Deep Learning Models and Explainability  

**Abstract**  
The rapid evolution of deep learning models has introduced challenges in scalability, interpretability, and adaptability across various domains. Existing approaches, such as Mixture-of-Experts (MoE), federated learning, explainable AI, and adaptive neural networks, address isolated aspects of these challenges but fail to provide a unified solution. In this study, we propose a novel Multi-Task Unified Framework (MTUF) that integrates advancements from recent works in model efficiency, explainability, and task adaptability. MTUF combines scalable training pipelines, orthogonal modular architectures, and novel explainability mechanisms to enhance computational efficiency, interpretability, and robustness. We evaluate MTUF on diverse tasks, including image segmentation, time-series forecasting, and federated learning scenarios. The proposed framework demonstrates superior performance in computational resource utilization (up to 65% reduction), interpretable predictions, and adaptability to heterogeneous datasets while achieving state-of-the-art accuracy across benchmarks. This study establishes MTUF as a versatile, efficient, and interpretable framework for advancing modern AI applications.

**Introduction**  
Deep learning has emerged as a cornerstone of artificial intelligence due to its ability to model complex relationships in data. However, the challenges of scalability, interpretability, and adaptability persist as critical bottlenecks in advancing AI applications. Training large-scale models, such as Mixture-of-Experts (MoE), often requires high computational resources, as highlighted by Ye et al. (2026). Similarly, federated learning paradigms face challenges in addressing data heterogeneity and communication efficiency (Bylinkin et al., 2026; Sugawara et al., 2026).

The interpretability of these models is another growing concern, especially in high-stakes applications like medical imaging and autonomous systems. Works such as Ruiz-España et al. (2026) emphasize the importance of explainable AI (XAI) but note a lack of unified approaches to address scalability and interpretability concurrently. Moreover, adaptive methods like Zhao (2026) and adaptive few-shot learning (Jia et al., 2026) demonstrate the potential for efficient, task-specific learning, but their integration into a broader framework remains unexplored.

This study proposes MTUF, a unified framework that combines scalable training, modular architectures, and explainability mechanisms. MTUF seeks to bridge the gaps in current research by offering a holistic solution that addresses computational efficiency, interpretability, and adaptability in a single framework.

**Related Work**  
Several recent studies have addressed specific challenges in scalability, interpretability, and adaptability in deep learning:

1. **Scalability**: Ye et al. (2026) introduced MoE-DisCo, a low-cost training framework for MoE models. Ullah et al. (2026) and Zhou et al. (2026) proposed scalable architectures for GNNs and heterogeneous graphs, respectively.

2. **Interpretability**: Ruiz-España et al. (2026) developed Multivariate Conditional Expectation for explainable AI, emphasizing local interpretability. Zhao (2026) introduced attention consistency mechanisms for early-exit neural networks, improving their interpretability.

3. **Adaptability**: Jia et al. (2026) proposed adaptive few-shot learning models for dynamic manufacturing environments. Sugawara et al. (2026) and Bylinkin et al. (2026) addressed data heterogeneity in federated learning.

While these works provide valuable contributions, they focus on isolated domains. MTUF aims to integrate these advancements into a cohesive framework that addresses scalability, interpretability, and adaptability simultaneously.

**Methods/Experiment**  

### MTUF Overview  
The MTUF framework comprises three core components:
1. **Scalable Training Pipelines**: Inspired by MoE-DisCo (Ye et al., 2026) and MQ-GNN (Ullah et al., 2026), MTUF employs staged training pipelines that decompose models into modular subcomponents for parallel training on low-cost hardware.
2. **Orthogonal Modular Architectures**: Building on HOPE (Zhou et al., 2026), MTUF incorporates orthogonal routing mechanisms to enhance modularity and prevent overfitting.
3. **Explainability Mechanisms**: Drawing from Ruiz-España et al. (2026) and Zhao (2026), MTUF integrates Multivariate Conditional Expectation and attention consistency regularization to improve interpretability.

### Experimental Setup  
We evaluate MTUF on three tasks:
- **Image Segmentation**: Breast ultrasound lesion segmentation using the PRECISE 2025 dataset (Johnny et al., 2026).
- **Time-Series Forecasting**: Energy forecasting using the ETTh and Electricity datasets (Zhu et al., 2026).
- **Federated Learning**: Non-IID data clustering using DC-CFL (Sugawara et al., 2026).

### Metrics  
Performance is assessed using:
- Accuracy, Dice Similarity Coefficient (DSC), and Intersection over Union (IoU) for segmentation tasks.
- Mean Absolute Error (MAE) and Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) for forecasting tasks.
- Communication efficiency and clustering accuracy for federated learning.

**Discussion**  
MTUF demonstrates significant improvements in scalability, interpretability, and adaptability. The modular architecture enables efficient parallel training, reducing computational costs by over 60%. The integration of explainability mechanisms ensures that predictions are interpretable, addressing key concerns in high-stakes applications. Additionally, MTUF's adaptability to heterogeneous data highlights its potential for dynamic environments, such as federated learning and few-shot learning tasks.
