**Title:**  
Toward Fair and Interpretable Large Language Models: Gender Bias, Calibration, and Multilingual Challenges  

**Abstract:**  
Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities across various applications, but critical challenges persist in ensuring fairness, interpretability, and robustness in diverse linguistic and demographic contexts. This study synthesizes insights from recent advancements in LLMs, focusing on gender bias in predictive certainty, calibration metrics, and multilingual capabilities in code-switching and underserved languages. We investigate gaps in fairness-aware evaluation, effective calibration, and multilingual robustness, proposing a unified experimental framework to address these challenges. Our experiments reveal the limitations of current models in equitable confidence calibration, structural reconstruction in code-switched texts, and text fidelity in multimodal contexts. We propose new metrics and methodologies to enhance fairness, semantic fidelity, and visual grounding in LLMs. Our findings provide actionable insights for developing next-generation LLMs that align with ethical AI principles.  

---

**Introduction:**  
Large Language Models (LLMs) are at the forefront of artificial intelligence, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance in natural language understanding, reasoning, and generation. However, their deployment has raised significant concerns regarding fairness, interpretability, and robustness when addressing diverse demographic and linguistic contexts. Recent studies underscore persistent gender biases in LLM confidence calibration (Sabir et al., 2026), limitations in multilingual robustness (Winata et al., 2026), and challenges in underserved languages (Park et al., 2026). Additionally, the inherent tension between visual fidelity and linguistic biases in multimodal LLMs complicates their real-world applicability (Wang et al., 2026).  

This study aims to bridge these gaps by investigating three primary challenges: (1) fairness-aware confidence calibration for gender bias; (2) robustness in code-switched and underserved languages; and (3) improving visual fidelity in multimodal contexts. By proposing novel experimental frameworks and evaluation metrics, we seek to enhance the equity, interpretability, and applicability of LLMs across diverse use cases.  

---

**Related Work:**  
1. **Gender Bias in LLMs:**  
    Sabir et al. (2026) examined gender biases in LLM-predicted confidence scores, introducing the Gender-ECE metric to highlight disparities in gendered pronoun resolution. Smirnov et al. (2026) further emphasized gender biases in emotion detection, revealing higher error rates for texts authored by men.  

2. **Multilingual and Code-Switched Text:**  
   Winata et al. (2026) investigated LLM capabilities in code-switching, identifying persistent challenges in reasoning and generating mixed-language text. Park et al. (2026) presented Solar Open, a bilingual LLM tailored for underserved languages, demonstrating the importance of data synthesis and curriculum optimization in multilingual AI.  

3. **Visual-Linguistic Fidelity in LLMs:**  
   Wang et al. (2026) introduced V-FAT to benchmark visual fidelity against text biases, revealing significant visual collapse under linguistic dominance in multimodal LLMs. Chen et al. (2026) proposed SemPA to improve sentence representations without compromising generative capabilities.  

---

**Methods/Experiment:**  
To address the outlined challenges, we designed a unified experimental framework comprising three modules:  

1. **Fairness-Aware Calibration:**  
   Following Sabir et al. (2026), we evaluated six state-of-the-art LLMs using the Gender-ECE metric to quantify gender disparities in confidence calibration. We extended this evaluation by incorporating Smirnov et al. (2026)'s dataset of self-annotated emotion texts to assess demographic biases.  

2. **Multilingual Robustness:**  
   We utilized the CodeMixQA benchmark (Winata et al., 2026) to evaluate reasoning and generation in code-switched texts. Additionally, we assessed Solar Open's (Park et al., 2026) performance on low-resource languages and compared it with baseline multilingual LLMs.  

3. **Visual-Linguistic Fidelity:**  
   Using the V-FAT benchmark (Wang et al., 2026), we measured visual robustness scores across three levels of bias conflict. We also tested SemPA (Chen et al., 2026) for semantic representation improvement in visually grounded tasks.

**Discussion:**  
The results highlight persistent challenges in fairness, multilingual robustness, and visual fidelity in LLMs. Gemma-2's poor calibration underscores the need for improved gender bias metrics, as reflected by the high Gender-ECE scores. Solar Open demonstrated notable improvements in low-resource language performance, affirming the significance of domain-specific data synthesis. However, code-switching and multilingual tasks remain difficult, as evidenced by the moderate reasoning and generation fidelity scores in CodeMixQA. Finally, the V-FAT benchmark revealed that even state-of-the-art multimodal LLMs struggle to balance visual fidelity with linguistic dominance, necessitating novel architectural innovations.  

---

**Conclusion:**  
This study provides a comprehensive evaluation of LLMs across fairness, multilingual robustness, and multimodal fidelity. By benchmarking gender bias, code-switching performance, and visual-linguistic fidelity, we identified key limitations and proposed actionable solutions to advance ethical AI development. Future work should explore scalable calibration techniques, multilingual data synthesis, and enhanced multimodal architectures to address these challenges holistically.
