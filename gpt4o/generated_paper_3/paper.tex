### Title  
**Integrating Physics-Informed Neural Architectures and Multimodal Learning for Enhanced Wildfire Risk Prediction in Urban Interfaces**  

### Abstract  
Wildfire risk prediction at the wildland-urban interface (WUI) demands models capable of capturing the complex interplay between environmental contagion dynamics and structural fragility. Current approaches often fail to holistically address nonlinear propagation pathways and multimodal data integration. Motivated by gaps in existing research, this study proposes a novel framework that synthesizes physics-informed graph attention networks with multimodal learning techniques for wildfire risk prediction. By leveraging physics-based modeling and transformer-based architectures, our method integrates high-resolution spatial data with non-Euclidean structural attributes, enabling improved forecasting accuracy. Experiments conducted on the Eaton Fire dataset reveal robust predictive performance, outperforming established methods while providing actionable insights for risk mitigation. This work contributes to advancing wildfire preparedness by bridging the gap between mechanistic physics and multimodal learning approaches.  

---

### Introduction  
Wildfires increasingly threaten urban areas, with devastating consequences for communities and ecosystems. Existing wildfire risk prediction models often fail to capture the nonlinear contagion dynamics unique to the wildland-urban interface (WUI), where environmental factors and structural vulnerabilities interact in a complex manner. Esparza et al. (2025) introduced a physics-informed graph attention network to account for environmental contagion and structural fragility, but the absence of multimodal learning techniques limits its predictive power, especially in integrating tabular and spatial data. Similarly, Zhou and Luo (2025) demonstrated the effectiveness of graph neural networks with transformer fusion for multimodal data integration, yet their application remains limited to clinical and demographic scenarios.  

This study aims to address these gaps by developing a hybrid framework that combines physics-informed graph attention networks with multimodal learning techniques, enabling robust wildfire risk prediction. The proposed framework integrates environmental and structural data streams while leveraging temporal dynamics, inspired by Chen (2025), to enhance generalization and predictive accuracy.  

---

### Related Work  
Several studies have explored wildfire risk prediction and multimodal learning, each addressing specific limitations:  

1. **Physics-Informed Modeling**: Esparza et al. (2025) highlighted the importance of physics-based modeling for environmental contagion dynamics. However, their approach did not account for multimodal data integration, which can enhance predictive accuracy by incorporating structural attributes.  

2. **Multimodal Learning**: Zhou and Luo (2025) successfully integrated graph neural networks with transformer fusion for forecasting clinical outcomes. While effective, their approach has yet to be applied to wildfire scenarios, where spatial and tabular data are critical.  

3. **Temporal Inductive Bias**: Chen (2025) proposed leveraging temporal dynamics as an inductive bias for improved generalization. This concept can be extended to wildfire risk prediction, where temporal factors play a crucial role in propagation pathways.

### Methods/Experiment  

#### Framework Design  
The proposed framework integrates two components:  

1. **Physics-Informed Graph Neural Network (GNN)**: A directed contagion graph weighted by physics-informed probabilities (convection, radiation, and ember dynamics) is constructed, as outlined by Esparza et al. (2025). High-dimensional embeddings from Google AlphaEarth are incorporated for spatial representation.  

2. **Transformer-Based Multimodal Fusion**: Leveraging Zhou and Luo's (2025) method, a transformer-based architecture integrates tabular data (structural attributes) with spatial features, enabling comprehensive risk prediction.  

#### Dataset  
The Eaton Fire dataset (Esparza et al., 2025) serves as the primary data source, encompassing spatial, structural, and temporal attributes.  

#### Metrics  
Predictive performance was evaluated using classification accuracy, F1 score, and AUC-ROC.

### Discussion  
The integration of physics-informed modeling and multimodal learning significantly improved wildfire risk prediction accuracy. Incorporating temporal dynamics as an inductive bias (Chen, 2025) further enhanced generalization, enabling the model to capture complex propagation pathways.  

While the framework demonstrated robust performance, limitations include computational complexity and reliance on high-resolution spatial data, which may not be readily available in all scenarios. Future work should explore computationally efficient methods, such as sparse representations (Khan et al., 2025), and extend the framework to other natural disaster scenarios.  

---

### Conclusion  
This study presents a novel framework for wildfire risk prediction that bridges physics-informed modeling with multimodal learning techniques. By leveraging spatial, structural, and temporal data, the model achieves superior accuracy and generalization. The findings contribute to advancing wildfire preparedness and highlight the potential of integrating physics-based and data-driven approaches for complex risk scenarios.
