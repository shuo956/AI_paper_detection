### Abstract
This study introduces a unified framework that integrates kernel-based learning methodologies with sparse representation techniques to address challenges across diverse domains, including computational pathology, molecular modeling, and energy forecasting. The framework leverages Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS) and sparse vector quantization to enhance interpretability, robustness, and efficiency in machine learning models. The proposed approach is evaluated using numerical experiments across datasets representing real-world phenomena, including histopathological image analysis, photovoltaic power predictions, and IoT-based anomaly detection. Results demonstrate superior performance in predictive accuracy and computational efficiency when compared to traditional baselines. This work contributes to the field by bridging theoretical insights with practical applications, advancing the state-of-the-art in data-driven methodologies.

### Introduction
Machine learning has revolutionized diverse fields such as computational pathology, energy systems, and molecular modeling. However, challenges remain in achieving robust, interpretable, and efficient models that can generalize across heterogeneous datasets. Recent advances, including kernel-based learning in Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS) (Feito-Casares et al., 2026) and sparse quantization techniques for large-scale models (Khasia, 2026), suggest potential pathways to address these limitations. This study unifies these methodologies into a cohesive framework, exploring their applications in AI and computational science.

We aim to answer the following research questions:
1. How can kernel-based learning and sparse representations be effectively integrated for diverse applications?
2. What are the benefits of this integration in terms of predictive accuracy, computational efficiency, and robustness?

### Related Work
#### Kernel-Based Learning
Kernel methods, particularly in RKHS, have shown promise in manifold learning and reconstruction tasks (Feito-Casares et al., 2026). These methods leverage the representer theorem to optimize reconstruction in high-dimensional spaces, extending their applicability to both scalar and vector-valued data.

#### Sparse Representations
Sparse quantization techniques, such as Hessian-Adaptive Sparse Vector Quantization (HAS-VQ) (Khasia, 2026), have been pivotal in compressing large-scale models without significant performance degradation. These methods decouple high-sensitivity parameters from the bulk weight distribution, enabling efficient deployment.

#### Applications in Computational Science
Applications of these methods span various domains. For instance, Lanzilao and Meyer (2026) demonstrated the utility of satellite-based models for photovoltaic power forecasting, while Alber et al. (2026) introduced foundation models for clinical pathology with improved robustness and efficiency.

**Metrics:**
- Predictive accuracy (mean squared error for regression tasks, classification accuracy for categorical tasks).
- Computational efficiency (runtime, memory usage).
- Robustness (performance on noisy or incomplete data).

**Implementation Details:**
- Kernel alignment was performed using autorepresentation geometry (Feito-Casares et al., 2026).
- Sparse quantization employed Hessian-based decoupling (Khasia, 2026).
- All models were implemented in Python, leveraging TensorFlow and PyTorch.
