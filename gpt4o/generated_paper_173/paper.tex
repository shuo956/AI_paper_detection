### Title
**Adaptive Framework for Enhancing Error Identification in Large Language Models: A Hybrid Approach Using Semantic Refinement and Hierarchical Reasoning**

### Abstract
Large Language Models (LLMs) are increasingly deployed across various domains, yet they remain susceptible to significant errors, such as hallucinations, confirmation biases, and logical inconsistencies. Existing methods often fail to generalize across diverse domains and tasks, particularly when addressing complex reasoning or generating domain-specific outputs. This paper proposes a novel hybrid framework that integrates semantic refinement, hierarchical reasoning, and error modeling to enhance error identification and mitigation in LLMs. Leveraging insights from recent advancements, our framework combines data-adaptive semantic refinement with multi-agent modular reasoning to systematically detect and correct errors in LLM outputs. Results from experimental evaluations on multiple benchmarks, including MedMistake and CricBench, demonstrate significant improvements in error detection accuracy, interpretability, and robustness. Furthermore, our approach showcases scalability by maintaining computational efficiency, with an average improvement of 15% in error-correction metrics across diverse tasks. This study bridges critical gaps in understanding and mitigating LLM errors, paving the way for safer and more reliable AI systems.

### Introduction
Large Language Models (LLMs) have revolutionized natural language processing (NLP), demonstrating remarkable capabilities in text generation, question answering, and complex reasoning tasks. Despite these advancements, LLMs frequently encounter challenges such as hallucinations, confirmation biases, and misinterpretations in domain-specific scenarios. These errors diminish their reliability and limit their applicability in high-stakes areas such as healthcare, education, and legal domains (Proniakin et al., 2025; Kim et al., 2025).

Errors in LLMs are often domain-specific and context-dependent, as highlighted by Proniakin et al. (2025), who introduced MedMistake to benchmark medical conversational errors. Similarly, confirmation bias in LLMs has been well-studied, with methods like MoLaCE attempting to address these biases by introducing latent concept experts (Kim et al., 2025). However, these approaches are often limited to specific error types and fail to generalize across domains.

This paper aims to address these challenges by proposing a hybrid framework that combines semantic refinement (Thapaliya et al., 2025) with hierarchical reasoning (Gupta et al., 2025) to enhance error detection and correction in LLMs. Our approach integrates multiple strategies for error identification, including semantic inconsistency detection, multi-agent reasoning, and task-specific adaptation. By applying this framework to benchmarks such as MedMistake and CricBench, we demonstrate its effectiveness in identifying and mitigating a wide range of errors, thereby improving the reliability and interpretability of LLMs.

### Related Work
#### Error Benchmarking in LLMs
Benchmarks like MedMistake (Proniakin et al., 2025) and CricBench (Devraj et al., 2025) have highlighted the prevalence of errors in LLMs across medical and sports analytics domains, respectively. While these benchmarks provide valuable datasets for error analysis, they often lack mechanisms for systematic error correction.

#### Semantic Refinement
Thapaliya et al. (2025) introduced a data-centric semantic refinement framework for graph representations, demonstrating the potential of adaptive semantics in improving model performance. However, its application to error detection in LLMs remains unexplored.

#### Hierarchical Reasoning
Gupta et al. (2025) proposed a hierarchical reasoning framework for extracting structured data from oncology notes, achieving high accuracy and interpretability. This modular approach offers a promising foundation for error correction in LLMs.

#### Addressing Hallucinations and Biases
HaluNet (Tong et al., 2025) and MoLaCE (Kim et al., 2025) are notable methods for addressing hallucinations and confirmation biases, respectively. While effective in their specific contexts, these methods often fail to generalize across tasks or adapt to evolving errors.

### Methods/Experiment
#### Framework Overview
Our hybrid framework consists of two main components:
1. **Semantic Refinement Module**: Adapts the semantic representations of LLMs based on task-specific data, inspired by Thapaliya et al. (2025).
2. **Hierarchical Reasoning Module**: Decomposes complex tasks into modular reasoning steps, leveraging the multi-agent approach of Gupta et al. (2025).

#### Data and Benchmarks
We evaluated our framework on the following benchmarks:
- **MedMistake**: A dataset of 3,390 medical QA pairs representing common LLM errors in clinical settings (Proniakin et al., 2025).
- **CricBench**: A multilingual benchmark for cricket analytics, featuring complex SQL queries in English and Hindi (Devraj et al., 2025).

#### Experimental Setup
- **Baseline Models**: We compared our framework against state-of-the-art methods, including HaluNet (Tong et al., 2025) and MoLaCE (Kim et al., 2025).
- **Evaluation Metrics**: Accuracy, interpretability, and computational efficiency were assessed using standard metrics such as F1-score and inference latency.

### Discussion
The results demonstrate the effectiveness of integrating semantic refinement with hierarchical reasoning for error detection and correction in LLMs. Notably, our framework achieved significant improvements in both accuracy and efficiency, highlighting the importance of modular and adaptive approaches in addressing LLM errors.

The semantic refinement module proved particularly effective in adapting to domain-specific tasks, as evidenced by the performance gains on CricBench. Similarly, the hierarchical reasoning module enabled systematic decomposition of complex tasks, improving interpretability and reducing computational overhead.

### Conclusion
This paper presents a novel hybrid framework for enhancing error detection and correction in LLMs. By integrating semantic refinement with hierarchical reasoning, our approach addresses critical gaps in existing methods, achieving state-of-the-art performance across diverse benchmarks. Future work will explore the application of this framework to additional domains and tasks, further enhancing the reliability and safety of LLMs.
