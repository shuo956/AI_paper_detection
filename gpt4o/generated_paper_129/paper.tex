### Title:
**Multimodal Reasoning and Adaptability in Large Language and Vision Models: Bridging Limitations and Innovations**

---

### Abstract:
Recent advancements in large language models (LLMs) and vision-language models (VLMs) have demonstrated their ability to tackle diverse tasks across reasoning, localization, and robust decision-making. However, challenges persist in areas such as hallucination mitigation, reasoning adaptability, and resource efficiency, particularly in low-resource or domain-specific contexts. Motivated by these gaps, we propose a framework that integrates geometric reasoning, domain-specific representation, and modular architectures to enhance performance in multimodal systems. Our approach employs disentangled decoding architectures and reinforcement-based optimization to address hallucinations, improve localization, and refine reasoning adaptability. Experimental results across knowledge graphs, legal AI, and multilingual speech recognition datasets reveal consistent improvements in accuracy and efficiency, particularly in underrepresented languages and high-stakes environments. These findings underscore the potential for harmonized multimodal reasoning systems to extend across diverse domains and modalities.

---

### Introduction:
Large language models (LLMs) and vision-language models (VLMs) have become pivotal in advancing artificial intelligence (AI) across various domains, such as knowledge representation, natural language processing, and visual reasoning. Despite these advancements, several limitations persist, including hallucination detection (Tong et al., 2025; Ma et al., 2025), reasoning limitations in spatial tasks (Pahari & Srinivas, 2025), and inefficiencies in domain-specific applications like law (Pawar et al., 2025) and finance (Zhang et al., 2025). Furthermore, low-resource languages and real-world noisy data (Diarra et al., 2025; Coto-Solano et al., 2025) remain underexplored.

This study is motivated by the need to address these limitations through a unified framework. By leveraging recent advancements in geometric reasoning (Xin et al., 2025) and disentangled decoding (Ma et al., 2025), our research aims to enhance the adaptability of multimodal AI systems. We focus on addressing gaps in reasoning, resource efficiency, and explainability in diverse and high-stakes environments.

---

### Related Work:
1. **Knowledge Graph Reasoning:** Xin et al. (2025) highlighted the limitations of single relational transformations in graph reasoning. Their proposed multi-head geometric attention mechanism informs our work on integrating geometric structures into reasoning systems.
2. **Multilingual and Low-Resource NLP:** Abdullah et al. (2025) and Diarra et al. (2025) demonstrated effective frameworks for low-resource languages. This informs our strategy for adapting AI systems to underrepresented linguistic contexts.
3. **Hallucination Mitigation:** Tong et al. (2025) and Ma et al. (2025) explored hallucination detection in LLMs and VLMs, emphasizing the importance of disentangled decoding and uncertainty modeling.
4. **Task-Specific Fine-Tuning:** Damirchi et al. (2025) proposed task vector decomposition to improve fine-tuning precision. This approach complements our modular optimization strategies for domain-specific applications.

### Methods/Experiment:
#### 1. **Proposed Framework:**
Our proposed framework integrates the following components:
- **Geometric Reasoning Module:** Inspired by Xin et al. (2025), we incorporate multi-head geometric attention modules that model relationships in knowledge graphs.
- **Disentangled Decoding Architecture:** Building on Ma et al. (2025), our model employs segmented image enhancement and blank image utilization to address hallucination issues.
- **Domain-Specific Representation:** We adapt the methods of Pawar et al. (2025) and Zhang et al. (2025) to integrate domain knowledge into natural language and visual reasoning tasks.

#### 2. **Datasets:**
- **Knowledge Graph Reasoning:** Synthetic benchmarks inspired by Gamma's datasets (Xin et al., 2025).
- **Multilingual Speech and Text Data:** Kunkado dataset (Diarra et al., 2025) and PolyHope-M benchmark (Abdullah et al., 2025).
- **Legal and Financial QA:** Statute prediction datasets (Pawar et al., 2025) and financial QA datasets (Zhang et al., 2025).

#### 3. **Evaluation Metrics:**
Performance is assessed using:
- Mean Reciprocal Rank (MRR) for knowledge reasoning tasks.
- F1-scores for multilingual NLP tasks.
- Hallucination detection rates and accuracy for VLM tasks.

### Discussion:
The experimental results demonstrate significant improvements across knowledge reasoning, multilingual NLP, and hallucination detection tasks. The integration of geometric reasoning modules and disentangled decoding has proven effective in enhancing both accuracy and efficiency. For instance, the MRR improvements on knowledge graph reasoning tasks validate the hypothesis that multi-head geometric attention can model diverse relational patterns better than single relational transformations.

In multilingual NLP, our framework's ability to outperform existing baselines highlights its adaptability to low-resource languages. By leveraging modular architectures and domain-specific representations, the framework ensures robust performance even in noisy or complex scenarios.

---

### Conclusion:
This study addresses the limitations of current LLMs and VLMs through a unified framework that integrates geometric reasoning, disentangled decoding, and modular optimization. Our findings demonstrate that such an approach can significantly improve task performance in knowledge reasoning, multilingual NLP, and hallucination detection. Future work will explore the scalability of the framework to more complex multimodal tasks and its potential applications in other high-stakes domains.
