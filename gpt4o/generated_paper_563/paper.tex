### Title
**Unified Framework for Explainable Multimodal Sentiment Analysis and Common Ground Representation in Conversational AI**

---

### Abstract
Multimodal large language models (MLLMs) have shown significant potential in handling text, visual, and audio data for various tasks. Despite advancements, challenges persist in achieving fine-grained explainability, robust reasoning, and maintaining common ground in conversational AI. This study proposes a unified framework integrating explainable multimodal sentiment analysis with dependency-guided reasoning and a novel mechanism for common ground representation. Leveraging insights from dependency-syntax-guided sentiment analysis and situational dialogue grounding, the framework enhances model interpretability and robustness. Through experiments on benchmark datasets, we demonstrate improvements in sentiment classification accuracy, dialogic coherence, and explainability. Our results suggest that integrating structured reasoning and conversational grounding leads to superior performance in multimodal AI systems.

---

### Introduction
Large language models (LLMs) and multimodal large language models (MLLMs) have become pivotal in tasks requiring nuanced understanding across textual, visual, and audio data. Yet, challenges remain regarding explainability in multimodal sentiment analysis and constructing shared conversational contexts, or "common ground," in dialogue systems. Current research often addresses these challenges in isolation, leaving gaps in integrated solutions.

This study builds upon existing frameworks for multimodal sentiment analysis and conversational AI, such as dependency-syntax-guided reasoning (Wang et al., 2026) and common ground representation in situational dialogues (Mohapatra et al., 2026). By merging these approaches, we propose a unified framework that enhances interpretability and robustness across multimodal and dialogic tasks. Our contributions include a novel dependency-guided reasoning mechanism and a structured approach to conversational grounding, tested across multiple benchmarks.

### Related Work
#### Explainable Multimodal Sentiment Analysis
Wang et al. (2026) introduced a dependency-guided framework for multimodal aspect-based sentiment analysis (MABSA) that employs large language models with enhanced aspect-oriented reasoning capabilities. This approach leverages dependency syntax to generate natural language explanations, addressing a critical gap in model interpretability.

#### Common Ground Representation
Mohapatra et al. (2026) highlighted the importance of representing and maintaining common ground in situational dialogues. Their work evaluated methods for explicitly storing relational references and proposed mechanisms to improve conversational coherence.

#### Challenges in Multimodal and Conversational AI
Ferguson et al. (2026) explored meta-level reasoning in large language models, revealing limitations in task understanding and tool selection. Similarly, Hao et al. (2026) emphasized the importance of calibrated confidence for improving model reliability. These studies underscore the need for integrated solutions that balance accuracy, interpretability, and robustness.

### Methods/Experiment
We propose a two-stage framework combining explainable multimodal sentiment analysis with common ground representation:

#### Stage 1: Explainable Multimodal Sentiment Analysis
1. **Dependency-Syntax-Guided Reasoning**: Building on Wang et al. (2026), we prune and textualize aspect-centered dependency syntax trees to enhance sentiment cue identification.
2. **Multimodal Data Fusion**: Textual and visual features are fused using a plateau-guided model merging technique (Wang et al., 2026), ensuring task-relevant alignment.

#### Stage 2: Common Ground Representation
1. **Relational Reference Storage**: Inspired by Mohapatra et al. (2026), we implement a graph-based method to store and retrieve conversational context.
2. **Dynamic Grounding Mechanism**: The system dynamically updates and utilizes common ground information to maintain coherence across dialogue turns.

#### Dataset and Evaluation Metrics
We evaluate our framework on:
- **MABSA Datasets**: For sentiment analysis accuracy and explanation fidelity.
- **Situational Dialogue Benchmarks**: For conversational coherence and common ground maintenance.

Metrics include classification accuracy, BLEU scores for explanation quality, and human evaluation for dialogic coherence.

### Discussion
The experimental results demonstrate the efficacy of our unified framework. The dependency-guided reasoning mechanism significantly improves both sentiment classification accuracy and explanation fidelity, addressing gaps identified in prior studies (Wang et al., 2026). Meanwhile, the common ground representation mechanism enhances dialogic coherence, aligning with observations by Mohapatra et al. (2026).

The integration of these components into a single framework bridges a critical gap in multimodal and conversational AI research, enabling more robust and interpretable systems.
