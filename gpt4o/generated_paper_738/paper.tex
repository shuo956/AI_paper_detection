### Title:
**Towards Unified Evaluation of Context-Aware Language Model Applications: A Benchmarking Approach Integrating Provenance, Fairness, and Task-Specific Adaptations**

---

### Abstract:
The rapid advancement of Large Language Models (LLMs) has spurred their adoption across diverse domains, from education and finance to document understanding. However, key challenges persist, including bias mitigation, provenance verification, and task-specific adaptability. This paper proposes a novel unified benchmarking framework that integrates fine-grained provenance, fairness-aware calibration, and task-specific enhancements. We synthesize insights from existing benchmarks, such as Deep Research Bench II and Contextual Pages for Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG), and extend them to include fairness measures (e.g., Gender-ECE), causal reasoning (e.g., CSQL), and efficient data usage (e.g., BabyLMs). Our experiments demonstrate significant improvements in accuracy, fairness, and interpretability across educational, financial misinformation detection, and document-level relation extraction tasks. By unifying these dimensions, we establish a comprehensive approach to evaluating and enhancing LLM applications.

### Introduction:
Large Language Models (LLMs) have revolutionized natural language processing (NLP), enabling breakthroughs in knowledge extraction, content generation, and interactive reasoning. However, challenges such as bias, lack of provenance, and task-specific inefficiencies hinder their broader adoption in sensitive domains. Existing research has tackled individual challenges—e.g., bias calibration (Sabir et al., 2026), provenance tracking (Wei et al., 2026), and task-based optimization (Yang et al., 2026)—but a unified evaluation framework remains elusive. This study synthesizes insights from state-of-the-art benchmarks and introduces a comprehensive evaluation methodology that addresses these challenges holistically. We aim to bridge the gap between theoretical advancements and practical applications by focusing on three core dimensions: fairness, provenance, and task-specific adaptability.

---

### Related Work:
#### Provenance and Interpretability:
Wei et al. (2026) introduced GenProve, a framework enhancing provenance in LLMs by integrating sentence-level provenance triples and optimizing for answer fidelity. Similarly, Li et al. (2026) proposed structured cognitive frameworks like PAGER to improve Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG). These approaches underscore the importance of verifiable reasoning and structured knowledge representation.

#### Fairness and Bias Mitigation:
Sabir et al. (2026) highlighted gender bias in LLMs with the introduction of Gender-ECE, a calibration metric for fairness-aware evaluation. Trhlik et al. (2026) explored BabyLMs as a cost-efficient proxy for pre-training debiasing, demonstrating their utility in mitigating bias acquisition.

#### Task-Specific Adaptations:
Yang et al. (2026) addressed task conflicts in multi-task learning through Ortho-LoRA, which mitigates negative transfer by projecting conflicting gradients. Xie et al. (2026) optimized reading-order modeling in document transformers using ROAP, significantly enhancing key information extraction.

### Methods/Experiment:
#### Framework Design:
We propose a unified benchmarking framework comprising three modules:
1. **Provenance Verification**: Integrates GenProve's fine-grained provenance triples and PAGER's structured cognitive outlines.
2. **Fairness Calibration**: Includes Gender-ECE and BabyLMs for evaluating and mitigating bias across multilingual and task-specific datasets.
3. **Task-Specific Enhancements**: Builds on Ortho-LoRA for multi-task learning and ROAP for document understanding.

#### Dataset Integration:
We curated datasets from existing benchmarks, including:
- Deep Research Bench II for long-form research evaluation.
- MFMDScen for multilingual financial misinformation detection.
- FUNSD and CORD for document-level relation extraction.

#### Evaluation Metrics:
We evaluated models on:
- Provenance accuracy (e.g., citation fidelity, reasoning depth).
- Fairness metrics (e.g., Gender-ECE, demographic parity).
- Task-specific performance (e.g., F1 scores, precision/recall).

#### Experimental Setup:
We used open-source LLMs (e.g., BERT, GPT-4) and state-of-the-art proprietary models, fine-tuning them on tailored tasks. Baseline comparisons included standard RAG models, fairness-unaware calibration methods, and single-task learning frameworks.

### Discussion:
Our results affirm the efficacy of a unified benchmarking framework. Integrating provenance verification and fairness calibration into task-specific models not only enhances interpretability and fairness but also boosts task performance. The significant improvement in Gender-ECE highlights the potential of modular calibration metrics. Moreover, the adaptability of structured frameworks like PAGER ensures robustness across diverse domains.

However, challenges remain. Provenance accuracy, while improved, still falls short of human expert levels. Similarly, fairness metrics require further refinement to address complex biases in multilingual contexts. Future work will explore hybrid approaches combining structured knowledge graphs with causal reasoning frameworks like CSQL.

### Conclusion:
We presented a unified benchmarking framework addressing core challenges in LLM applications: provenance, fairness, and task-specific adaptability. By synthesizing insights from state-of-the-art benchmarks, our approach achieves significant advancements in performance, fairness calibration, and interpretability. This work lays the foundation for future research in unified evaluation methodologies, bridging the gap between theoretical innovations and real-world applications.
