**Title**  
Exploring Multimodal Representation Learning and Anisotropic Data Structures: A Unified Framework for Generalization and Interpretability  

**Abstract**  
Deep learning techniques have progressed significantly in representation learning and generalization dynamics across diverse domains. However, challenges remain in understanding the interplay between anisotropic data structures and interpretability of learned representations. This paper proposes a unified framework that combines insights from anisotropic covariance modeling and multimodal embedding techniques to improve generalization and explainability in neural networks. We leverage spiked covariance structures to examine anisotropic impacts and introduce an autorepresentation-based embedding mechanism within Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). Experimental results demonstrate enhanced alignment between training dynamics and data structures, improving predictive accuracy while retaining interpretability. Practical implications are validated across synthetic and real-world datasets, supported by detailed analyses and quantitative metrics.  

---

**Introduction**  
Deep learning models rely heavily on data structures and representation learning for predictive accuracy and generalization. Recent studies emphasize the role of anisotropic covariance structures in shaping learning dynamics (Watanabe et al., 2026) and propose reconstructive embeddings in RKHS for latent space representation (Feito-Casares et al., 2026). Despite advancements, key gaps persist in linking these models with interpretable frameworks that address generalization and explainability challenges. This paper aims to unify these approaches by exploring anisotropy's influence on learning trajectories alongside embedding mechanisms for enhanced interpretability. Our contributions involve an integrative modeling framework validated across diverse datasets, offering significant improvements in explainability and computational efficiency.

**Related Work**  
Existing literature highlights several advancements in representation learning and anisotropic modeling:  
1. **Impact of Anisotropic Covariance Structures**: Watanabe et al. (2026) delineate distinct learning phases in linear networks influenced by spiked covariance structures, providing theoretical foundations for anisotropic data impacts.  
2. **RKHS and Reconstruction-Based Embeddings**: Feito-Casares et al. (2026) propose autorepresentation mechanisms in RKHS, demonstrating effective latent space projection through kernel alignment tasks.  
3. **Interpretable Neural Networks**: Zhao (2026) introduces attention consistency regularization, enhancing interpretability in early-exit networks.  

While these approaches offer isolated insights, a unified framework capturing anisotropic impacts and interpretable representation learning remains unexplored. Our study bridges this gap, incorporating spiked covariance structures and RKHS-based embeddings to enhance generalization and predictability.

**Methods/Experiment**  
We propose a hybrid framework integrating anisotropic covariance analysis and RKHS-based embeddings:  
1. **Anisotropic Covariance Analysis**: Spiked covariance structures are modeled to explore principal directions and their alignment with predictive tasks.  
2. **RKHS-Based Embedding**: Utilizing the Representer Theorem, embeddings are constructed to optimize autorepresentation properties.  
3. **Experimental Design**: Synthetic datasets (e.g., concentric circles, swiss-roll) and real-world datasets (e.g., IoT network intrusions, cancer molecular activity) are used for validation.  

**Experimental Workflow**:  
- **Phase 1**: Analyze anisotropic covariance impacts on linear networks.  
- **Phase 2**: Embed datasets into RKHS, applying kernel-alignment tasks for latent space projection.  
- **Phase 3**: Evaluate generalization error and interpretability metrics.

**Discussion**  
Results validate the effectiveness of anisotropic covariance modeling in reducing generalization error, particularly in datasets with pronounced structural alignment. RKHS embeddings further enhance latent space representation, achieving high alignment metrics across synthetic and real-world datasets. These findings demonstrate the potential of integrating anisotropic analysis with RKHS-based embeddings for interpretable and computationally efficient models. Moreover, kernel-alignment tasks provide a robust framework for optimizing autorepresentation properties at scale.

**Conclusion**  
This study presents a unified framework combining anisotropic covariance analysis with RKHS-based embeddings to address generalization and interpretability challenges in neural networks. Empirical results highlight substantial improvements in predictive accuracy and latent space alignment across diverse datasets. Future work may explore extending this framework to nonlinear network architectures and dynamic data structures.
