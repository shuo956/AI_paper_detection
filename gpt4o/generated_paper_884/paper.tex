### Title  
Integrating Federated Causal Discovery with Heterogeneous Feature Learning: A Novel Framework for Robust, Scalable, and Explainable AI  

---

### Abstract  
Causal discovery in high-dimensional and heterogeneous data environments has been a major challenge in machine learning due to computational constraints, latent confounders, and diverse feature distributions. While existing methods tackle subproblems such as differentiable causal learning or missing feature imputation, an integrated framework addressing both causality and data heterogeneity remains unexplored. This paper introduces a novel Federated Heterogeneous Causal Discovery Framework (FHCD), leveraging super-structure learning for causal optimization and federated learning for multi-granularity data. FHCD incorporates sparse precision matrix decomposition and hierarchical imputation to address missing data while maintaining robustness to heterogeneous features across distributed nodes. Extensive experiments, conducted on synthetic, real-world healthcare datasets, and federated benchmarks, demonstrate state-of-the-art performance in causal accuracy, feature imputation, and scalability. FHCD bridges the gap between causal inference and federated learning, providing a reliable foundation for explainable AI solutions in distributed environments.

### Introduction  
Causal discovery and feature heterogeneity are central challenges in modern machine learning, especially in fields like healthcare and finance, where data are incomplete, distributed, and high-dimensional. Recent studies (e.g., Ma et al., 2026) have explored super-structure-guided causal discovery to improve efficiency, while others, such as Ferrini et al. (2026), address missing features in Graph Neural Networks (GNNs). However, the integration of these techniques in federated environments, where data cannot be centralized due to privacy constraints, remains unexplored.  

In federated systems, the diversity of feature distributions across nodes adds complexity to causal discovery. Standard methods, such as those proposed by Jiang et al. (2026) for layer-parallel training, fail to address node-specific heterogeneity. Similarly, imputation techniques like Luo et al. (2026) neglect causal relationships between features, risking biased inference.  

This paper proposes a Federated Heterogeneous Causal Discovery Framework (FHCD) that unifies causality, imputation, and federated learning. FHCD employs sparse precision matrix decomposition for causal structure learning and hierarchical imputation to handle heterogeneous features under federated constraints. Our contributions include:  

1. A causal discovery pipeline enhanced by federated super-structure learning.  
2. A hierarchical imputation mechanism for handling missing data across nodes.  
3. Comprehensive evaluation on synthetic and real-world federated datasets.

### Related Work  
#### Differentiable Causal Discovery  
Ma et al. (2026) propose ALVGL, highlighting the benefits of sparse and low-rank decomposition for causal discovery. However, their method is limited to centralized datasets, making it unsuitable for federated systems.  

#### Handling Missing Features  
Ferrini et al. (2026) introduce GNNmim for missing feature imputation in GNNs, emphasizing the importance of feature sparsity. Luo et al. (2026) extend imputation to heterogeneous feature types but lack causal integration.  

#### Federated Learning for Heterogeneous Data  
Zhou et al. (2026) present PiXTime, a federated model for time-series forecasting, addressing granularity differences across nodes. However, it does not consider causal relationships, limiting its interpretability.  

#### Bridging the Gap  
Our framework combines these advances, integrating the strengths of federated learning, causal discovery, and imputation into a cohesive system.

### Methods/Experiment  
#### Framework Overview  
FHCD consists of three core components:  
1. **Federated Super-Structure Learning**: Extends ALVGL by incorporating node-specific granularity through hierarchical aggregation.  
2. **Hierarchical Imputation**: Employs a two-stage imputation mechanism, inspired by Luo et al. (2026), to estimate missing data while preserving causal dependencies.  
3. **Causal Graph Initialization**: Initializes causal discovery with federated node-specific priors, aligning with Zhou et al. (2026).  

#### Experimental Setup  
We evaluated FHCD on three datasets:  
1. Synthetic data with known causal graphs.  
2. Real-world healthcare data from Ganjkhanloo et al. (2026).  
3. Federated time-series benchmarks from Zhou et al. (2026).  

Metrics included causal discovery accuracy (SHD, AUC), imputation performance (RMSE), and scalability (runtime).

### Discussion  
FHCD outperformed baselines in all metrics, particularly in causal discovery accuracy and scalability. The hierarchical imputation mechanism proved effective in handling heterogeneous features, while federated super-structure learning demonstrated robustness to node-specific granularity.  

However, FHCD's reliance on node-specific priors may limit its applicability to nodes with minimal data. Future extensions could explore adaptive prior initialization to mitigate this limitation.

### Conclusion  
This work presents FHCD, a novel framework integrating causal discovery and federated learning. By addressing feature heterogeneity and missingness, FHCD advances the state-of-the-art in scalable, explainable AI for distributed systems.
