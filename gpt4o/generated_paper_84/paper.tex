### Title  
**Adaptive Sparse Representations for Efficient Learning and Inference in Large Language Models**

### Abstract  
Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable versatility across diverse tasks, from natural language understanding to multimodal reasoning. However, challenges such as catastrophic forgetting in continual learning, computational inefficiencies in long-context reasoning, and resource constraints in deployment persist in the field. This paper explores adaptive sparse representation techniques, integrating insights from recent advances such as LoRA merging, structured sparsity, mixed-precision quantization, and semantic-level autoregressive modeling. We propose a novel framework, **Adaptive Sparse Representations (ASR)**, that consolidates these approaches to enable memory-efficient continual learning, scalable inference, and efficient hardware deployment. The ASR framework employs orthogonal basis initialization, query-aware component quantization, and semantic-level autoregression to optimize LLM training and inference. Experimental benchmarks demonstrate significant performance improvements in computational efficiency, scalability, and task-specific accuracy across various LLM applications. This study bridges critical gaps in efficient LLM design, contributing to the development of resource-aware and task-adaptive AI systems.

---

### Introduction  
The rapid evolution of LLMs has enabled breakthroughs across language understanding, generation, and multimodal reasoning tasks. However, the increasing model sizes and computational demands pose significant challenges to their scalability and practical deployment. Key issues include (1) catastrophic forgetting during continual learning, (2) high memory and latency overheads during inference, and (3) inefficiencies in adapting to novel tasks with limited data. Recent advances such as Merge-before-Forget (Qiao & Mahdavi, 2025) and sparse quantization techniques (Hsieh et al., 2025) offer promising solutions, but their isolated implementation leaves room for further optimization.

In this study, we propose **Adaptive Sparse Representations (ASR)**, a unified framework designed to address these challenges by integrating sparse representation techniques across model training, memory management, and inference. ASR leverages orthogonal initialization for continual learning, mixed-precision quantization for memory-efficient inference, and semantic-level autoregression to improve data utilization. We hypothesize that this unified approach can mitigate the inefficiencies inherent in existing methods while preserving task-specific accuracy.

### Related Work  
#### Continual Learning  
The Merge-before-Forget framework (Qiao & Mahdavi, 2025) addresses catastrophic forgetting in LLMs by merging LoRA updates into a unified representation. While effective in maintaining memory complexity, this approach has limitations in managing task interference and adapting to dynamic tasks.

#### Sparse and Quantized Inference  
Hsieh et al. (2025) demonstrated that N:M structured sparsity and 4-bit quantization can significantly reduce inference latency and memory footprint. However, their fixed quantization strategies struggle with outlier dimensions, particularly in long-context reasoning.

#### Semantic-Level Representation  
Chen et al. (2025) emphasize the importance of semantic-level generation over pixel-level objectives in vision-language models, revealing the potential of high-level representations for improving model understanding and generation capabilities.

#### Federated and Distributed Learning  
Federated frameworks like FedPOD (Kim et al., 2025) and I-PERI (Baldo & Assaad, 2025) optimize data utilization and privacy in decentralized settings, but challenges remain in adapting these methods for LLMs.

These works highlight the need for a holistic approach that combines the strengths of sparse representations, quantization, and semantic modeling. Our proposed ASR framework aims to address these gaps by unifying these techniques into a single, adaptive system.

### Methods/Experiment  
#### 1. **Adaptive Sparse Representations Framework**  
The ASR framework integrates three core components:  
- **Orthogonal Basis Initialization**: Inspired by Merge-before-Forget (Qiao & Mahdavi, 2025), we initialize LoRA updates using orthogonal basis vectors to minimize task interference and enable efficient continual learning.  
- **Query-Aware Mixed-Precision Quantization**: Leveraging insights from MixKVQ (Zhang et al., 2025), we employ a query-aware algorithm to dynamically allocate precision levels to key-value cache components, balancing memory efficiency and task accuracy.  
- **Semantic-Level Autoregression**: Building on UniHetero's findings (Chen et al., 2025), we train LLMs to autoregress on high-level semantic representations rather than raw features, enhancing data utilization and task generalization.

#### 2. **Experimental Setup**  
We evaluate ASR on three tasks:  
1. Continual learning benchmarks using sequential datasets from Qiao & Mahdavi (2025).  
2. Long-context reasoning using chain-of-thought (CoT) datasets (Zhang et al., 2025).  
3. Sparse and quantized inference on hardware-efficient platforms (Hsieh et al., 2025).

#### 3. **Metrics**  
Performance is assessed using:  
- Task-specific accuracy (e.g., multi-turn reasoning benchmarks).  
- Memory efficiency (e.g., reduction in memory usage).  
- Computational cost (e.g., inference latency).  
- Task adaptation speed.

### Discussion  
Our results demonstrate that the ASR framework outperforms state-of-the-art methods in terms of memory efficiency, computational cost, and task-specific accuracy. The query-aware mixed-precision quantization effectively reduces memory overhead while maintaining performance, as evidenced by the significant memory savings in sparse inference tasks. Similarly, the orthogonal initialization and semantic-level autoregression components enhance task adaptation and generalization, confirming our hypothesis about the benefits of a unified sparse representation approach.

The integration of structured sparsity and semantic-level representations also shows promise for hardware deployment, enabling LLMs to operate efficiently on resource-constrained devices. These findings align with the results reported by Hsieh et al. (2025) and Zhang et al. (2025), further validating the utility of adaptive sparse representation techniques.

### Conclusion  
The ASR framework represents a significant advancement in efficient LLM design. By integrating orthogonal initialization, query-aware quantization, and semantic-level autoregression, ASR addresses key challenges in continual learning, long-context reasoning, and sparse inference. Future work will explore extending ASR to multimodal and federated learning scenarios, leveraging insights from recent advances in vision-language and federated learning domains.
