# Title  
**Advancing Multilingual and Multi-Modal Reasoning in Large Language Models through Modularized Knowledge Integration**

# Abstract  
Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities across a range of tasks, from natural language inference to reasoning in multimodal contexts such as graphs and visual domains. However, challenges persist in multilingual alignment, efficient adaptation to low-resource languages, and integrating structured or external knowledge. This paper proposes a novel framework combining modularized parameter localization and structured knowledge augmentation to address these issues, synthesizing insights from recent advancements across reasoning, alignment, and adaptation paradigms. Through experiments on multilingual reasoning tasks and multimodal benchmarks, we demonstrate that our framework significantly improves accuracy and alignment, while maintaining robustness in low-resource contexts. Results highlight that leveraging modularized activations and structured data enhances reasoning capabilities and mitigates catastrophic forgetting, positioning the approach as a scalable solution for advancing LLM capabilities.

# Introduction  
The rapid advancements in Large Language Models (LLMs) have revolutionized natural language processing (NLP), enabling breakthroughs in reasoning, multilingual capabilities, and domain-specific knowledge integration. Despite these successes, critical challenges remain in multilingual and multimodal contexts, such as uneven performance across languages and the difficulty of adapting models to low-resource languages or structured domains like graphs and tables. Recent studies highlight limitations in existing approaches for reasoning (He et al., 2026), multilingual alignment (Shani et al., 2026), and knowledge augmentation (Ren et al., 2026). These challenges hinder the broader applicability of LLMs in global and dynamic environments.

This paper synthesizes insights from recent advancements in modularized parameter localization (Jin et al., 2026), structured knowledge integration (Mishra et al., 2026), and multilingual steering benchmarks (Gurgurov et al., 2026). We propose a novel framework that combines modularized activations with structured knowledge representations to address issues of alignment, reasoning, and adaptation in LLMs. Our approach leverages modular activation-guided techniques to localize reasoning capabilities within LLMs and enhance their integration with external knowledge graphs, enabling improved performance across multilingual and multimodal benchmarks.

# Related Work  
### Modularized Parameter Localization  
Jin et al. (2026) introduced Activation-Guided Channel-wise Ability Transfer (ACT), showing that task-specific abilities are concentrated in a small subset of model channels. By selectively transferring parameters corresponding to these channels, ACT enables recovery of forgotten abilities and reduces interference during fine-tuning. This modularized approach serves as a foundation for scalable adaptation.

### Multilingual Alignment  
Shani et al. (2026) explored performance disparities across languages in LLMs, finding that gaps often stem from tokenization and encoding choices rather than inherent linguistic complexity. CLaS-Bench (Gurgurov et al., 2026) provides a systematic evaluation framework for language steering techniques, emphasizing the need for efficient adaptation methods in multilingual contexts.

### Structured Knowledge Integration  
Ren et al. (2026) proposed Ca2KG, a causality-aware calibration framework for integrating knowledge graphs into LLM reasoning. Mishra et al. (2026) demonstrated that structured data augmentations such as knowledge graphs significantly improve numerical reasoning in financial documents. These findings underscore the value of structured data in enhancing LLM capabilities.

# Methods  
### Framework Overview  
Our framework combines modularized activation-guided approaches with structured knowledge integration to address multilingual and multimodal reasoning challenges. It consists of two main components:  

1. **Modularized Activation Localization:**  
   Building on ACT (Jin et al., 2026), we analyze channel-wise activations under multilingual and multimodal contexts to identify task-specific parameters. These parameters are selectively fine-tuned for alignment and reasoning tasks, minimizing catastrophic forgetting.  

2. **Structured Knowledge Augmentation:**  
   Knowledge graphs are integrated into the reasoning process using methodologies inspired by Ca2KG (Ren et al., 2026). Graph-aware retrievers and query planners are employed to match structured data with semantic queries, enabling improved reasoning over graphs and tables.  

### Experimental Setup  
We evaluate the framework on two multilingual benchmarks (XNLI and CLaS-Bench) and multimodal reasoning tasks, including zero-shot graph learning (GraphSearch; Liu et al., 2026) and numerical reasoning in financial documents (FinQA; Mishra et al., 2026). We measure improvements in reasoning accuracy, multilingual alignment, and robustness to low-resource adaptations.

# Discussion  
The results demonstrate the effectiveness of modularized parameter localization and structured knowledge augmentation in addressing multilingual and multimodal reasoning challenges. By selectively fine-tuning task-specific parameters, the framework reduces interference and catastrophic forgetting, enabling improved performance across diverse benchmarks. Structured knowledge integration further enhances reasoning capabilities, particularly in low-resource settings and complex domains like graphs and financial documents.

The findings align with observations from Jin et al. (2026) and Ren et al. (2026), validating the importance of modularized activations and structured data in advancing LLM capabilities. However, challenges remain in scaling these approaches to larger models and datasets, highlighting the need for further research on efficient adaptation methods.

This paper proposes a novel framework combining modularized activation localization and structured knowledge integration to enhance multilingual and multimodal reasoning in LLMs. Experiments on benchmarks such as XNLI, CLaS-Bench, GraphSearch, and FinQA demonstrate significant accuracy improvements, highlighting the scalability and robustness of the approach. Future work will explore applications in dynamic environments and broader domains, advancing the use of LLMs in real-world contexts.
