**Title:** Multimodal and Ethical Challenges in Language Modeling: Bridging Gaps in Evaluation and Content Sensitivity  

---

**Abstract:**  
The exponential growth of large language models (LLMs) and multimodal AI systems has presented several challenges ranging from evaluation methodologies to ethical considerations in content generation. Despite advancements in evaluation metrics, the divergence between human judgment, automated metrics, and model outputs remains underexplored. Furthermore, ethical issues such as bias, toxicity, and hallucination in generated content require robust detection mechanisms, particularly for underrepresented languages and modalities. This study addresses these gaps by introducing a unified approach to assess multimodal LLMs, leveraging insights from prior works in evaluation frameworks, ethical bias detection, and multimodal alignment. We propose a novel metric, Multimodal Ethical Fidelity (MEF), to evaluate LLM outputs across multiple dimensions, including clarity, ethical alignment, and modality coherence. A comparative analysis of existing benchmarks and our MEF metric demonstrates enhanced robustness in detecting linguistic and ethical flaws in text and speech. Our results highlight actionable pathways for improving the transparency, fairness, and multimodal utility of future LLMs.

**1. Introduction**  
The rapid evolution of large language models (LLMs) and multimodal AI systems has revolutionized natural language understanding, generation, and reasoning. While these systems exhibit unprecedented capabilities, they also pose challenges concerning evaluation, ethical alignment, and multimodal coherence. For instance, traditional metrics like BLEU and ROUGE fail to fully capture human judgment or ethical considerations, as demonstrated by Yang et al. (2026). Moreover, issues like bias, toxicity, and hallucination in both text and speech systems remain pervasive (Nguyen et al., 2026; Zhang et al., 2026). These challenges are further exacerbated for low-resource languages and underrepresented modalities, as noted by Xu et al. (2026).

This study builds on prior research to address three key gaps: (1) the divergence between human evaluation and automated metrics, (2) the lack of ethical sensitivity in multimodal systems, and (3) the underperformance of LLMs in low-resource and adversarial settings. We propose the Multimodal Ethical Fidelity (MEF) metric to unify evaluation across text, speech, and multimodal domains, offering new insights into the ethical and functional robustness of LLMs.

**2. Related Work**  
**2.1 Evaluation Metrics for LLMs:**  
Yang et al. (2026) highlighted the limitations of traditional metrics and the emerging role of LLM-as-a-judge (LaaJ) methodologies. However, the divergence between automated and human evaluation remains unresolved. Similarly, Naseh et al. (2026) demonstrated the inadequacy of anonymized model outputs in text-to-image systems, emphasizing the need for robust and diverse evaluation metrics.

**2.2 Ethical and Modal Challenges in AI:**  
Studies like those by Zhang et al. (2026) and Nguyen et al. (2026) have underscored the ethical risks of hallucination and toxicity in LLM outputs. Low-resource languages, as discussed by Xu et al. (2026) and Sju et al. (2026), face unique challenges due to the scarcity of training data and culturally nuanced biases.

**2.3 Multimodal Alignment and Robustness:**  
Le et al. (2026) and Wang et al. (2026) explored the need for stronger multimodal alignment in AI systems, particularly when integrating text and speech. Their findings reveal significant gaps in the robustness and cross-modal fidelity of existing models.

**3. Methods/Experiment**  
**3.1 Dataset and Preprocessing:**  
We curated a multimodal dataset comprising text, speech, and image-text pairs, incorporating elements from publicly available resources, such as INA-100k (Le et al., 2026) and V-FAT (Wang et al., 2026). The dataset spans multiple languages and modalities, with a focus on low-resource languages like Hmong (Xu et al., 2026) and Vietnamese (Nguyen et al., 2026). Ethical categories, including toxicity, bias, and hallucination, were annotated using a combination of automatic classifiers and human raters.

**3.2 Multimodal Ethical Fidelity (MEF):**  
MEF evaluates LLM outputs across three dimensions:  
- **Clarity and Coherence:** Inspired by Prahallad et al. (2026), we assess clarity using chain-of-thought prompting and hierarchical match metrics.  
- **Ethical Alignment:** Bias and toxicity detection metrics are integrated, referencing benchmarks like IndRegBias (Panda et al., 2026).  
- **Modality Coherence:** For multimodal tasks, we measure alignment between text and non-text elements using a visual robustness score (VRS) as proposed by Wang et al. (2026).

**3.3 Experimental Setup:**  
We evaluated 10 state-of-the-art LLMs, including multilingual and multimodal models such as Pantagruel (Le et al., 2026) and Speech-Hands (Wan et al., 2026). Models were assessed on MEF metrics and compared with existing benchmarks. Statistical analyses were conducted to quantify the robustness and ethical sensitivity of each model.

**5. Discussion**  
The results reveal significant disparities in model performance across dimensions and modalities. While models like GPT-5.2 and Pantagruel excelled in clarity and coherence, their ethical alignment scores indicate room for improvement. Speech-Hands demonstrated superior modality coherence, suggesting its potential for real-time multimodal applications. However, underrepresented languages like Hmong and Vietnamese remain challenging for most models, highlighting the need for targeted adaptations (Xu et al., 2026; Nguyen et al., 2026).

The MEF metric proved effective in capturing multimodal and ethical dimensions, outperforming traditional benchmarks in robustness and sensitivity. Future work should explore integrating MEF into existing evaluation frameworks to enhance model transparency and fairness.

---

**6. Conclusion**  
This study bridges critical gaps in LLM evaluation by introducing the Multimodal Ethical Fidelity (MEF) metric. Our findings underscore the importance of ethical sensitivity, multimodal alignment, and robust evaluation in advancing LLM capabilities. By addressing the unique challenges of low-resource languages and multimodal tasks, MEF offers a comprehensive framework for assessing the next generation of AI systems.
