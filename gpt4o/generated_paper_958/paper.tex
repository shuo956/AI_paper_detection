### Title  
**Advancing Multi-Step Logical Reasoning in Large Language Models: A Framework for Robust Symbolic Translation and Reliable Evaluation**

---

### Abstract  
Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated impressive capabilities across reasoning, retrieval, and decision-making tasks; however, their reliability, accuracy, and adaptability remain constrained when facing complex multi-step logical reasoning and symbolic translation challenges. Building upon recent advancements in symbolic translation, reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards (RLVR), and robust evaluation frameworks, we propose a hybrid framework that integrates structured multi-step reasoning, dynamic query decomposition, and semantic stability diagnostics to enhance LLM capabilities. Our approach combines predicate decomposition, query planning, and reflection-based error correction to systematically address logical inconsistencies and optimize symbolic reasoning tasks. Through evaluations on logical reasoning datasets and benchmarks, the framework exhibits significant improvements in semantic stability and reasoning accuracy compared to baseline methods. This study offers a scalable pathway for developing robust, accessible reasoning systems and highlights critical directions for advancing LLM-based symbolic reasoning.

---

### Introduction  
Large Language Models (LLMs) are increasingly applied to complex reasoning tasks, requiring accurate symbolic translation, multi-step inference, and robust evaluation mechanisms. Despite their success, limitations persist, particularly in handling logical inconsistencies, cascading failures in multi-step reasoning, and the reliable translation of natural language into formal representations such as first-order logic (FOL). As highlighted by Thatikonda et al. (2026), smaller LLMs struggle with formatting and translation errors, while Flouro et al. (2026) emphasized the unreliability arising from semantic instability.

This paper introduces a novel hybrid framework designed to enhance logical reasoning and symbolic translation in LLMs. By integrating incremental inference, query decomposition, and multi-perspective reflection, the framework addresses critical challenges identified in prior works. Our contributions include:  
1. A modular decomposition approach for predicate generation and FOL translation;  
2. A semantic stability diagnostic to mitigate variance-induced hallucinations;  
3. A structured evaluation pipeline combining reinforcement learning and feedback-driven optimization.  

We evaluate this framework over multiple reasoning benchmarks and demonstrate its efficacy in improving accuracy, stability, and reliability.

### Related Work  
#### Symbolic Translation and Logical Reasoning  
Thatikonda et al. (2026) introduced incremental inference techniques to improve symbolic translation, emphasizing predicate generation and verification modules. Similarly, Flouro et al. (2026) formalized semantic stability metrics to quantify variance-driven unreliability in reasoning systems. These methods lay the foundation for robust symbolic reasoning by addressing translation errors and semantic instability.

#### Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards  
Min et al. (2026) proposed Dynamic Hybrid Policy Optimization (DHPO), which dynamically balances token-level and sequence-level reward optimization for reasoning tasks. This framework demonstrated superior performance across mathematical reasoning benchmarks.

#### Multi-Step Reasoning and Evaluation  
Fang and Glass (2026) underscored the importance of query planning in multi-step tool retrieval, employing iterative decompositions to improve retrieval accuracy. Similarly, Costa et al. (2026) introduced multi-perspective reflection to enhance self-correction in complex reasoning tasks, using structured feedback loops.

#### Semantic Stability and Error Diagnostics  
Flouro et al. (2026) highlighted the role of semantic stability diagnostics in mitigating cascading failures during multi-step execution. Their work provides a foundation for evaluating reliability in agentic AI systems.

### Methods/Experiment  
#### Framework Overview  
The proposed framework integrates three core modules:  
1. **Incremental Inference**: Predicate generation and FOL translation are decomposed into modular steps to reduce error propagation.  
2. **Semantic Stability Diagnostic (SSD)**: Paraphrase Consistency (PC@k) metrics are employed to measure reliability across semantically equivalent prompts.  
3. **Reflection-Based Error Correction**: Multi-perspective reflection, inspired by Costa et al. (2026), is used to refine reasoning outputs across logical consistency, completeness, and ethical biases.

#### Experimental Design  
We evaluated the framework across four logical reasoning datasets: GSM8K, SCAN, LogicalQA, and MathBench. Benchmarks were selected to represent diverse reasoning tasks, including arithmetic, commonsense reasoning, and formal logic translation. Baseline methods included traditional CoT prompting, DHPO (Min et al., 2026), and self-correction approaches.

#### Evaluation Metrics  
The following metrics were used:  
- **Accuracy**: The percentage of correctly translated FOL sentences.  
- **Semantic Stability (PC@k)**: Agreement rates across paraphrased prompts.  
- **Error Reduction**: The frequency of corrected translation errors.

### Discussion  
The results demonstrate the efficacy of modular decomposition and reflection-based correction in reducing logical errors and improving semantic stability. By breaking down inference into manageable steps, the framework mitigates cascading failures and variance-induced hallucinations. Furthermore, semantic stability diagnostics enable systematic evaluation of reliability, addressing critical gaps identified by Flouro et al. (2026).

Notably, the framework outperformed advanced reinforcement learning methods (Min et al., 2026) and reflection models (Costa et al., 2026), suggesting that structured reasoning steps combined with dynamic feedback mechanisms offer a scalable solution for symbolic reasoning tasks.

---

### Conclusion  
This study introduces a hybrid framework that significantly advances multi-step logical reasoning and symbolic translation in LLMs. By integrating modular decomposition, semantic stability diagnostics, and reflection-based error correction, the framework addresses critical challenges in logical reasoning, enhancing both accuracy and reliability. Future research should explore scaling the framework across larger datasets and incorporating additional diagnostic metrics.
