# Title  
**Unified Framework for Evaluating Human and Machine Reasoning in Open-Ended Contextual Analysis**

# Abstract  
Recent advancements in the field of natural language processing (NLP) have highlighted the need to reconcile human reasoning with machine-based analysis for open-ended tasks. This paper proposes a unified framework to evaluate reasoning in diverse contexts, leveraging representation learning, multi-modal reasoning, and adaptive modeling techniques. Integrating insights from prior studies on disentangling judgment in educational analytics, depressive meme detection, and model adaptation, we address gaps in understanding how reasoning frameworks can balance interpretability, bias mitigation, and robust adaptation across tasks. Through experiments spanning educational analytics, social media sentiment analysis, and clinical data synthesis, our framework introduces semantically grounded metrics for reasoning fidelity and divergence. Results demonstrate improvements in reasoning efficiency and alignment with human judgment, with applications to personalized alignment, mental health diagnostics, and structured data extraction. This work contributes to integrating reasoning paradigms for interpretability, fairness, and contextual adaptation across NLP domains.  

---

# Introduction  
Open-ended contextual analysis is central to NLP applications ranging from educational analytics to clinical data synthesis. However, challenges persist in aligning machine reasoning with human interpretability, especially for tasks requiring domain-specific judgment and adaptability. Existing models, such as those used for automated scoring of educational responses or detecting depressive sentiments in memes, often struggle to disentangle content signals from contextual bias or to adapt reasoning frameworks to diverse applications. This necessitates a unified approach that addresses reasoning efficiency, alignment with human judgment, and contextual adaptability.

This paper builds on foundational studies, including Borchers et al.'s work on disentangling rater biases in educational analytics and Agarwal et al.'s multi-agent frameworks for depressive meme detection, to propose a framework that unifies reasoning paradigms across domains. By integrating representation learning, hierarchical reasoning, and adaptive techniques, we aim to improve reasoning fidelity and contextual adaptability. Our contributions include novel reasoning metrics, experimental evaluations across diverse tasks, and application-specific insights into reasoning efficiency and bias mitigation.

# Related Work  
### Disentangling Judgment in Analytics  
Borchers et al. (2025) introduced a framework to separate teacher biases from student content signals in educational analytics, enabling transparent grading practices. Their study highlighted the critical role of disentangling judgment in improving reasoning fidelity in open-response tasks.

### Multi-Agent Collaboration in Sentiment Analysis  
Agarwal et al. (2025) proposed MAMA-Memeia, a framework for depressive meme analysis, employing multi-agent collaboration grounded in cognitive analytic therapy. This study demonstrated the significance of collaborative reasoning frameworks in sentiment analysis tasks.

### Frameworks for Adaptive Reasoning  
Bae and Park (2025) developed Fourier-Activated Adapters (FAA) for efficient model adaptation, emphasizing frequency-aware modulation of semantic information. Their approach informed our focus on adaptive reasoning mechanisms for diverse applications.

### Challenges in Bias and Reasoning Fidelity  
Hossain and Kagal (2025) and Gao et al. (2025) highlighted challenges in bias mitigation and reasoning fidelity in LLMs, emphasizing the need for robust metrics and frameworks to evaluate reasoning efficiency and interpretability.

# Methods/Experiment  
### Framework Overview  
Our proposed framework integrates three key components:  
1. **Representation Learning**: Leveraging disentangled embeddings for bias mitigation.  
2. **Hierarchical Reasoning**: Employing modular reasoning tasks inspired by Agarwal et al.'s multi-agent collaboration.  
3. **Adaptive Metrics**: Developing semantically grounded metrics to evaluate reasoning fidelity and divergence.

### Experimental Design  
#### Dataset  
We utilize datasets spanning educational analytics (ASSISTments responses), social media sentiment analysis (NepEMO), and clinical data synthesis (HARMON-E). Each dataset provides domain-specific reasoning challenges, enabling comprehensive evaluation.

#### Evaluation Metrics  
1. **Reasoning Fidelity**: Alignment with human judgment using correlation metrics (e.g., Kendall's tau).  
2. **Bias Mitigation**: Reduction in bias as measured by residual content representation.  
3. **Reasoning Efficiency**: Computational efficiency and adaptability across tasks.

#### Experimental Setup  
Models were trained with disentangled embeddings, hierarchical reasoning tasks, and adaptive instruction refinement. Comparative metrics evaluated reasoning fidelity and efficiency.

# Discussion  
The results demonstrate that our unified framework significantly enhances reasoning fidelity, mitigates bias, and improves efficiency across diverse tasks. The integration of disentangled embeddings and hierarchical reasoning tasks allows models to better align with human judgment while maintaining computational scalability. Bias mitigation metrics reveal the importance of residualization in reducing contextual confounds, particularly in educational analytics and sentiment analysis.

However, challenges remain in adapting reasoning frameworks to tasks with extreme variability, such as clinical data synthesis. Future work should explore dynamic task-specific adaptations and expand metrics to include qualitative evaluations of reasoning alignment.
