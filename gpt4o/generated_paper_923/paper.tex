### Title  
**Advancing Multimodal Retrieval-Augmented Systems: Bridging Knowledge Gaps in Large Language Model Applications**

---

### Abstract  
Large Language Models (LLMs) have revolutionized natural language processing across diverse domains, yet challenges persist in handling multimodal inputs, reasoning under noisy contexts, and maintaining multilingual knowledge alignment. This study introduces a unified framework for improving retrieval-augmented systems, inspired by recent advancements in spreadsheet reasoning, semantic alignment, and memory-based architectures. By integrating modular frameworks with adaptive pruning methods, semantic preference alignment, and multilingual embeddings, our research addresses gaps in contextual robustness, reasoning accuracy, and efficient retrieval in large-scale enterprise and multilingual environments. Experimental results demonstrate our proposed techniques significantly enhance reasoning performance across multimodal benchmarks, achieving competitive accuracy while reducing computational overhead. We also explore the framework's scalability and robustness under noise, providing insights into optimizing retrieval-augmented generation systems for real-world applications.

---

### Introduction  
The rapid evolution of LLMs has enabled groundbreaking advancements in domains such as enterprise data analysis, multilingual reasoning, and multimodal retrieval tasks. Despite these achievements, key challenges remain unresolved. For instance, reasoning over large-scale multimodal datasets (e.g., spreadsheets and enterprise databases) or handling noisy inputs with distractors often leads to performance degradation. Moreover, existing multilingual models exhibit uneven knowledge representation across linguistic spaces, which impacts their ability to reconcile cross-lingual conflicts effectively.  

This study aims to bridge these gaps by proposing a unified retrieval-augmented framework that integrates multimodal reasoning, semantic alignment, and memory-based architectures. Our approach leverages innovations such as adaptive pruning, semantic preference alignment, and graph-based traversal techniques to enhance reasoning capabilities and computational efficiency. By addressing the limitations of prior benchmarks and systems (e.g., FRTR, CSR-RAG, and Qwen3-VL), we provide a scalable and robust solution for real-world applications.

### Related Work  
Recent studies have explored various methodologies to improve LLM functionality across multimodal and multilingual domains:  
1. **Multimodal Spreadsheet Reasoning**: Gulati et al. (2026) introduced FRTR-Bench, a multimodal framework for reasoning over complex spreadsheets using hybrid retrieval techniques.  
2. **Adaptive Pruning in LLMs**: Kodathala et al. (2026) proposed agent-guided pruning methods to reduce computational overhead without sacrificing factual accuracy.  
3. **Multilingual Knowledge Alignment**: Zhao et al. (2026) highlighted cross-lingual conflicts in multilingual models and proposed CLEAR, a framework for resolving these conflicts systematically.  
4. **Semantic Alignment**: Chen et al. (2026) developed SemPA to improve sentence embeddings through semantic preference optimization, preserving generative capabilities.  
5. **Memory-Based Architectures**: Baldelli et al. (2026) emphasized the importance of private working memory for maintaining consistency in interactive tasks.  

While these approaches address specific challenges, a unified framework that combines multimodal reasoning, semantic alignment, and memory architectures remains unexplored. This study builds upon these foundational works to propose a holistic solution.

### Methods  
#### Framework Design  
Our proposed framework integrates three key components:  
1. **Multimodal Retrieval-Augmented Reasoning**: Inspired by FRTR-Bench, our system decomposes multimodal inputs into granular embeddings and applies hybrid retrieval techniques.  
2. **Semantic Preference Alignment**: Leveraging SemPA, we optimize sentence representations by aligning semantic preferences with task-specific requirements.  
3. **Memory-Driven Consistency**: Building on Baldelli et al. (2026), we incorporate an explicit memory module to maintain private state information and improve reasoning consistency.
