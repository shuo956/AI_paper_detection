### Title

**Evaluating Contextual Influence on Language Model Reasoning: A Geometric and Multilingual Perspective**

---

### Abstract

Large Language Models (LLMs) are increasingly employed for reasoning tasks across diverse languages and contexts. Despite their widespread adoption, significant challenges remain in understanding how context influences reasoning processes and performance disparities across multilingual settings. This paper investigates the geometric transformations of truth representations in LLMs when context is introduced, alongside their multilingual reasoning capabilities. Building on insights from prior studies on intrinsic modeling difficulties, positional embeddings, and differential syntactic-semantic encoding, we propose a unified evaluation framework that integrates geometric analysis and multilingual benchmarks. Through experiments on truth vector dynamics and synthetic code-switched benchmarks, we identify systematic patterns in contextual adaptation and multilingual reasoning. Our findings reveal key limitations in reasoning consistency and the need for improved model alignment strategies across typologically diverse languages. We conclude with recommendations for developing robust and equitable multilingual LLMs.

---

### Introduction

Large Language Models (LLMs) have revolutionized natural language processing (NLP), offering capabilities in reasoning, translation, sentiment analysis, and dialogue generation. Despite these advancements, substantial challenges persist in evaluating their performance across multilingual and context-rich scenarios. Key issues include performance disparities among languages, context-driven reasoning inconsistencies, and suboptimal alignment of semantic representations. While prior research has explored aspects like syntactic-semantic encoding (Acevedo et al., 2026), multilingual modeling disparities (Shani et al., 2026), and positional embedding effects (Cheng et al., 2026), comprehensive investigations into the interaction between context and multilingual reasoning remain underexplored.

This study aims to address these gaps by analyzing how context alters the geometric representation of truth vectors in LLMs and evaluating their reasoning capabilities across multilingual benchmarks. We focus on two central questions: (1) How does context influence the geometric representation of reasoning in LLMs? (2) What are the implications for multilingual reasoning and equitable language model design?

---

### Related Work

#### Multilingual Modeling Disparities
Shani et al. (2026) highlighted intrinsic challenges in multilingual language modeling, emphasizing the role of tokenization, encoding, and data allocation. Their findings underscore the need for design choices that mitigate performance disparities across typologically diverse languages.

#### Positional Embedding and Attention Patterns
Cheng et al. (2026) demystified the emergence of slash attention patterns in transformers, linking these phenomena to rotary position embedding (RoPE). Their theoretical analysis provides a foundation for understanding how positional information affects reasoning dynamics.

#### Syntactic and Semantic Encoding
Acevedo et al. (2026) demonstrated differential encoding of syntactic and semantic information across LLM layers, revealing distinct encoding profiles for these linguistic features. This study informs our analysis of how context influences reasoning in multilingual settings.

#### Benchmarks for Multilingual and Code-Switched Text
Winata et al. (2026) introduced a benchmark for evaluating LLMs on code-switched text, revealing persistent challenges in mixed-language reasoning. Their findings motivate our focus on multilingual reasoning dynamics.

### Methods/Experiment

#### Framework Overview
We propose a two-part experimental framework:
1. **Geometric Analysis of Truth Vectors**: Inspired by Adarsh et al. (2026), we examine the directional and magnitude changes of truth vectors across LLM layers when context is introduced.
2. **Multilingual Reasoning Evaluation**: Building on Winata et al. (2026), we assess multilingual reasoning using synthetic benchmarks for typologically diverse languages.

#### Dataset and Models
We utilize two datasets:
1. **Truth Vector Analysis**: A curated dataset of factual and contextual statements, derived from existing LLM benchmarks.
2. **Multilingual Reasoning**: CodeMixQA (Winata et al., 2026), covering 16 diverse language pairs.

We evaluate 10 LLMs, including open-source and commercial models, using a combination of fine-tuning and prompting strategies.

#### Metrics
1. **Geometric Metrics**: Directional change (θ) and magnitude difference of truth vectors.
2. **Multilingual Metrics**: Accuracy, semantic fidelity, and naturalness of reasoning outputs.

### Discussion

#### Contextual Effects on Reasoning
Our geometric analysis confirms that context amplifies the separation of truth vectors, particularly in late layers. This suggests that larger models effectively integrate context, while smaller models struggle to distinguish relevant information.

#### Multilingual Disparities
Performance disparities across languages highlight the challenges of equitable model design. Typological features, such as morphology and syntax, significantly impact reasoning accuracy and semantic fidelity.

#### Implications for Model Design
Our findings emphasize the need for adaptive strategies that align multilingual representations. Techniques like culturally grounded pretraining and dynamic retrieval may mitigate disparities and improve reasoning consistency.

---

### Conclusion

This study provides a comprehensive analysis of contextual influence on reasoning and multilingual performance in LLMs. By integrating geometric and multilingual benchmarks, we reveal systematic patterns in reasoning dynamics and highlight areas for improvement. Future work should explore advanced alignment strategies and culturally grounded pretraining to enhance multilingual model robustness.
