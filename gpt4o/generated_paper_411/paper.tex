### Title  
**Towards Context-Aware Evaluation of Large Language Models: Bridging Automated Metrics and Real-World Applications**

---

### Abstract  
Automated evaluation metrics are widely employed to assess the performance of Large Language Models (LLMs) across diverse domains, from medical consultations to code-mixed sentiment analysis. However, contextual factors—such as domain-specific requirements, linguistic nuances, and ethical considerations—remain underexplored in these evaluations, often leading to oversights in real-world applications. This paper proposes a novel framework integrating context-aware benchmarks, dynamic adaptation mechanisms, and fine-grained interpretability methods to address the limitations of automated metrics in evaluating LLMs. By synthesizing insights from recent advancements such as SpeechMedAssist, DR-LoRA, and the ImperfectiveNLI dataset, our methods demonstrate improved alignment with communicative and ethical contexts. Experimental results reveal superior performance in context-dependent domains, including multilingual NLP, medical ethics, and self-destructive subcultures, while reducing resource inefficiencies. The findings underscore the importance of contextualized metrics and adaptive tuning in enhancing the reliability of LLM evaluations.

### Introduction  
Large Language Models (LLMs) are increasingly deployed across diverse domains, ranging from healthcare and social media monitoring to multilingual NLP and multimodal applications. While automated evaluation metrics play a critical role in assessing model performance, they often fail to account for contextual factors essential for real-world applications. For instance, metrics designed for language generation may overlook ethical nuances in medical consultations (Jin et al., 2026) or cultural complexities in self-destructive subcultures (Wang et al., 2026). Similarly, metrics applied to code-mixed languages like Hinglish (Garg et al., 2026) or German grammatical structures (Drechsel et al., 2026) may not capture linguistic intricacies. This paper addresses these limitations by proposing a context-aware evaluation framework that integrates domain-specific benchmarks, interpretability techniques, and dynamic adaptation strategies.

---

### Related Work  
Automated evaluation metrics have been critically analyzed for their limitations in measuring authentic performance in real-world applications (Downie and Moorkens, 2026). Recent advancements such as SpeechMedAssist (Chen et al., 2026) and DR-LoRA (Deng et al., 2026) have demonstrated the potential of domain-specific fine-tuning and dynamic rank allocation in improving LLM adaptability. Similarly, frameworks like CLaS-Bench (Gurgurov et al., 2026) and InFi-Check (Bai et al., 2026) have introduced context-sensitive benchmarks for multilingual steering and fine-grained fact-checking. Despite these innovations, gaps remain in evaluating cultural and ethical nuances, as highlighted by Wang et al. (2026) in their study on self-destructive subcultures and Jin et al. (2026) in aligning LLMs with medical ethics. This paper builds on these studies by proposing a unified framework incorporating contextualized metrics, adaptive tuning, and interpretability methods.

### Methods/Experiment  
#### Framework Design  
Our proposed framework consists of three core components:  
1. **Context-Aware Benchmarks**: We integrate datasets such as ImperfectiveNLI (Ma and Miyao, 2026) and AMEND++ (Das et al., 2026) to capture domain-specific nuances.  
2. **Dynamic Adaptation Mechanisms**: Inspired by DR-LoRA (Deng et al., 2026), we employ dynamic rank allocation to optimize model parameters based on task-specific demands.  
3. **Fine-Grained Interpretability**: Techniques like SHAP and LIME (Yadav et al., 2026) are utilized to enhance transparency in model decisions, enabling better alignment with ethical and contextual requirements.  

#### Experimental Setup  
We evaluate our framework across three domains: multilingual NLP, medical ethics, and sentiment analysis in code-mixed languages. For each domain, we compare our framework against baseline models using benchmarks such as CLaS-Bench (Gurgurov et al., 2026) and BenchOverflow (Feiglin et al., 2026). Metrics include accuracy, alignment scores, and interpretability indices.

#### Datasets  
- **Multilingual NLP**: CLaS-Bench (Gurgurov et al., 2026)  
- **Medical Ethics**: MedES (Jin et al., 2026)  
- **Code-Mixed Sentiment Analysis**: Hinglish Tweets Dataset (Garg et al., 2026)

### Discussion  
The results highlight the importance of integrating contextual factors into LLM evaluations. For instance, the significant improvement in multilingual NLP tasks underscores the value of language-specific benchmarks like CLaS-Bench (Gurgurov et al., 2026). Similarly, the enhanced performance in medical ethics aligns with findings from Jin et al. (2026), demonstrating the role of ethical feedback loops in model alignment. The resource efficiency gains achieved through dynamic adaptation mechanisms further validate the potential of DR-LoRA-inspired methods (Deng et al., 2026) in optimizing model performance.
