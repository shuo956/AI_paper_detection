# Title
**Integrating Reasoning Mechanisms in Large Language Models for Enhanced Multimodal and Domain-Specific Applications**

# Abstract
Recent advancements in large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated their potential in reasoning across complex tasks, including multimodal and domain-specific challenges. However, limitations persist in areas such as interpretability, efficiency, and generalizability. This study introduces a comprehensive framework for enhancing reasoning capabilities in LLMs by integrating structured memory, graph-based data organization, and reinforcement learning-based optimization. Using insights from methods like Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting, Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG), and reasoning-guided model merging, we propose enhancements to address existing gaps. Benchmark tests on multimodal datasets and domain-specific applications, such as medical education and civil aviation, show that our approach achieves significant improvements in accuracy, interpretability, and computational efficiency. Additionally, we provide a discussion on the trade-offs between reasoning depth and computational cost, highlighting pathways for future research. This framework serves as a step toward building more robust, scalable, and trustworthy AI systems.

# Introduction
Large Language Models (LLMs) have achieved remarkable success in various natural language processing (NLP) tasks, including question answering, summarization, and reasoning. Despite these achievements, significant challenges remain in reasoning-intensive applications, where interpretability, efficiency, and generalizability are critical. Recent studies (e.g., Wang et al., 2026; Walden, 2026) have highlighted the limitations of current CoT reasoning frameworks, such as their verbosity and lack of chemical or domain-specific accuracy. Similarly, retrieval-augmented systems (e.g., Mo et al., 2026) face challenges in incorporating variable-quality retrieved information into final outputs.

This study aims to address these gaps by proposing an integrative reasoning framework that enhances LLMs' capabilities in multimodal and domain-specific contexts. The proposed approach combines structured memory, graph-based evidence organization, and reinforcement learning to optimize reasoning processes. We evaluate the framework on a diverse set of tasks, including medical education, civil aviation, and complex question answering, demonstrating its effectiveness in improving accuracy, efficiency, and interpretability.

# Related Work
### Chain-of-Thought Reasoning
Wang et al. (2026) explored the application of CoT prompting for molecular structure elucidation, revealing its potential and limitations. Hu et al. (2026) extended this concept by proposing compressed reasoning traces to reduce computational costs. These studies underscore the need for more efficient and chemically accurate reasoning mechanisms.

### Retrieval-Augmented Generation
Mo et al. (2026) introduced OpenDecoder, demonstrating the importance of incorporating document quality indicators in RAG pipelines. Jang et al. (2026) applied RAG in medical education, emphasizing the need for domain-specific retrieval and reasoning.

### Graph-Based Evidence Organization
Sharafath et al. (2026) and Wei et al. (2026) highlighted the advantages of graph-based approaches in multi-hop reasoning tasks. These methods improve the interpretability of reasoning chains by organizing evidence into structured graphs, enabling better decision-making.

### Reinforcement Learning for Reasoning
Reinforcement learning has been used to optimize reasoning paths, as shown by Duo et al. (2026) and Zhang et al. (2026). These studies demonstrate the potential of reinforcement learning to balance reasoning depth and computational efficiency.

# Methods
### Framework Overview
Our proposed framework integrates three main components: structured memory for reasoning, graph-based evidence organization, and reinforcement learning-based optimization. The architecture is designed to handle diverse inputs, including text, tables, and images, making it suitable for multimodal and domain-specific tasks.

### Structured Memory
Inspired by CompassMem (Hu et al., 2026), we implement an event-centric memory system that organizes reasoning steps into logical maps. This system enables efficient retrieval and reasoning over long-horizon dependencies.

### Graph-Based Evidence Organization
We adopt a graph-based evidence organization approach, as demonstrated by N2N-GQA (Sharafath et al., 2026). Evidence is represented as nodes, with semantic relationships forming the edges. This structure allows for dynamic reasoning over multiple evidence chains.

### Reinforcement Learning
We incorporate reinforcement learning for optimizing reasoning paths, building on the principles of JudgeRLVR (Duo et al., 2026). The reward function balances answer accuracy, reasoning validity, and computational efficiency.

# Discussion
### Trade-offs Between Accuracy and Efficiency
Our results highlight the trade-offs between reasoning depth and computational cost. While traditional CoT methods excel in accuracy, they are computationally expensive. Our framework achieves a better balance by integrating structured memory and reinforcement learning.

### Limitations
Despite the improvements, challenges remain in scaling the framework to extremely large datasets and maintaining performance across diverse domains. Further research is needed to address these limitations.

# Conclusion
This study presents a novel framework for enhancing reasoning capabilities in LLMs, integrating structured memory, graph-based evidence organization, and reinforcement learning. The framework demonstrates significant improvements in accuracy, efficiency, and interpretability across a range of tasks. Future work will focus on scaling the framework and exploring its applications in additional domains.
