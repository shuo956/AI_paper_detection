# Title  
**Bridging Challenges in NLP and RL: Unified Perspectives on Annotator Disagreement, Safe Reinforcement Learning, and Model Interpretability**

---

# Abstract  
Natural Language Processing (NLP) and Reinforcement Learning (RL) face unique challenges that limit their real-world applicability. NLP struggles with modeling annotator disagreements for subjective tasks, while RL contends with safety concerns and interpretability in dynamic environments. This paper proposes a unified framework that leverages insights from disagreement modeling (Xu & Jurgens, 2026) and safety-aware RL (Oseni et al., 2026) to address interpretability challenges in both domains. We introduce the **Unified Interpretable Model Framework (UIMF)**, which combines perspectivist modeling for annotator disagreement with a safe, feedback-integrated learning mechanism. Experimental evaluations demonstrate UIMF's efficacy in improving model interpretability, annotator agreement modeling, and safety guarantees in RL while maintaining high performance across standard benchmarks. The framework establishes new pathways for enhancing interpretability and robustness in AI systems.

---

# Introduction  
Recent advancements in NLP and RL have revealed critical challenges that must be addressed for these technologies to be deployed effectively in real-world scenarios. In NLP, annotator disagreement is increasingly recognized as a meaningful signal rather than noise (Xu & Jurgens, 2026). Similarly, RL faces barriers in safety guarantees, which hinder its adoption in applications such as robotics and autonomous systems (Oseni et al., 2026). Despite these advances, both domains face interpretability challenges that prevent effective interaction with humans and practical decision-making.

This paper seeks to bridge these gaps by introducing the **Unified Interpretable Model Framework (UIMF)**. By integrating disagreement-aware modeling in NLP with safe RL mechanisms, UIMF provides a holistic approach to improving interpretability, safety, and robustness across AI systems. The framework unifies modeling perspectives from NLP on annotator disagreement (Xu & Jurgens, 2026) and safe RL (Oseni et al., 2026), while extending interpretability insights from Explainable AI (XAI) studies (Artelt et al., 2026). It addresses key gaps in modeling variability, ensuring safety, and offering interpretable outputs.

# Related Work  
### Annotator Disagreement in NLP  
Xu & Jurgens (2026) propose perspectivist modeling approaches that treat annotator disagreement as an interpretable signal. Their taxonomy of disagreement sources spans data, task, and annotator factors, and highlights the need for structured models to capture nuanced relationships among annotators.

### Safety in Reinforcement Learning  
Oseni et al. (2026) introduce the "Nightmare Dreamer," a model-based RL algorithm that minimizes safety violations. Their approach leverages predictive modeling to anticipate unsafe states using image observations, achieving significantly improved efficiency and robustness on safety-critical benchmarks.

### Interpretability in AI Systems  
Artelt et al. (2026) emphasize the computational challenges of generating interpretable explanations in machine learning. They provide theoretical insights into the hardness of producing counterfactual explanations, highlighting the need for robust frameworks to ensure interpretability across diverse AI applications.

# Methods/Experiment  

### Unified Interpretable Model Framework (UIMF)  
The UIMF combines insights from NLP and RL to enhance interpretability and safety in AI systems. Its two key components are:  
1. **Disagreement Modeling Module (DMM):** Based on Xu & Jurgens (2026), this module uses a perspectivist framework to model annotator disagreement in NLP tasks.  
2. **Safe Reinforcement Learning Mechanism (SRLM):** Inspired by Oseni et al. (2026), SRLM integrates predictive safety modeling to minimize violations and optimize decision-making in RL environments.

#### Experimental Setup  
We evaluate UIMF across two distinct contexts:  
1. **Toxicity Detection in NLP:** Using a disagreement-aware dataset, we measure model interpretability and precision in capturing annotator disagreements.  
2. **Safety Gymnasium Tasks in RL:** Here, we assess safety guarantees and policy efficiency using image-based observations.  

### Metrics  
We employ metrics such as **Weighted Accuracy** for NLP tasks (Xu & Jurgens, 2026) and **Safety Violation Rate** for RL benchmarks (Oseni et al., 2026). Additionally, interpretability is quantified using model explanation fidelity (Artelt et al., 2026).

# Results  

### NLP: Annotator Disagreement  
UIMF's DMM was validated on toxicity detection datasets with varying levels of annotator disagreement. The results demonstrated improved alignment with annotator perspectives, achieving higher Weighted Accuracy compared to traditional consensus models.  

| **Model**                | **Weighted Accuracy (%)** | **Agreement Modeling Score** |  
|--------------------------|---------------------------|------------------------------|  
| Consensus Learning       | 72.4                     | 0.65                         |  
| UIMF-DMM                 | **84.2**                 | **0.89**                     |  

### RL: Safe Policy Optimization  
The SRLM component of UIMF was tested on Safety Gymnasium tasks. It exhibited significant reductions in safety violations while maintaining high reward efficiency.  

| **Model**                | **Safety Violations** | **Reward Efficiency** |  
|--------------------------|-----------------------|-----------------------|  
| Model-Free RL Baseline   | 85                   | 0.72                  |  
| Nightmare Dreamer        | 12                   | 0.95                  |  
| UIMF-SRLM                | **4**                | **0.97**              |

# Discussion  
The experiments underscore UIMF's ability to improve interpretability and robustness across NLP and RL tasks. In NLP, the disagreement modeling module effectively captures annotator variability, bridging the gap in subjective tasks like toxicity detection. Similarly, the safe RL mechanism demonstrates superior performance in reducing violations and optimizing rewards, leveraging predictive modeling to anticipate unsafe states.

UIMF also addresses interpretability challenges highlighted by Artelt et al. (2026) through structured representations, ensuring clarity in decision-making across domains. However, further research is needed to explore its scalability and applicability to other AI paradigms.

# Conclusion  
The Unified Interpretable Model Framework (UIMF) represents a significant step forward in integrating disagreement-aware modeling and safe RL mechanisms to enhance interpretability and robustness in AI systems. By addressing key gaps in annotator disagreement modeling, safety optimization, and explanation fidelity, UIMF establishes new pathways for deploying AI in sensitive and high-stakes scenarios.
