### Gaps in Existing Research

1. **Interpretable and Explainable Models**: While many studies focus on improving the performance of machine learning models, there is a growing need for models that are not only accurate but also interpretable and explainable. This is particularly important in fields like healthcare, power systems, and natural sciences where understanding the underlying mechanisms is crucial.

2. **Handling High-Dimensional Data and Complex Dynamics**: Existing methods often struggle with high-dimensional data and complex dynamical systems. There is a need for frameworks that can effectively handle such data while providing robust and reliable predictions.

3. **Scalability and Adaptability**: Many existing models are not well-suited for systems with varying scales or dynamic environments. Developing models that can adapt to different scales and remain robust under changing conditions is a significant challenge.

4. **Integration of Domain Knowledge**: Incorporating structured domain knowledge into machine learning models can improve their performance and robustness. However, there is limited work on how to effectively encode and utilize such knowledge.

5. **Efficient Learning from Noisy and Incomplete Data**: Handling noisy and incomplete data is a common challenge in real-world applications. Developing methods that can effectively learn from such data without requiring extensive preprocessing is an area of active research.

### Motivation for New Study

Given these gaps, the proposed new study aims to develop a framework that addresses these challenges by integrating structured domain knowledge, handling high-dimensional and complex data, and ensuring scalability and adaptability. Specifically, the study will focus on developing a scalable, interpretable, and robust framework for power flow analysis in power systems.

### Main Idea for New Research Work

The main idea is to propose a Scale-Adaptive Power Flow Analysis (SaMPFA) framework that incorporates a Local Topology Slicing (LTS) technique and a Reference-free Multi-task Graph Learning (RMGL) model. The LTS technique will extract subgraphs of different scales from the complete power network to enhance cross-scale learning capabilities. The RMGL model will predict bus voltages and branch powers directly, avoiding the risk of error amplification in branch power calculations. Additionally, the loss function will encourage the model to capture physical patterns of angle differences and power transmission.
