# Introduction
ReCAPTCHA is a vital component in web security, designed to distinguish humans from bots by requiring users to complete a series of tasks or solve puzzles. While effective, traditional reCAPTCHA systems often suffer from high false-positive rates, leading to user dissatisfaction and inconvenience. This issue is particularly acute as more websites adopt advanced security measures. To address this challenge, we propose an innovative approach utilizing machine learning techniques to improve the accuracy of reCAPTCHA systems.

---

# Related Work
Previous research has explored various methods to enhance reCAPTCHA accuracy. For instance, Chen et al. (2021) proposed a hybrid model combining classical machine learning algorithms and neural networks, achieving a notable improvement in accuracy but still facing limitations in handling complex CAPTCHA images. Similarly, Li et al. (2022) developed a deep learning-based approach that utilized convolutional neural networks (CNNs) for image recognition, yet the model's performance was constrained by the size of the training dataset. Our work builds upon these studies by leveraging more sophisticated deep learning architectures and larger datasets to achieve superior accuracy.

---

# Methods/Experiment
Our proposed method involves training a deep learning model on a large dataset of labeled CAPTCHA images and user interactions. The dataset includes a variety of CAPTCHA types, such as distorted text, geometric shapes, and complex puzzles. The model architecture consists of a combination of convolutional layers, max-pooling layers, and fully connected layers. We employ transfer learning to initialize the model weights using pre-trained CNNs, followed by fine-tuning on our specific dataset.

The experiment was conducted on a subset of the dataset, with 70% used for training and 30% for testing. The performance of the model was evaluated using metrics such as accuracy, precision, recall, and F1-score. The training process involved optimizing the model using stochastic gradient descent (SGD) with a learning rate of 0.001 and a batch size of 32.
