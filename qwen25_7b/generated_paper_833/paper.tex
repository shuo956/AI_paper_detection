\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
The proliferation of deep learning has led to impressive achievements in various fields, but its effectiveness often hinges on large datasets. In real-world applications, however, acquiring sufficient labeled data can be challenging and costly. Consequently, the ability to fine-tune pre-trained models with minimal data becomes crucial. Existing approaches such as transfer learning and data augmentation have shown promise but face limitations in terms of computational resources and generalization across different domains. This paper addresses these challenges by proposing a novel framework for fine-tuning pre-trained models in low-data scenarios.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related-work}
Recent studies have explored various strategies to improve model performance in low-data settings. Transfer learning \cite{pan2009survey} involves leveraging a pre-trained model on a large dataset and fine-tuning it on a smaller target dataset. Data augmentation techniques \cite{zoph2017learning} aim to artificially expand the training dataset through transformations. However, these methods often require substantial computational resources and may not generalize well across different domains. Another line of research focuses on developing more efficient training algorithms \cite{liu2019few} and regularization techniques \cite{zhang2016understanding} to improve model performance with limited data.

\section{Methods/Experiment}
\label{sec:methods}
Our proposed framework consists of two key components: knowledge distillation and adaptive regularization. Knowledge distillation \cite{hinton2015distilling} involves transferring the knowledge from a larger, pre-trained model to a smaller, target model. Adaptive regularization \cite{wu2018rethinking} dynamically adjusts the regularization strength based on the available data. We hypothesize that combining these techniques will lead to better fine-tuning results in low-data scenarios.

\subsection{Knowledge Distillation}
During the fine-tuning process, the target model is trained to mimic the predictions of a larger, pre-trained model. This can be achieved through a soft-target loss function that measures the similarity between the target model's outputs and the pre-trained model's outputs.

\subsection{Adaptive Regularization}
Regularization is crucial for preventing overfitting, especially when the amount of data is limited. Our approach dynamically adjusts the regularization strength based on the number of available data points. This ensures that the model remains robust even when the training data is scarce.
