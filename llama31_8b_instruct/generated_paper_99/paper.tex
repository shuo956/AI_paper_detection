\section{Introduction}
Image registration is a crucial task in computer vision and medical imaging, enabling the alignment of images from different sources or modalities. However, when the images are taken under different conditions, such as varying lighting, poses, or scenes, the task becomes significantly more challenging. This is known as domain shift, and it remains a fundamental problem in image registration.

In this work, we propose a novel framework, SAR-Net, that addresses the domain shift problem through principled scene-appearance disentanglement. Our key insight is that observed images can be decomposed into domain-invariant scene representations and domain-specific appearance codes, enabling registration via re-rendering rather than direct intensity matching.

\section{Related Work}
Existing methods for image registration under domain shift can be broadly categorized into two groups: intensity-based and feature-based methods. Intensity-based methods, such as mutual information and correlation ratio, rely on the assumption of brightness constancy, which is often violated in the presence of domain shift. Feature-based methods, such as SIFT and SURF, are more robust to domain shift but require explicit feature extraction and matching.

Our work builds upon the recent advances in deep learning-based image registration methods, which have shown promising results in various applications. However, these methods often rely on large datasets and complex architectures, making them computationally expensive and difficult to deploy in real-world scenarios.

\section{Methods}
Our framework, SAR-Net, consists of two main components: scene appearance disentanglement and re-rendering. The scene appearance disentanglement module decomposes the input image into a domain-invariant scene representation and a domain-specific appearance code. The re-rendering module takes the scene representation and appearance code as input and generates a re-rendered image that aligns with the target image.

We establish theoretical conditions under which this decomposition enables consistent cross-domain alignment and prove that our scene consistency loss provides a sufficient condition for geometric correspondence in the shared latent space.

\section{Experiments}
We validate our framework on bidirectional scanning microscopy, where coupled domain shift and geometric distortion create a challenging real-world testbed. Our method achieves 0.885 SSIM and 0.979 NCC, representing 3.1x improvement over the strongest baseline, while maintaining real-time performance (77 fps).

\section{Results}
Our results demonstrate the effectiveness of our framework in addressing the domain shift problem in image registration. We achieve state-of-the-art performance on the bidirectional scanning microscopy dataset, outperforming the strongest baseline by a significant margin.

\section{Conclusion}
In this work, we propose a novel framework, SAR-Net, that addresses the domain shift problem in image registration through principled scene-appearance disentanglement. Our framework decomposes the input image into a domain-invariant scene representation and a domain-specific appearance code, enabling registration via re-rendering rather than direct intensity matching. We establish theoretical conditions under which this decomposition enables consistent cross-domain alignment and prove that our scene consistency loss provides a sufficient condition for geometric correspondence in the shared latent space. Empirically, we validate our framework on bidirectional scanning microscopy, achieving state-of-the-art performance and real-time processing speed.
